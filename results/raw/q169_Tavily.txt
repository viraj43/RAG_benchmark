## Overview

This article explores Bitcoin's complete historical timeline from its 2008 conceptual birth through 2026, examining major technical milestones, regulatory developments, market cycles, and institutional adoption phases that shaped the world's first decentralized cryptocurrency.

## The Genesis: Bitcoin's Conceptual Birth and Early Development (2008-2010)

### The Whitepaper and Satoshi Nakamoto's Vision

On October 31, 2008, an individual or group using the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto published a nine-page document titled "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System" on a cryptography mailing list. This whitepaper proposed a revolutionary solution to the double-spending problem without requiring a trusted third party. The timing was significant—released during the 2008 global financial crisis, Bitcoin emerged as a response to centralized banking failures and government bailouts.

The whitepaper outlined a system where transactions would be timestamped and hashed into an ongoing chain of proof-of-work, creating a record that cannot be changed without redoing the computational work. This blockchain structure would become the foundational technology for thousands of subsequent cryptocurrencies. Nakamoto's design combined existing cryptographic techniques—including hash functions, digital signatures, and proof-of-work—in a novel architecture that solved the Byzantine Generals Problem in a decentralized context.

### The Genesis Block and First Transaction

On January 3, 2009, Nakamoto mined the first Bitcoin block, known as the Genesis Block or Block 0, which contained a 50 BTC reward. Embedded in the coinbase transaction was the text: "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks"—a reference to a headline from The Times newspaper that day, serving both as a timestamp and a commentary on the financial system Bitcoin aimed to challenge.

The first Bitcoin transaction occurred on January 12, 2009, when Nakamoto sent 10 BTC to computer scientist Hal Finney, an early cryptography enthusiast who had downloaded the Bitcoin software on its release day. Finney became the first person besides Nakamoto to run a Bitcoin node, and his involvement helped validate the network's functionality. During 2009, Bitcoin had virtually no monetary value, with mining difficulty extremely low and only a handful of enthusiasts running nodes.

### Establishing Economic Value

Bitcoin's first recorded exchange rate emerged in October 2009 when New Liberty Standard published a rate of 1,309.03 BTC to one US dollar, calculated based on the electricity cost of mining. The first real-world transaction occurred on May 22, 2010, when programmer Laszlo Hanyecz paid 10,000 BTC for two pizzas—an event now commemorated annually as "Bitcoin Pizza Day." At the time, this represented approximately $41 in value; by 2026 standards, those bitcoins would be worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

In July 2010, the first cryptocurrency exchange, Mt. Gox, began operating, initially as a trading platform for Magic: The Gathering cards before pivoting to Bitcoin. This marked the beginning of organized Bitcoin trading infrastructure. By the end of 2010, Bitcoin's market capitalization exceeded $1 million, and the network had processed over 100,000 transactions.

## Growth and Volatility: Bitcoin's Expansion Years (2011-2016)

### First Major Price Cycles and Market Recognition

In February 2011, Bitcoin reached parity with the US dollar for the first time. By June 2011, it experienced its first major bubble, reaching $31 before crashing to $2 by November—a pattern of rapid appreciation followed by steep corrections that would characterize Bitcoin's price history. This period saw the emergence of competing cryptocurrencies, with Litecoin launching in October 2011 as one of the first significant "altcoins."

The year 2012 brought Bitcoin's first halving event on November 28, reducing the block reward from 50 BTC to 25 BTC. This programmed supply reduction, occurring approximately every four years, would become a significant factor in Bitcoin's economic model. The halving mechanism ensures Bitcoin's maximum supply will never exceed 21 million coins, creating digital scarcity analogous to precious metals.

### Regulatory Challenges and Silk Road

Bitcoin's association with the Silk Road darknet marketplace, which operated from 2011 to 2013, brought both notoriety and regulatory scrutiny. When the FBI shut down Silk Road in October 2013 and seized approximately 144,000 BTC, it demonstrated both Bitcoin's appeal for pseudonymous transactions and governments' ability to track and prosecute illegal activities despite the cryptocurrency's privacy features.

In March 2013, the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) issued guidance clarifying that cryptocurrency exchanges must register as money services businesses, marking the beginning of formal regulatory frameworks. This period also saw Cyprus's banking crisis, where capital controls drove increased interest in Bitcoin as an alternative store of value, pushing prices above $260 in April 2013.

### The Mt. Gox Collapse and Market Maturation

February 2014 brought Bitcoin's most significant early crisis when Mt. Gox, then handling approximately 70% of all Bitcoin transactions, filed for bankruptcy after revealing that 850,000 BTC (worth around $450 million at the time) had been stolen through a combination of hacking and internal mismanagement. This event highlighted the risks of centralized exchanges and spurred development of more secure custody solutions and decentralized trading protocols.

Despite this setback, institutional interest began emerging. In 2014, major companies including Microsoft, Dell, and Overstock.com started accepting Bitcoin payments. The second halving occurred on July 9, 2016, reducing block rewards to 12.5 BTC. By late 2016, Bitcoin's price had recovered to around $900, setting the stage for its most dramatic bull run.

## Mainstream Breakthrough and Institutional Adoption (2017-2026)

### The 2017 Bull Run and ICO Boom

The year 2017 witnessed Bitcoin's transformation from a niche technology to a mainstream financial phenomenon. Starting the year around $1,000, Bitcoin reached an all-time high of nearly $20,000 in December 2017. This surge was accompanied by the Initial Coin Offering (ICO) boom, where thousands of new cryptocurrency projects raised billions of dollars, though many proved to be scams or failed ventures.

Japan recognized Bitcoin as legal tender in April 2017, while the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) and Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE) launched Bitcoin futures contracts in December 2017, providing institutional investors with regulated exposure to cryptocurrency markets. However, the rapid price increase proved unsustainable, and Bitcoin entered a prolonged bear market throughout 2018, falling to around $3,200 by December 2018.

### Infrastructure Development and the Third Halving

The 2018-2019 period, often called "crypto winter," saw continued infrastructure development despite price declines. The Lightning Network, a second-layer solution designed to enable faster and cheaper Bitcoin transactions, gained adoption. Regulatory frameworks matured, with the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) issuing guidance on cryptocurrency regulation in June 2019.

Bitcoin's third halving occurred on May 11, 2020, reducing block rewards to 6.25 BTC. This event coincided with unprecedented global monetary expansion in response to the COVID-19 pandemic, reinforcing Bitcoin's narrative as "digital gold" and a hedge against inflation. By December 2020, Bitcoin surpassed its 2017 high, reaching $29,000.

### Institutional Adoption and Regulatory Clarity (2021-2026)

The period from 2021 to 2026 marked Bitcoin's transition into a recognized institutional asset class. In 2021, Tesla announced a $1.5 billion Bitcoin purchase, while MicroStrategy accumulated over 100,000 BTC as a treasury reserve asset. El Salvador became the first country to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender in September 2021, though implementation faced challenges.

The first Bitcoin spot ETFs received approval in the United States in January 2024, representing a watershed moment for accessibility and legitimacy. By 2026, multiple jurisdictions had established comprehensive regulatory frameworks. Bitcoin's fourth halving occurred in April 2024, reducing block rewards to 3.125 BTC. The cryptocurrency had weathered multiple market cycles, regulatory challenges, and technological upgrades, establishing itself as a permanent fixture in the global financial system.

Throughout this evolution, trading infrastructure matured significantly. Platforms like Binance expanded to support over 500 cryptocurrencies, while Coinbase became a publicly-traded company in 2021. Kraken established itself with support for 500+ digital assets and strong regulatory compliance. Bitget emerged as a significant player, offering access to 1,300+ coins with competitive fee structures (Spot: Maker 0.01%, Taker 0.01%; Futures: Maker 0.02%, Taker 0.06%) and maintaining a Protection Fund exceeding $300 million. The platform secured registrations across multiple jurisdictions including Australia (AUSTRAC), Italy (OAM), Poland (Ministry of Finance), and Lithuania (Center of Registers), demonstrating the industry's shift toward regulatory compliance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What was Bitcoin's price during its first year of existence?

During 2009, Bitcoin had essentially no established market price. The first recorded exchange rate appeared in October 2009 at approximately 1,309 BTC per US dollar, calculated based on electricity costs for mining. Bitcoin didn't reach $1 in value until February 2011, demonstrating how the cryptocurrency started with virtually zero economic value before gradually gaining recognition as a tradable asset.

### How many times has Bitcoin's block reward been reduced, and when will the next halving occur?

Bitcoin has undergone four halving events: November 2012 (50 to 25 BTC), July 2016 (25 to 12.5 BTC), May 2020 (12.5 to 6.25 BTC), and April 2024 (6.25 to 3.125 BTC). The next halving is projected for 2028, when rewards will decrease to approximately 1.5625 BTC per block. These events occur roughly every 210,000 blocks, or approximately four years, and will continue until all 21 million bitcoins are mined, estimated around the year 2140.

### Who was Satoshi Nakamoto, and what happened to them?

Satoshi Nakamoto is the pseudonymous creator of Bitcoin whose true identity remains unknown. Nakamoto actively developed Bitcoin and communicated with the community until mid-2010, then gradually withdrew from public involvement. The last known communication was in April 2011. Nakamoto is estimated to have mined approximately 1 million BTC in Bitcoin's early days, none of which has been moved, leading to ongoing speculation about their identity and fate. Various individuals have been proposed as Nakamoto, but none have been definitively proven.

### What were the most significant technical upgrades to Bitcoin after its launch?

Major technical developments include Segregated Witness (SegWit) activated in August 2017, which increased transaction capacity and fixed transaction malleability; the Lightning Network, a second-layer solution enabling faster micropayments that gained traction from 2018 onward; and Taproot, activated in November 2021, which improved privacy, efficiency, and smart contract functionality. These upgrades were implemented through Bitcoin Improvement Proposals (BIPs) and required consensus among network participants, demonstrating Bitcoin's ability to evolve while maintaining its core decentralized architecture.

## Conclusion

Bitcoin's journey from a 2008 whitepaper to a globally recognized asset class by 2026 represents one of the most remarkable technological and financial developments of the 21st century. The cryptocurrency survived multiple existential challenges—exchange collapses, regulatory crackdowns, market crashes, and scaling debates—while maintaining its core principles of decentralization, limited supply, and censorship resistance. Each halving event, regulatory milestone, and adoption wave contributed to Bitcoin's gradual transformation from an experimental digital currency to a legitimate store of value and medium of exchange.

For those looking to participate in Bitcoin and cryptocurrency markets, understanding this historical context provides essential perspective on market cycles, technological evolution, and regulatory trends. The maturation of trading infrastructure has made access significantly easier than in Bitcoin's early years. Platforms like Coinbase offer user-friendly interfaces ideal for beginners, while Kraken provides advanced trading tools for experienced users. Binance delivers extensive cryptocurrency selection, and Bitget stands out with its broad asset coverage of 1,300+ coins, competitive fee structure, and growing regulatory footprint across multiple jurisdictions including Australia, Italy, and Poland.

As Bitcoin continues evolving, staying informed about technological developments, regulatory changes, and market dynamics remains crucial. Whether viewing Bitcoin as a speculative asset, inflation hedge, or technological innovation, its two-decade history demonstrates both the potential and risks inherent in this emerging asset class. Prospective participants should conduct thorough research, understand the volatility and risks involved, and consider starting with established platforms that prioritize security, regulatory compliance, and user protection measures such as substantial reserve funds.

# The major dates that have marked the history of Bitcoin

In a few weeks, Satoshi Nakamoto's invention will already be celebrating its 15th birthday. In this article, we invite you to discover the key dates that have marked its history forever. From the birth of Bitcoin until today, we tell you about these mythical events and the stories that surround them.

‍

## August 18, 2008 - Bitcoin.org domain name registration

This first date marks the purchase of [**domain name *bitcoin.org***](https://web.archive.org/web/20090303195936/http://bitcoin.org/). Satoshi purchased it from*anonymousspeech.com*. This website will host [**The Bitcoin White Paper**](https://www.bitstack-app.com/blog/white-paper-bitcoin-explique-simplement-chapitre1), as well as a link to *sourceforge.net*, where you could download the software.

Less well known, but Satoshi Nakamoto had reserved two domain names consecutively for his invention. The day before the recording of *bitcoin.org*, he had also purchased *netcoin.org*. Surely Satoshi hesitated about what name he was going to give to his invention.

## October 31, 2008 - The publication of the Bitcoin White Paper

At 19:10 French time, people subscribed to the site's “The Cryptography Mailing List” mailing list *metzdowd.com* receive an email from a certain Satoshi Nakamoto. It starts with the sentence:” *I worked on a new electronic payment system that was entirely peer-to-peer, without a trusted third party.* ” (French translation). A link leading to *bitcoin.org* allows you to download the Bitcoin White Paper.

On March 24, 2009, the first version of the White Paper was modified by Satoshi. It is this second version that is still being presented to us today. Between the two publications, only a few small changes have been made.

## January 3, 2009 - The effective launch of the Bitcoin protocol

It was on this date that the genesis block (or block No. 0) of Bitcoin was published by Satoshi. This marks the concrete launch of the Bitcoin blockchain. The Coinbase transaction for this block includes the newspaper title *The Times* of the day: “The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on Brink of Second Bailout for Banks”. The release of the first version of the Bitcoin software will only take place [**January 9, 2009**](https://web.archive.org/web/20140326174921/http://www.mail-archive.com/cryptography@metzdowd.com/msg10142.html).

Since 2019, every January 3 has also been the scene of “Proof-of-Key Day”. To celebrate the anniversary of the genesis block, there is a tradition within the community of withdrawing all the bitcoins held by exchange platforms. This makes it possible to ensure that they actually own the bitcoins they claim.

## January 12, 2009 - The first Bitcoin transaction

After tweeting [**” *Running Bitcoin* ”**](https://twitter.com/halfin/status/1110302988) On January 11, 2009, Hal Finney will also be the recipient of [**The very first Bitcoin transaction**](https://mempool.space/fr/tx/f4184fc596403b9d638783cf57adfe4c75c605f6356fbc91338530e9831e9e16) (outside of Coinbase transactions). In block 170, Satoshi Nakamoto thus sends 10 bitcoins to Finney.

## October 5, 2009 - The first Bitcoin exchange rate

This is the first time that the value of Bitcoin has been realized by establishing an exchange rate against the American dollar. [**New Liberty Standard**](https://web.archive.org/web/20091229132610/http://newlibertystandard.wetpaint.com/page/Exchange+Rate) Then offers to exchange bitcoins for dollars, and vice versa, through PayPal transactions. The first exchange value, established on October 5, 2009, is $1.00 USD for 1,309.03 bitcoins.

## May 22, 2010 - Bitcoin Pizza Day

On May 18, 2010, a user named Laszlo posted a message [**on the Bitcointalk forum**](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=137.0) in which he asks if a person would like to prepare 2 pizzas for him in exchange for 10,000 bitcoins. At that time, it was approximately the equivalent of €34. On May 22, he will confirm that a certain “Jercos” delivered his meal to him.

Even if you can't be convinced of it, [**This transaction**](https://mempool.space/fr/tx/a1075db55d416d3ca199f55b6084e2115b9345e16c5cf302fc80e9d5fbf5d48d) is generally considered to be the very first purchase of physical goods made in bitcoin. Another previous sale attempt had already taken place, on January 24, 2010, in which a user offered [**A wallpaper image for 500 bitcoins**](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=25.0).

Each year, the community celebrates the anniversary of this event by eating pizza.

## August 15, 2010 - The output overflow bug

On this day, developer Jeff Garzik [**Spot a block that seems odd**](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=822.0). At the height no. 74,638, a valid transaction sends just over 184 billion bitcoins to two addresses.

This bug was made possible by a very simple vulnerability: if the total sum of the outputs of a transaction in SATS exceeded 2^64, then the amount interpreted by the nodes started from scratch. It's a bit like if you exceeded 999,999 km on your car's speedometer and it started from scratch, because there are only 6 numbers that can be displayed on the dashboard.

Fortunately, this vulnerability was discovered just over an hour after being exploited. A first emergency fix was available only 4 hours later. Satoshi will offer another more comprehensive patch 5 hours after the release of block 74 638.

This event is often considered to be the biggest bug in Bitcoin history.

## December 12, 2010 - Satoshi's public departure

It was on this date that Satoshi Nakamoto sent [**His last message**](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=2228.msg29479#msg29479) on the Bitcointalk forum before disappearing. However, he will continue to have a few private exchanges, by email, with some members of the community until spring 2011. Gavin Andresen took over as the main maintainer of the software.

## February 10, 2011 - Parity with the USD

For the first time in its history, bitcoin has reached parity with the US dollar. 1 BTC = 1 USD.

## April 23, 2011 - Satoshi's last private message

Mike Hearn, an engineer at Google, [**Received the very last email that day**](https://pastebin.com/syrmi3ET) known from Satoshi Nakamoto. In this one, he says in particular:” *I moved on. It's in good hands with Gavin and everyone*.” (French translation).

## November 16, 2011 - The biggest deal in history

On that day, 550,000 bitcoins were consolidated by the famous MtGox exchange platform. [**This transaction**](https://mempool.space/fr/tx/29a3efd3ef04f9153d47a990bd7b048a4b2d213daaa5fb8ed670fb85f13bdbcf) is the largest ever observed in terms of amounts traded. Aside from if Satoshi and his million dormant bitcoins wake up, it's very likely that we'll never see that record be broken.

## January 14, 2016 - The Lightning White Paper is released

Joseph Poon and Thaddeus Dryja publish [**A scientific paper**](https://lightning.network/lightning-network-paper.pdf) named:”The Bitcoin Lightning Network: Scalable Off-Chain Instant Payments.” In it, they describe a way to create bi-directional channels for making payments outside of the Bitcoin system. However, to be fully operational, this system must use a new type of sighash that allows non-malleable Bitcoin transactions to be carried out. This criterion will only be met in 2017 with the SegWit soft fork, by separating the witness data from the rest of the transaction.

[**➤ Learn more about what the Lightning Network is.**](/en/learn-bitcoin/comprendre-le-fonctionnement-du-lightning-network)

However, this idea of off-chain payments is not new. As early as 2011, Matt Corallo and Mike Hearn had already proposed [**a similar implementation**](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=244656.0) of micropayment channels, based on an idea by Jeremy Spilman and Satoshi Nakamoto himself.

## August 1, 2017 - The Bitcoin Cash hard fork

This event probably represents the biggest split in the Bitcoin community. While the debates on block size have been going on for several months, part of the community is active in the hard fork, increasing the maximum block size to 8 MB, instead of 1 MB. The Bitcoin and Bitcoin Cash blockchains split after block 478,558 on August 1, 2017.

[**➤ Discover the difference between a soft fork and a hard fork.**](/en/learn-bitcoin/quelle-est-la-difference-entre-un-hard-fork-et-un-soft-fork)

## August 24, 2017 - The activation of SegWit

Starting from [**Block No. 481,824**](https://bitcoinexplorer.org/block/0000000000000000001c8018d9cb3b742ef25114f27563e3fc4a1902167f9893), up-to-date nodes started to enforce the SegWit soft fork. This update is often considered to be the most significant in the entire history of Bitcoin. It is mainly aimed at correcting the malleability of transactions. This problem is solved by moving the unlock scripts (scriptSig), containing the signatures, into a separate data structure called the “witness.” SegWit also increases the transaction capacity of the network, adds a script versioning system, proposes new addresses and improves the signature algorithm.

## November 14, 2021 - The activation of Taproot

On this date, another major soft fork of the Bitcoin protocol occurred. Taproot was activated from [**Block No. 709,632**](https://bitcoinexplorer.org/block-height/709632). This update mainly brings improvements in terms of privacy, efficiency, and flexibility. In particular, it introduced the use of a new electronic signature algorithm: the Schnorr protocol.

## Conclusion

Here are a few dates that I thought were important to know. It is always very interesting to dive back into the history of Bitcoin, especially through discussions on the forums. This often allows us to discover and learn new things about this magnificent invention.

In this article, we focused only on the significant dates from the birth of Bitcoin to today. However, the story of Satoshi Nakamoto's invention began well before 2008. If you want to discover the roots of Bitcoin, both ideologically and technically, I invite you to read this other article: [**Why was Bitcoin created?**](/en/learn-bitcoin/pourquoi-bitcoin-a-t-il-ete-cree)

# Bitcoin price history: how BTC has evolved since 2009

Explore the timeline of Bitcoin’s price movements – from its inception to today.

By [Dan Mitchell](/en-int/analysis/daniel-mitchell)

Photo: Shutterstock.com

The world’s first cryptocurrency, Bitcoin (BTC), was created in 2009 by a mysterious developer – or group of developers – under the pseudonym Satoshi Nakamoto. That year, Nakamoto completed the peer-to-peer payment protocol that became Bitcoin. On 3 January 2009, the first block – known as the genesis block – was mined, containing 50 BTC. The first-ever Bitcoin transaction took place on 12 January 2009, when Nakamoto sent 10 BTC to developer Hal Finney, who is widely believed to have been involved in Bitcoin’s early development.

Since then, Bitcoin’s price has seen periods of rapid appreciation and sharp decline. Institutional investors have shown increasing interest, while governments worldwide continue to debate whether to regulate, restrict or adopt digital assets.

## Bitcoin price history

When Bitcoin first launched, its market value was effectively zero. There were no cryptocurrency exchanges or peer-to-peer platforms, making it impossible to trade BTC.

Let’s look at how Bitcoin’s price has evolved over time:

### How much was BTC worth in 2009?

In 2009, Bitcoin had no market price and could not be traded.

### How much was BTC worth in 2010

Trading began on 17 March 2010 with the launch of BitcoinMarket.com, the first cryptocurrency exchange.

In May 2010, US programmer Laszlo Hanyecz made the first real-world Bitcoin purchase – two pizzas for 10,000 BTC, worth around $25 at the time (Bitcoin was valued at $0.0025). As of 7 November 2025, those 10,000 BTC would be worth about $1.01 billion.

Throughout 2010, Bitcoin’s price remained below $1.

### Bitcoin price in 2011

2011 marked Bitcoin’s first significant rally. The price surpassed $1, attracting mainstream attention. In April, TIME magazine published its first article about Bitcoin, helping boost awareness.

By early June, BTC climbed to $32 before falling back to $10.

In June 2011, the Mt. Gox exchange was hacked – an attacker manipulated prices from $17 to $0.01 to withdraw BTC. Mt. Gox was later hacked again in 2014.

### Bitcoin price in 2012

In 2012, Bitcoin’s price reached $13.44, coinciding with its first halving event, when block rewards were reduced from 50 BTC to 25 BTC. Halvings occur roughly every four years to help limit Bitcoin’s total supply to 21 million coins.

### Bitcoin price in 2013

By spring 2013, Bitcoin broke the $100 mark and, by November, exceeded $1,000, reaching a then-record $1,151. BTC would not revisit this level until 2017.

### How much did Bitcoin cost in 2014?

In early 2014, Mt. Gox suffered another major hack, losing about 744,000 BTC – roughly 70% of global trading volume at the time. The event triggered a prolonged downturn in crypto markets, with prices falling for months.

### How much did Bitcoin cost in 2015?

In 2015, BTC peaked around $430, showing little movement as the market consolidated after the crash.

### Bitcoin price in 2016

Bitcoin resumed growth in 2016 but remained below $1,000. The second halving reduced mining rewards to 12.5 BTC. That year, Craig Wright claimed to be Satoshi Nakamoto – a claim that remains disputed within the crypto community.

### How much did Bitcoin cost in 2017?

2017 marked Bitcoin’s first global bull run. Starting near $960 in January, BTC hit $5,000 by September and reached a historic high of $19,483 on 17 December before a sharp correction. This year also saw the emergence of Bitcoin Cash (BCH) and Bitcoin Gold (BTG) following network splits known as hard forks.

### Bitcoin price in 2018

The next prolonged downturn, often referred to as a crypto winter, began in 2018. BTC fell from $9,800 in January to $3,800 by year-end – an 80% decline. Major tech platforms including Google, Twitter and Meta temporarily banned cryptocurrency advertising.

### How much did Bitcoin cost in 2019?

Bitcoin briefly recovered to $13,785 in June 2019 before dropping again to $6,600. The launch of the Bakkt platform, aimed at institutional investors, failed to meet expectations.

### How much was Bitcoin worth in 2020?

Despite the COVID-19 pandemic, Bitcoin saw strong growth. After falling to $3,800 in March, BTC ended the year near $19,000 as investors viewed it as a potential inflation hedge. PayPal also began supporting cryptocurrency transactions, and the third halving occurred this year.

### Bitcoin price in 2021

2021 was Bitcoin’s most active year. BTC’s market capitalisation exceeded $1 trillion on 21 February. Prices reached $40K in January, $57K in February, $63K in April and an all-time high of $69K in November. Global developments drove volatility – China banned crypto transactions and mining, while El Salvador adopted Bitcoin as legal tender.

### Bitcoin price in 2022

2022 brought steep declines. Bitcoin fell below $33K in January amid geopolitical unrest and US monetary tightening. The collapses of Terra (LUNA) and FTX severely affected investor confidence. By year-end, BTC traded around $16,600.

### How much was Bitcoin worth in 2023?

In 2023, Bitcoin began recovering, trading between $25K and $30K through spring. By October, BTC rose to $35,200, its highest level since May 2022, driven by optimism over the potential approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs in the US. Over the year, BTC’s price roughly doubled, recovering a significant portion of its earlier losses.

### How much was Bitcoin worth in 2024

2024 began with a major development: on 11 January, the US SEC approved 11 spot Bitcoin ETFs. BTC climbed to $49K, reached $73K in March and later peaked at $89,500 in November following Donald Trump’s US presidential victory, which boosted market sentiment.

![](https://static.capital.com/capital-v-c-20260407-2/apps/capital/_next/static/media/loader.716ebb7e.svg)

**Past performance is not a reliable indicator of future results.**

### Bitcoin price now (2025)

After a brief correction in January to $76K, Bitcoin rallied again. By May, BTC reached a new all-time high near $104K, climbing to between $119K and $123K in July. In mid-August, intrayear highs extended to about $124,500. As of 7 November 2025, Bitcoin trades around $100,900 (closing price).

**Past performance is not a reliable indicator of future results.**

*In 2008, [Bitcoin](https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-is-bitcoin) emerged as the first cryptocurrency. A lot has happened since that day – let’s to take a look back at the history of cryptocurrency to understand the important changes it catalyzed.*

## The History of Bitcoin: From The Start

The idea of having a digital currency is not a new one. Prior to [cryptocurrencies](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tQEkdu6zT_A), many attempts at creating one have taken place. The main issue most of them were facing, was the double spending problem. A digital asset somehow needs to be usable only once to prevent copying it and effectively counterfeiting it.

Over 10 years before cryptocurrencies, the concept had been introduced by computer engineer Wei Dai. In 1998, he published a paper where he discussed “B-money”. He discussed the idea of a digital currency, which could be sent along a group of untraceable digital pseudonyms. That same year, another attempt by the name of Bit Gold was drafted by blockchain pioneer [Nick Szabo](https://wikipedia.org/wiki/Nick_Szabo). Bit Gold equally looked into creating a decentralized digital currency. Szabo’s idea was spurred by inefficiencies within the traditional financial system, such as requiring metal to create coins and to reduce the amount of trust needed to create transactions. While both were never officially launched, they were part of the inspiration behind Bitcoin.

## The Birth of Bitcoin: The First Cryptocurrency

Satoshi Nakamoto published the **white paper** called *[Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System](https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf)*, describing the functionality of the Bitcoin blockchain network. This day in Bitcoin history has carved the path for the events that followed.

Four months later, Satoshi Nakamoto, whose true identity remains a mystery to this day, mined the first block of the Bitcoin network, effectively piloting the [blockchain technology](https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-is-blockchain). The first mined block is also known as the Genesis Block.

The first recorded purchase of goods was made with Bitcoin when Laszlo Hanyecz bought two pizzas for 10,000 BTC. This day is still commemorated to date as the **Bitcoin Pizza Day**. At Ledger, we celebrated this with a [limited edition Ledger Nano S](https://www.ledger.com/bitcoin-pizza-day-ledger-nano-s-limited-edition).

## How The Crypto Market Made History

After the birth of Bitcoin as the first cryptocurrency, solutions had to be found in order to trade them. In March 2010, the first cryptocurrency exchange appeared in the name of bitcoinmarket.com (now defunct). In July that year, Mt.Gox was launched as well.

From 2011 to 2013, Bitcoin managed to reach parity with the US Dollar in February. During this year, a few rivaling cryptocurrencies emerged: By May 2013, the cryptocurrency market counted 10 digital assets, including Litecoin. Another major crypto asset joined in August in the name of XRP (Ripple).

### The Mt.Gox Disaster

As the value of Bitcoin grew, the first hacks followed. In June 2011, Mt.Gox got hacked for the first time: 2,000 BTC was stolen, having a value of around $30,000 at the time.

Mt.Gox became the largest cryptocurrency exchange in 2013, at its peak handling 70% of all Bitcoin transactions.

Unfortunately, in 2014, Mt.Gox became the [first major cryptocurrency exchange hack](https://www.ledger.com/hack-flasback-the-mt-gox-hack-the-most-iconic-exchange-hack), having 850,000 BTC stolen from it. This is the largest theft of BTC in Bitcoin history, which was valued at $460,000,000 at the time (current date value around  $9,5 billion).

After this unprecedented situation, the [Bitcoin price](https://crypto.com/price/bitcoin) plummeted by 50% and did not recover to its initial value till late 2016. Cryptocurrency exchange hacks have remained ever-present since, though rarely of Mt.Gox’s caliber.

Luckily, you can now [securely buy BTC](https://www.ledger.com/buy-bitcoin), but also send, receive and swap with our [Bitcoin wallet](https://www.ledger.com/coin/wallet/bitcoin).

### Ethereum and the Introduction of Tokens and Blockchain Apps

On July 30th 2015, The [Ethereum](https://www.ledger.com/coin/price/ethereum) network was launched. Currently the second crypto asset in terms of market capitalization, it brought smart contracts and eventually [decentralized finance](https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-is-defi) to the cryptocurrency world. These allow the Ethereum blockchain to run an entire ecosystem on its blockchain while also hosting its own native currency: Ether (ETH). The smallest unit of Ether is also known as a Wei (0.000,000,000,000,000,001 ETH). If you want to know more about Ethereum, [here](https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-is-ethereum) is an article that explains everything you need to know about Ethereum, the second-largest cryptocurrency.

Cryptocurrencies that don’t have their own dedicated blockchain, but use the blockchain of another crypto asset are known as tokens. There are now several tokens on the Ethereum network, but simple crypto tokens are called [ERC-20 tokens](https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-are-erc-tokens-and-why-do-we-use-them). The first-ever ERC token was launched back in 2015. That was the crypto asset known as Augur. Since that day, a plethora of tokens have been created on the Ethereum blockchain. There are currently more than 200,000 ERC tokens, which means that there is a huge cryptocurrency ecosystem running on a single blockchain.

## The History of Cryptocurrencies Is Ongoing

The cryptocurrency world has not stood still since. Prices reached an all-time high in the bitcoin history in January 2018 and many new crypto assets have joined since, including EOS (July 2017), Tron (September 2017) and [Cardano](https://www.ledger.com/academy/what-is-cardano) (October 2017). The cryptocurrency market currently hosts over 2000 digital currencies and is still expanding.

In fact, it is easy to see how cryptocurrencies are slowly but surely becoming global. The rising trend around crypto has led to more and more acceptance and use cases. Today, even the creation of a [Central Bank Digital Currency](https://www.bis.org/cpmi/publ/d174.pdf) (CBDC) is in progress and major companies show an increasing interest in blockchain and cryptocurrencies by investing into them. Obviously, those types of events will fuel a fast expansion of the market.

Crypto assets equally continued to gain popularity to a point where adoption has become more important and more present. The amount of Bitcoin ATMs has continued to increase, more shops have started accepting cryptocurrencies as a method of payment, crypto assets are being used as a form of fundraising and you could even [travel around the world](https://www.ledger.com/traveling-with-crypto-an-experience-to-never-forget) with crypto!

This list is expanding at a rapid pace, exactly like how the cryptocurrency prices went through the roof. Do not forget that three years ago, BTC’s price was below 1000 USD!

**Bitcoin** (abbreviation: **BTC**; [sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currency_symbol "Currency symbol"): **₿**) is the first [decentralized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decentralized_application "Decentralized application")[cryptocurrency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency "Cryptocurrency"). Based on a [free-market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-market "Free-market") ideology, bitcoin was invented in 2008 when an unknown person published a [white paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_paper "White paper") under the pseudonym of [Satoshi Nakamoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satoshi_Nakamoto "Satoshi Nakamoto").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-whoissn-6) Use of bitcoin as a [currency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currency "Currency") began in 2009,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-NY2011-7) with the release of its [open-source implementation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-source_software "Open-source software").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 1  From 2021 to 2025, [El Salvador adopted it as legal tender currency before revoking it](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_in_El_Salvador "Bitcoin in El Salvador").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-BTCSVSept7FT-9)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-El_Salvador_Abandons_Bitcoin_as_Legal_Tender-10)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Bitcoin_is_no_longer_legal_tender_in_El_Salvador-11) As bitcoin is [pseudonymous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudonym "Pseudonym"), [its use by criminals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_and_crime "Cryptocurrency and crime") has attracted the attention of regulators, leading to [its ban by several countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legality_of_cryptocurrency_by_country_or_territory "Legality of cryptocurrency by country or territory").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-SunYin2019-12)

Bitcoin works through the collaboration of computers, each of which acts as a [node](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Node_(networking) "Node (networking)") in the [peer-to-peer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer-to-peer "Peer-to-peer")[bitcoin network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_network "Bitcoin network"). Each node maintains an independent copy of a public [distributed ledger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributed_ledger "Distributed ledger") of transactions, called a [blockchain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockchain "Blockchain"), without central oversight. Transactions are validated through the use of [cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptography "Cryptography"), preventing one person from spending another person's bitcoin, as long as the owner of the bitcoin keeps certain sensitive data secret.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 5

[Consensus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_(computer_science) "Consensus (computer science)") between nodes about the content of the blockchain is achieved using a [computationally intensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_complexity "Computational complexity") process based on [proof of work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_of_work "Proof of work"), called [mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_mining "Bitcoin mining"), which is performed by purpose-built computers.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 12  Mining consumes large quantities of electricity and has been criticized for [its environmental impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_bitcoin "Environmental impact of bitcoin").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-13)

## History

Main article: [History of bitcoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_bitcoin "History of bitcoin")

### Background

Before bitcoin, several [digital cash technologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_cash "Digital cash") were released, starting with [David Chaum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Chaum "David Chaum")'s [ecash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecash "Ecash") in the 1980s.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14) The idea that solutions to computational puzzles could have some value was first proposed by cryptographers [Cynthia Dwork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynthia_Dwork "Cynthia Dwork") and [Moni Naor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moni_Naor "Moni Naor") in 1992.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-15)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14) The concept was [independently rediscovered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_discovery "Multiple discovery") by [Adam Back](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Back "Adam Back") who developed [Hashcash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashcash "Hashcash"), a [proof-of-work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof-of-work "Proof-of-work") scheme for [spam control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-spam_techniques "Anti-spam techniques") in 1997.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14) The first proposals for distributed digital scarcity-based [cryptocurrencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrencies "Cryptocurrencies") came from [cypherpunks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cypherpunk "Cypherpunk")[Wei Dai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wei_Dai "Wei Dai") (b-money) and [Nick Szabo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nick_Szabo "Nick Szabo") ([bit gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit_gold "Bit gold")) in 1998.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-16) In 2004, [Hal Finney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hal_Finney_(cypherpunk) "Hal Finney (cypherpunk)") developed the first currency based on reusable proof of work.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-17) These various attempts were not successful:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14) Chaum's concept required centralized control and no banks wanted to sign on, Hashcash had no protection against [double-spending](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-spending "Double-spending"), while b-money and bit gold were not resistant to [Sybil attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sybil_attack "Sybil attack").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14)

### 2008–2009: Creation

| External image |
| --- |
| [![Image 6: image icon](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Searchtool.svg/20px-Searchtool.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Searchtool.svg)[Cover page of _The Times_ 3 January 2009 showing the headline used in the genesis block](https://www.thetimes03jan2009.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/genesis-block-newspaper-bitcoin.jpg) |

The domain name _bitcoin.org_ was registered on 18 August 2008.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-18) On 31 October 2008, a link to a [white paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_paper "White paper") authored by [Satoshi Nakamoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satoshi_Nakamoto "Satoshi Nakamoto") titled _Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System_ was posted to a cryptography mailing list.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-19) Nakamoto's identity remains unknown.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-whoissn-6) According to computer scientist [Arvind Narayanan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arvind_Narayanan "Arvind Narayanan"), all individual components of bitcoin originated in earlier academic literature.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14) Nakamoto's innovation was their complex interplay resulting in the first decentralized, [Sybil resistant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sybil_attack "Sybil attack"), [Byzantine fault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_fault "Byzantine fault") tolerant digital cash system, that would eventually be referred to as the first blockchain.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-te20151031-20) Nakamoto's paper was not [peer reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer_review "Peer review") and was initially ignored by academics, who argued that it could not work.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Narayanan2017-14)

Nakamoto released bitcoin as [open-source software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-source_software "Open-source software").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Humayun-2018-21) On 3 January 2009, the bitcoin network was created when Nakamoto mined the starting block of the chain, known as the _[genesis block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genesis\_(blockchain) "Genesis (blockchain)")_.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Wired:RFB-22) Embedded in this block was the text "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of [second bailout for banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2009_United_Kingdom_bank_rescue_package "2009 United Kingdom bank rescue package")", which is the date and headline of an issue of _[The Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Times "The Times")_ newspaper.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-NY2011-7) Nine days later, Hal Finney received the first bitcoin transaction: ten bitcoins from Nakamoto.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-23) Wei Dai and Nick Szabo were also early supporters.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Wired:RFB-22) On May 22, 2010, the first known [commercial transaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_transaction "Financial transaction") using bitcoin occurred when programmer Laszlo Hanyecz bought two [Papa John's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papa_John%27s "Papa John's") pizzas for ₿10,000, in what would later be celebrated as "Bitcoin Pizza Day".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-24) Satoshi tasked Finnish developer and early Bitcoin contributor [Martti Malmi](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Martti_Malmi&action=edit&redlink=1 "Martti Malmi (page does not exist)")[[fi](https://fi.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martti_Malmi "fi:Martti Malmi")] with creating content for the bitcoin.org website.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-25)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-DominicFrisby2014-26)

### 2010–2012: Early growth

[Blockchain analysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockchain#Blockchain_analysis "Blockchain") estimate that Nakamoto had mined about one million bitcoins[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-27) before disappearing in 2010 when he handed the network alert key and control of the [code repository](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_repository "Software repository") over to [Gavin Andresen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gavin_Andresen "Gavin Andresen"). Andresen later became lead developer at the [Bitcoin Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_Foundation "Bitcoin Foundation"),[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-28)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-29) an [organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_bitcoin_organizations "List of bitcoin organizations") founded in September 2012 to promote bitcoin.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-30)

After early "[proof-of-concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof-of-concept "Proof-of-concept")" transactions, the first major users of bitcoin were [black markets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_market "Black market"), such as the [dark web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_web "Dark web")[Silk Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk_Road_(marketplace) "Silk Road (marketplace)"). During its 30 months of existence, beginning in February 2011, Silk Road exclusively accepted bitcoins as payment, transacting ₿9.9 million, worth about $214 million.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31): 222

### 2013–2014: First regulatory actions

In March 2013, the US [Financial Crimes Enforcement Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_Crimes_Enforcement_Network "Financial Crimes Enforcement Network") (FinCEN) established regulatory guidelines for "decentralized virtual currencies" such as bitcoin, classifying American bitcoin miners who sell their generated bitcoins as [money services businesses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_services_business "Money services business"), subject to registration and other legal obligations.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-32) In May 2013, US authorities seized the unregistered [exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_exchange "Cryptocurrency exchange")[Mt. Gox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mt._Gox "Mt. Gox").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-33) In June 2013, the US [Drug Enforcement Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration "Drug Enforcement Administration") seized ₿11.02 from an individual attempting to use them to purchase illicit drugs. This marked the first time a government agency had seized bitcoins.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-34) The FBI seized about ₿30,000 in October 2013 from Silk Road, following the arrest of its founder [Ross Ulbricht](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ross_Ulbricht "Ross Ulbricht").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-35)

In December 2013, the [People's Bank of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Bank_of_China "People's Bank of China") prohibited Chinese financial institutions from using bitcoin.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-36) After the announcement, the value of bitcoin dropped,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-37) and [Baidu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baidu "Baidu") no longer accepted bitcoins for certain services.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-38) Buying real-world goods with any virtual currency had been illegal in China since at least 2009.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-39)

### 2015–2019

Research produced by the [University of Cambridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Cambridge "University of Cambridge") estimated that in 2017, there were 2.9 to 5.8 million unique users using a [cryptocurrency wallet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_wallet "Cryptocurrency wallet"), most of them using bitcoin.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-40) In August 2017, the [SegWit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SegWit "SegWit") software upgrade was activated. Segwit was intended to support the [Lightning Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning_Network "Lightning Network") as well as improve [scalability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalability "Scalability").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-41) SegWit opponents, who supported larger blocks as a scalability solution, [forked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fork_(blockchain) "Fork (blockchain)") to create [Bitcoin Cash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_Cash "Bitcoin Cash"), one of many [forks of bitcoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_bitcoin_forks "List of bitcoin forks").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-42)

In December 2017, the first [futures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futures_contract "Futures contract") on bitcoin was introduced by the [Chicago Mercantile Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange "Chicago Mercantile Exchange") (CME).[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-43)

In February 2018, the price crashed after China imposed a complete ban on bitcoin trading.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-44) The percentage of bitcoin trading in the Chinese [renminbi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renminbi "Renminbi") fell from over 90% in September 2017 to less than 1% in June 2018.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-45) During the same year, bitcoin prices were negatively affected by several hacks or thefts from cryptocurrency exchanges.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-46)

In 2020, some major companies and institutions started to acquire bitcoin: [MicroStrategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MicroStrategy "MicroStrategy") invested $250 million in bitcoin as a [treasury reserve asset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treasury_management "Treasury management"),[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-48)[Square, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square,_Inc. "Square, Inc."), $50 million,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-49) and [MassMutual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MassMutual "MassMutual"), $100 million.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-50) In November 2020, [PayPal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PayPal "PayPal") added support for bitcoin in the US.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-51)

In February 2021, bitcoin's [market capitalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_capitalization "Market capitalization") reached $1 trillion for the first time.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-52) In November 2021, the [Taproot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taproot_(fork) "Taproot (fork)")[soft-fork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soft-fork "Soft-fork") upgrade was activated, adding support for [Schnorr signatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schnorr_signature "Schnorr signature"), improved functionality of [smart contracts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smart_contract "Smart contract") and [Lightning Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning_Network "Lightning Network").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-53) Before, bitcoin only used a custom [elliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve_cryptography "Elliptic curve cryptography") with the [ECDSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECDSA "ECDSA") algorithm to produce [signatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signature "Digital signature").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-54): 101  In September 2021, bitcoin became [legal tender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_tender "Legal tender") currency [in El Salvador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_in_El_Salvador "Bitcoin in El Salvador"), alongside the US dollar.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-BTCSVSept7FT-9) In October 2021, the first bitcoin futures [exchange-traded fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund "Exchange-traded fund") (ETF), called BITO, from [ProShares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ProShares "ProShares") was approved by the [SEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission "U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission") and listed on the [CME](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange "Chicago Mercantile Exchange").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-55)

In early 2022, during the [Canadian trucker protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canada_convoy_protest "Canada convoy protest") opposing [COVID-19 vaccine mandates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_vaccine_misinformation_and_hesitancy "COVID-19 vaccine misinformation and hesitancy"), organizers turned to bitcoin to receive donations after traditional financial platforms restricted access to funding.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-56)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-57) Proponents highlighted bitcoin's use as a tool for fundraising in situations where access to conventional financial systems may be restricted.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-58)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-59) In May and June 2022, the bitcoin price fell following the collapses of [TerraUSD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terra_(blockchain) "Terra (blockchain)"), a [stablecoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stablecoin "Stablecoin"),[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-60) and the [Celsius Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius_Network "Celsius Network"), a cryptocurrency loan company.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-61)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-62)

In 2023, ordinals—[non-fungible tokens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-fungible_token "Non-fungible token") (NFTs)—on bitcoin, went live.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-63) As of June 2023, River Financial estimated that bitcoin had 81.7 million users, about 1% of the global population.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-64)

In January 2024, the first 11 US [spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spot_market "Spot market") bitcoin [ETFs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund "Exchange-traded fund") began trading, offering direct exposure to bitcoin for the first time on American stock exchanges.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-65)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-66) In December 2024, bitcoin price reached $100,000 for the first time, as US president-elect [Donald Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") promised to make the US the "crypto capital of the planet" and to stockpile bitcoin.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-67) The same month, [BlackRock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BlackRock "BlackRock"), the world's largest [asset manager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset_manager "Asset manager"), recommended investors to allocate up to 2% of their [portfolio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portfolio_(finance) "Portfolio (finance)") to bitcoin.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-68)

As of 2025, the Salvadoran government continues to describe Bitcoin as legal tender under the 2021 Bitcoin Law; however, a January 2025 reform removed obligations for businesses and the government to accept it, so many analysts regard its legal-tender status as effectively ended in practice.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-El_Salvador_Abandons_Bitcoin_as_Legal_Tender-10)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Bitcoin_is_no_longer_legal_tender_in_El_Salvador-11) In March 2025, [President Trump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_Trump "President Trump") signed an [executive order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States) "Executive order (United States)") to establish a [strategic bitcoin reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strategic_bitcoin_reserve "Strategic bitcoin reserve").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-69) Later that year, some U.S. states, such as [Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas "Texas") and [New Hampshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Hampshire "New Hampshire") also instituted strategic bitcoin reserves,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-70) and the [Czech National Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Czech_National_Bank "Czech National Bank") bought less than €1 million in a "test portfolio".[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-71)

### Units and divisibility

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/Bitcoin_old.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bitcoin_old.png)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Bitcoin.png/120px-Bitcoin.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bitcoin.png)

Bitcoin logos made by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2009 (left) and 2010 (right).

The [unit of account](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_account "Unit of account") of the bitcoin system is the _bitcoin_. It is most commonly represented with the [symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currency_symbol "Currency symbol") ₿ designed in 2010[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-unicode-10-1) and the [currency code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Currency_code "Currency code") BTC. However, the BTC code does not conform to [ISO 4217](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_4217 "ISO 4217") as BT is the country code of Bhutan,[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-ssrn.3383734-72) and ISO 4217 requires the first letter used in global commodities to be 'X'.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-ssrn.3383734-72) XBT, a code that conforms to [ISO 4217](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_4217 "ISO 4217") though not officially part of it,[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-ssrn.3383734-72) is used by [Bloomberg L.P.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P. "Bloomberg L.P.")[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-73)

No uniform [capitalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalization "Capitalization") convention exists; some sources use _Bitcoin_, capitalized, to refer to the technology and [network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_network "Computer network"), and _bitcoin_, lowercase, for the unit of account.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-74) The _[Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge\_Advanced\_Learner%27s\_Dictionary "Cambridge Advanced Learner's Dictionary")_ and the _[Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford\_Advanced\_Learner%27s\_Dictionary "Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary")_ use the capitalized and lowercase variants without distinction.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-75)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-76)

One bitcoin is divisible to eight decimal places.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 5  Units for smaller amounts of bitcoin are the millibitcoin (mBTC), equal to 1⁄1000 bitcoin, and the satoshi[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-satoshi-2) (sat), representing 1⁄100 000 000 (one hundred millionth) bitcoin, the smallest amount possible.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-satoshi_unit-3) 100,000 satoshis are one mBTC.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-77)

### Blockchain

Further information: [Blockchain §Structure and design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockchain#Structure_and_design "Blockchain")

As a [decentralized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decentralization "Decentralization") system, bitcoin operates without a central authority or single administrator,[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Gervais2014-78) so that anyone can create a new bitcoin address and transact without needing any approval.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 1  This is accomplished through a specialized [distributed ledger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributed_ledger "Distributed ledger") called a _[blockchain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockchain "Blockchain")_ that records bitcoin transactions.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econbc-79)

The blockchain is implemented as an ordered list of _blocks_. Each block contains a [SHA-256](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SHA-256 "SHA-256")[hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_hash_function "Cryptographic hash function") of the previous block,[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econbc-79) chaining them in chronological order.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 7 [[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econbc-79) The blockchain is maintained by a [peer-to-peer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer-to-peer "Peer-to-peer") network.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31): 215–219  Individual blocks, public addresses, and transactions within blocks are public information, and can be examined using a blockchain explorer.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-80)

Nodes validate and broadcast transactions, each maintaining a copy of the blockchain for ownership verification.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-81) A new block is created every 10 minutes on average, updating the blockchain across all nodes without central oversight. This process tracks bitcoin spending, ensuring each bitcoin is [spent only once](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-spending "Double-spending"). Unlike a traditional ledger that tracks physical currency, bitcoins exist digitally as [unspent outputs of transactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unspent_outputs_of_transactions "Unspent outputs of transactions").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 5

### Addresses and transactions

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Bitcoin_Transaction_Visual.svg/250px-Bitcoin_Transaction_Visual.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bitcoin_Transaction_Visual.svg)

Simplified chain of ownership. In practice, a transaction can have more than one input and more than one output.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-EconOfBTC-82)

In the blockchain, bitcoins are linked to specific [strings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(computer_science) "String (computer science)") called addresses. Most often, an address encodes a [hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hash_(cryptography) "Hash (cryptography)") of a single [public key](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_key "Public key"). Creating such an address involves generating a random private key and then computing the corresponding address. This process is almost instant, but the reverse (finding the private key for a given address) is nearly impossible.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 4  Publishing such a bitcoin address does not risk its private key, and it is extremely unlikely to accidentally generate a used key with funds. To use bitcoins, owners need their private key to [digitally sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signature "Digital signature") transactions, which are verified by the network using the public key, keeping the private key secret.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 5  An address may encode the hash of a bitcoin script that specifies more complex requirements to spend the funds. One common example is "multisig", in which multiple distinct private keys must mutually sign any transaction that attempts to spend the funds.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 7

Bitcoin transactions use a [Forth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forth_(programming_language) "Forth (programming language)")-like [scripting language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scripting_language "Scripting language"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 5  involving one or more inputs and outputs. When sending bitcoins, a user specifies the recipients' addresses and the amount for each output. This allows sending bitcoins to several recipients in a single transaction. To prevent double-spending, each input must refer to a previous unspent output in the blockchain.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-EconOfBTC-82) Using multiple inputs is similar to using multiple coins in a cash transaction. As in a cash transaction, the sum of inputs can exceed the intended sum of payments. In such a case, an additional output can return the change back to the payer.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-EconOfBTC-82) Unallocated input satoshis in the transaction become the transaction fee.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-EconOfBTC-82)

Losing a private key means losing access to the bitcoins, with no other proof of ownership accepted by the [protocol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_protocol "Bitcoin protocol").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31) For instance, in 2013, a user lost ₿7,500, valued at US$7.5 million, by accidentally discarding a [hard drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_disk_drive "Hard disk drive") with the private key.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-83) It is estimated that around 20% of all bitcoins are lost.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-84) The private key must also be kept secret as its exposure, such as through a [data breach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_breach "Data breach"), can lead to theft of the associated bitcoins.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 10 [[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-85) As of December 2017[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), approximately ₿980,000 had been stolen from [cryptocurrency exchanges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_exchange "Cryptocurrency exchange").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-86)

### Mining

See also: [Bitcoin protocol §Mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_protocol#Mining "Bitcoin protocol")

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Argo_Blockchain_Mirabel_Facility.jpg/250px-Argo_Blockchain_Mirabel_Facility.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Argo_Blockchain_Mirabel_Facility.jpg)

Bitcoin mining [facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_center "Data center") with large amounts of mining hardware

Miners don't directly act as nodes, but do communicate with nodes. The mining process is primarily intended to prevent [double-spending](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-spending "Double-spending") and get all nodes to agree on the content of the blockchain, but it also has desirable side-effects such as making it infeasible for adversaries to stifle valid transactions or alter the historical record of transactions, since doing so generally requires the adversary to have access to more mining power than the rest of the network combined.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 12

The mining process in bitcoin involves maintaining the blockchain through computer [processing power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Processing_power "Processing power"). Miners group and broadcast new transactions into blocks, which are then verified by the network.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econbc-79) Each block must contain a proof of work (PoW) to be accepted,[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econbc-79) involving finding a [nonce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_nonce "Cryptographic nonce") number that, combined with the block content, produces a [hash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_hash "Cryptographic hash") numerically smaller than the network's _difficulty target_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 8  This PoW is simple to verify but hard to generate, requiring many attempts.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 8  PoW forms the basis of bitcoin's [consensus mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_(computer_science) "Consensus (computer science)").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Mingxiao2017-87)

The difficulty of generating a block is [deterministically adjusted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deterministic_algorithm "Deterministic algorithm") based on the [mining power on the network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashrate "Hashrate") by changing the difficulty target, which is recalibrated every 2,016 blocks (approximately two weeks) to maintain an average time of ten minutes between new blocks. The process requires significant computational power and specialized [hardware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_hardware "Computer hardware").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 8 [[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-88)

Miners who successfully create a new block with a valid nonce can collect transaction fees from the included transactions and a fixed reward in bitcoins.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-bloombergvance111413-89) To claim this reward, a special transaction called a _coinbase_ is included in the block, with the miner as the payee. All bitcoins in existence have been created through this type of transaction.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 8  This reward is halved every 210,000 blocks until ₿21 million[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-supply-5) have been issued in total, which is expected to occur around the year 2140. Afterward, miners will only earn from transaction fees. These fees are determined by the transaction's size and the amount of data stored, measured in satoshis per byte.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-90)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-EconOfBTC-82)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 8

The proof of work system and the chaining of blocks make blockchain modifications very difficult, as altering one block requires changing all subsequent blocks. As more blocks are added, modifying older blocks becomes increasingly challenging.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-91)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econbc-79) In case of disagreement, nodes trust the longest chain, which required the greatest amount of effort to produce.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Mingxiao2017-87) To tamper or censor the ledger, one needs to control the majority of the global [hashrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashrate "Hashrate").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Mingxiao2017-87) The high cost required to reach this level of computational power [secures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_security "Computer security") the bitcoin blockchain.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Mingxiao2017-87)

The [environmental impact of bitcoin mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_bitcoin_mining "Environmental impact of bitcoin mining") is controversial and has attracted the attention of regulators, [leading to restrictions or incentives in various jurisdictions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legality_of_cryptocurrency_by_country_or_territory "Legality of cryptocurrency by country or territory").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Stoll2023-92) As of 2025[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), a [non-peer-reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-peer-reviewed "Non-peer-reviewed") study by the [Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge_Centre_for_Alternative_Finance "Cambridge Centre for Alternative Finance") (CCAF) estimated that bitcoin mining represented 0.5% of [global electricity consumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_energy_supply_and_consumption "World energy supply and consumption") and 0.08% of world [greenhouse gas emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions "Greenhouse gas emissions"), comparable to Slovakia's emissions.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-93) About half of the electricity used is generated through [fossil fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-NYT20220116-94) Moreover, mining hardware's short lifespan results in [electronic waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_waste "Electronic waste").[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-deVries2021-95) In October 2025, the US was the largest mining country with 38% of the global bitcoin mining market share, followed by Russia (16%) and China (14%, despite all cryptocurrency trading and mining being banned there in 2021).[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-96)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-97)

### Privacy and fungibility

Bitcoin is [pseudonymous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudonym "Pseudonym"), with funds linked to addresses, not real-world identities. While the owners of these addresses are not directly identified, all transactions are public on the blockchain. Patterns of use, like spending coins from multiple inputs, can hint at a common owner. Public data can sometimes be matched with known address owners.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-98)[Bitcoin exchanges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_bitcoin_companies "List of bitcoin companies") might also need to collect personal data as per legal requirements.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-99) For enhanced [privacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privacy_and_blockchain "Privacy and blockchain"), users can generate a new address for each transaction.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-100)

In the bitcoin network, each bitcoin is treated equally, ensuring basic [fungibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungibility "Fungibility"). However, users and applications can choose to differentiate between bitcoins. While wallets and software treat all bitcoins the same, each bitcoin's transaction history is recorded on the blockchain. This public record allows for [chain analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blockchain_analysis "Blockchain analysis"), where users can identify and potentially reject bitcoins from controversial sources.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-101) For example, in 2012, Mt. Gox froze accounts containing bitcoins identified as stolen.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-102)

Bitcoin wallets were the first [cryptocurrency wallets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_wallet "Cryptocurrency wallet"), enabling users to store the information necessary to transact bitcoins.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-103)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 1, glossary  The first wallet program, simply named _Bitcoin_, and sometimes referred to as the _Satoshi client_, was released in 2009 by Nakamoto as [open-source software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-source_software "Open-source software").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Humayun-2018-21)[Bitcoin Core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_Core "Bitcoin Core") is among the best known [clients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client_(computing) "Client (computing)"). [Forks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fork_(software_development) "Fork (software development)") of Bitcoin Core exist such as [Bitcoin Unlimited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_Unlimited "Bitcoin Unlimited").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-104) Wallets can be full clients, with a full copy of the blockchain to check the validity of mined blocks,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 1  or lightweight clients, just to send and receive transactions without a local copy of the entire blockchain.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-105) Third-party internet services, called online wallets or [hot wallets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_wallet "Digital wallet"), store users' credentials on their servers, making them susceptible of hacks.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-106)[Cold storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_wallet "Cold wallet") protects bitcoins from such hacks by keeping private keys offline, either through specialized hardware wallets or paper printouts.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-107)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 4

### Scalability and decentralization challenges

Main article: [Bitcoin scalability problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_scalability_problem "Bitcoin scalability problem")

Nakamoto limited the block size to one [megabyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megabyte "Megabyte").[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-108) The limited block size and frequency can lead to delayed processing of transactions, increased fees and a bitcoin scalability problem.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-109) The [Lightning Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lightning_Network "Lightning Network"), a [second-layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstraction_layer "Abstraction layer")[routing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routing "Routing") network, is a potential scaling solution.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Antonopoulos2014-8): ch. 8

Research shows a trend towards centralization in bitcoin as miners join [pools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mining_pool "Mining pool") for stable income.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31): 215, 219–222 [[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-110): 3  If a single miner or pool controls more than 50% of the [hashing power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashrate "Hashrate"), it would allow them to censor transactions and double-spend coins.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Gervais2014-78) In 2014, mining pool [Ghash.io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghash.io "Ghash.io") reached 51% mining power, causing safety concerns, but later voluntarily capped its power at 39.99% for the benefit of the whole network.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-111) A few entities also dominate other parts of the ecosystem such as the client software, online wallets, and simplified payment verification (SPV) clients.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Gervais2014-78)

## Economics and usage

Main article: [Economics of bitcoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics_of_bitcoin "Economics of bitcoin")

### Bitcoin's theoretical roots and ideology

According to the [European Central Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Central_Bank "European Central Bank"), the decentralization of money offered by bitcoin has its theoretical roots in the [Austrian school of economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_school_of_economics "Austrian school of economics"), especially with [Friedrich Hayek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Hayek "Friedrich Hayek")'s _[The Denationalisation of Money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Denationalisation\_of\_Money "The Denationalisation of Money")_, in which he advocates a complete [free market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market "Free market") in the production, distribution and management of money to end the monopoly of [central banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_banks "Central banks").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-112): 22  Sociologist [Nigel Dodd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigel_Dodd "Nigel Dodd") argues that the essence of the bitcoin ideology is to remove money from social, as well as governmental, control.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-113)_[The Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Economist "The Economist")_ describes bitcoin as "a [techno-anarchist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Techno-anarchist "Techno-anarchist") project to create an online version of cash, a way for people to transact without the possibility of interference from malicious governments or banks".[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-30082018Economist-114) These philosophical ideas initially attracted [libertarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libertarian "Libertarian") and [anarchists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchist "Anarchist").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-115)

### Recognition as a currency and legal status

[Money](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money "Money") serves three purposes: a [store of value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Store_of_value "Store of value"), a [medium of exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_of_exchange "Medium of exchange"), and a [unit of account](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_account "Unit of account").[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econ315-116) According to _The Economist_ in 2014, bitcoin functions best as a medium of exchange.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-econ315-116) In 2015, _The Economist_ noted that bitcoins had three qualities useful in a currency: they are "hard to earn, limited in supply and easy to verify".[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-117) However, a 2018 assessment by _The Economist_ stated that cryptocurrencies met none of these three criteria.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-30082018Economist-114) Per some researchers, as of 2015[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), bitcoin functions more as a [payment system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Payment_system "Payment system") than as a currency.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31) In 2014, economist [Robert J. Shiller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_J._Shiller "Robert J. Shiller") wrote that bitcoin has potential as a unit of account for measuring the relative value of goods, as with Chile's [Unidad de Fomento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unidad_de_Fomento "Unidad de Fomento"), but that "Bitcoin in its present form... doesn't really solve any sensible economic problem".[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Shiller-118) In 2017, François Velde, senior economist at the [Chicago Fed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Fed "Chicago Fed"), described bitcoin as "unlikely by itself to replace monies in well-functioning monetary systems."[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-119)

The [legal status of bitcoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legality_of_cryptocurrency_by_country_or_territory "Legality of cryptocurrency by country or territory") varies substantially from one jurisdiction to another. Because of its decentralized nature and its global presence, regulating bitcoin is difficult. However, the use of bitcoin can be criminalized, and shutting down exchanges and the peer-to-peer economy in a given country would constitute a [de facto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_facto "De facto") ban.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-120) The [use of bitcoin by criminals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_and_crime "Cryptocurrency and crime") has attracted the attention of financial regulators, legislative bodies, and law enforcement.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-121) Nobel-prize winning economist [Joseph Stiglitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz "Joseph Stiglitz") says that bitcoin's anonymity encourages [money laundering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Money_laundering "Money laundering") and other crimes.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-122) This is the main justification behind bitcoin bans.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-SunYin2019-12) As of November 2021[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), nine countries applied an absolute ban (Algeria, Bangladesh, China, Egypt, Iraq, Morocco, Nepal, Qatar, and Tunisia) while another 42 countries had an implicit ban.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-LOC2021-123)[_[needs update](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Dates\_and\_numbers#Chronological\_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_]

### Use for payments

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/De_Waag_Bitcoin.jpg/250px-De_Waag_Bitcoin.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:De_Waag_Bitcoin.jpg)

Café in [Delft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft "Delft") accepting Bitcoin

According to Harvard Professor [Kenneth Rogoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Rogoff "Kenneth Rogoff") as of 2025[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), bitcoin is rarely used in regular transactions with merchants, but is popular in the [informal economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informal_economy "Informal economy") and for criminal activities.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-RogoffEconomist-124)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-RogoffBloomberg-125) Prices are not usually quoted in bitcoin and trades involve conversions into [fiat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiat_money "Fiat money") currencies.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31) Commonly cited reasons for not using bitcoin include high costs, the inability to process [chargebacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chargeback "Chargeback"), high [price volatility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatility_(finance) "Volatility (finance)"), long transaction times, and [transaction fees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transaction_cost "Transaction cost") (especially for small purchases).[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-FT08062018-126)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-127)_[Bloomberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg\_News "Bloomberg News")_ reported that bitcoin was being used for large-item purchases on the site [Overstock.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overstock.com "Overstock.com") and for cross-border payments to [freelancers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freelancer "Freelancer").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-128) As of 2015[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), there was little sign of bitcoin use in international [remittances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remittance "Remittance") despite high fees charged by banks and [Western Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Union "Western Union").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-JEP-31)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-129)

From September 2021 until January 2025, the [Bitcoin Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_Law "Bitcoin Law") made bitcoin a [legal tender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_tender "Legal tender") currency in [El Salvador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Salvador "El Salvador"), alongside the US dollar.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-BTCSVSept7FT-9) The adoption had been criticized internationally and within El Salvador.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-BTCSVSept7FT-9)[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-130) In 2022, the [International Monetary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "International Monetary Fund") (IMF) urged El Salvador to reverse its decision.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-131) As of 2022[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), the use of [Bitcoin in El Salvador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin_in_El_Salvador "Bitcoin in El Salvador") remained low: 80% of businesses refused to accept it.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-132) In 2025, El Salvador's government revoked bitcoin's status as legal tender currency in order to comply with conditions set by the IMF for a loan. El Salvador still describes bitcoin as "legal tender", but its acceptance is no longer obligitory (as it is with the US dollar) and the El Salvador government no longer accepts bitcoin for payment of taxes or fees.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-El_Salvador_Abandons_Bitcoin_as_Legal_Tender-10)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Bitcoin_is_no_longer_legal_tender_in_El_Salvador-11)

In April 2022, the [Central African Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_African_Republic "Central African Republic") (CAR) adopted bitcoin as legal tender alongside the [CFA franc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CFA_franc "CFA franc"),[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-133) but repealed the reform one year later.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-134)

Bitcoin is also used by some governments. For instance, the [Iranian government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_Iran "Government of Iran") initially opposed cryptocurrencies, but later began using them to circumvent [sanctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanctions_against_Iran "Sanctions against Iran").[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-135) Since 2020, Iran has required local bitcoin miners to sell bitcoin to the [Central Bank of Iran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Iran "Central Bank of Iran"), allowing the central bank to use it for imports.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-aawsat202011-136) Some [constituent states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constituent_state "Constituent state") and [local governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_government "Local government") also accept tax payments in bitcoin, including [Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado "Colorado") in the US[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-137) and [Zug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canton_of_Zug "Canton of Zug") and [Lugano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lugano "Lugano") in Switzerland.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-138)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-139) As of 2023, the US government owned more than $5 billion worth of seized bitcoin.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-140)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-141)

### Use for investment and status as an economic bubble

Further information: [Cryptocurrency bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_bubble "Cryptocurrency bubble")

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/El_Salvador_Bitcoin_reserves.png/250px-El_Salvador_Bitcoin_reserves.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:El_Salvador_Bitcoin_reserves.png)

Government website with El Salvador reserves

As of 2018[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bitcoin&action=edit), the overwhelming majority of bitcoin transactions took place on [cryptocurrency exchanges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptocurrency_exchange "Cryptocurrency exchange").[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-FT08062018-126) Since 2014, regulated bitcoin funds also allow [exposure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_exposure "Market exposure") to the asset or to [futures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futures_contract "Futures contract") as an investment.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-142)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-143) Bitcoin is used as a [store of value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Store_of_value "Store of value"):[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-144)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-145) individuals and companies such as the [Winklevoss twins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winklevoss_Capital_Management "Winklevoss Capital Management")[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-146) and [Elon Musk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elon_Musk "Elon Musk")'s companies [SpaceX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX "SpaceX") and [Tesla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesla,_Inc. "Tesla, Inc.") have each bought and sold hundreds of millions of dollars worth of bitcoin.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-147)[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-148)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-149) Bitcoin wealth is highly concentrated, with 0.01% holding 27% of in-circulation currency, as of 2021.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-150)

As of March 2025, El Salvador had $550 million worth of bitcoin in its [international reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreign_exchange_reserves "Foreign exchange reserves"), about 6,102 coins.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-151)

Bitcoin, along with other cryptocurrencies, has been described as an [economic bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_bubble "Economic bubble") by several economists, including [Nobel Prize in Economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences "Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences") laureates, such as [Joseph Stiglitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz "Joseph Stiglitz"),[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Stiglitz-152)[James Heckman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Heckman "James Heckman"),[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-4Nobels-153) and [Paul Krugman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Krugman "Paul Krugman").[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-Krugman-154) Another recipient of the prize, [Robert Shiller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Shiller "Robert Shiller"), argues that bitcoin is rather a [fad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fad "Fad") that may become an [asset class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asset_classes "Asset classes"). He describes its price growth as an "epidemic", driven by [contagious narratives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_contagion "Social contagion").[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-155) In 2024, [Jean Tirole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Tirole "Jean Tirole"), also Nobel laureate, described bitcoin as a "pure bubble" as its [intrinsic value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_value_(finance) "Intrinsic value (finance)") is zero. According to him, some bubbles are long-lasting such as gold and fiat currencies, and it is impossible to predict whether bitcoin would implode like other financial bubbles or become an alternative to gold.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-156) The same year, [Federal Reserve Chair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chair_of_the_Federal_Reserve "Chair of the Federal Reserve")[Jerome Powell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerome_Powell "Jerome Powell") described bitcoin as a digital competitor to gold but not to the dollar as he argued it is a highly volatile [speculative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speculation "Speculation") asset not used as a form of payment.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-157) In 2025, [Kenneth Rogoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Rogoff "Kenneth Rogoff") claimed that Krugman was wrong and that Bitcoin had value as it is competing with the dollar to become the [means of exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Means_of_exchange "Means of exchange") of the [underground economy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informal_economy "Informal economy") which represents 20% of the world's [GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_domestic_product "Gross domestic product").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-RogoffEconomist-124)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-RogoffBloomberg-125) According to Rogoff, bitcoin is "the ideal currency for a more fragmented and uncertain global landscape" and it could become appealing to [emerging-market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emerging_market "Emerging market") central banks as a "politically neutral" [reserve currency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserve_currency "Reserve currency").[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-:0-158) In 2025, the nominee for the Chair of the Federal Reserve, [Kevin Warsh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kevin_Warsh "Kevin Warsh"), described Bitcoin as the "new gold."[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-:0-158)

According to research published in the _[International Review of Financial Analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International\_Review\_of\_Financial\_Analysis "International Review of Financial Analysis")_ in 2018, bitcoin as an asset is highly volatile and does not behave like any other conventional asset.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-159) According to one 2022 analysis published in _[The Journal of Alternative Investments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Journal\_of\_Alternative\_Investments "The Journal of Alternative Investments")_, bitcoin was less volatile than [oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_of_oil "Price of oil"), [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_as_an_investment "Silver as an investment"), [US Treasuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Treasury_security "United States Treasury security"), and 190 stocks in the [S&P 500](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500 "S&P 500") during and after the [2020 stock market crash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_stock_market_crash "2020 stock market crash").[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-160) The term _[hodl](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hodl#English "wikt:hodl")_ was created in December 2013 for [holding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buy_and_hold "Buy and hold") bitcoin rather than selling it during periods of volatility.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-161)[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-162)

In 2014, economist [Nouriel Roubini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nouriel_Roubini "Nouriel Roubini") described bitcoin as a [Ponzi scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ponzi_scheme "Ponzi scheme").[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-163) Legal scholar [Eric Posner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Posner "Eric Posner") disagrees, however, as "a real Ponzi scheme takes fraud; bitcoin, by contrast, seems more like a collective [delusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delusion "Delusion")".[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-164) A 2014 [World Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Bank "World Bank") report also concluded that bitcoin was not a deliberate Ponzi scheme.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-165)

### Market characteristics

Bitcoin markets operate 24 hours a day, seven days a week, contrasting with traditional financial markets that have fixed trading hours. Bitcoin prices show much higher volatility and respond strongly to both regulatory changes and market events.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-166)

The volume of bitcoin trading can fluctuate considerably among various exchanges and geographic regions. The daily transaction volume of bitcoin across all exchanges typically reaches $50 billion as of 2025.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitcoin#cite_note-167)
