## Revenue vs. Profit: An Overview

Revenue is the total amount of income generated by the sale of goods or services related to a company's primary operations. Profit is the amount of income that remains after accounting for all expenses, debts, additional income streams, and operating costs. Profit is typically referred to as [net profit](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/122414/net-income-same-profit.asp) or the bottom line.

### Key Takeaways

- Revenue is the total amount of income generated by the sale of goods or services related to a company's primary operations.
- Also known simply as sales, revenue doesn't deduct any costs or expenses associated with operating the business.
- Profit is the amount of income that remains after accounting for all expenses, debts, additional income streams, and operating costs.
- Revenue and profit both refer to money a company earns but a company can generate revenue and still have a net loss.
- Companies report revenue and profit on their income statement but record them in different areas of the report.

## What Is Revenue?

[Revenue](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/revenue.asp) is often referred to as the top line because it appears at the top of the [income statement](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/incomestatement.asp). It's the income that a company generates before any expenses are subtracted. Income from sales and operations isn't considered revenue if the company also has [income](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/income.asp) from investments or a [subsidiary](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/subsidiary.asp) company. Additional income streams and various types of expenses are accounted for separately.

Companies often report gross revenue and/or net revenue on their financial statements.

- Gross revenue is the total sales a company makes before any returns or pricing discounts. The company then reports net sales or net revenue when these residual sale items are accounted for.
- Net revenue doesn't include company expenses. It only reports the aggregated revenue factoring in certain aspects of revenue that may reduce the amount.

### What Impacts Revenue?

Many factors can impact the revenue a company brings in as part of its operations, including the following:

- **Demand:** Revenue can increase when a company's products or services are in high [demand](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/demand.asp) and can drop as demand falls.
- **Pricing:** Companies must be sensitive to price changes, which are crucial in determining a company's revenue. Increasing prices can lead to a decrease in demand and a drop in revenue.
- **Competition:** A company may earn less revenue because of external competition. A company may have to take certain steps like lowering its prices or risk missing out on certain customers if it faces intense competition.

### Important

A company can earn record-high revenue and still report a negative profit or a net loss.

## What Is Profit?

[Profit](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/profit.asp) is referred to as [net income](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/netincome.asp) on the income statement. Most people know it as the bottom line. Variations of profit on the income statement are used to analyze a company's financial performance. The term may emerge in the context of [gross profit](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/grossprofit.asp) and operating profit.

- Gross profitis revenue minus the [cost of goods sold (COGS)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/cogs.asp), which are the direct costs attributable to the production of the goods sold by a company. This amount includes the cost of the materials that are used in creating a company's products along with the direct labor costs used to produce them.
- [Operating profit](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/operating_profit.asp) is gross profit minus all other fixed and variable expenses associated with operating the business such as rent, utilities, and [payroll](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/payroll.asp).

The revenue a company earns is also impacted by general economic conditions. [Consumer spending](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/consumer-spending.asp) may decrease during a [recession](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/recession.asp). This can also be the case for products that are seasonal because a company may simply be at the whim of cyclical demand such as retails during the holidays.

### What Impacts Profit?

Profit is a component of revenue so everything that impacts revenue also impacts profit. But, profit is impacted by more factors because there are more items involved in the calculation. Companies may have escalating costs for COGS or other direct costs associated with producing or purchasing the products they sell. They may increase their profit without impacting revenue if they can manufacture their goods more efficiently.

Companies are also usually mindful of [operating expenses (OpEx)](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/040915/what-are-different-types-operating-expenses.asp). These are the expenses that a company incurs to run its business. It can increase its profits without having to sell any additional goods if the company can reduce its operating expenses.

Companies can also be mindful of net profit by considering taxes and interest. Companies may have to raise capital by offering equity to avoid interest expenses but this can detract from retained earnings in the long run if investors demand [dividends](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dividend.asp).

Companies must do considerable planning and they must implement legal avoidance strategies to avoid taxes. A company would reduce its expenses in both areas and ultimately increase profit if it were mindful of both, again without having to earn any additional revenue.

## Key Differences

|  | Revenue | Profit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Definition** | Money earned from operations | Money kept after deducting expenses |
| **Reported** | Near the top of the income statement | Further down the page of the income statement |
| **Decision-Making** | When setting expectations | When deciding on how to allocate future capital |
| **Influenced by** | Market performance; less susceptible to bookkeeping variations and is usually a purer number | Accounting rules; may rely heavily on management projections |

Most people don't refer to gross or operating profit when they speak about a company's profit. This is usually net income—what's left after paying expenses or the net profit. A company can generate revenue and still have a [net loss](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/netloss.asp) at the same time.

One of the primary differences between revenue and profit is where each number is reported on a company's income statement. Revenue is reported near the top because it's less inclusive. Profit is further down because it incorporates expenses. This leads to another key difference: Revenue incorporates money taken in while profit reflects a company's inflows and outflows.

Companies use each metric differently to make decisions:

- Revenue projections are used to set manufacturing expectations because forecasted quantities of goods sold drive what inventory to create.
- Profit helps companies decide how to best allocate future capital, investing heavily when strong profits are to be expected or building its reserves when they're weaker.

Each category is influenced by accounting rules although revenue is often a purer number that's less susceptible to variation due to bookkeeping. There may be reliance on management estimates and more general ledger account balances when accounting for profit. Profit can be more impacted by accounting rules. Revenue is generally more influenced by market performance.

## Calculating Revenue to Profit

Companies begin their income statements by reporting revenue. They end them by reporting net profit. Several steps are involved in getting from one category to the other. The formula for calculating net income and each step in the process are:1

> Net Profit = (Net) Revenue - COGS - OpEx - Interest Expenses - Taxes

1. Calculate net revenue.This entails gathering all revenue sources and factoring in all appropriate items such as returns that directly reduce gross revenue.
2. Calculate the COGS. COGS is the cost of producing or purchasing the products that are sold during the period. It includes the cost of [raw materials](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/rawmaterials.asp), labor, and other direct costs. These expenses are only attributable to creating inventory to be sold. They don't include administrative costs that are geared more toward operating a business.
3. Calculate gross profit.Subtracting the COGS from gross sales gives you the gross profit.
4. Calculate OpEx. These are expenses that are incurred to run the business. They include rent, utilities, salaries, [marketing](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketing.asp) expenses, maintenance and repairs, and [property taxes](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/propertytax.asp). These costs are necessary to run the business, but they're not necessarily correlated to the production of a specific good that's sold.
5. Calculate the operating profit.Deduct the operating expenses from the gross profit to arrive at the operating profit.
7. Calculate interest and taxes.Interest and income taxes are two expenses that aren't usually included in OpEx. They're reported below operating profit and included when calculating net profit or net income.
8. Calculate net profit. Deduct the interest expenses and taxes paid during the period from the operating profit to arrive at the net income.

## Example of Revenue vs. Profit

Amazon ( [AMZN](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=AMZN)) reported its fiscal year 2024 results in February 2025. The company generated $272.31 billion in net product sales and $365.65 billion in net service sales during that period. Both of these amounts include contra-revenue accounts such as returns but the general direction of these accounts is to report only money earned before broader company expenses.2

The company's total net revenue was therefore $637.96 billion for the 12 months ending Dec. 31, 2024, even though the word sales is used on Amazon's financial statements.2

Amazon, 2024 Income Statement (select accounts).

Analysts must review the expense side of operations to bridge from total revenue to total profit. Amazon spent over $326.29 billion on the COGS. The company had over $569.37 billion of total operating expenses in total omitting expenses such as taxes or interest.

Amazon, 2024 Income Statement (select accounts).

Amazon reported $637.96 billion in revenue and a profit of $59.25 billion. Keep in mind that revenue isn't always an indication of profit. Some companies, including Amazon, may report record levels of revenue though they have minimal or no net positive profit because of the cost structure and one-time expense implications.

## Other Related Terms

Accrued revenue is the same as unrealized revenue. It's the revenue earned by a company for the delivery of goods or services that customers have yet to pay for.

Let's say a company sells widgets for $5 each on net-30 terms to all its customers. It sells 10 widgets in August. It invoices its customers on net-30 terms, so they won't have to pay until 30 days later on Sept. 30. August's revenue will be considered accrued revenue as a result until the company receives [payment](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/payment.asp) from its customers.

The company would recognize $50 in revenue on its income statement and $50 in accrued revenue as an asset on its [balance sheet](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/balancesheet.asp). The cash account on the income statement increases when the company collects the $50. The accrued revenue account decreases, and the $50 on the balance sheet remains unchanged.

Accrued revenue isn't the same as unearned revenue. It's the opposite. Unearned revenue accounts for money prepaid by a customer for goods or services that haven't yet been delivered. A company would recognize the revenue as unearned and wouldn't record it on its income statement until the period for which the goods or services were delivered, if it requires [prepayment](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/prepayment.asp) for its goods.

## Can Profit Be Higher Than Revenue?

Revenue sits at the top of a company's income statement. It's the top line. Profit is referred to as the bottom line. Profit is less than revenue because expenses and liabilities have been deducted.

## Is Revenue the Same As Sales?

Revenue is commonly referred to as sales but it's any income that a company generates before expenses are subtracted. Sales are what the firm earns from selling goods and services to its customers.

## What Is More Important, Profit or Revenue?

Both are important but profit gives a more accurate picture of a company's financial position because a company's liabilities and other expenses such as payroll are already accounted for when its profit is calculated.

## How Much of Revenue Is Profit?

Profit is whatever remains from revenue after a company accounts for expenses, debts, additional income, and operating costs.

## The Bottom Line

Revenue and profit show up on a company's income statement. Revenue is called the top line, while profit is the bottom line. Both are important to consider when making investment decisions, but investors should remember that revenue is the income a firm makes without taking expenses into account. Be sure to account for all the expenses a company has, including wages, debts, taxes, and other expenses, when determining its profit.

# Understanding Profit vs. Revenue: Key Differences Explained

Understand the difference between profit and revenue, including their definitions, importance in business, and how they influence financial success.

May 12 2025 \| 6 min read

![](https://www.pnc.com/content/dam/pnc-thought-leadership/small-business/managing-business-finances/pnc_insights_sb_revenue-vs-profit-in-depth-comparison.jpg)

- Revenue is the total income before expenses, showing demand for your products or services.
- Profit is what’s left after costs, ensuring growth, reinvestment, and financial security.
- Tracking both revenue and profit helps you balance growth with cost efficiency for sustainable success.
- Understanding the relationship between revenue and profit is crucial for long-term financial health and success.

Running a business means juggling numerous priorities, but understanding the core financial metrics — revenue and profit — is essential for making smart decisions that lead to long-term success. In this guide, you'll learn the differences between these two vital figures and why tracking both is key to your business’s growth and financial health

## What is Profit?

Profit is the money your business keeps after paying for all its costs, such as rent, supplies, employee wages, and other expenses.

## Definition of Profit

If you earn more than you spend, you make a profit. If your costs exceed what you bring in, you operate at a loss. Profit is essential because it allows you to grow, invest in new opportunities, and keep your business running successfully.

For example, if your business earns $10,000 in total revenue but spends $7,000 on expenses — such as rent, employee wages, and the [cost of goods sold](https://www.pnc.com/insights/small-business/running-your-business/cogs-to-track-profitability-inform-business-decisions.html) (COGS) — you’re left with a profit of $3,000. This profit can be [reinvested into your business](https://www.pnc.com/insights/small-business/growing-your-business/strategic-reinvestment-in-your-small-business.html), saved for future needs, or distributed to stakeholders.

## Types of Profit: Gross, Operating, and Net

Profit is categorized into three main types, each offering a different insight into your business’s financial health. These include:

- **Gross profit:** This is the earnings remaining after subtracting COGS. This figure highlights how efficiently you produce and sell your products or services.
- **Operating profit:** Also known as operating income, this metric deducts operating expenses — including wages, rent, and utilities — from gross profit, providing insight into your core operations.
- **Net profit:** This is the final profit figure after all expenses, including taxes and interest, are deducted. Net profit, or net income, is often referred to as the “bottom line” and reflects your overall profitability.

## Importance of Profit in Business

Profit is crucial for your business’s long-term success, providing the resources needed for growth, stability, and financial security. It allows you to reinvest in your business — whether through product improvements, expansion, or marketing — while also building reserves to weather unexpected expenses or economic downturns. Profit ensures fair compensation for you, your employees, and investors and makes your business more appealing to lenders and investors for future funding. By focusing on profitability, you create a sustainable and financially healthy foundation that supports smarter decision-making and long-term growth.

## Understanding Revenue

Now, let’s shift focus to revenue. In this section, you'll come to understand what revenue is, where it comes from, and why it’s an essential metric for your business.

## Definition of Revenue

Revenue, often called total revenue or the “top line,” is the total money your business earns from selling products or services before any costs are subtracted. Think of it as all the money flowing into your business.

For example, if you own a bakery and sell 100 loaves of bread at $5 each, your revenue is $500 (100 x $5). This is before considering costs like ingredients, rent, or employee wages.

## Different Sources of Revenue

Revenue can come from various sources, depending on the nature of your business:

- **Sales revenue**: The primary income from selling goods or services.
- **Subscription or recurring revenue**: Income from memberships, retainers, or licensing fees
- **Interest or investment income**: Earnings from financial investments or holdings.
- **Other revenue streams**: Includes rental income, royalties, or asset sales.

## Importance of Revenue as a Metric

Revenue is one of the most critical indicators of your business’s success and long-term sustainability. It reflects how well your products or services are selling and whether there is demand in the market. A steady or [growing revenue stream](https://www.pnc.com/insights/small-business/manage-business-finances/5-ways-to-find-new-sources-of-revenue-for-your-small-business.html) can signal business expansion, customer interest, and overall financial health. However, while strong revenue is a positive sign, it’s only one piece of the puzzle — managing expenses effectively is just as important for maintaining profitability.

## Key Differences Between Revenue and Profit

**In this section**, you'll discover how to calculate [revenue and profit](https://www.pnc.com/insights/small-business/manage-business-finances/revenue-vs-profit-in-depth-comparison.html), understand their placement in financial reports, and see real-world examples that illustrate why profit matters just as much as revenue. By the end, you'll have a clearer picture of how these key financial metrics impact your business’s success.

## Calculation Methods for Revenue and Profit

Revenue and profit are calculated in different ways:

- **Revenue formula**: Total revenue = Price per unit × Number of units sold
- **Profit formula**: Net profit = Total revenue - (COGS + Operating expenses + Taxes + Interest)

## Revenue vs. Profit in Financial Reports

Revenue is listed at the top of an income statement, while profit appears at various levels — gross profit, operating profit, and net profit. Each profit metric provides deeper insights into different aspects of your business’s financial health. To recap, gross profit reflects the direct costs of production, operating profit accounts for ongoing business expenses, and net profit reveals the overall profitability after all costs are considered. By examining these figures together, you may be able to identify areas for improvement, such as reducing operational expenses or boosting sales, to drive stronger financial performance.

## Real-World Examples Comparing Revenue and Profit

To gain a clearer understanding of these two metrics, let’s consider two businesses:

- **Business A** reports $1 million in total revenue but incurs $950,000 in expenses, leaving only $50,000 in net profit.
- **Business B** reports $750,000 in total revenue but manages costs effectively, resulting in a net profit of $200,000.

Despite lower revenue, **Business B** is more profitable. This example highlights why you must focus on both metrics, not just revenue.

## Calculating Profit vs. Revenue

**In this section**, you'll learn key calculations and metrics to assess your business’s financial performance, helping you track and improve both revenue and profit effectively.

## How to Calculate Profit

You can determine profit by subtracting different types of costs from revenue:

- **Gross profit** = Total revenue - COGS
- **Operating profit** = Gross profit - Operating expenses
- **Net profit** = Operating profit - Taxes and interest

## How to Calculate Revenue

Revenue calculation depends on your sales volume and pricing:

- **Product-based business**: Revenue = Units sold × Selling price per unit
- **Service-based business**: Revenue = Total billable hours × Rate per hour

## Understanding Revenue to Profit Ratios

You can assess your financial health using key ratios:

- **Profit margin**: (Net profit / Total revenue) × 100
- **Gross margin**: (Gross profit / Total revenue) × 100
- **Operating margin**: (Operating profit / Total revenue) × 100

These ratios help you evaluate efficiency and profitability.

## Strategic Considerations

**In this section**, you’ll explore key strategic decisions between growing revenue and [optimizing profit](https://www.pnc.com/insights/small-business/manage-business-finances/key-to-larger-profits-strategic-margin-management.html), with insights on how to balance both for long-term success.

## Increasing Revenue vs. Increasing Profit

Increasing revenue focuses on boosting sales and attracting more customers. Strategies include:

- Expanding product lines or services to appeal to new customers.
- Entering new markets to tap into different customer bases.
- Ramping up marketing efforts to drive awareness and demand.

Increasing profit aims to maximize efficiency and improve the bottom line. This includes:

- Reducing operating costs by streamlining processes and cutting unnecessary expenses.
- Improving profit margins by reducing production costs or increasing pricing on certain products.
- Optimizing the supply chain and operational efficiency to cut costs and increase profits.

A successful strategy is to have a balanced approach — one that focuses on growing revenue while managing costs effectively for long-term sustainability.

## Evaluating What’s Better for Business Growth

While increasing revenue signals business expansion, increasing profit ensures your business remains financially stable and sustainable in the long run. Balancing both is crucial, depending on your business goals and current financial health.

## Case Studies on Revenue and Profit Strategies

To help you further understand how different businesses balance revenue and profit strategies, let’s take a look at three case studies from various industries.

- **A SaaS company** focuses on increasing subscriptions to boost revenue while optimizing cloud infrastructure to reduce costs. By improving its server efficiency, the company lowers operational expenses, leading to a higher net profit despite the increased customer base.
- **A clothing brand** introduces premium pricing on best-selling items, which increases both revenue and gross profit. By targeting high-demand products, the business is able to generate more income per item sold and improve its profitability without increasing overall costs.
- **A local café** increases revenue by expanding its menu offerings and introducing a new delivery service. Meanwhile, the café reduces profit-draining waste by [improving inventory management](https://www.pnc.com/insights/small-business/running-your-business/guide-to-managing-inventory-liquidity-operational-expenses.html). This combination of boosting revenue through new services and controlling costs results in increased profitability.

These examples show how companies from different sectors balance revenue growth and cost management to maximize their financial performance. By carefully considering these strategies, you may be able to apply similar tactics to achieve success in your own business.

## Conclusions

- Revenue is the total income your business earns before any expenses, showing how much demand there is for your products or services. Profit is the money left after you subtract all costs, with net profit being the most complete measure of what’s actually earned.
- Both revenue and profit are essential for making smart financial decisions and growing your business.

## Why Understanding Both Metrics Matters

Tracking both revenue and profit is essential for a clear picture of your financial health. While high revenue shows demand, it doesn't guarantee profitability if costs are too high. Strong profit margins, on the other hand, indicate efficient cost management and long-term viability. Balancing both metrics allows you to make decisions that drive sustainable growth and avoid potential financial pitfalls.

## Final Thoughts on Financial Health and Success

Understanding the relationship between revenue and profit is key to making strategic decisions that foster business growth. By focusing on both increasing revenue and optimizing profit, you can build a resilient, financially stable business that thrives in the long run.

Imagine you launch a business, and it takes off faster than expected. Customers are lining up, sales are booming, and cash is rolling in.

Everything feels like a win. You've built something people want, but as you look at the cash in your bank account, a question comes to mind: Is that money revenue or profit?

The difference between revenue and profit isn't just a choice of words or a technicality. It's a make-or-break distinction for your business. Understanding the differences between revenue vs. profit is key to whether your business thrives or dives, so let's cover the fundamentals.

Revenue is the income generated when you [sell your products or services](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/sales-revenue/) — before you consider any expenses. It's sometimes called "top-line revenue" because it appears at the top of your income statement.

Most of your revenue will come from the sales made through your primary business activities, and it may also include income from secondary sources like investment earnings or one-time gains from asset sales. For example, if you're in the business of selling heating and air conditioning services, this may include:

- **Primary sources of revenue:** Sales of maintenance services, installation of HVAC units, annual service agreements, etc.
- **Secondary sources of revenue:** Income earned from training other maintenance workers, rebates, affiliate earnings from online sales of air filters, etc.

Revenue is directly tied to sales and your team's ability to sell in the marketplace. This means the following factors could affect revenue:

- **Product-market fit**

How well your product or service meets your target audience's wants and needs makes a big difference in total revenue. A strong [product-market fit](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/product-market-fit/) indicates high demand for your product and, therefore, substantial revenue.

**Example:** Imagine you're selling productivity software. A strong product-market fit means your software directly addresses the challenges your target audience faces — disorganized workflows, missed deadlines, or difficulty with team collaboration. By showcasing features that solve these pain points, your sales pitch becomes more compelling. That results in higher [conversion rates](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/analytics/sales-conversion-rate/) and, ultimately, more revenue.

- **Your value proposition**

Why would someone buy your product or service? That's your [value proposition](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/customer-value-proposition/). Think of your value proposition as your elevator pitch to a potential customer. A compelling value prop makes it clear that what you're selling is better than your competition's offering, which often drives a big boost in revenue.

**Example:** One of your competitors offers basic accounting software, but your company sells an integrated solution that automates tasks and generates reports with a single click. By emphasizing your value proposition, you can stand out from the competition and close more deals.

- **Your pricing strategy**

How you price your products matters. Pricing directly affects how much you'll bring in when you make a sale, and it can also affect how consumers perceive your business or your product.

**Example:** You are the sales rep for a clothing manufacturer. After doing some market research, you've found that almost all the retail shops that purchase and sell your brand sell to more affluent clientele. In response, you adjust your [price anchoring strategy](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/price-anchoring/). You raise prices to align with what the end customer expects to see in premium shopping areas. The higher price point enhances the perceived quality of your products, encouraging customers to buy more. This tactic is called [prestige pricing](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/prestige-pricing/).

- **Competitive strength**

Your ability to compete influences earnings. If your product has unique features or higher quality, you're more likely to attract customers and generate more revenue.

**Example:** Let's say your company sells [customer relationship management](https://www.salesforce.com/crm/what-is-crm/) (CRM) software. Your competitor offers only basic [contact management](https://www.salesforce.com/small-business/contact-management/) features. Your brand includes those same features as well as advanced capabilities like marketing automation tools, lead scoring, and in-depth customer analytics. This clear differentiation helps your sales team target customers who value these advanced features. That could command a higher price point per user, increasing your revenue.

- **Accessibility to the market**

Revenue hinges on reaching your target audience. What's the point in selling something no one can buy? If your website is clunky, your [sales pipeline](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/pipeline/) is too complex, you have no valuable [sales channels](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/partner-relationship-management/channel-sales-guide/), or your marketing efforts don't connect with people interested in your product, you're unlikely to make many sales.

**Example:** You're selling a new line of exercise bikes. Having a user-friendly online store with clear product information, multiple payment options, and fast delivery makes it easier for customers to complete a purchase. Similarly, offering free trials and demos, along with accessible sales reps, can streamline the sales funnel and convert more leads into paying customers.

- **Access to resources**

Resources like cash, assets, skilled labor, and technology mean everything when developing and marketing your product or service. A lack of resources can kill your ability to serve your audience, which could also hurt revenue growth.

**Example:** You sell for a B2B software company with a shoestring budget. Their limited capital prevents them from investing in research and development for cutting-edge features that your customers want. This could hinder your ability to maintain market share or attract new customers.

- **External disruptions**

Events around the world play a role in your business's success. While you can't control everything that happens, you can prepare your organization to handle disruptions like economic downturns, rising interest rates, and regulatory changes.

**Example:** You sell cars for a dealership that works with only one manufacturer. Supply chain disruptions can disrupt your revenue flow. To build cars, your manufacturer needs a steady supply of parts and materials like semiconductors, steel, and rubber. Shortages of any of these can stop production lines and delay deliveries, which results in fewer cars on the lot for you to sell.

Now let's look at the various types of revenue you will encounter and how to calculate them.

Revenue is a broad term that refers to the money a business earns. But you can break down the term further to provide more detailed information.

To help you understand the different types of revenue, we'll walk through an example. Let's say you run a boutique hotel. You look at your income statement and see the following revenue-related terms:

- **Gross revenue:** [Gross revenue](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/gross-revenue/) is your total sales number. This line item accounts for all the guests who booked a hotel room, ordered room service, or used chargeable amenities. It does not include discounts or refunds.
- **Net revenue:** [Net revenue differs from gross revenue](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/gross-revenue-vs-net-revenue/). Also called net sales, it’s the total revenue a company earns from sales after deducting returns, allowances, and discounts. You would take your gross revenue number and subtract discounts on rooms, refunds for stays, or room service.
- **Non-operating revenue:** This is income that comes from sources outside your daily operations. For a hotel, that might include commercial rental income (leasing a portion of your hotel to another business), revenue from selling advertising space in your lobby, or proceeds from selling old bed frames after a renovation.
- **Average revenue per unit (ARPU):** This metric is used to understand the average revenue generated per unit sold. The meaning of "unit" will vary depending on the business. For your hotel, that is how much revenue you generate per night booked, on average.
- **Monthly recurring revenue (MRR):** This metric focuses on the predictable, [recurring revenue](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/how-to-calculate-recurring-revenue/) a business generates each month. An example would be if your hotel offers a monthly subscription to exclusive hotel amenities.
- **Annual recurring revenue (ARR):** This is the recurring revenue your business generates every year. It would be applicable if your hotel offers a membership subscription that collects an annual fee.

What's the one thing all these revenue types share? They all report the sales you've made and the income you've earned without considering any expenses.

Grab your income statement and let's start at the top.

#### Gross revenue formula

Gross revenue = (number of sales x sales price per unit) + other sales

- **Example:** At your hotel, you booked 650 nights in a month. The average daily room rate for the month was $120. You also sold room service totaling $22,000 for the month. Your gross revenue would be:



(650 nights (number of sales) x $120 (sales price per unit)) + $22,000 (other sales) = $100,000 (gross revenue)

#### Net revenue formula

Net revenue = gross revenue – discounts and refunds

- **Example:** Let's say that during that same month, you offered 50 guests half off their room rate. First, find the total discount value: 50 guests at a $60 discount is a total discount of $3,000. If this were the only discount you offered for the month, then your net revenue would be:



$100,000 (gross revenue) – $3,000 (discounts and refunds) = $97,000 (net revenue)

#### Average revenue per unit formula

ARPU = net revenue / total number of units

- **Example:** To find your hotel's ARPU for that month, we will need your net revenue number ($97,000) and how many nights (i.e., units) were booked (650).



$97,000 (net revenue) / 650 (total number of units) = $149.23 per night (ARPU)

#### Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) formula

MRR = average revenue per unit x total units

- **Example:** Your hotel offers visitors a monthly subscription of $20/month for spa services. You have 200 people signed up for the month.



$20 (average revenue per unit) x 200 (total units) = $4,000 (MRR)

#### Annual recurring revenue (ARR) formula

ARR = MRR x 12

- **Example:** To determine your ARR, we need your MRR figure ($4,000), which we will multiply by 12 (months). Effectively, this is your MRR annualized.



$4,000 (MRR) x 12 = $48,000 (ARR)

Understanding each type of revenue is essential for assessing the health of your business. Keeping accurate records allows these revenue figures and metrics to help you identify which parts of your business are doing well, find opportunities for growth or improvement, and make better sales decisions.

But revenue is only one piece of the financial picture. Profit determines whether your business can truly succeed. While revenue shows how well you sell, profit reflects how efficiently you make those sales. Let's take a closer look at profit.

[**_▸▸▸ See how to manage revenue on one platform_**](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/demos/agentforce-revenue-management-demo/?d=pb) _Watch a demo of Revenue Cloud in action and discover how to speed up growth with help from agents._

Profit is the money a business has left over after all expenses are subtracted from revenue. Profit is located at the bottom of your income statement. When someone asks what your bottom line is, they are asking about profit, not revenue.

When we discussed net revenue before, we only subtracted the expenses directly tied to the revenue, mainly discounts and refunds. Profits go a step further by deducting all other expenses from revenue: [cost of goods sold](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/calculate-cost-of-goods-sold/) (COGS), administrative costs, and any other expenses.

Your profit is directly related to revenue, meaning that all the factors that affect revenue will also affect profit. But a few additional factors have a more direct impact on your profit, and all these factors are related to expenses.

- **Economies of scale**

The economies of scale principle states that as a business grows and production volume increases, the average cost per unit of production decreases. Lower costs per unit result in bigger [profit margins](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/net-profit-margin/).

**Example:** You're a sales rep for a bicycle manufacturer. You've expanded into new markets and had your company's bikes featured in some of the major retail stores in your state. Your company is thrilled to see the increased sales because they've been able to request bulk discounts from their steel, rubber, and aluminum suppliers. This lowers the per-unit cost of bikes, meaning you make more money on each unit sold, increasing overall profitability for the business.

- **Efficiency and effectiveness**

When your business becomes more efficient, you can generate the same income with fewer resources. This might come from streamlining operations, a well-trained team, or minimizing errors with automation or [AI technology](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/ai/), all of which cut costs and lead to higher profits.

**Example:** You're the sales team leader at a software company. You've noticed that the team members who make the most sales are those with the highest customer satisfaction ratings. You recommend that your company invest in training modules that teach reps how to answer customer questions more efficiently. This decreases the average time spent per sales call, enabling the team to reach more potential customers and boost profits over time.

- **Business model superiority**

A well-designed business model that nails both revenue generation and cost management can mean maximum profit.

**Example:** You work for a startup that offers marketing automation tools for contractors. Your small, highly skilled sales team uses a proprietary method to find and connect with construction companies (your client base), saving you time on cold calls and reducing other sales-related costs. This helps you focus your energy elsewhere and leads to greater profits.

- **Cash flow management**

Improving your cash flow management can improve profits in a few ways. First, you'll have cash on hand to pay your debts, which reduces the chance of paying penalties for late payments on supplier invoices. Second, having enough cash on hand helps you avoid the high interest costs of short-term borrowing.

**Example:** You are the sales rep for an HVAC company. Even though you've shown high sales numbers, your CFO asks why customer payments haven't been coming in. When reviewing your accounts, you notice a few problems. First, you see that most of your customers wait to pay their bills until the very end of their 60-day payment deadline. Second, you notice that some of them don't pay their [invoices](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/what-is-an-invoice/) until you send a reminder. You encourage your company to (1) switch from net-60 payment terms to a net-30 invoicing model, and (2) switch to a [billing software](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-lifecycle-management/billing-software/) that sends customers automatic payment reminders. Both solutions will give you faster access to cash, helping your company rely less on short-term loans to fund the next round of HVAC installs, therefore saving money and boosting profits.

- **Debt structure and interest costs**

Interest expenses can quickly eat into profits if you're not careful. The more debt you carry, the more interest costs you'll have. And if you have a high debt-to-equity ratio, your bank also may see you as a credit risk and charge you higher interest rates, costing you more.

**Example:** A dog grooming business uses a high-interest line of credit to cover weekly operating costs. You're working on improving cash flow, but in the meantime, you decide to refinance your line of credit. Transferring your it to another bank offering lower interest rates will save you money over time and increase profits.

There are two types of profit: gross profit and net profit.

- **Gross profit:** Gross profit is the amount of money a business retains after subtracting the [cost of goods sold](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/calculate-cost-of-goods-sold/) (COGS) from its total revenue. The COGS covers the direct costs of producing and selling your products or services.
- **Net profit:** Also called operating profit, net profit is what you get when you take your gross profit and subtract all fixed and variable expenses like employee salaries, utilities, and rent.

Let's check back in with your income statement and learn how to calculate both gross and net profit.

#### Gross profit formula

Gross profit = net revenue – COGS

- **Example:** In our boutique hotel example, we found its net revenue to be $97,000. Now you need to figure out the total COGS. COGS are the direct expenses incurred to provide a room to a guest for one night. This might include linens and amenities. Let's say COGS is $30 per night. At 650 nights, this makes the total COGS $19,500. This is how we would determine gross profit:



$97,000 (net revenue) – $19,500 (COGS) = $77,500

#### Net profit formula

Net profit = gross profit – operating expenses

- **Example:** We know your gross profit is $77,500. The total operating expenses for running your hotel — which includes salaries, property taxes, rent, marketing, vendor payments, and utilities — is $45,000. Your net profit would be:



$77,500 (gross profit) – $45,000 (operating expenses) = $32,500

Revenue and profit have a close relationship, but they have key differences.

- **What they include:** Revenue includes sales numbers only. Profit includes both sales and expenses.
- **What they measure:** Revenue measures business activity, or how much your sales team is bringing in, while profit measures financial performance, or how much money you keep at the end of the day.
- **Location on the income statement:** Revenue appears at the top of your income statement, and profit is listed at the bottom. This placement reflects the logical flow of business activities. You start with total sales, and after deducting expenses, you arrive at profit.

Both revenue and profit are important metrics, but they serve different purposes. Each tells a different story about your business's health, growth, and potential.

To better understand when to focus on revenue vs. profit, let's examine some common business scenarios:

To estimate customer demand, focus on revenue. Monitoring revenue trends over time allows you to predict sales volumes, helping you plan operational needs effectively.

For example, imagine you're selling a project management tool. If you can forecast your [monthly recurring revenue](https://www.salesforce.com/blog/monthly-recurring-revenue/)(MRR), you can predict how many subscribers you'll likely need to onboard in the coming months. You can make sure sales reps are available and ready to get them familiar with the system. Consider using a [revenue intelligence platform](https://www.salesforce.com/sales/revenue-intelligence/) to help track trends and compare data period over period.

If you're deciding where and how much to invest in your business, you'll want to focus on profit. Profit indicates how much money remains after all expenses are paid, which is what you have available to reinvest. And if you can estimate the profit you'll have at the end of the month or quarter, you can find investment opportunities that fit within that budget.

Let's say you're considering buying new payroll software to make your bi-weekly payroll process more efficient. You'll need to know your expected profit at the end of the quarter to see if you can afford the new software. Revenue wouldn't provide you with this same information.

When evaluating your business's overall financial health, both metrics matter. High revenue can signal strong demand, but if expenses are eating up all your earnings, your business may not be sustainable.

Let's imagine the following scenario: Your software business made a whopping $2.5 million in revenue last year. But as a startup, there were many expenses. Last year, expenses totaled $2.6 million, which puts you in a loss. While your revenue is impressive, negative profit shows that your business was not financially successful that year.

Let's compare that business to another business you own, a lawn care company. That business only reported revenue of $400,000. But you've been running this business for a while, and you know how to do it efficiently. Your business expenses were only $250,000, leaving you with a profit of $150,000.

If you only look at revenue, you may think that the software business is more successful. But in the end, your software company lost $100,000. Your lawn care company may not have had very high revenue, but it produced a $150,000 profit, making it the more financially successful business.

Both metrics are important.

- Use revenue to track growth and forecast demand.
- Use profit to gauge whether your expenses are reasonable and to make smart investments.
- Use both to get a clear picture of performance and financial stability.

The relationship between revenue and profit is crucial to understand. Think of running a business like flying a plane: Revenue is the engine thrust that propels you forward, and profit is the lift that keeps you in the air. You need both for a successful flight.

# Revenue vs. Profit: Key Differences and Which Matters More

December 29, 2025
\|
Nicole Bennett
\|

Reading Time - 8 minutes

![](https://www.venasolutions.com/hs-fs/hubfs/03%20Blog%20Files/Blog%20Images/Blog%20Articles/Revenue%20vs%20Profit/Vena-Blog-Header_Revenue%20vs%20Profit_1200px.jpg?width=358&name=Vena-Blog-Header_Revenue%20vs%20Profit_1200px.jpg)

Revenue and profit are frequently mentioned together, but they are not the same.

Revenue is the money a business earns before expenses, while profit is what’s left after those expenses are deducted.

Each metric tells a different story about financial performance.

Let’s take a closer look.

## Key Takeaways

- Revenue, sometimes referred to as “top line,”is the amount of money a company earns before expenses. Commonly used revenue formulas are gross revenue, net revenue, average revenue per unit, monthly recurring revenue and annual recurring revenue.


- Profitis the money left over after expenses are deducted from revenue earned. A common term that is sometimes used when discussing profit is Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization (EBITDA). Profit is sometimes referred to as“the bottom line”.


- Both revenue and profit are essential to understand and track, but profit provides a more complete picture of a company's financial health.


- Increased revenue is generally achieved through expansion and scaling, while higher profits are reached through cost and operational optimization.


- Companies prioritize revenue and profit differently at various lifecycle stages.


- Revenue and profit can both be tracked more seamlessly and effectively using FP&A software.


## What Is Revenue?

Revenue is the amount of money a company generates before subtracting expenses. It’s also referred to as the top line because it appears at the top of an [income statement](https://www.venasolutions.com/templates/reporting/income-statement) and is the starting point for analyzing an organization’s[cash flow](https://www.venasolutions.com/blog/what-is-a-cash-flow-statement).

Revenue is important because it is a key indicator of market demand, business scale, and growth trajectory.

Revenue is generally focused on money earned from the direct sale of goods and services and not income from other sources (such as IP ownership or rental properties).

### Types of Revenue

Some of the metrics businesses use to measure revenue include:

- Gross Revenue:Total revenue before deductions


- Net Revenue:Revenue after discounts, returns, and allowances


- Average Revenue Per Unit (ARPU):Revenue per customer or unit


- Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR):Predictable monthly revenue for subscription based businesses


- Annual recurring revenue (ARR):Yearly equivalent of MRR.(For an in-depth discussion of ARR, visit [Annual Recurring Revenue: What Is ARR & How To Calculate It](https://www.venasolutions.com/finance-glossary/annual-recurring-revenue)


### How To Calculate Revenue

#### Gross Revenue

Gross revenue is the most straight forward type of revenue calculation. It's the total revenue you earn from all sales. Gross revenue is calculated by multiplying sales volume by sales price per unit.

Gross Revenue = Total Sales Volume × Sales Price Per Unit

#### Net Revenue

Net revenue is revenue earned after allowances such as discounts and returns have been deducted. This number gives you a clearer picture of actual earnings versus simply calculating money coming in. Note that net revenue only deducts expenses related directly to the sale of goods.

Net Revenue = Gross Revenue − Directly Related Selling Expenses

#### Average Revenue Per Unit/User/Account(ARPU)

Average revenue per unit/user (ARPU) or account (ARPA) is the revenue earned from the sale of a single unit sold or single contract account closed. It's calculated by dividing your total revenue by the number of users/accounts during a given time period.

Average Revenue Per User = Total Revenue ÷ Number of Users/Accounts During a Given Period

#### Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)

Monthly recurring revenue (MRR) is a benchmark [metric used by SaaS companies](https://www.venasolutions.com/blog/saas-kpis-metrics-to-track) and other subscription-based businesses to track predictable revenue on a monthly basis. It is calculated by multiplying the total number of active users in a given month by the ARPU.

Monthly Recurring Revenue = Average Revenue Per Account × Total Number of Active Monthly Users

#### Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)

Annual recurring revenue (ARR) is similar toMRR but calculates predictable revenue on an annual basis rather than a monthly one. It's calculated by multiplying the MRR by 12.

Annual Recurring Revenue = (Average Revenue Per Account × Total Number of Active Monthly Users) × 12

## What Is Profit?

Profit is the amount of money a company earns after expenses are deducted from revenue. It is also referred to as the bottom line and appears as net income on financial statements.

Profit is important because it is an indicator of a business’ financial health, efficiency, and sustainability. And of course, profit allows companies to grow, invest in technologies to drive efficiency and hire new people, to name just a few benefits.

### Types of Profit

Some of the metrics businesses use to measure profit include:

- Gross Profit: Total after subtracting COGS from revenue

- Operating Profit (or EBIT): Total after subtracting both COGS and operating expenses from revenue


- Net Profit:Total after subtracting all other expenses


### How To Calculate Profit

#### Gross Profit

Gross profit is the total profit earned after subtractingthe costs of goods sold (COGS) from net revenue.

Gross Profit = Net Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold

#### Operating Profit (or EBIT)

Operating Profit is similar to EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization) but Operating Profit does not include depreciation and amortization (which is why it can also be referred to as EBIT).

Operating Profit = (Net Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold) − Operating Expenses − Depreciation − Amortization

#### Net Profit

Net profit accounts for all other operating expenses and is calculated by subtracting these expenses which can include rent and utilities, marketing and advertising, salaries, accounting, vendor payments and more from gross profit.

Net Profit = Operating Profit − Taxes, Interest and Other Costs

| Definition | Total earnings before expenses | Money left after all expenses are deducted |
| Appears on Financial Statement | Top line of the income statement | Bottom line of the income statement |
| Indicates | Business scale and growth | Business efficiency and sustainability |
| Focus for Growth | Market expansion, sales growth | Cost control, efficiency and pricing optimization |

## Revenue vs. Profit: Which Should Businesses Prioritize?

Revenue and profit are both essential financial metrics to measure, but the importance of each to a particular business can vary based on its size,[maturity](https://www.venasolutions.com/blog/rule-of-40), current goals and the specific point in time when it's being measured. To understand why, it's important to know what each metric indicates about a company's financial performance.

Every businessis different, and there is no “one size fits all” answer as to whether your business should prioritize revenue above profit or vice versa.However, there are some guidelines—guardrails,if you will—that apply to most businesses.

In general, for mature and stable companies, profit is the ultimate indicator of financial health.

However, revenue is an essential precursor to profitability, particularly for young or growing companies that may want to prioritize capturing market share before focusing on optimizing profitability.

### When To Focus on Revenue Growth

Focusing onrevenue growth is typically a good strategy for startups, high-growth companies, and companies seeking market expansion. Think of the old adage “it takes money to make money.”

That is becauserevenueindicates[the size and scale of a business](https://www.venasolutions.com/blog/small-business-revenue-statistics), market demand, market share, sales volume and growth potential. It can be used to attract investors and secure financing, and it provides a foundation for strategizing about future profitability.

### When To Focus on Profitability

Profit measures a company's efficiency and viability. It reflects the long-term sustainability of a business based on how its leaders are managing funds to stay profitable. Maintaining high profitability enables reinvestment in R&D and other new growth opportunities, and it also influences a company's valuation to attract investors.

This is why mature businesses and companies undergoing investor scrutiny tend to focus on profitability.

Also, it is wise for most companies to focus on profitability duringeconomic downturns.

### Balancing Both for Long-Term Success

Ultimately, the ideal balance between revenue and profit depends on the specific goals, industry and life stage of the company. Thats why both metrics are crucial to always track, even if one may be prioritized over the other as a company grows and evolves.

As an FP&A professional, it’s your job to support the business in finding the right equilibrium based on industry, lifecycle, and strategy.

## The difference between profit vs. revenue

Revenue is the money a business earns by selling a product or service, and profit is the money your business keeps after accounting for all the expenses involved in generating that revenue.

Here’s an example: suppose your SaaS business’s revenue is $50,000 but you spend $40,000 on marketing, the profit from that revenue is $10,000.

[<<<Download the SaaS metrics cheat sheet>>.](https://www.hibob.com/financial-tools/saas-metrics-cheat-sheet/)

You need to have a read on your business’s financial health to make informed business decisions.

Revenue and profit are the two most important metrics to get that valuable insight into your business’s financial health. These metrics help you understand your financial statements, manage your cash flows, and prepare budgets for the next month, quarter, and year.

Read on to understand the difference in revenue vs profit, how to calculate both, and how you can increase them to improve your financial performance.

## What is revenue?

Revenue (also called the topline) is the money your business earns from core operations—like selling products or providing services.

It’s often called the top line because it’s the first number on your income statement. Revenue doesn’t include income from things like rent on company property or interest on savings—only what you earn from day-to-day business.

You might also hear it called net sales or net revenue. That’s because you subtract things like discounts, credits, and refunds from your total sales to get to your actual revenue.

For a SaaS company, revenue includes everything customers pay—monthly or annually—to use the software.

Understanding your revenue helps you track incoming cash flow, build budgets, and forecast your [revenue run rate](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/revenue-run-rate/). It’s a starting point for shaping financial plans and projecting future growth.

### **Annual recurring revenue (ARR)**

If your business runs on long-term subscription contracts, you might also track [annual recurring revenue (ARR)](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/annual-recurring-revenue-arr/)—the total revenue you expect from active contracts over a 12-month period.

ARR helps you gauge product-market fit, assess momentum, and [benchmark your business](https://www.hibob.com/hr-glossary/hr-benchmarking/) against others in your space.

For example, in 2020, the median growth rate for SaaS companies with less than $1 million in ARR was [47% in 2020](https://www.saas-capital.com/research/2021-private-saas-company-growth-rate-benchmarks/)—down from 68% before the pandemic.

Are you growing faster or slower than that? And what does your growth path look like in the months ahead?

![Growth rate comparison chart for 2019 and 2020 based on ARR categories, showing distinct median growth percentages. growth-rate, ARR-chart](https://res.cloudinary.com/www-hibob-com/fl_lossy,f_webp,q_auto/wp-website/uploads/Revenue-vs.-Profit-Image2-scaled.png)ARR growth benchmarks from SaaS Capital

## What is profit?

Profit is what’s left after your business covers all its expenses. That includes things like operating costs, taxes, and depreciation—when assets lose value over time.

Unlike revenue, profit also reflects income from outside your core business, such as rental or interest income.

To truly understand your company’s financial health, it’s important to look at profit from different angles. That’s why businesses use multiple types of profit—each one showing how earnings and expenses stack up at different stages.

**Here are the most important types of profits you should be familiar with:**

### **1\. Gross profit**

[Gross profit](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/gross-profit/) is what you get when you subtract the cost of goods sold (COGS) from your revenue. COGS includes the direct costs of delivering your product or service—like server expenses or third-party subscription fees for a SaaS company.

You can also look at gross profit as a margin: gross profit margin shows how much of your revenue remains after covering those direct costs. It’s a useful way to measure profitability and compare your performance to similar businesses.

In SaaS, there are two types of gross profit margins: subscription gross margin and total gross margin. Subscription gross margin excludes customer support costs, so you can focus on the profitability of your core offering.

On the other hand, the total gross margin accounts for customer support expenses.

In 2021, [KeyBanc](https://www.key.com/kco/images/2021_kbcm_saas_survey_final.pdf) surveyed senior executives of 350 SaaS companies with median recurring revenue of $8.5 million (in 2020) and found the median subscription margin to be 80% and total gross margin to be 73%.

To gauge your company’s profitability, you can benchmark the subscription and total gross margins with these numbers.

![Size of Company Revenue Chart showing Subscription Gross Margin and Total Gross Margin across various revenue ranges., GAAP Revenue chart, Gross Margin comparison](https://res.cloudinary.com/www-hibob-com/fl_lossy,f_webp,q_auto/wp-website/uploads/Revenue-vs.-Profit-Image3-scaled.png)Gross margin benchmarks from KeyBanc

[Software Equity Group](https://softwareequity.com/blog/gross-margin-saas/) recommends that healthy, privately-held SaaS businesses have a total gross margin of over 70%, but there are exceptions. For instance, if you’re a SaaS startup and providing frequent discounts, your gross margins may be lower than average for the first couple of months.

### **2\. Operating profit**

Operating expenses are the everyday costs of running your business—like rent, salaries, marketing, and utilities. When you subtract those from your gross profit, you get operating profit (also called [net operating income](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/net-operating-income/)).

Operating profit reflects only the cash flow from your core business. It excludes taxes, interest payments, or one-time income like asset sales. That makes it a strong indicator of how your business is really performing.

Let’s say your SaaS startup carries some debt that’s reducing your overall profit. If you’re still seeing positive operating profit, it’s a sign your core business model is sound—and you’re heading in the right direction.

### **3\. Net profit**

Net profit is your company’s true bottom line—what’s left after you’ve accounted for every income source and expense. That includes taxes, loan interest, one-time costs, and income from non-core activities like selling assets or investments.

It’s called the “bottom line” because it appears at the end of your income statement—and gives you a full picture of profitability.

For many VC-backed SaaS companies, early-stage net losses are common due to heavy upfront investments. But that’s not necessarily a red flag. If your total gross profit margin is healthy, it’s a good signal that your business fundamentals are strong.

## Is revenue or profit more important?

Profit matters—especially when you’re aiming to deliver returns. For example, if your SaaS product brings in $100 million in ARR but isn’t profitable, you’ll eventually need to find a path to positive [net income](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/net-income/).

But it’s not about choosing between revenue or profit. You need to track both to get a full view of your company’s financial performance.

Revenue helps you measure growth—specifically, how many subscriptions you’ve sold over a given time. Steady growth is a good sign. But it’s just the starting point.

Ask yourself:

- Did you have to spend heavily to drive that growth?
- Did you offer deep discounts—and what was the cost?
- Did a marketing push boost signups?

The answers show up in your expenses and ultimately impact your net profit. If profit rises alongside revenue, that growth is sustainable.

Bottom line: Revenue shows how fast you’re growing. Profit shows how efficiently you’re growing. Together, they give you a more complete picture of business health.

## How to increase revenue and profit

Improving top-line and bottom-line growth largely depends on having an excellent product, but having the product noticed by the right audience can fuel growth too.

Let’s talk about what you can do to increase revenue and profit.

Increasing the company’s revenue boils down to selling more. You can do so by running effective marketing campaigns, upselling your product to increase the average revenue per user (ARPU), and improving retention rates.

An increase in total revenue typically, though not always, results in an increase in profit. You can also increase profit by adding non-operating revenue by renting out a building owned by your company.

Besides that, you can increase the profitability from sales revenue by reducing costs or increasing the price. You can reduce costs for your SaaS business by finding discounted deals or getting flexible plans for server space.

On the other hand, increasing the price can backfire since the market controls the price, and the price hike may impact the demand. However, you can opt for a flexible cost structure to cater to most customers.

You can also reduce total expenses by adjusting your operating leverage, [increasing productivity](https://www.hibob.com/guides/harnessing-the-power-of-productivity/), and using automation.

## Key takeaways

- **Revenue and profit are distinct but vital financial metrics:** Revenue represents the total income from sales, while profit is the remaining income after all expenses are deducted. Both metrics are essential for understanding a business’s financial health and performance.
- **Revenue is crucial for growth insights:** By tracking revenue, you can gauge growth trends and understand the effectiveness of sales strategies. This metric helps you plan budgets and forecast future earnings.
- **Profit provides a deeper financial picture:** Profitability measures like gross, operating, and net profit give insights into your business’s efficiency and overall financial stability. These metrics help you assess operational effectiveness and investment potential.
- **Balancing revenue and profit is key:** While revenue indicates business growth, profit reflects financial sustainability. Understanding and managing both can lead to informed decision-making and strategic planning for long-term success.

## How to calculate revenue vs. profit

Calculating revenue vs. profit is simple when you know the formulas. But you’ll always need to calculate revenue first since the profit formula requires revenue.

To calculate the revenue, multiply the subscription fee by the number of subscriptions during a time period and then subtract any refunds.

![Revenue formula: Subscription Price x Number of Subscriptions - Refunds. Make sure to use data for the same time frame to collect revenue. For instance, if you’re calculating monthly revenue, use the monthly subscription price and the total number of monthly subscriptions.

Here, the subscription price may vary depending on different tiers (or plans). Similarly, you may offer a discount for an annual subscription. If that’s the case, multiply the different subscription fees by the number of subscribers using that plan and sum up the acquired products.

Once you have the revenue, you can calculate profit (or net profit) by subtracting total expenses (COGS, operating expenses, debts, and taxes) from total revenue and other income.

Essentially, you account for all [cash inflows and outflows](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/cash-inflow-outflow/) to reach your profit or bottom line. Here, other income includes income from investments or the sale of an asset.

CAC is important for almost any industry you can think of, but more so for the SaaS industry since the SaaS business model’s profitability depends on the customer’s lifetime value.

## Revenue vs. profit FAQs

### **Can profit be higher than revenue?**

Theoretically, net profit can be higher than revenue when a company’s income through non-core business operations, such as the sale of investments, temporarily exceeds operating costs. For example, if a business earning $50,000 in revenue with operating costs of $10,000 sells assets worth $20,000, their net profit of $60,000 would exceed revenue.

### **How much of revenue is profit?**

The amount of profit generated compared to your revenue depends on your gross margin. It subtracts the cost of revenue from [net revenue](https://www.hibob.com/financial-metrics/net-and-gross-dollar-retention/) to tell you how much money you keep for every dollar you make.

### **What is a good annual revenue?**

Benchmarks for “good” annual revenue depend heavily on the growth stage for VC-backed SaaS companies. While there’s rarely a one-size-fits-all answer, here are some rough guidelines for what good annual revenue is for a startup about to raise a round:

- Series A: $1 million to $2 million ARR
- Series B: $3 million to $7 million ARR
