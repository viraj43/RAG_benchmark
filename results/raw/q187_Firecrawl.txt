Title: List of highest mountains on Earth
# List of highest mountains on Earth

List of highest mountains on Earth

This article is about mountains by height above sea level. For a list of mountains by topographic prominence, see [List of mountain peaks by prominence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_mountain_peaks_by_prominence "List of mountain peaks by prominence"). For other uses, see [List of mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_mountains "List of mountains").

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg/960px-Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mount_Everest_as_seen_from_Drukair2_PLW_edit.jpg) Aerial view of [Mount Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Everest "Mount Everest") from the south. The peak rises over [Lhotse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lhotse "Lhotse"), while [Nuptse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuptse "Nuptse") is the ridge on the left.

There are at least 108 [mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain "Mountain") on Earth with [elevations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elevation "Elevation") of 7,200m (23,622ft; 4mi) or greater above [sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level "Sea level"). Of these, 14 are more than 8,000m (26,247ft; 5mi).[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-1) The vast majority of these mountains are part of either the [Himalayas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalayas "Himalayas") or the [Karakoram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakoram "Karakoram") mountain ranges located on the edge of the [Indian plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_plate "Indian plate") and [Eurasian plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasian_plate "Eurasian plate") in [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal"), and [Pakistan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pakistan "Pakistan").[\[a\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-Kashmir-2)

## Discussion

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Relative-height.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relative-height.png) Figure demonstrating the concept of [topographic prominence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topographic_prominence "Topographic prominence"): The prominence of a peak is the height of the peak's [summit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summit "Summit") above the lowest contour line encircling it and no higher summit. For example, vertical arrows show the topographic prominence of three peaks on an island. A dotted horizontal line links each peak (except the highest) to its key [col](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Col "Col").

The dividing line between a mountain with multiple peaks and separate mountains is not always clear (see also [Highest unclimbed mountain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highest_unclimbed_mountain "Highest unclimbed mountain")). A popular and intuitive way to distinguish mountains from subsidiary peaks is by their height above the highest saddle connecting it to a higher summit, a measure called [topographic prominence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topographic_prominence "Topographic prominence") or re-ascent (the higher summit is called the "parent peak"). A common definition of a mountain is a summit with 300m (984ft) prominence. Alternatively, a relative prominence (prominence/height) is used (usually 7–8%) to reflect that in higher mountain ranges everything is on a larger scale. The table below lists the highest 100 summits with at least 500m (1,640ft) prominence, approximating a 7% relative prominence. A drawback of a prominence-based list is that it may exclude well-known or spectacular mountains that are connected via a high ridge to a taller summit, such as [Eiger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eiger "Eiger"), [Nuptse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuptse "Nuptse") or [Annapurna IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annapurna_IV "Annapurna IV"). A few such peaks and mountains with nearly sufficient prominence are included in this list, and given a rank of "S".

It is very unlikely that all given heights are correct to the nearest metre; indeed, the sea level is often problematic to define when a mountain is remote from the sea. Different sources often differ by many metres, and the heights given below may well differ from those elsewhere in this encyclopedia. As an extreme example, [Ulugh Muztagh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulugh_Muztagh "Ulugh Muztagh") on the north [Tibetan Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau "Tibetan Plateau") is often listed as 7,723m (25,338ft) to 7,754m (25,440ft), but appears to be only 6,973m (22,877ft) to 6,987m (22,923ft). Some mountains differ by more than 100m (330ft) on different maps, while even very thorough current measurements of [Mount Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Everest#Surveys "Mount Everest") range from 8,840m (29,003ft) to 8,849m (29,032ft). These discrepancies serve to emphasize the uncertainties in the listed heights.

Though some parts of the world, especially the most mountainous parts, have never been thoroughly mapped, it is unlikely that any mountains this high have been overlooked, because [synthetic aperture radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar "Synthetic aperture radar") can and [has been](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shuttle_Radar_Topography_Mission "Shuttle Radar Topography Mission") used to measure elevations of most otherwise inaccessible places. Still, heights or prominences may be revised, so that the order of the list may change and even new mountains could enter the list over time. To be safe, the list has been extended to include all 7,200m (23,622ft) peaks.

The highest mountains above sea level are generally not the highest mountains above the surrounding terrain, also called the highest free-standing mountains. There is no precise definition of surrounding base, but [Denali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denali "Denali"),[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-3) [Mount Kilimanjaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro "Mount Kilimanjaro")[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-4) and [Nanga Parbat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanga_Parbat "Nanga Parbat")[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-5) are possible candidates for the tallest mountain on land by this measure.

The bases of mountain islands are below sea level, and given this consideration. [Mauna Kea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauna_Kea "Mauna Kea") (4,207m (13,802ft) above sea level) is the [world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World "World")'s tallest mountain and [volcano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano "Volcano"), rising about 10,203m (33,474ft) from the [Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean") floor. [Mount Lamlam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Lamlam "Mount Lamlam") on [Guam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guam "Guam") is periodically claimed to be among the world's highest mountains because it is adjacent to the [Mariana Trench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariana_Trench "Mariana Trench"); the most extreme claim is that, measured from [Challenger Deep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenger_Deep "Challenger Deep") 313 kilometres (194mi) away, Mount Lamlam is 11,530 metres (37,820ft) tall.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-6)[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-7) [Ojos del Salado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ojos_del_Salado "Ojos del Salado") has the greatest rise on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"): 13,420m (44,029ft) vertically to the summit\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] from the bottom of the [Atacama Trench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Trench "Atacama Trench"), which is about 560km (350mi) away, although most of this rise is not part of the mountain.

The highest mountains are also not generally the most voluminous. [Mauna Loa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauna_Loa "Mauna Loa") (4,169m or 13,678ft) is the largest mountain on Earth in terms of base area (about 5,200km2 or 2,000sqmi) and volume (about 42,000km3 or 10,000mi3), although, due to the intergrade of [lava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lava "Lava") from [Kilauea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilauea "Kilauea"), [Hualalai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hualalai "Hualalai") and [Mauna Kea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauna_Kea "Mauna Kea"), the volume can only be estimated based on surface area and height of the edifice. [Mount Kilimanjaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro "Mount Kilimanjaro") is the largest non-shield volcano in terms of both base area (635km2 or 245sqmi) and volume (4,793km3 or 1,150mi3). [Mount Logan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Logan "Mount Logan") is the largest non-volcanic mountain in base area (311km2 or 120sqmi).

The highest mountains above sea level are also not those with peaks farthest from the centre of the Earth, because the [shape of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_Earth "Shape of the Earth") is not spherical. Sea level closer to the equator is several kilometres farther from the centre of the Earth. The summit of [Chimborazo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chimborazo_(volcano) "Chimborazo (volcano)"), [Ecuador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador")'s tallest mountain, is usually considered to be the farthest point from the Earth's centre, although the southern summit of [Peru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peru "Peru")'s tallest mountain, [Huascarán](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huascar%C3%A1n "Huascarán"), is another contender.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-8) Both have elevations above sea level more than 2km (1.2mi) less than that of Everest.

Almost all mountains in the list are located in the [Himalaya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalaya "Himalaya") and [Karakoram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karakoram "Karakoram") ranges to the south and west of the Tibetan Plateau. All peaks 7,000m (22,966ft) or higher are located in [East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia"), [Central](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Asia "Central Asia") or [South Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Asia "South Asia") in a rectangle edged by [Noshaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noshaq "Noshaq") (7,492m or 24,580ft) on the [Afghanistan–Pakistan border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durand_Line "Durand Line") in the west, [Jengish Chokusu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jengish_Chokusu "Jengish Chokusu") (Tuōmù'ěr Fēng, 7,439m or 24,406ft) on the [Kyrgyzstan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyrgyzstan "Kyrgyzstan")– [Xinjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xinjiang "Xinjiang") border to the north, [Gongga Shan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gongga_Shan "Gongga Shan") (Minya Konka, 7,556m or 24,790ft) in [Sichuan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sichuan "Sichuan") to the east, and [Kabru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kabru "Kabru") (7,412m or 24,318ft) on the [Sikkim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sikkim "Sikkim")– [Nepal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nepal "Nepal") border to the south.

As of November2025[\[update\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth&action=edit), the highest peaks on four of the mountains— [Gangkhar Puensum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gangkhar_Puensum "Gangkhar Puensum"), [Labuche Kang III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labuche_Kang_III/East "Labuche Kang III/East"), [Tongshanjiabu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tongshanjiabu "Tongshanjiabu") and [Apsarasas Kangri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsarasas_Kangri "Apsarasas Kangri")[\[b\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-Apsarasas-9), all located in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), [Bhutan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhutan "Bhutan") or [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China")—have not been ascended. The most recent peak to have its first ever ascent is [Karjiang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karjiang "Karjiang"), in [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China"), on 13 August 2024.

The highest mountain [outside of Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Summits "Seven Summits") is [Aconcagua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aconcagua "Aconcagua") (6,962m or 22,841ft), the 188th highest in the world.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-10)

## Notes

1. [↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_ref-11)Peaks which are considered sub-prominences are given a rank of "S".
2. [↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_ref-13)For Nepal, the heights indicated on the Nepal Topographic Maps are followed. For China and the [Baltoro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltoro_Muztagh "Baltoro Muztagh") Karakoram, the heights are those of Mi Desheng's "The Maps of Snow Mountains in China". For the [Hispar Karakoram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hispar_Muztagh "Hispar Muztagh") the heights on a Russian 1:100,000 topo map.[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-12) Elsewhere, unless otherwise indicated, heights are those in [Neate (1989)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#CITEREFNeate1989).
3. [↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_ref-16)Prominences over 1,500m (4,900ft) are from Peaklist.org,[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-pklist-14) the remainder from Jurgalski and de Ferrantin.[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-15)
4. [↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_ref-17)Coordinates were established by comparing topographical maps with satellite images and [SRTM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shuttle_Radar_Topography_Mission "Shuttle Radar Topography Mission")-derived terrain maps. The terrain maps and satellite images often do not match exactly. An asterisk (\*) indicates that the map and image are shifted by more than 100m (330ft) and/or that the landscapes around the summit do not match.
5. [↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_ref-18)The first higher mountain beyond the key saddle with at least 500m (1,600ft) prominence itself.
6. [↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_ref-21)The number of ascents and failed attempts up to 2004 is extracted from Alpine Club Himalayan Index.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-19) These are the number of expeditions (not individuals) that announced their ascent or attempt in a journal. They are probably quite accurate for the rarely climbed peaks (though omissions were noted), but greatly underestimate the number of ascending parties on the easier and/or more popular mountains, like most [eight-thousanders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eight-thousander "Eight-thousander"). For instance, up to 2004 Mount Everest was scaled 2,251 times by individuals.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_highest_mountains_on_Earth#cite_note-20)

# **What is the highest point on Earth as measured from Earth's center?**

## The top of **Mount Chimborazo** is farther from the Earth's center than Mount Everest.

![illustration of tallest mountains](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/highestpoint.jpg)

_The highest point above Earth’s center is the peak of Ecuador’s Mount Chimborazo, located just one degree south of the Equator where Earth’s bulge is greatest._ \| [Infographic Text](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/highestpoint.html#transcript1)

##### **What does this have to do with the OCEAN service?**

_The [National Geodetic Survey](https://geodesy.noaa.gov/) (part of the National Ocean Service) measures and monitors our [ever-changing planet](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/geodesy.html) by mapping its gravity field and determining the precise location of points on Earth’s surface. Measurements of the Earth contribute to a wide range of important activities, including mapping and charting, navigation, flood risk determination, transportation, land use, and ecosystem management._

Mount Everest, located in Nepal and Tibet, is usually said to be the highest mountain on Earth. Reaching **8,848 meters** [![29,029 feet](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/bs4/img/question.svg)](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/highestpoint.html# "") at its summit, Everest is indeed the highest point above global mean sea level—the average level for the ocean surface from which elevations are measured. But the summit of Mt. Everest is not the farthest point from Earth’s _center_.

Earth is not a perfect [sphere](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/earth-round.html), but is a bit thicker at the Equator due to the centrifugal force created by the planet’s constant rotation. Because of this, the highest point above Earth’s center is the peak of Ecuador’s Mount Chimborazo, located just one degree south of the Equator where Earth’s bulge is greatest. The summit of Chimborazo is **6,268 meters** [![20,564 feet](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/bs4/img/question.svg)](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/highestpoint.html# "") above sea level. However, due to the Earth’s bulge, the summit of Chimborazo is over **2,072 meters** [![6,800  feet](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/bs4/img/question.svg)](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/highestpoint.html# "")
farther from the center of the Earth than Everest’s peak. That makes Chimborazo the closest point on Earth to the stars.

You may be surprised to learn that Everest is not the tallest mountain on Earth, either. That honor belongs to Mauna Kea, a volcano on the Big Island of Hawaii. Mauna Kea originates deep beneath the Pacific Ocean, and rises more than **10,210 meters** [![33,500  feet](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/bs4/img/question.svg)](https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/highestpoint.html# "") from base to peak.

* * *

### Infographic Transcript: Highest mountain on Earth?

### The answer is debatable.

- Mount Everest's peak is the highest altitude above mean sea level at 29,029 feet \[8,848 meters\].
- Mount Chimborazo's peak is the furthest point on Earth from Earth's center. The summit is over 6,800 feet \[2,072 meters\] farther from Earth's center than Mount Everest's summit.
- Mauna Kea is the tallest mountain from base to peak at more than 33,500 feet \[10,210 meters\].

October 25, 2011

Fourteen mountain peaks on Earth stand taller than 8,000 meters (26,247 feet). The tallest of these [“eight-thousanders”](http://www.earthobservatory.nasa.gov/Features/8000MeterPeaks/) is [Mount Everest,](http://explore.glacierworks.org/en/#everest/explore) the standard to which all other mountains are compared. The Nepalese name for the mountain is Sagarmatha: “mother of the universe.”

Everest’s geological story began 40 million years ago when the Indian subcontinent began a [slow-motion collision](http://pubs.usgs.gov/gip/dynamic/himalaya.html) with Asia. The edges of two continents jammed together and pushed up the massive ridges that make up the Himalayas today. Pulitzer-winning journalist [John McPhee](http://us.macmillan.com/author/johnmcphee) summed up the wonder of the mountain’s history when he wrote Annals of the Former World: “The summit of Mount Everest is marine limestone. This one fact is a treatise in itself on the movements of the surface of the Earth. If by some fiat, I had to restrict all this writing to one sentence; this is the one I would choose.”

In other words, when climbers reach the top of Mount Everest, they are not standing on hard [igneous rock](http://www.windows2universe.org/earth/geology/ig_intro.html) produced by volcanoes. Rather, they are perched on softer [sedimentary rock](http://www.windows2universe.org/earth/geology/sed_intro.html) formed by the skeletons of creatures that lived in a warm ocean off the northern coast of India tens of millions of years ago.

Meanwhile, glaciers have chiseled Mount Everest’s summit into a huge, triangular pyramid, defined by three faces and three ridges that extend to the northeast, southeast, and northwest. The southeastern ridge is the most widely used climbing route. It is the one that [Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay](http://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/IOTD/view.php?id=8396) followed in May 1953 when they became the first climbers to reach the summit and return safely.

Climbers who follow this route begin by trekking past [Khumbu glacier](http://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/IOTD/view.php?id=81823) and through the [Khumbu ice fall,](http://www.alanarnette.com/images/mteverest/Mt.%20Everest/9/med/everest_2008_907.htm) an extremely dangerous area where ice tumbles off the mountain into a chaotic waterfall of ice towers and crevasses. Next, climbers reach a bowl-shaped valley—a cirque—called the Western Cwm (pronounced coom) and then the foot of the [Lhotse Face,](http://www.alanarnette.com/images/mteverest/Mt.%20Everest/11/med/everest_2008_0654.htm) a 1,125-meter (3,691-foot) wall of ice. Climbing up the Lhotse face leads to the [South Col,](http://www.alanarnette.com/images/mteverest/Mt.%20Everest/14/med/everest_2003_224.htm) the low point in the ridge that connects Everest to [Lhotse.](http://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/Features/8000MeterPeaks/page12.php) It is from the South Col that most expeditions launch their final assault on the [summit,](http://www.alanarnette.com/images/mteverest/Mt.%20Everest/16/med/DSC01163.htm) following a route up the southeastern ridge.

Some climbers opt for the northern ridge, which is known for having harsher winds and colder temperatures. That is the path that British climbers [George Mallory and Andrew Irvine](http://www.wou.edu/las/physci/poston/everest/) used in 1924 during what may, in fact, have been the first ascent. Whether the pair made it to the summit remains a topic of controversy, but what is known for certain is that the men were spotted pushing toward the peak just before the arrival of a storm. Mallory’s corpse was discovered near the northeast ridge at 8,160 meters (26,772 feet) by an American climber in 1999, but it still isn’t clear whether he reached the summit.

Despite [its reputation](http://gizmodo.com/5755875/abandoned-on-mt-everest) as an extremely dangerous mountain, commercial guiding has done much to tame Everest in the last few decades. As of March 2012, there had been 5,656 successful ascents of Everest, while 223 people had died—a fatality rate of 4 percent.

[Mount Everest](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mount-Everest) is best described as the _highest_ [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) in the world. In other words: it has the greatest elevation, at more than 29,000 feet (about 8,850 meters) above [sea level](https://www.britannica.com/science/sea-level).

Other mountains, though, have made claims on being the tallest when measured from points other than sea level. [Chimborazo](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chimborazo-mountain-Ecuador) in Ecuador, for example, has been [cited](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/cited) as the tallest peak when measured from the center of [Earth](https://www.britannica.com/place/Earth). At more than 20,000 feet (6,000 meters) in elevation, it falls short of Everest. But Chimborazo is located near the Equator, where Earth bulges, which means that it is about 6,800 feet (about 2,070 meters) farther above Earth’s center than Everest. All in all, Chimborazo reaches nearly 4,000 miles (6,400 kilometers) above the center of Earth.

Measured from base to peak, Hawaii’s [Mauna Kea](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mauna-Kea), which originates at the bottom of the ocean, is far taller than Everest, at about 33,500 feet (10,210 meters). Measured from sea level, Mauna Kea reaches only about 13,800 feet (4,200 meters).

**mesa**, (Spanish: “table”), flat-topped tableland with one or more steep sides, common in the [Colorado Plateau](https://www.britannica.com/place/Colorado-Plateau) regions of the United States; a butte is similar but smaller. Both are formed by erosion; during denudation, or downcutting and stripping, areas of harder rock in a [plateau](https://www.britannica.com/science/plateau-landform) act as [flat](https://www.britannica.com/science/playa) protective caps for portions of [underlying](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/underlying) land situated between such places as stream valleys, where [erosion](https://www.britannica.com/science/erosion-geology) is especially active. This results in a [table](https://www.britannica.com/topic/table-furniture) [mountain](https://www.britannica.com/science/mountain-landform) (mesa) or fortress hill ( [butte](https://www.britannica.com/science/butte-geology); _q.v._).
