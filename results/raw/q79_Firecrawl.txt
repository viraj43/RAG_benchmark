Title: If your iPhone or iPad is running slow - Apple Support
# If your iPhone or iPad is running slow

If your iPhone or iPad freezes or has performance issues, try these tips.

## Check your network conditions

Apps often need an internet connection, and slow performance can occur due to network congestion or when a device reconnects to cell towers while moving. Even with a strong signal, you might need to wait, try a different location, or use Wi-Fi. Contact your carrier if cellular data issues persist across multiple locations.

[Learn more about cellular data](https://support.apple.com/en-us/120000)

## Close an app that's not responding

To force close an app on your iPhone or iPad:

1. Swipe up from the bottom of the screen (or double-click the Home button on older iPhones and iPads).

2. Find the app.

3. Swipe up on its preview to close the app.


Force closing apps unnecessarily can slow down your device because force closing apps takes longer to reload all of the data when you reopen the app.

[Learn what to do if an app repeatedly stops responding](https://support.apple.com/en-us/119876)

## Check your device's available storage

iOS and iPadOS automatically manage storage during tasks like app installs and updates by removing items that can be downloaded again or that aren't needed anymore. To avoid slowdowns, maintain at least 1 GB of free space.

1. Go to Settings

2. Tap General.

3. Tap \[Device\] Storage.


## Offload or delete apps to free up storage

Your apps are listed on the \[Device\] Storage screen along with the amount of space that they use. iOS and iPadOS offer storage-saving recommendations on the \[Device\] Storage screen.

You can enable these recommendations or review apps to free up more space. If you don't see any recommendations, or if you need to free up even more space, review the apps on your device.

Tap an app, then choose from the options:

- Offload the app, which frees up storage used by the app, but keeps its documents and data.

- Delete the app, which removes the app and its related data.

- Depending on the app, you might be able to delete some of its documents and data.


[Learn more ways to optimize your storage](https://support.apple.com/en-us/108429)

## Turn off Low Power Mode

Low Power Mode is a feature that helps extend battery life by limiting device power consumption. When enabled, certain features are disabled or work slower, and the battery icon turns yellow.

Low Power Mode can be turned on by going to Settings and tapping Battery. Consider leaving Low Power Mode off if you need your iPhone or iPad to function without low-power restrictions.

[Learn more about Low Power Mode](https://support.apple.com/en-us/101604)

## Keep your device from getting too hot or cold

When your iOS or iPadOS device gets too hot or cold, it might run slower. To fix this, move it to a more moderate temperature.

[Learn more about acceptable operating temperatures](https://support.apple.com/en-us/118431)

## Check your battery health

All rechargeable batteries are consumable components that become less effective as they chemically age. Over time, the rechargeable batteries in all iPhone and iPad models will diminish in their capacity and peak performance and will eventually need to be replaced. iOS can show show battery health\* and recommend if an iPhone battery needs to be replaced.

1. Go to Settings.

2. Tap Battery.

3. Tap Battery Health.


\*Battery Health is available only on iPhone.

[Learn how to check your iPhone battery](https://support.apple.com/en-us/101575#health)

## Get more help

- A touch screen that responds to taps incorrectly can make your device seem slow. If you think that [your touch screen isn't responding correctly](https://support.apple.com/kb/HT201406), try restarting your [iPhone](https://support.apple.com/kb/ht201559), [iPad](https://support.apple.com/kb/HT210631), or [iPod touch](https://support.apple.com/kb/HT210632).

- If your iPhone or iPad touch screen still isn't responding, you might need service. [Get service](https://getsupport.apple.com/?caller=kbase)

I really, really don’t think it’s a browser problem - it’s a web problem and a greed problem. It’s a part of what I’m starting to call a direct capitalistic software development pattern \- and it manages to turn almost any software it touches to complete and utter garbage.

The most important rule to note in anything and everything that includes computer programming is:

There’s no such thing as a free lunch!

The point is, that any asset (image, file etc.) and action in a programming project have a cost that are measured in time, processor usage and RAM usage

Let me show you a few places where it usually goes wrong:

* * *

Advertisements are probably by far the most egregious resource hog of any website that has them.

Your typical web ad usually contains:

- Assets in the form of images or videos that are shown to the user.
- Some degree of scripting, styling and HTML to seamlessly embed the ad into a site.
- In many cases some sort of telemetry scripting for recording interaction (clicks, location etc.)

Programming and making embeddable ads like these are more often than not a rush-job, leading to some very sloppy code and optimization. Any scripting (JS, vue, react etc.) for any given ad may also “chain load” further scripts and assets, which in turn are downloaded and run on your computer.

Remember the rule about free lunches? All of these ads consume resources and are processed on your computer. Of course you have to store the things you process in RAM and the CPU has to use precious cycles on rendering the ad and sending off telemetry.

Now - there are websites out there (especially blogs and social media) that have a habit of absolutely carpet-bombing the entire page with ads. Even just 10 poorly optimized ads can bog down even a modern casual gaming computer significantly, especially if all the said ads display improperly scaled video content (which, often being at the VERY least 720p, does consume quite a lot of resources considering the task the ad is trying to perform: making you aware of a product so you might buy it).

In some really bad cases, you can scrub about 70–90% of the data traffic between your computer and the web page just by installing a good ad blocker extension or use a LAN-wide ad filter like piHole. Often, running said websites on 10–15 year old equipment is actually feasible again, without lag or slowdowns as neither the CPU nor RAM are bogged down.

In my experience, blogs and fandom wiki pages are among the worst offenders - often loading irrelevant videos and other content on the side.

* * *

Scripts may sometimes enrich the look, feel and functionality of the web page, but is also an area where some web designers tend to go overboard.

Javascript does the job on most of the Internet, and it does work just fine if the author knows and minds the quirks of the language. However, it’s not exactly efficient at its job - a lot of power goes into interpreting the script and inferring data types.

In bad cases, websites end up a gigantic mess of scripts, dependencies, dependencies on dependencies, more scripts, frameworks, dependencies for frameworks and plugins. Javascript (and anything based on it) can very quickly turn into a huge mess.

Not only do these sites and these untamed script globs take multiple megabytes of data transfer - it may well also suck gigabytes out of your RAM budget…

Javascript can also really misbehave if the code hasn’t been tested and vetted properly - It’s especially noticeable on large sites like Facebook and Youtube. Of course the amount of content answers a bit, but some of the features I have my doubts about. IMHO they (and the bugs that often follow) detract a lot from the feel and experience of such places.

To take just Youtube as an example:

- Why is there a play function on the front page where videos play when you hover the mouse? The sound is muted and the video is basically like looking through a tiny hole. Why was the thumbnail image and text not enough? (I can especially ask this of the playlist function)
- Why that subtle “ambilight” effect around videos when not in full screen? For me it’s barely noticeable anyway - that’s also basically wasted RAM, CPU and GPU power…
- On slower machines, Youtube can sometimes be observed in “trying out” all sorts of strange layout configurations when switching from normal video to fullscreen.
- When using uBlock, you can see how many tracking and ad scripts and cookies it intercepts for a given site. When casually browsing or even just leaving the computer idle on the page, that number often rises over time (sometimes counting hundreds or even thousands).

* * *

Cookies and tracking are probably the third greatest offender in the equation. Recording data and sending it off again costs us time and resources due to our rule about free lunches.

EU citizens do have a bit of leeway in this department, thanks to the mandatory cookie warnings sites are required to display if they want to conform to EU law:

![](https://qph.cf2.quoracdn.net/main-qimg-89fbe1bea4b85479dbfbf53e0e716f2b)

But sometimes, they still go overboard. In cases where they show a number of “partners” (for marketing, ads and stats), these can still number in the hundreds or thousands.

If you know how to browse cookies and traffic in your browser or specialized programs, it can sometimes get quite scary how much traffic goes back and forth.

* * *

In the end, my point is that all of these things have a cost that adds up as more is added. It’s a bad trade, however - You pay with your information, system resources, hardware purchases and electric bills. The companies behind lots of the services, on the other hand, earn money on serving you ads and selling your information. Individuals lose, capitalism wins.

I’d actually like to congratulate and thank every single web browser developer in history for creating such broad and versatile pieces of software. They did an absolute splendid job making it possible to see, create and contribute to a mind-boggingly huge pool of content and information.

Browser engine code (yes, even 🤢Internet Explorer) is usually quite efficiently written, considering what it can do.

However, I’ve seen website projects spin completely out of control on their tech stacks, sometimes including huge frameworks for relatively few, sometimes even basic, functions.

For another example - also take a look at how AI really is starting to infest just about everything imagineable. However in a lot of cases it can really be argued that AI became a part of the end product just for the heck of it.

Last example - and probably the best - is just to take a look at the disaster that is the “freemium” smartphone app market, especially some of the games.

Some mobile game makers keep their games relatively free of ads and make sure they are relatively simple, especially on slower devices.

However \- there are also the games that go completely overboard on ads. But the worst offender by far has got to be those “try them out” ads for (sometimes fake) games and apps that has a little “demo” in them.

I have a 10 year old smartphone and a 3 year old low/mid segment tablet - they both do web browsing and 1080p video quite fine - but some of these ads immediately heat their poor CPUs to the temperature of the Sun and slow the devices down to a dog crawl even if the game that opened said ad runs just fine.

But I also know that there are non-hacky ways to run heavy sites like Youtube on 15 year old hardware. A proper ad- and tracking blocker in the browser actually comes an impressively long way in getting them to work nicely on older or limited hardware. If anything, that is proof how much RAM and CPU cycles go to waste on something that is useless to you in the end…

Oh dear. Well, this is a difficult question to answer with no context at all. It's kind of like if I ask you "what should I have for dinner?" There are so many variables that go into it and not knowing me, my circumstances or what I even consider "dinner" to mean, you'd be really hard-pressed to give me an answer that actually would solve my problem of wanting a delicious dinner.

Having said that, I'm going to attempt to give you some basic ideas and questions to start asking yourself (and others) about this app. Just as it helps for you to answer my dinner question with the facts that I'm vegan, about-to-pass-out starving and dining this evening with two children under 10 years old who refuse to leave the house, getting some more information on your user will help you start to answer your own question.

So, you need to understand who that "user" person is, what their circumstances are (the context of use) and what they really want to do with this mobile app of yours.

I'd suggest you do a little thinking about the following basic questions:

1\. What is my user trying to do? What's his/her end goal? If he/she didn't have the app, how would he/she accomplish this end goal? (these answers give you an indication of what alternative means your user has - you want your app to be easier/faster/more appealing than the alternatives)

2\. When and where does this user use the app? What else might be competing for the user's attention? How technically sophisticated is my user? (these answers give you an idea how simple the app needs to be - if there's lots competing in the environment, then you need to make it falling-off-a-log easy. hint: mobile environments always have lots of competition)

3\. What are the most common use cases the user wants? (this gives you an idea what part of the app needs to be easiest - you need to design for the most frequent use cases, not edge cases)

4\. Is the app visually appealing? Do all the visuals draw a user in an make them curious, interested, compelled to use the app?

5\. How familiar is the language used?Does the navigation follow how users think about the task(s) at hand? Is the content simple to read? (hint: jargon is a big problem for users).

I'd also suggest you show the app to a few people who know nothing about how it was developed. Ask them to do a task or two (one simple task, one more complicated, for example). Say nothing while they do it. Notice where they stumble.

All of these steps will start to point you in a direction of improving the user experience. The other way to do it is to find yourself a good UX expert and ask for a heuristic review. They'll go through the app and give you much more specific feedback on how to make the experience great.

Considering the topic fills several books and university courses, and we spend years getting any good at it, a short answer here can only scratch the surface.

I'll give you the summary. You take it from there.

First, understand who your customers are and what your app does for them. You want to know when they'll use it, what they're doing at the time, any distractions or problems — time crunch, slow Internet connection, glare, noise, crowds, naggy kids — and what's important to them about the experience.

How do you get this? By talking to target customers. Shadow them in person if you can. Interview them. Talk to them. Do not rely on demographics or cardboard cutouts with made-up names and a bunch of demographic averages passing for personas. Do not pass out surveys or hold focus groups asking people what they want or give them a list of features to choose from. Talk to real people.

Now write some user stories. These are tasks — if you use this app, what is it you want to do? Is there a certain order to tasks that make most sense? What's important for each task — what information do you need, what does the app provide, how can the UI make it easy?

Map stuff out on paper. What goes where, what's grouped under what, and how do you get from this to that? Don't start giving your groups clever labels. Clever labels stink. Call things what they are. What you have now is an information architecture. It'sgoing to morph into your navigation.

If you want to test your work so far, run tree testing ( [Tree testing - Wikipedia](https://en.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_testing "en.m.wikipedia.org")). You can do it relatively inexpensively online.

Next draw wireframes. Pen or marker on paper or whiteboard. Keep it rough. You want to decide what you're screens are, what goes on each, and what your common elements are. For each individual screen, roughly organize any information, form controls, and other elements.

You can test these. On paper. No kidding. Do yourself a favor and follow some of these links: [guerrilla usability testing - Google Search](https://www.google.com/search?q=guerrilla%20usability%20testing "www.google.com")

You're looking for problems, not validation. Look for problems real people have knowing which screens do what, where to go to do X, and so on. You do this so you can fix them before you start coding.

Next, design your mock-ups, your prototypes, and whatever else gets you close to what you need to code. Now is where you do your visual design and your UI detail. Color, space, dimensions, copy, form elements, and error handling. Usability test again. Get a few more users and do it more formally this time if you can.

Then code.

Now, at this point, you may hopefully have concluded that cool is not much of a business objective unless you're selling entertainment, hats, or sugary beverages. You should be wanting to solve problems for your users and making it easy for them. A great user experience is one that doesn't call attention to itself but gets out of the way so people can do what they use the app to do easily and smoothly. Do the legwork before your code, and you may have a shot at it.

Q: Why do apps and websites need loading (when it takes so long to load)?

You probably have a very slow computer.

On my main desktop PC many of my Apps load in less than a second. Personally I don’t think that is a long time.

For websites, many sites can load in a similar time of less than one second. However, some sites are naturally slow in supplying the information. Some sites link in pages from other sites that can add significant delays. Some websites include html, java or other code to be processed in the browser on the target computer. If you are using a slow browser, or the code in the web page is poorly written it can take a very long time relatively to process and display a page.

Of course, a slow Internet connection can make a web page take a long time to load; however, it should not affect the speed pf loading apps from local storage.

Title: Why is my app slow, and what can I do? | Blog
# Why is my app slow, and what can I do?

Is your app running slowly and frustrating your users? Learn the common causes of slow apps, advanced tips to fix performance issues, and how to keep your app fast and user-friendly.

![](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/673de0892a8f074d29b38f1f/686775fedc4b788c9eed5695_Why%20is%20my%20app%20slow%20and%20what%20can%20I%20do_.png)

When users experience a slow or laggy app, it directly affects engagement, reviews, and even revenue. Whether you're launching a new product or maintaining an existing one, performance issues can quietly damage your user experience.

This guide outlines the **12 most common reasons apps slow down**, along with clear **solutions and practical tips** to help you design and develop apps that stay fast, smooth, and user-friendly.

Let’s get straight to it.

‍

### **1\. Your App Is Trying to Do Too Much**

**Problem:** Too many features, too early = bloat and confusion.

**Fix:** Start small, scale later.

- Launch with only **core features**.

- Use **lazy loading** so features load only when needed.

- Audit and remove unused features regularly.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Keep your code modular so you can turn features off/on easily.

- _Design:_ Avoid clutter. Clean UIs load faster _and_ feel better.

‍

### **2\. It Depends Too Much on a Good Internet Connection**

**Problem:** No signal = app becomes unusable.

**Fix:** Add offline support and smart caching.

- Cache data locally (e.g., with SQLite, Room, or IndexedDB).

- Handle poor connectivity with proper loaders and retries.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Use tools like SWR or React Query to manage fetch states.

- _Design:_ Show friendly messages like “You’re offline, showing last saved data.”

‍

### **3\. Your Images and Videos Are Just... Too Big**

**Problem:** Heavy media = slow app.

**Fix:** Compress everything.

- Use formats like **WebP** or **AVIF**.

- Optimize images with tools like TinyPNG or Cloudinary.

- Lazy-load images and videos.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Avoid loading media above-the-fold until it’s needed.

- _Design:_ Use vector icons (SVGs) instead of PNGs wherever possible.

‍

### **4\. Unnecessary Re-renders Are Slowing Things Down**

**Problem:** Your app redraws stuff even when it doesn’t need to.

**Fix:** Optimize rendering logic.

- Use **memoization** (like React.memo, useMemo, etc.).

- Break UI into smaller components that render independently.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Profile re-renders using tools like React DevTools or Flutter DevTools.

- _Design:_ Avoid fancy animations unless they _really_ add value.

‍

### **5\. It Takes Forever to Launch**

**Problem:** You're loading everything upfront.

**Fix:** Only load what you need at startup.

- Delay non-essential features (like analytics, chat, etc.).

- Use splash screens smartly to improve _perceived_ speed.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Use performance tracing tools like Firebase Performance or Flipper.

- _Design:_ Keep splash screens light. Avoid large images or video intros.


‍

### **6\. Your Backend or Database Is Slowing You Down**

**Problem:** Unoptimized APIs and messy database queries.

**Fix:** Clean them up.

- Add **indexes**, **pagination**, and **query caching**.

- Avoid n+1 problems or making 10 small API calls when 1 will do.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Use tools like Postman and New Relic to test and monitor performance.

- _Design:_ Don’t show giant lists, paginate, or show “Load More” buttons.

‍

### **7\. You’re Not Tracking Performance Properly**

**Problem:** You don’t know what’s slow.

**Fix:** Start monitoring everything.

- Use tools like **Sentry**, **Datadog**, or **Firebase Performance**.

- Log crashes, load times, and slow interactions.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Set up alerts for API latency or crash spikes.

- _Design:_ Use error boundaries to catch problems gracefully.

‍

### **8\. You Have Memory Leaks**

**Problem:** Your app is eating RAM and never giving it back.

**Fix:** Clean up after yourself.

- Unsubscribe from listeners and observers.

- Clear intervals, timeouts, and event handlers on unmount.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Use Xcode Instruments (iOS) or Android Profiler to track memory.

- _Design:_ Don’t create unnecessarily long animations or videos.

‍

### **9\. It Doesn’t Work Well on Older Devices**

**Problem:** Your app runs great on your phone, but lags on budget phones.

**Fix:** Test broadly.

- Optimize images, animations, and third-party code for low-end devices.

- Reduce heavy JS or unnecessary background activity.


**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Use emulators or real-device testing for older phones.

- _Design:_ Avoid high-res images and heavy components on mobile.

‍

### **10\. You’re Running Too Many Background Tasks**

**Problem:** Background syncs, GPS, or push notifications are killing performance.

**Fix:** Manage them better.

- Use background schedulers (WorkManager, Background Fetch).

- Don’t run updates unless necessary.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Profile background threads and avoid blocking the main thread.

- _Design:_ Use subtle indicators when background tasks are active.

‍

### **11\. Some SDKs Are Slowing You Down**

**Problem:** 3rd-party SDKs (like ads or analytics) can be heavy.

**Fix:** Audit and delay them.

- Only use what you need. Ditch the rest.

- Load SDKs _after_ the UI is usable.

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Lazy-load heavy SDKs (chat, payments, etc.) post-initial load.

- _Design:_ Don’t depend on 3rd-party UI kits unless they’re proven fast.


‍

### **12\. You Didn’t Plan for Localisation**

**Problem:** The app breaks or slows down in different languages or regions.

**Fix:** Make it global-friendly.

- Use proper string formatting and avoid hardcoded layouts.

- Optimize for languages that take more space (e.g. German, Hindi).

**Tips:**

- _Dev:_ Use locale testing in CI/CD.

- _Design:_ Always design for flexible layouts and RTL languages.

‍

## **Quick Summary**

Here’s a recap of what you can do to fix a slow app:

- Strip away bloat

- Compress assets

- Cache smartly

- Monitor performance

- Audit APIs and SDKs

- Profile devices regularly

- And always design with performance in mind from Day 1
