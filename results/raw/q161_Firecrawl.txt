Title: Transportation in the San Francisco Bay Area - Wikipedia
# Transportation in the San Francisco Bay Area

Transportation in the San Francisco Bay Area

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bart_coliseum_station.jpg/500px-Bart_coliseum_station.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bart_coliseum_station.jpg) [BART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Area_Rapid_Transit "Bay Area Rapid Transit") is a major provider of regional and transbay transit (seen here is a train approaching [Coliseum station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland_Coliseum_station "Oakland Coliseum station"))[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/I-80_Eastshore_Fwy.jpg/500px-I-80_Eastshore_Fwy.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:I-80_Eastshore_Fwy.jpg) [Interstate 80](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_80_in_California "Interstate 80 in California") is a major urban freeway in the Bay Area (seen here in [Berkeley, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkeley,_California "Berkeley, California"), as the _[Eastshore Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastshore_Freeway "Eastshore Freeway")_).

People in the [San Francisco Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area "San Francisco Bay Area") rely on a complex multimodal [transportation infrastructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_infrastructure "Transportation infrastructure") consisting of roads, [bridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#Bridges), highways, rail, tunnels, airports, [seaports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#Seaports), and bike and pedestrian paths. The development, maintenance, and operation of these different modes of transportation are overseen by various agencies, including [Caltrans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltrans "Caltrans"), the [Association of Bay Area Governments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Association_of_Bay_Area_Governments "Association of Bay Area Governments"), [San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Municipal_Transportation_Agency "San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency"), and the [Metropolitan Transportation Commission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Transportation_Commission_(San_Francisco_Bay_Area) "Metropolitan Transportation Commission (San Francisco Bay Area)"). These and other organizations collectively manage several [interstate highways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_highway "Interstate highway") and [state routes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_highways_in_California "State highways in California"), eight passenger rail networks, eight trans-bay bridges, transbay ferry service, local and transbay [bus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_bus "Transit bus") service, three [international airports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_airport "International airport"), and an extensive network of roads, tunnels, and bike paths.

The Bay Area, especially [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco,_California "San Francisco, California"), are frequently listed as one of the best and most extensive cities and/or metropolitan areas in the United States for public transportation.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-1)[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-2) Local trips on transit are frequently accomplished by bus services. Different agencies serve different corners of the Bay Area, such as [SamTrans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SamTrans "SamTrans") serving mostly [San Mateo County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Mateo_County "San Mateo County") and [County Connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/County_Connection "County Connection") connecting the suburbs of [Contra Costa County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contra_Costa_County "Contra Costa County"); though some bus agencies operate transbay services, such as [Golden Gate Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Transit "Golden Gate Transit"). While ferries also connect communities across the bay, most transbay and longer-distance trips on public transportation, however, use rail-based transit. [Bay Area Rapid Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Area_Rapid_Transit "Bay Area Rapid Transit") (BART) is the sole [rapid transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapid_transit "Rapid transit") system within the bay and the dominant provider of regional transportation between San Francisco, northern San Mateo County, and much of the East Bay. The Bay Area is also home to various [commuter rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commuter_rail "Commuter rail") services, such as [SMART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoma%E2%80%93Marin_Area_Rail_Transit "Sonoma–Marin Area Rail Transit") within Sonoma and Marin counties, [Caltrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltrain "Caltrain") on the [San Francisco Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Peninsula "San Francisco Peninsula"), [ACE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altamont_Corridor_Express "Altamont Corridor Express") between [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California") and [Stockton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stockton,_California "Stockton, California"), and various [Amtrak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amtrak "Amtrak") routes out of [Oakland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland,_California "Oakland, California") and San Jose. San Francisco is also the home of the [world's last manually-operated cable car system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_cable_car_system "San Francisco cable car system"), and both San Francisco's [Muni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Municipal_Transportation_Agency "San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency") and Santa Clara's [VTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_Valley_Transportation_Authority "Santa Clara Valley Transportation Authority") operate [light rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_rail "Light rail") networks to complement their bus services. With few exceptions, most public transit within the Bay Area can be paid for by using the [Clipper card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clipper_card "Clipper card").

Though not as extensive as [Southern California's freeways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_California_freeways "Southern California freeways"), the Bay Area is also home to an extensive network of highways. Four bridges traverse the San Francisco Bay itself, and four more traverse the northern [San Pablo Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Pablo_Bay "San Pablo Bay"), in addition to more localized expressways such as [US 101](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_101_in_California "U.S. Route 101 in California") and [Interstate 280](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_280_(California) "Interstate 280 (California)") in the Peninsula, Interstates [680](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_680_(California) "Interstate 680 (California)") and [880](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_880_(California) "Interstate 880 (California)") in the East Bay, and [Interstate 505](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_505 "Interstate 505") in the north. Many highways have tolled express lanes, paid for by using [FasTrak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FasTrak "FasTrak"). Streets within the Bay Area vary from wider [stroads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroad "Stroad") such as [El Camino Real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Camino_Real_(California) "El Camino Real (California)") in the Peninsula, to denser slower streets within urban cores, to scenic routes like [California State Route 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_1 "California State Route 1"). However, San Francisco has historically approached freeways with hostility, and activists have moved to stop the construction of new highways and tear down existing ones, most notably inciting the 1991 demolition of the [Embarcadero Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_480 "California State Route 480"). The city today is seen as the birthplace of [American highway revolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highway_revolts_in_the_United_States "Highway revolts in the United States").[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-:0-3)

## Airports

See also: [List of airports in the San Francisco Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_airports_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area "List of airports in the San Francisco Bay Area")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/San_Francisco_International_Airport_at_night.jpg/500px-San_Francisco_International_Airport_at_night.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:San_Francisco_International_Airport_at_night.jpg) An aerial view of [San Francisco International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_International_Airport "San Francisco International Airport") at night.

The Bay Area has four airports served by commercial airlines, three of which are international airports. In addition to these airports, there are many general aviation airports in the region.

- **[San Francisco International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_International_Airport "San Francisco International Airport") (SFO)** The busiest in the region and the second busiest in the state after [Los Angeles International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles_International_Airport "Los Angeles International Airport"). A [hub](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airline_hub "Airline hub") for [Alaska Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska_Airlines "Alaska Airlines") and [United Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Airlines "United Airlines"). For United, SFO is the primary transpacific gateway for its route network.
- **[San Jose International Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose_International_Airport "San Jose International Airport") (SJC)** The second-busiest and fastest-growing airport in the Bay Area.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-Airport_Ranking-4)[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-5) It serves as a [focus city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Focus_city "Focus city") for Alaska Airlines.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-Alaska_Airlines_hubs-6)
- **[Oakland San Francisco Bay Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland_San_Francisco_Bay_Airport "Oakland San Francisco Bay Airport") (OAK)** The third-busiest airport in the region and an operating base for [Southwest Airlines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southwest_Airlines "Southwest Airlines").[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-Airport_Ranking-4) Oakland San Francisco Bay Airport is the oldest of the Bay Area's civilian airports still in use. The site was chosen due to good weather conditions for aircraft operations.
- **[Charles M. Schulz–Sonoma County Airport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_M._Schulz%E2%80%93Sonoma_County_Airport "Charles M. Schulz–Sonoma County Airport") (STS)** The smallest of the Bay Area airports served by commercial airlines.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-7)

### Airport transportation

All major Bay Area airports are located near freeways and are served by public transportation, ride-share services, and various private shuttle bus operators.

#### Major airport/public transportation connections

- San Francisco International Airport can be directly accessed through [BART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Area_Rapid_Transit "Bay Area Rapid Transit") at the [SFO station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_International_Airport_station "San Francisco International Airport station") at the International Terminal. Using BART, riders can also transfer to [Caltrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltrain "Caltrain") at the nearby [Millbrae station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millbrae_station "Millbrae station"). SFO also has an inter-terminal [AirTrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AirTrain_(San_Francisco_International_Airport) "AirTrain (San Francisco International Airport)") service.
- San Jose International Airport can be accessed by [VTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_Valley_Transportation_Authority "Santa Clara Valley Transportation Authority") Route [60](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Santa_Clara_VTA_bus_routes#60 "List of Santa Clara VTA bus routes"), which connects to [BART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BART "BART"), [Caltrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltrain "Caltrain"), [VTA light rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VTA_light_rail "VTA light rail") and other bus and rail services at the [Santa Clara Transit Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_Transit_Center "Santa Clara Transit Center"), [Metro/Airport station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metro/Airport_station "Metro/Airport station") and [Milpitas station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milpitas_station "Milpitas station"). The airport also operates an inter-terminal shuttle bus.
- Oakland San Francisco Bay Airport is connected to BART by the [Oakland Airport Connector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland_Airport_Connector "Oakland Airport Connector") between the airport and [Coliseum station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland_Coliseum_station "Oakland Coliseum station").
- Sonoma County Airport is directly connected to [SMART](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoma%E2%80%93Marin_Area_Rail_Transit "Sonoma–Marin Area Rail Transit") commuter rail at the [Sonoma County Airport station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoma_County_Airport_station "Sonoma County Airport station").

## Public transportation

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/SF_Bay_Rail_Transit.svg/500px-SF_Bay_Rail_Transit.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SF_Bay_Rail_Transit.svg) Map of rail services in the Bay Area region

Public transportation in the San Francisco Bay Area is quite extensive, including one [rapid transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapid_transit "Rapid transit") system, three [commuter rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commuter_rail "Commuter rail") lines, two [light rail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_rail "Light rail") systems, two ferry systems, [Amtrak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amtrak "Amtrak") inter-city rail services, and four major overlapping bus agencies, in addition to dozens of smaller ones. Most agencies accept the [Clipper Card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clipper_Card "Clipper Card"), a reloadable universal electronic payment card.

An extensive rail infrastructure that provides a mix of services exists within the nine Bay Area counties. [Bay Area Rapid Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Area_Rapid_Transit "Bay Area Rapid Transit"), commonly known as BART, provides [rapid transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapid_transit "Rapid transit") service between [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_County,_California "San Francisco County, California") and [Contra Costa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contra_Costa_County,_California "Contra Costa County, California"), [Alameda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alameda_County,_California "Alameda County, California"), [San Mateo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Mateo_County,_California "San Mateo County, California"), and [Santa Clara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_County,_California "Santa Clara County, California") counties. [Caltrain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caltrain "Caltrain"), which runs on the right-of-way of the historic [Southern Pacific Railroad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsula_Commute "Peninsula Commute"), provides commuter rail service on the [San Francisco Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Peninsula "San Francisco Peninsula"), linking the cities of [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco,_California "San Francisco, California"), [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California"), [Gilroy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilroy,_California "Gilroy, California"), and numerous peninsula cities in between. The [Millbrae Intermodal Terminal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millbrae_Intermodal_Terminal "Millbrae Intermodal Terminal") provides transfers between Caltrain and BART. The [Altamont Corridor Express](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altamont_Corridor_Express "Altamont Corridor Express"), commonly known as ACE, also provides commuter rail service, but from the [Central Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Valley_(California) "Central Valley (California)") into [Silicon Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley "Silicon Valley"), terminating at San Jose's [Diridon Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose_Diridon_station "San Jose Diridon station"). To the north, [Sonoma–Marin Area Rail Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoma%E2%80%93Marin_Area_Rail_Transit "Sonoma–Marin Area Rail Transit") (SMART) line provides commuter rail service in [Sonoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoma_County,_California "Sonoma County, California") and [Marin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_County,_California "Marin County, California") counties.

In addition, [Amtrak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amtrak "Amtrak") has a presence throughout the Bay Area. There are two intercity services: the _[Capitol Corridor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitol_Corridor "Capitol Corridor")_ connects Bay Area cities to [Sacramento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacramento,_California "Sacramento, California"), and the _[Gold Runner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_Runner "Gold Runner")_ connects to cities across the [San Joaquin Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley "San Joaquin Valley"). Additionally, there are two long-distance services, the _[Coast Starlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coast_Starlight "Coast Starlight")_ offers service to [Seattle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seattle,_Washington "Seattle, Washington") and [Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California "Los Angeles, California") while [California Zephyr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Zephyr "California Zephyr") runs to [Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois "Chicago, Illinois") via [Denver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denver,_Colorado "Denver, Colorado").

The Bay Area also has two light rail systems: one run by [San Francisco Municipal Railway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Municipal_Railway "San Francisco Municipal Railway") called [Muni Metro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muni_Metro "Muni Metro"), which operates within the city of San Francisco, and the other run by the [Santa Clara Valley Transportation Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_Valley_Transportation_Authority "Santa Clara Valley Transportation Authority"), which operates within [Santa Clara County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_County "Santa Clara County").

A series of overlapping bus agencies provide additional public transit coverage to Bay Area regions both served and not served by rail transit. The four largest agencies, [Muni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Municipal_Railway "San Francisco Municipal Railway"), [AC Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AC_Transit "AC Transit"), [SamTrans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SamTrans "SamTrans"), and [VTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_Valley_Transportation_Authority "Santa Clara Valley Transportation Authority") operate within the City of San Francisco, East Bay, the Peninsula, and South Bay respectively, although their service areas generally overlap with neighboring agencies and numerous smaller agencies. All of these agencies also provide limited [late night bus service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All_Nighter_(night_bus_service) "All Nighter (night bus service)"), which are intended to "shadow" the rail routes that are closed during the nighttime hours for maintenance. In addition, the four bus agencies are each independently pursuing constructing [bus rapid transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit "Bus rapid transit") systems by developing separated right-of-ways and traffic signaling on busy corridors, including on [Geary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geary_Boulevard "Geary Boulevard") and [Van Ness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Ness_Avenue "Van Ness Avenue") for Muni, [El Camino Real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_82 "California State Route 82") for SamTrans and VTA, and [International Boulevard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Boulevard_(Oakland,_California) "International Boulevard (Oakland, California)") for AC Transit.

Although BART and certain bus agencies provide travel over (or under) the [San Francisco Bay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay "San Francisco Bay"), [Golden Gate Ferry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Ferry "Golden Gate Ferry") and [San Francisco Bay Ferry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Ferry "San Francisco Bay Ferry") provide ferry service across the bay.

Most systems allow bicycles onto their systems with no additional charge. In addition, Bay Area residents may rent bicycles from the [Bay Wheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Wheels "Bay Wheels") bike share in certain parts of San Francisco, San Mateo, and Santa Clara counties.

### Bus services and stations

The [Transbay Terminal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transbay_Terminal "Transbay Terminal") serves as the terminus for [Greyhound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greyhound_Lines "Greyhound Lines") long-distance bus services and as a hub for regional bus systems [AC Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AC_Transit "AC Transit") ( [Alameda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alameda_County,_California "Alameda County, California") and [Contra Costa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contra_Costa_County,_California "Contra Costa County, California") counties), [WestCAT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WestCAT "WestCAT"), [SamTrans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SamTrans "SamTrans") ( [San Mateo County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Mateo_County,_California "San Mateo County, California")), and [Golden Gate Transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Transit "Golden Gate Transit") ( [Marin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_County,_California "Marin County, California") and [Sonoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sonoma_County,_California "Sonoma County, California") counties).[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-13)

There are several [bus stations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_station "Bus station") in the San Francisco Bay Area including [Fairfield Transportation Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairfield_Transportation_Center "Fairfield Transportation Center"), [Richmond Parkway Transit Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richmond_Parkway_Transit_Center "Richmond Parkway Transit Center"), Naglee Park and Ride, [Hercules Transit Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules_Transit_Center "Hercules Transit Center"), [Curtola Park & Ride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curtola_Park_&_Ride "Curtola Park & Ride"), [Eastmont Transit Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastmont_Town_Center "Eastmont Town Center"), [San Rafael Transit Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Rafael_Transit_Center "San Rafael Transit Center") and many bus bays at BART stations.

### Bike and scooter sharing

See also: [Bay Wheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Wheels "Bay Wheels"); [Cycling in San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycling_in_San_Francisco "Cycling in San Francisco"); and [Cycling in San Jose, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycling_in_San_Jose,_California "Cycling in San Jose, California")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Bay_Area_Bike_Share_launch_in_San_Jose_CA.jpg/500px-Bay_Area_Bike_Share_launch_in_San_Jose_CA.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bay_Area_Bike_Share_launch_in_San_Jose_CA.jpg) A [bike share](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle-sharing_system "Bicycle-sharing system") station in [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California").

[Bay Wheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bay_Wheels "Bay Wheels") (launched as Bay Area Bike Share) is a regional public [bicycle sharing system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle_sharing_system "Bicycle sharing system") that serves the cities of [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco,_California "San Francisco, California"), [Oakland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland,_California "Oakland, California"), [Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkeley,_California "Berkeley, California"), [Emeryville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emeryville,_California "Emeryville, California"), and [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California").

The bicycles are available 24 hours a day, seven days a week to anyone who purchases a membership, with three options, annual fee of US$150, US$29 for a month or US$15 for 24 hours.[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-15) Any rider may take unlimited trips of up to 30 minutes, as measured from the time the bike is withdrawn from a dock to the time it is returned. Bikes can be picked up at any of the stations using a key fob or electronic code, and dropping them off at any station. Longer trips incur additional fees starting at US$4 for the first additional half-hour, since the idea of bike sharing is to make bicycles available for short trips.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-CBSSF-16) A replacement fee of $1,200 is charged if a rented bike is lost.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-CBSSF-16)

Several companies previously operated [dockless bicycle sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicycle-sharing_system#Dockless_bikes "Bicycle-sharing system") systems in the Bay Area,[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-17)[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-18) however, as of 2024 only Bay Wheels operates dockless bicycles in most of the Bay Area (with some exceptions such as HOPR in [Fremont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fremont,_CA "Fremont, CA")).[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-19) Dockless systems differ from the docked in that bicycles can be parked freely on the street and do not need to be docked at a designated station.

In 2018, several companies started offering dockless [scooter-sharing systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scooter-sharing_system "Scooter-sharing system") in Bay Area cities such as San Francisco and Oakland.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-20)[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-21) These systems offer electric kick scooters for rent, similar to dockless bicycle sharing systems. Some operators, such as Lime, operate both scooter and bicycle sharing systems. These shared scooters were temporarily banned in San Francisco during summer 2018, but as of October15,2018[\[update\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area&action=edit) are available under two operators: [Skip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skip_(company) "Skip (company)") and [Scoot Networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scoot_Networks "Scoot Networks").[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-22)

### Public transportation statistics

The average amount of time people spend commuting with public transit in San Francisco, for example to and from work, on a weekday is 77 min. 23% of public transit riders, ride for more than 2 hours every day. The average amount of time people wait at a stop or station for public transit is 13 min, while 17% of riders wait for over 20 minutes on average every day. The average distance people usually ride in a single trip with public transit is 9.1km, while 20% travel for over 12km in a single direction.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-23)

A 2011 [Brookings Institution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brookings_Institution "Brookings Institution") study ranked the [San Francisco MSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco%E2%80%93Oakland%E2%80%93Fremont,_CA_Metropolitan_Statistical_Area "San Francisco–Oakland–Fremont, CA Metropolitan Statistical Area") and the [San Jose MSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose%E2%80%93Sunnyvale%E2%80%93Santa_Clara,_CA_MSA "San Jose–Sunnyvale–Santa Clara, CA MSA") sixteenth[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-24) and second,[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-25) respectively, on transit coverage to job access. Another nationwide study, conducted by the [University of Minnesota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Minnesota "University of Minnesota") in 2014, ranked the San Francisco MSA second and San Jose MSA tenth.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-26) In 2012 it was the joint winner of the [Sustainable Transport Award](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_Transport_Award "Sustainable Transport Award"). Despite this, the San Francisco Bay Area remains the second most traffic-congested region in the country with a declining per capita use of public transit.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

In 2013, the San Francisco-Oakland-Hayward metropolitan statistical area (San Francisco MSA) had the second lowest percentage of workers who commuted by private automobile (69.8 percent), with 7.6 percent of area workers traveling via bus. During the period starting in 2006 and ending in 2013, the San Francisco MSA had the greatest percentage decline of workers commuting by automobile (3.8 percent) among MSAs with more than a half million residents.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-27)

### Named interchanges

The [Alemany Maze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alemany_Maze "Alemany Maze") is the [interchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interchange_(road) "Interchange (road)") between the [James Lick Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayshore_Freeway "Bayshore Freeway") ( [Highway 101](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_101_in_California "U.S. Route 101 in California")) and [I-280](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_280_(California) "Interstate 280 (California)").

The [MacArthur Maze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacArthur_Maze "MacArthur Maze") is the interchange between the [Eastshore Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastshore_Freeway "Eastshore Freeway") ( [I-80](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_80_in_California "Interstate 80 in California") east / [I-580](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_580_(California) "Interstate 580 (California)") west), [Nimitz Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_880_(California) "Interstate 880 (California)") ( [I-880](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_880_(California) "Interstate 880 (California)") south), and [MacArthur Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_580_(California) "Interstate 580 (California)") ( [I-580](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_580_(California) "Interstate 580 (California)") east) at the east end of the [Bay Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco%E2%80%93Oakland_Bay_Bridge "San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge") ( [I-80](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_80_in_California "Interstate 80 in California") west).

The [Joe Colla Interchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Colla_Interchange "Joe Colla Interchange") is the interchange between [Highway 101](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_101_in_California "U.S. Route 101 in California"), [I-280](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_280_(California) "Interstate 280 (California)"), and [I-680](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_680_(California) "Interstate 680 (California)"). Both I-280 and I-680's southern termini is located as this interchange.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-28)

### San Francisco streets

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Market_Street_San_Francisco_From_Twin_Peaks.jpg/500px-Market_Street_San_Francisco_From_Twin_Peaks.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Market_Street_San_Francisco_From_Twin_Peaks.jpg) [Market Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_Street_(San_Francisco) "Market Street (San Francisco)"), the main thoroughfare in San Francisco, as seen from [Twin Peaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twin_Peaks_(San_Francisco) "Twin Peaks (San Francisco)").

Main article: [List of streets in San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_streets_in_San_Francisco "List of streets in San Francisco")

Due to its unique geography, and the [freeway revolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freeway_and_expressway_revolts#San_Francisco "Freeway and expressway revolts") of the late 1950s,[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-freeway-29) San Francisco is one of the few American cities served primarily by [arterial roads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arterial_roads "Arterial roads") for most trips within city limits, rather than a freeway network supplemented by arterial roads.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

[Interstate 80](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_80_in_California "Interstate 80 in California") begins at the approach to the [Bay Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco%E2%80%93Oakland_Bay_Bridge "San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge") and is the only direct automobile link to the East Bay. [U.S. Route 101](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_101_in_California "U.S. Route 101 in California") connects to the western terminus of Interstate 80 and provides access to the south of the city along San Francisco Bay toward [Silicon Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley "Silicon Valley"). Northward, the routing for U.S. 101 uses arterial streets [Mission Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mission_Street_(San_Francisco) "Mission Street (San Francisco)") (northbound) and South Van Ness Avenue (southbound), [Van Ness Avenue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Ness_Avenue_(San_Francisco) "Van Ness Avenue (San Francisco)"), [Lombard Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lombard_Street_(San_Francisco) "Lombard Street (San Francisco)"), [Richardson Avenue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richardson_Avenue_(San_Francisco) "Richardson Avenue (San Francisco)"), and Doyle Drive to connect to the [Golden Gate Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge "Golden Gate Bridge"), the only direct automobile link to [Marin County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_County "Marin County") and the North Bay.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Van_Ness_Avenue_in_2024.png/500px-Van_Ness_Avenue_in_2024.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Van_Ness_Avenue_in_2024.png) Van Ness Avenue near [San Francisco City Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_City_Hall "San Francisco City Hall") in 2024

[State Route 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_1 "California State Route 1") also enters San Francisco from the north via the Golden Gate Bridge, but turns south away from the routing of U.S. 101, first onto Park Presidio Blvd through Golden Gate Park, and then bisecting the west side of the city as the [19th Avenue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/19th_Avenue_(San_Francisco) "19th Avenue (San Francisco)") arterial thoroughfare, joining with [Interstate 280](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstate_280_(California) "Interstate 280 (California)") at the city's southern border. Interstate 280 continues south from San Francisco. Interstate 280 also turns to the east along the southern edge of the city, terminating just south of the Bay Bridge in the [South of Market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_of_Market_(San_Francisco) "South of Market (San Francisco)") neighborhood. After the [1989 Loma Prieta earthquake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1989_Loma_Prieta_earthquake "1989 Loma Prieta earthquake"), city leaders decided to demolish the [Embarcadero Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embarcadero_Freeway "Embarcadero Freeway"), and a portion of the [Central Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Freeway "Central Freeway"), converting them into street-level boulevards.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-freeway-29)

[Route 35](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_35 "California State Route 35") enters the city from the south as [Skyline Boulevard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_35 "California State Route 35"), following city streets until it terminates at its intersection with Highway 1. [Route 82](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_82 "California State Route 82") enters San Francisco from the south as [Mission Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mission_Street "Mission Street"), following the path of the historic [El Camino Real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Camino_Real_(California) "El Camino Real (California)") and terminating shortly thereafter at its intersection of I-280. Major east–west thoroughfares include [Geary Boulevard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geary_Boulevard "Geary Boulevard"), the [Lincoln Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_streets_in_San_Francisco "List of streets in San Francisco")/ [Fell Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_streets_in_San_Francisco "List of streets in San Francisco") corridor, and [Market Street](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_Street_(San_Francisco) "Market Street (San Francisco)")/ [Portola Drive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_streets_in_San_Francisco "List of streets in San Francisco").\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

The Western Terminus of the historic transcontinental [Lincoln Highway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_Highway "Lincoln Highway"), the first road across America, is in San Francisco's [Lincoln Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lincoln_Park_(San_Francisco) "Lincoln Park (San Francisco)").\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

### Highway revolts

Main article: [Highway revolts in the United States §California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highway_revolts_in_the_United_States#California "Highway revolts in the United States")

San Francisco is the birthplace of highway revolts in the United States, and highways have historically been approached with hostility by locals across the Bay Area. Protests have occurred against highways as early as 1955, and these protests eventually cancelled the construction of additional highways through [Golden Gate Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Gate_Park "Golden Gate Park") and the [Presidio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Presidio_of_San_Francisco "Presidio of San Francisco"), and further led to the demolishing of the [Embarcadero Freeway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Route_480 "California State Route 480") in the early 1990s.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-:0-3)[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-30) Protests additionally occurred throughout the East Bay, cancelling projects such as the Ashby Freeway through [Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkeley,_California "Berkeley, California") and the Richmond Boulevard Freeway in Oakland.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-31)[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-32)

## San Francisco Bay Trail

Main article: [San Francisco Bay Trail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Trail "San Francisco Bay Trail")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/San_Francisco_Bay_Trail_Map.pdf/page1-500px-San_Francisco_Bay_Trail_Map.pdf.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:San_Francisco_Bay_Trail_Map.pdf) The San Francisco Bay Trail alignment.

The San Francisco Bay Trail is a bicycle and pedestrian trail that will eventually allow continuous travel around the shoreline of San Francisco Bay. As of 2016, 350 miles (560km) of trail have been completed, while the full plan calls for a trail over 500 miles (800km) long that link the shoreline of nine counties, passing through 47 cities and crossing seven toll bridges. Sections of the Bay Trail exist in all nine Bay Area counties. The longest continuous segments include 26 miles (42km) primarily on gravel levees between [East Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Palo_Alto,_California "East Palo Alto, California") and [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California") in [Santa Clara County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_County "Santa Clara County"); 25 miles (40km) in [San Mateo County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Mateo_County "San Mateo County") between [Millbrae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millbrae,_California "Millbrae, California") and [San Carlos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Carlos,_California "San Carlos, California"); 17 miles (27km) in central [Alameda County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alameda_County "Alameda County") from [San Leandro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Leandro,_California "San Leandro, California") to [Hayward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hayward,_California "Hayward, California"); and 15 miles (24km) along the shoreline and on city streets through [Richmond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richmond,_California "Richmond, California") in [Contra Costa County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contra_Costa_County "Contra Costa County"). The northernmost trail section passes through [San Pablo Bay National Wildlife Refuge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Pablo_Bay_National_Wildlife_Refuge "San Pablo Bay National Wildlife Refuge").

## Seaports

See also: [Piers in San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piers_in_San_Francisco "Piers in San Francisco")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Oakland_California_aerial_view.jpg/500px-Oakland_California_aerial_view.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Oakland_California_aerial_view.jpg) Aerial view of the [Port of Oakland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Oakland "Port of Oakland")

The [Port of San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_San_Francisco "Port of San Francisco") was once the largest and busiest seaport on the West Coast. It featured rows of [piers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pier "Pier") perpendicular to the shore, where cargo from the moored ships was handled by cranes and manual labor and transported to nearby warehouses. The port handled cargo to and from trans-Pacific and Atlantic destinations, and was the West Coast center of the [lumber trade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Coast_lumber_trade "West Coast lumber trade"). The [1934 West Coast Longshore Strike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1934_West_Coast_Longshore_Strike "1934 West Coast Longshore Strike"), an important episode in the history of the [American labor movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labor_unions_in_the_United_States "Labor unions in the United States"), brought most ports to a standstill. The advent of [container shipping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Container_shipping "Container shipping") made pier-based ports obsolete, and most commercial berths moved to the [Port of Oakland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Oakland "Port of Oakland") and [Port of Richmond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Port_of_Richmond_(California) "Port of Richmond (California)"). A few active berths specializing in [break bulk cargo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Break_bulk_cargo "Break bulk cargo") remain alongside the [Islais Creek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islais_Creek "Islais Creek") Channel.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

The port currently uses [Pier 35](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pier_35_(San_Francisco) "Pier 35 (San Francisco)") to handle the 60–80 [cruise ship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cruise_ship "Cruise ship") calls and 200,000 passengers that come to [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco,_California "San Francisco, California").[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-34) Itineraries from San Francisco usually include round trip cruises to [Alaska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska "Alaska") and [Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mexico "Mexico"). The James R. Herman Cruise Terminal Project at [Pier 27](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pier_27_(San_Francisco)?action=edit&redlink=1 "Pier 27 (San Francisco)") opened in 2014 as a replacement. The previous primary terminal at Pier 35 had neither the sufficient capacity to allow for the increasing length and passenger capacity of new cruise ships nor the amenities needed for an international cruise terminal.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-35)

On March 16, 2013, [Princess Cruises](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princess_Cruises "Princess Cruises") _[Grand Princess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_Princess "Grand Princess")_ became the first ship to home port in San Francisco year round. The ship offers cruises to Alaska, California Coasts, [Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaii "Hawaii"), and Mexico. _Grand Princess_ will be stationed in San Francisco until April 2014. Princess will also operate other ships during the summer of 2014, making it the only cruise line home porting year round in San Francisco.[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transportation_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area#cite_note-36)

# Transportation Basics: How to Get Around San Francisco

San Francisco is made up of 49 square miles of long boulevards, curvy streets, hidden alleyways and lots of hills. But have no fear! Follow these tips and you’ll be the transportation hero of your San Francisco adventure.


## Getting to San Francisco

### Flying into San Francisco International Airport (SFO)

[SFOOpens in new window](https://expedia.com/affiliate/YEl9BWp) is the preferred airport for visiting San Francisco. It's phenomenally well-connected to dozens of domestic and international cities, and serves as the west coast gateway for much of the Asia-Pacific region. It's also one of the cleanest, safest, and most unique airports in the world. How many airports have you been through that have their own accredited museum or a dedicated [Wag BrigadeOpens in new window](https://www.flysfo.com/services-amenities/wag-brigade)?

Once you land at SFO, it's easy to get into the city itself. Here are a few different ways:

### BART

Bay Area Rapid Transit (BART) all-electric trains are a fast, inexpensive, and dependable way to get from SFO to San Francisco. Once you have your luggage, follow the signs for AirTrain, a free tram service to all terminals. Take either the Red or Blue AirTrain line to the Garage G and BART stop in International Terminal G. Proceed to the platform and board the San Francisco/Antioch train.

BART fares are based on distance traveled; the greater the distance, the higher the fare. A round-trip fare from SFO to downtown San Francisco is $22.30 ($11.15 one-way).

When you arrive at your destination, you must scan your Clipper Card again to exit the station. If you don’t have enough money on your ticket to exit, don’t worry. You can add more at the Add Fare machine in the station.

### Ride-Sharing Apps

With app-based transportation services, including [LyftOpens in new window](https://www.lyft.com/) and [UberOpens in new window](https://www.uber.com/), you can arrange to ride with drivers using their personal, non-commercial vehicles via smartphone. These services pick up passengers on Level 5 of the Domestic Garage. [This websiteOpens in new window](https://www.flysfo.com/passengers/ground-transportation/lyft-uber-rides) describes how to find your ride at SFO. Fares and travel times vary according to demand and traffic.

### Shared Van & Shuttle Service

Several privately-owned, shared-ride vans provide service between SFO and passenger-specified locations. The one-way rate from SFO to the city center is approximately $17. Depending on the number of passengers, shared ride vans may make multiple stops, so the travel time can vary. In the Domestic Terminals, shuttles pick up passengers curbside on the Arrivals Level. In the International Terminal, passenger pick-up is on the Arrivals Level roadway center island.

### Taxis & Limousines

SFO is served by a number of taxi and limousine companies. Taxis depart from roadway islands on the lower level outside the Arrivals/Baggage Claims area in all terminals. Uniformed taxi coordinators work from 7 a.m. until 1 a.m. to ensure a smooth process for travelers. All cabs are metered. The travel time to popular drop-off points in downtown San Francisco is usually around 30 minutes (depending on traffic) and fares range from $45 to 60.

For a limousine, arrange service in advance with your company of choice or upon arrival via the Lodging, Transportation and Attraction Boards, which provide free phone connections to participating limousine operators. These boards are located on the Arrivals/Baggage Claim Level of all terminals. Contact the service provider for pricing and availability.

### SamTrans Buses

The San Mateo County Transit Agency (SamTrans) runs public buses between SFO and downtown San Francisco on the SFO, 292, and 398 lines for $2-$5 one-way for adults. Find SamTrans bus stops in the following locations at each terminal:

- Terminal 2: Arrivals/Baggage Claim Level, center island adjacent to Courtyard 2.
- Terminal 3: Arrivals/Baggage Claim Level, curbside adjacent to Courtyard 4.
- International Terminal: Level 1 at Courtyard A and curbside adjacent to Courtyard G.

### Parking

San Francisco neighborhoods and popular tourist destinations are often sprinkled with a myriad of parking options, including metered parking.  However, we encourage visitors to take the confusion and anxiety out of the equation and heading to [SpotHeroOpens in new window](https://spothero.app.link/ts1p2NqSe1?kind=city&id=26&$3p=a_hasoffers&$affiliate_json=http%3A%2F%2Ftracking.spothero.com%2Faff_c%3Foffer_id%3D1%26aff_id%3D1612%26format%3Djson) to reserve parking ahead of your trip or while you're in town.

## Flying into San Francisco Bay Oakland International Airport (OAK)

### BART

Taking BART from OAK is an easy and low-cost option to get to San Francisco. After you’ve picked up your luggage, look for BART signs in the baggage claim. The station is located across from the Terminal 1 baggage claim area and a short walk from Terminal 2. Board a train for the quick eight-minute ride from OAK to the BART Coliseum station, where you can transfer to the BART train bound for your final destination in San Francisco.

OAK trains depart every 5 minutes from 8 a.m. to 8 p.m. daily. Service is less frequent at other times. Round trip BART fares from OAK to San Francisco ($25.30), downtown Oakland ($8.50) or other East Bay cities are less than half the cost of taxis or shuttles to the same destination.

- **More Info:** [How to Get to San Francisco from Oakland](https://www.sftravel.com/info/transportation-basics-how-to-get-to-san-francisco-oakland "Transportation Basics: How to Get to San Francisco from Oakland")

### Ride-Sharing Apps

Smartphone app-based transportation services like [LyftOpens in new window](https://www.lyft.com/) and [UberOpens in new window](https://www.uber.com/) serve OAK, providing rides via drivers in their personal, non-commercial vehicles. Arrange your ride via the app, and then follow the signs for Smartphone App Rides in Terminals 1 and 2. Drivers wait for passengers on the second curb of the pickup area. Fares and travel times vary according to demand and local traffic, but a typical ride from OAK to San Francisco takes approximately 45 minutes and ranges in price from $30 to 45.

Shuttle, taxi and limo service prices vary for shuttles, taxis and limousines from OAK to San Francisco. There are multiple options and special prices for traveling groups.

## Flying into San Jose Airport (SJC)

### Caltrain

Caltrain provides commuter rail service along the San Francisco Peninsula. From the San Jose Airport, take the Airport Flyer-VTA Route #60 bus to the Santa Clara Transit Center. A new shuttle departs approximately every 15 to 30 minutes from 5 a.m. to 11:30 p.m. One-way fare is $2.

Caltrain fares are based on the number of zones that are partially or wholly traveled through by the passenger. There are two Caltrain stops in San Francisco: 22nd Street and Fourth Street. Both are in Zone 1. Allow a minimum of 60 minutes to arrive in San Francisco. Occasionally, the trip might take longer.

- **More Info:** [How to Get to San Francisco from San Jose](https://www.sftravel.com/info/transportation-basics-how-to-get-to-san-francisco-sjc "Transportation Basics: How to Get to San Francisco from SJC")

### Shuttles

Prices vary for shuttle service from SJC. There are also multiple options and special prices for traveling groups.

### Arriving by Train

Amtrak offers a comfortable and enjoyable way to get to San Francisco. The city is served via Thruway bus connections at Emeryville. Ride with the convenience of amenities and picturesque scenery right outside your window as you explore what the Bay Area has to offer.

- From the North, take the Coast Starlight from Seattle and Portland.
- From the South, the Coast Starlight travels daily from Los Angeles.
- From the East, the California Zephyr travels from Chicago and Denver daily.
- The Amtrak San Joaquin offers departures from Bakersfield and Fresno.

The Capitol Corridor, also operated by Amtrak, serves 18 stops between Sacramento and Silicon Valley.

## Getting Around San Francisco

### Muni

Muni is San Francisco’s bus and metro system that runs throughout the city, operating buses, trains, cable cars and the F-line streetcar. Muni buses remain above ground and on streets while Muni Metro runs on rails and sometimes go underground. Bus stops come in many forms: small bus shelters, yellow paint on street poles, and white paint on streets. Metro stops can be found on an island in the middle of the street and in underground stations. Using the [Metro MapOpens in new window](https://www.sfmta.com/maps/muni-metro-map), find the nearest Metro to your location.

- **More Info:** [How to use Muni and Muni Metro](https://www.sftravel.com/info/transportation-basics-how-to-use-muni "Transportation Basics: How to Use Muni")

### Cable Cars and Street Cars

[Cable cars](https://www.sftravel.com/things-to-do/attractions/iconic-sf/cable-cars "Cable Cars") and street cars are San Francisco’s historic public transit. Cable cars are located downtown and run on three lines: [Powell-HydeOpens in new window](https://www.sftravel.com/article/your-guide-to-powell-hyde-cable-car-line), [Powell-MasonOpens in new window](https://www.sftravel.com/article/your-guide-to-powell-mason-cable-car-line), and [California StreetOpens in new window](https://www.sftravel.com/article/your-guide-to-san-franciscos-california-street-cable-car). The F-Line streetcar runs up and down Market Street all the way to Fisherman’s Wharf. Purchase fares in advance at multiple locations or via smartphone using the [MuniMobile appOpens in new window](https://www.sfmta.com/getting-around/transit/munimobile). A Clipper Card will work on both cable cars and streetcars.

### PresidiGo Shuttle

[The​ PresidiGo ShuttleOpens in new window](https://www.presidio.gov/transportation/presidigo) system provides free and accessible transportation to an​d around the Presidio from three routes in the city, seven days a we​ek. Many stops are within a short walking distance from Muni or Golden Gate Transit bus lines.

### Ride Sharing

There are a few on-demand car services to choose from, most of them started by San Francisco locals. You must first download the app, register, and set up a credit card payment system to use them. To use these services, set a pick-up location and destination in the app. Within minutes, you will be matched with a driver. Make sure to communicate with your driver; call, text, or wave down their car and always confirm that you are getting in the correct vehicle.

### Taxi

As is customary in most U.S. cities, visitors may hail a taxi directly or use a smartphone app.

### Bike and Scooter

You can get around San Francisco on two wheels thanks to the many bikes and scooters available.

Across the city, there are 5,500 scooters provided by [LimeOpens in new window](https://www.li.me/en-us/adaptive-vehicles/) and [SpinOpens in new window](https://www.spin.app/). Download any one of their apps to your phone to search for available motorized scooters nearby and enjoy a fun and scenic ride between your destinations.

The [Bay WheelsOpens in new window](https://www.lyft.com/bikes/bay-wheels) bike share program, operated by Lyft, provides 4,500 bikes and more than 6,000 docks for them across San Francisco. A bike share is great for one-way trips between neighborhoods and is often the quickest, most affordable way to get around. For longer trips or crossing the Golden Gate Bridge, consider renting a bike or signing up for a [guided tour](https://www.sftravel.com/article/find-perfect-bike-tour-san-francisco "Find the Perfect Bike Tour in San Francisco").

If you are going to explore San Francisco on two wheels, here are some vital do's and don'ts to remember:

- **Do** check brakes and wheels before riding.
- **Do** wear a helmet and follow traffic rules. Only ride in the bike or travel lane.
- **Do** obey traffic signs and signals.
- **Do** park your bike or scooter upright and with care on the sidewalk or in a designated bike rack.
- **Don't** ride on sidewalks.
- **Don't** park in ramps or at blue, yellow or white curbs. Blocked paths can be dangerous for people with disabilities.
- **Don't** block sidewalks or building entrances.

### Ferries

A ferry ride on the bay is a fun way to travel beyond San Francisco. Enjoy lunch on the water in Sausalito (there's shopping, too) or visit the quaint town of Tiburon with scenic views of the city. The [Golden Gate FerryOpens in new window](https://www.goldengate.org/ferry/schedules-maps/) will take you to Angel Island State Park, Larkspur, Sausalito, and Tiburon to the north and all Golden Gate ferries depart from Gate B at the north side of the Ferry Building. Don't miss the [Giants FerryOpens in new window](https://www.goldengate.org/ferry/oracle-park-service/) to Oracle Park or the [Warriors FerryOpens in new window](https://www.goldengate.org/ferry/chase-center-service/) to the Chase Center!

The [San Francisco Bay FerryOpens in new window](https://sanfranciscobayferry.com/oakland-alameda-ferry-route) can take you to Alameda, Oakland, Richmond, Treasure Island, and Vallejo to the east and they operate out of the three gates south of the Ferry Building. There are several ways to pay: by [Clipper CardOpens in new window](https://www.clippercard.com/ClipperWeb/), [San Francisco Bay Ferry mobile appOpens in new window](https://sanfranciscobayferry.com/sf-bay-ferry-mobile-app-tickets), paper ticket, at the ferry ticket office, or onboard **most** ferries with cash or credit card. There's even room for bikes on the ferries (space available).

### Renting A Car

If you want to explore destinations [beyond San Francisco](https://www.sftravel.com/beyond-sf "Beyond SF"), you can use any of our local [tour partners](https://www.sftravel.com/things-to-do/tours "Tours") or you can rent a car yourself.

Like any big city, San Francisco has to deal with issues like vandalism and theft. If you plan on renting a car, please note wherever you park your car, no matter the time of day or night, be sure to take all valuables with you. If you love it, don't leave it.

## Tap to Pay on BART and Muni

Did you know that you can now use your own preferred payment form on BART and Muni? Simply tap your credit or debit card of choice upon entering and exiting the system. Accepted brands include Mastercard, Visa, Discover, and American Express. Apple Wallet and Google Wallet payments will be accepted, too!
