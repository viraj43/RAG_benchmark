# AI in 2026: The New Infrastructure Layer of the Global Economy

Today, Artificial Intelligence is embedded in infrastructure across every major industry, delivering measurable ROI in real-world operations. This reality isn't a narrow consumer web play like the dot-com era. It is foundational intelligence powering ambulances, trading floors, self-driving fleets, classrooms, power grids, and factories.

Below is a snapshot of the 2026 AI ecosystem, spanning foundation models, compute platforms, and sector-specific systems already deployed at scale.

## The Physical Substrate: Compute & Infrastructure

These platforms are the silicon, networking, and data center architecture that enable the entire ecosystem.

*   NVIDIA: Full-stack AI systems, including GPUs and "AI Factory" architecture powering advanced reasoning models.
*   AMD: High-memory, cost-efficient accelerators used for large-scale inference and open-weight moTdel hosting.
*   Intel: Gaudi architecture focused on enterprise AI acceleration and on-premise deployments.
*   Custom Silicon: Google TPU and AWS Trainium/Inferentia provide hyperscale training and cost-optimized inference.
*   Edge AI: Qualcomm provides on-device acceleration for mobile, automotive, and edge systems.

## The Engines: Foundation Models

These base-layer engines are widely known, broadly adopted, and increasingly commoditized.

*   Proprietary Leaders: GPT-5 (OpenAI), Command (Cohere), Nova (Amazon), and Phi (Microsoft).
*   Open-Weight Pioneers: Llama (Meta) , Mistral , Qwen (Alibaba) , DeepSeek , Gemma (Google) , and Falcon (TII).

## Specialized Intelligence: Where the Real Value Lives

While base layers consolidate, the most significant value is being created in specialized layers above them.

AI’s most meaningful economic value in 2026 is emerging not in the foundation models, but in the specialized systems built on top of them. Across industries, highly targeted AI models are already deployed in real operations and delivering measurable ROI.

In emergency and medical response, systems like Corti are supporting ambulance dispatchers with real‑time triage, while AITRiPS is improving trauma prediction for critical care teams. Tools such as Annalise are assisting radiologists across hospitals worldwide, accelerating diagnostic accuracy and reducing clinical workload.

In precision medicine, companies like Tempus and Paige are transforming oncology and pathology. Tempus One provides point-of-care decision support for oncologists, while Paige Predict enhances cancer detection and grading through AI‑driven pathology.

In science and research, models such as AlphaFold, GNoME, and GraphCast are reshaping discovery workflows. AlphaFold has accelerated protein structure prediction, GNoME is identifying new materials at scale, and GraphCast is outperforming traditional weather forecasting models.

In finance and risk, domain‑specific intelligence is becoming essential infrastructure. BloombergGPT supports market analysis, Scienaptic AI enhances credit underwriting, and Norm Ai automates compliance reasoning for regulated environments.

In transportation, autonomy continues to advance. Waymo Driver is operating fully driverless robotaxis, Tesla FSD is deployed at consumer scale, and NVIDIA DRIVE powers OEM autonomy stacks across global automakers.

In education, AI is reshaping personalized learning. Khanmigo supports K‑12 tutoring, LearnLM brings pedagogical alignment to learning models, and ChatGPT Edu is being adopted across universities.

In creative and industrial media, generative systems are becoming production tools. Sora and Veo enable high‑resolution video generation, while DALL‑E, Midjourney, and Flux support commercial creative workflows. On‑device generation is emerging as well, with Google’s Nano Banana enabling lightweight multimodal capabilities on local hardware. For enterprises requiring rights‑managed content, Adobe Firefly provides a safe, compliant alternative.

## The Verdict: Bubble or Bedrock?

The base-layer players (OpenAI, Google, Meta) will likely consolidate, as did the survivors of the dot-com collapse. However, the underlying demand for specialized, customized software, data pipelines, and human oversight is accelerating.

AI isn't replacing people; it is shifting their roles. For example, the best developers are now 10x more productive, moving away from manual coding toward orchestrating agentic systems, physical AI, and AI FinOps. Just as the internet gave us connectivity, AI enables scalable computing and a new wave of essential products. AI is the next permanent layer of the global economy.

What do you think? Are we repeating the internet bubble of 2000, or are we building the infrastructure that will still be here in 2050? Which specialized sector do you believe will see the highest ROI by the end of next year?

## April 2026 Monthly Review: The AI Race Moved Beyond Chips

_Current through 24 April 2026_

April clarified the central argument of **Geopolitics of AI**: artificial intelligence should not be analysed only through model releases, benchmark scores, or commercial adoption. It should be treated as a geopolitical system made of materials, industrial capacity, infrastructure, regulation, platforms, standards, and strategic competition.

The month’s strongest signal was that the [AI race is moving beyond the familiar “chip war” frame](http://research/ai-power-isn-t-just-about-better-models-it-s-about-who-controls-the-systems-they-run-on). Chips remain central. But the more complete picture now includes compute thresholds, model access, energy systems, data-centre buildout, advanced packaging, high-bandwidth memory, cloud infrastructure, cybersecurity deployment, procurement, compliance, and the strategic role of frontier firms.

The decisive question is therefore not only who can invent the strongest model. It is who can convert artificial intelligence into durable capability under real-world constraints.

## The month’s main signal: from chip access to system control

The dominant pattern of April was the shift from **point control** to **system control**.

In the earlier phase of AI competition, the control logic appeared relatively simple: restrict access to advanced chips and you restrict a rival’s ability to train or deploy frontier systems. April showed that this logic is now too narrow. Power is distributed across the system that links models, compute, memory, packaging, cloud platforms, electricity, data centres, cybersecurity, and compliance institutions.

The month’s evidence points to a wider strategic field: the United States is trying to preserve control over compute and model access; China is adapting through domestic substitution; Europe is trying to move from regulation to capacity; middle powers are seeking sovereign compute; and frontier firms are becoming security and infrastructure intermediaries.

Ten developments capture the pattern.

### 1. The United States moved further toward compute-threshold governance

The United States continued to explore restrictions based not only on named chips, but on the computing power that advanced chips can make available. This matters because the real strategic variable is not the brand or catalogue label of a chip, but the level of training and inference capability that a system can support.

This shifts export control from static product classification toward deployable compute. In practical terms, Washington is trying to govern what an AI system can actually do once chips, networking, memory, and data-centre infrastructure are assembled.

### 2. Anti-circumvention became part of the battlefield

April also showed that setting a rule is not enough. Washington’s control strategy increasingly depends on closing workaround channels: modified chips, reporting gaps, performance-density loopholes, indirect access, and supplier-level ambiguity.

This is governance as infrastructure. The power of export controls depends not only on legal authority, but on monitoring, classification, enforcement, allied compliance, and the ability to update controls faster than the workaround market can adapt.

### 3. Model access became a strategic leakage problem

The White House accusation that China had engaged in large-scale extraction of value from American AI systems pushed the debate beyond hardware. The issue is now also the model layer: proxy accounts, jailbreaking, model distillation, and indirect access to frontier capabilities.

This is a major conceptual shift. If model access becomes treated as strategic leakage, then the next phase of AI control may extend more deeply into Application Programming Interface access, user verification, model-security monitoring, and cyber compliance.

### 4. China accelerated the Huawei–DeepSeek stack

Huawei’s Ascend supernode infrastructure being positioned to support DeepSeek V4 was one of the clearest signs that China is trying to align domestic chips, domestic models, domestic infrastructure, and domestic deployment channels.

The key question is not whether Huawei immediately matches Nvidia one-for-one. The more important question is whether China can assemble a sufficiently capable alternative stack under pressure. If it can, U.S. controls may still raise costs, but they may no longer function as a decisive barrier.

### 5. Nvidia’s H200 case showed that decoupling can become two-sided

The reported absence of Nvidia H200 sales to China, despite earlier conditional approval, showed that AI decoupling is not only imposed by the incumbent power. Beijing may also discourage reliance on U.S. accelerators in order to strengthen domestic suppliers.

This matters because it complicates the usual export-control story. Even when hardware is nominally available, political clearance, procurement rules, industrial policy, and domestic substitution strategies can make it strategically unusable.

### 6. Allied export-control alignment moved forward

The advancement of the Multilateral Alignment of Technology Controls on Hardware Act, or MATCH Act, pointed to the multilateralisation of AI hardware controls. The United States is not simply trying to control its own exports. It is trying to align allied controls around semiconductor hardware and production chokepoints.

This is the logic of networked hegemony: control is strongest when it is embedded across suppliers, allies, jurisdictions, and compliance systems.

### 7. Europe’s data-centre gap became harder to ignore

Nokia’s warning that Europe risks falling behind the United States and China in AI data-centre buildout captured the European problem clearly. Europe has regulatory power, but regulatory power does not automatically become operational power.

The operational gap lies in data centres, grid access, connectivity, cooling, permitting, cloud infrastructure, and the ability to host and scale AI systems within politically controlled environments. Europe’s AI problem is therefore not only a matter of rules or talent. It is also a matter of infrastructure depth.

### 8. The European Union tried to move from regulation to capacity

The European Commission’s AI Continent Action Plan milestones showed that Brussels understands the need to move beyond the Artificial Intelligence Act alone. The European Union is trying to convert industrial policy, trust, standards, and market size into AI capacity.

The strategic question is whether Europe can generate enough compute, procurement, adoption, public-sector deployment, and industrial scaling to compete with the United States and China. Without infrastructure, Europe risks becoming a regulatory power without sufficient operational command.

### 9. Memory and packaging became hard infrastructure chokepoints

TSMC’s planned chip-packaging plant in Arizona and SK Hynix’s major investment in South Korean AI memory capacity showed that the AI supply chain is not only about graphics processing units.

High-bandwidth memory, advanced packaging, substrates, thermal engineering, and integration capacity are becoming central to the distribution of AI power. A country may attract fabrication investment and still remain dependent if it lacks the support layers required to turn chips into usable accelerator systems.

### 10. Frontier firms moved deeper into security and allied infrastructure

OpenAI’s reported briefings to U.S. agencies, state governments, and Five Eyes partners on a cybersecurity-focused AI product, Anthropic’s Project Glasswing, and Microsoft’s large Australia investment all pointed in the same direction: frontier firms are becoming geopolitical intermediaries.

They are not replacing states. But they increasingly shape how states access, deploy, secure, and scale AI. Their role is no longer only commercial. It is infrastructural, security-relevant, and strategic.

## Frontier firms: from vendors to geopolitical intermediaries

April strengthened one of the most important claims of the Geopolitics of AI framework: large frontier firms should be analysed as geopolitical actors, not only as technology companies.

Their power is not sovereign power. They do not possess formal state authority. But they control capabilities and infrastructures that states increasingly need. They shape access to models, cloud platforms, cybersecurity tools, data-centre capacity, software stacks, and compliance environments. This makes them intermediaries between public authority and technological capability.

### OpenAI

OpenAI mattered in April because its reported cybersecurity briefings to U.S. agencies and Five Eyes partners showed how frontier capability can be introduced through trusted institutional channels. The significance is not simply that a new defensive product exists. The significance is that AI capability becomes state capacity only when it is routed through public agencies, allied security networks, and controlled-access structures.

### Anthropic

Anthropic’s Project Glasswing was the clearest April case of controlled release. The company did not treat Claude Mythos Preview as a standard commercial model to be broadly released. It treated it as a sensitive cybersecurity capability whose access had to be restricted, mediated, and sequenced through a defensive coalition.

That makes Glasswing a case study in the politics of access. Who gets early access? Who verifies vulnerabilities? Who coordinates disclosure? Who decides when information is safe to circulate? Who bears responsibility if access is too open, or too restricted?

### Microsoft

Microsoft’s A$25 billion Australia investment showed how cloud and AI firms can multiply allied power through infrastructure packages. The investment combined AI and cloud infrastructure, cybersecurity, workforce skilling, and cooperation with safety institutions. This makes Australia more important as an Indo-Pacific AI node, but it also reinforces dependence on U.S.-linked cloud infrastructure.

### Nvidia

Nvidia’s April role was not only about accelerators. Its post-GTC infrastructure messaging and Kubernetes-related materials pointed toward control over orchestration, workload allocation, secure multi-tenant execution, and AI-factory architecture. This shows that power is moving below the chip into the software-defined infrastructure that makes compute usable.

### Google Cloud and Amazon

Google Cloud Next ’26 showed how cloud, Nvidia infrastructure, enterprise deployment, and frontier-model serving are being assembled into a public cloud-AI bloc. Amazon’s large Spain investment added another angle: Europe may gain hosted AI capacity while still leaving substantial control in the hands of foreign hyperscalers.

The broader conclusion is that frontier firms and cloud infrastructure firms are increasingly co-producing geopolitical capability. They do not simply sell tools to states. They structure the conditions under which states can use AI.

## Documents that define: Project Glasswing

Project Glasswing deserves special treatment because it captures a wider governance problem.

The significance of Project Glasswing is not only technical. Anthropic says Claude Mythos Preview identified large numbers of serious software vulnerabilities, many of them still unpatched. But the political meaning lies in the release structure. Anthropic did not move immediately toward broad diffusion. It adopted restricted access through a network of trusted institutional partners.

This matters for three reasons.

First, it shows that some frontier AI capabilities may enter the world through **controlled deployment** rather than ordinary product release.

Second, it shows that cybersecurity models create a distinctive governance problem. The same capability that can help defenders identify vulnerabilities could also become dangerous if released too broadly or too early.

Third, it shows how private firms can shape public capacity. Anthropic is not sovereign, but in this case it is helping decide who gets access to a security-relevant capability, when, and under what institutional safeguards.

Glasswing therefore belongs in the “Documents that Define” category because it points to a broader future: frontier AI capabilities may increasingly be governed through selective access, staged disclosure, institutional screening, and trusted coalitions rather than open commercial circulation.

## Framework reading: how April maps onto the Geopolitics of AI model

The April evidence maps directly onto the site’s analytical framework.

### Upstream

The upstream layer includes critical minerals, rare earths, raw materials, and the supply chains through which they move. April was not primarily an upstream month, but upstream dependencies remain latent in the background. Every expansion in graphics processing units, high-bandwidth memory, data-centre hardware, grid infrastructure, and cooling systems increases pressure on material supply chains.

The upstream layer matters because it sets the outer boundary of industrial scale. The model layer may move quickly, but physical inputs remain slower, geographically concentrated, and politically exposed.

### Industrial

The industrial layer was April’s dominant layer. Compute thresholds, Nvidia H200 access, Huawei–DeepSeek alignment, TSMC packaging, SK Hynix memory, Taiwan export orders, the MATCH Act, and European hosting expansion all pointed to the same conclusion: AI power depends on industrial depth.

Industrialisation means more than having research talent. It means the ability to produce, assemble, package, supply, and maintain the hardware systems that make AI scale possible. It also means having the legal and institutional capacity to govern access to those systems.

### Operational

The operational layer was where April’s strategic meaning became clearest. Data-centre delays, electricity demand, Europe’s infrastructure gap, Microsoft’s Australia package, OpenAI’s cyber briefings, Anthropic’s Project Glasswing, and potential military AI procurement all showed that AI becomes power only when it can be deployed.

Operationalisation includes electricity, grid access, cooling, siting, permitting, cloud deployment, regulatory implementation, compliance systems, procurement, cybersecurity, and organisational capacity. This is where technical capability becomes usable national capability.

### Governance across layers

Governance is not a separate fourth layer. It cuts across all three.

In April, governance appeared as export control, anti-circumvention enforcement, model-access monitoring, cybersecurity disclosure, trusted-chip compliance, AI Continent industrial planning, trade strategy, investment screening, and controlled release.

This is why the AI race is not simply a technological race. It is a contest over the rules, institutions, infrastructures, and firms through which technological capability is converted into power.

## Conference watch: April widened the field

April’s conference coverage reinforced the same conclusion: AI geopolitics is no longer confined to frontier labs, chip policy, or Washington-Beijing competition. It is spreading into development cooperation, multilateral governance, sovereign AI, enterprise compliance, infrastructure standards, and full-stack deployment.

### CSIS Futures Summit 2026

The CSIS Futures Summit 2026 reframed AI as a development-power multiplier. The key shift was from AI as frontier risk to AI as institutional, economic, and infrastructural capacity. This matters because it places AI inside development cooperation, human security, trade, and global governance.

### UN Global Dialogue on AI Governance

The UN Global Dialogue on AI Governance matters because it tests whether the United Nations can become a credible venue for global AI governance rather than merely a symbolic forum. Its focus on interoperability, capacity gaps, human rights, trustworthy AI, and access makes it especially relevant for Global South positioning.

### Nvidia GTC 2026

Nvidia GTC 2026 mattered because it crowned the full-stack integration contest. The strategic story was not only hardware performance. It was the fusion of chips, networking, systems, software, cloud integration, and AI factories into a coherent infrastructure doctrine.

### AI for Good 2026

AI for Good 2026 signalled a multilateral shift toward sovereign AI and capacity building. It matters because Geneva is becoming a venue where AI governance, standards, development, energy, and access politics overlap.

### IAPP AI Governance Global Europe 2026

IAPP AI Governance Global Europe 2026 mattered because it showed the operationalisation of AI governance in Europe. The relevant themes were compliance, risk management, agentic AI, governance maturity, controls, and practical implementation inside organisations.

### UNITAR Brussels Forum

The UNITAR Brussels Forum connected European AI law with global governance. Its importance lies in the intersection between the European Union’s regulatory model and wider debates over international coordination, standards, and institutional capacity.

### India AI Impact Summit 2026

India AI Impact Summit 2026 remained important because India is one of the clearest middle-power examples of sovereign compute accumulation. Its official materials pointed to more than 38,000 provisioned graphics processing units and another 20,000 to be added under the IndiaAI Mission.

## What to watch next

### 1. Compute-threshold rules

Watch whether Washington formalises tighter rules based on actual compute capability rather than named chip models.

### 2. Model-access governance

Watch whether concerns about distillation, proxy accounts, jailbreaking, and model extraction lead to stricter controls on Application Programming Interface access, user verification, and frontier-model monitoring.

### 3. China’s alternative stack

Watch whether Huawei, DeepSeek, domestic cloud providers, and Chinese state procurement begin to form a coherent alternative AI infrastructure stack.

### 4. Europe’s capacity gap

Watch whether AI Continent, AI Factories, and European trade strategy produce real compute, power, procurement, and hosting capacity, or whether Europe remains a regulatory power dependent on foreign infrastructure.

### 5. Energy and data-centre constraints

Watch whether grid access, permitting, cooling, land, and local opposition begin to slow visible AI infrastructure expansion in the United States and Europe.

### 6. Memory and packaging

Watch whether high-bandwidth memory, advanced packaging, substrates, and thermal integration become the next major chokepoints after graphics processing units.

### 7. Frontier firms as security actors

Watch whether OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft, Nvidia, Google, Amazon, and other frontier or cloud actors become normalised as intermediaries between governments, cyber-defence institutions, cloud systems, and critical infrastructure.

### 8. Middle-power sovereign compute

Watch whether India’s graphics processing unit accumulation becomes a model for other middle powers seeking bargaining room without full technological decoupling.

### 9. Military operationalisation

Watch whether reported budgetary shifts toward AI-powered warfare become procurement, doctrine, and operational force design rather than signalling alone.

### 10. Hosted dependence

Watch whether Europe and other regions continue adding AI capacity through foreign hyperscalers without parallel tools for sovereign control.

## Closing assessment

April confirmed the central thesis of Geopolitics of AI: the AI race is increasingly decided by capability conversion.

The winners will not simply be those who invent the strongest models. They will be the states, firms, and alliances able to industrialise, host, power, secure, govern, and operationalise artificial intelligence at scale.

The chip war remains real. But it is no longer sufficient as a complete explanation.

The strategic contest has moved into the systems that make AI usable.

# AI drives race for billion-dollar infrastructure

Distributed by EIN Presswire

SãO PAULO, BRAZIL, April 20, 2026 /[EINPresswire.com](https://www.einpresswire.com/)/ — A robust physical foundation is required to train Artificial Intelligence systems, which could boost productivity by up to 2.3% per year in Latin America by addressing one of the region’s biggest economic bottlenecks, says José Maurício Caldeira

As Artificial Intelligence increasingly becomes part of the professional and personal routines of companies and individuals, tech giants are engaged in an intense race to come out on top. This year alone, Meta, Alphabet, Microsoft, and Amazon are expected to invest more than $600 billion in AI infrastructure—the physical and technological backbone needed to train generative AI models.

This includes high-performance chips, massive data centers, networks and data storage, as well as advanced cooling systems, since AI chips generate significant heat. “Investments are massive because no one wants to be left behind,” says José Maurício Caldeira, partner and board member at Colpar Brasil, a holding company operating in sectors such as agribusiness, industry, and urban development. “No one wants to risk falling behind in the leadership of this trillion-dollar market and miss the opportunity of a lifetime,” he adds.

The geopolitical dimension is also evident in the race for AI leadership. The United States and China— which shook the generative AI market, previously dominated by Americans, with the launch of DeepSeek—are competing for technological leadership. Both are seeking to strengthen their capabilities in strategic areas such as advanced chips, cloud computing, and algorithm research. For many analysts, AI is likely to become one of the main drivers of economic competitiveness and global influence in the coming decades.

Big Tech’s major bet is that the productivity gains enabled by Artificial Intelligence will offset these massive investments. However, the market fears the possibility of an AI bubble that could burst soon, similar to the dot-com bubble of the early 2000s and other innovations throughout history.

So far, the outlook appears positive. Moody’s, a risk rating agency that compiles data and information from companies and organizations across various sectors, estimates that AI could deliver average productivity gains of 1.5% per year over the next decade. This percentage will depend on factors such as workforce composition, demographics, technological maturity, unemployment levels, and labor costs, according to Moody’s.

According to consulting firm McKinsey, Artificial Intelligence holds significant potential in Latin America. In a study presented at the latest World Economic Forum in Davos, Switzerland, the firm showed that advancing AI adoption in the region could increase productivity by between 1.9% and 2.3% per year through 2030, and generate between $1.1 trillion and $1.7 trillion in additional annual economic value locally.

“The low productivity of Latin America’s economy, which has remained below the global average for years, has long been a barrier to strong and sustained growth in the region,” says José Maurício Caldeira. “We are facing an opportunity that requires coordinated efforts and investments to deliver results.”

According to McKinsey’s research, there is still much work ahead. Currently, only a quarter of Latin American organizations are generating any economic value from AI use, and only 6% report significant value creation.

The consultancy suggests that to accelerate this transition, companies must rethink both core processes and business models—not just seek tools that deliver incremental productivity gains. Sectors such as agriculture, mining, energy, and tourism, where Latin America already excels, could yield the greatest results, turning into lasting competitive advantages.

“Due to its transformative power, AI has the potential to be one of the most significant technological advances in recent history, with impacts spanning multiple economic sectors,” says José Maurício Caldeira. “We need an approach that includes physical and technological infrastructure, workforce upskilling, governance, and regional collaboration to fully leverage this moment of global transformation.”

# AI Is Now a Macro Variable. Are You Positioned?

Mar 9, 2026

AI is no longer a theme: It’s an industrial buildout, a key driver of GDP and a geopolitical football. With nearly $3 trillion in infrastructure spending still ahead, the question now is who monetizes it—and who gets disrupted.

## Why It Matters

* With ~$2.9 trillion in global data center construction projected through 2028, AI has become a structural force in economic expansion
* Although 21% of S&P 500 companies now cite AI benefits, adopters delivering measurable results are seeing cash flow margin expansion at roughly 2x the global average
* U.S.-China competition across chips, compute, energy, and data will elevate the strategic premium on secure domestic infrastructure

i

The Big Picture: AI is no longer a tech story—it is a macro variable influencing GDP, earnings, credit markets and geopolitics at industrial scale. With trillions in infrastructure still to be deployed, and monetization now separating winners from laggards, understanding where value accrues is critical for investors and corporates.

Recent events in the Middle East remind us that geopolitics are intertwined with financial decision-making in evolving ways: National security, energy, supply chains and technology are increasingly interrelated. In this environment, artificial intelligence is no longer just a disruption theme. It’s emerging as a strategic asset — central to economic competitiveness, military capability and projections for energy needs. In other words, a fast-moving innovation cycle on a global and historical scale. As such, AI is a central force shaping both risk and reward in the macro and markets outlook for 2026.

Morgan Stanley Research estimates that nearly $3 trillion of AI-related infrastructure investment will flow through the global economy by 2028, with more than 80% of that spending still ahead. At the same time, adoption is shifting, with fewer pilots and greater tangible productivity solutions. That should lift GDP, earnings and capital markets activity.

But, as has become evident in recent weeks, the AI trend is also large enough to trigger valuation resets and sector rotation, as the world weighs the potential benefits and disruption to workers and existing industry. The geopolitical competition for AI leadership adds another layer of risk complexity.

Amidst this uncertainty and opportunity, here’s what decision-makers need to know.

**1) The AI build-out is real and is powering growth this year:**

AI-related investment now looks more like **industrial build-out** than speculative tech spending. Morgan Stanley Research [estimates **~$2.9 trillion in global data center construction cost alone through 2028**](/content/dam/msdotcom/en/assets/pdfs/Research_Bridging-Data-Center-Gap.pdf), fueled by sustained demand for compute that vastly exceeds supply. This feeds directly into industrial output, power investment and services spend—providing real macro support with an expected contribution of ~25% of U.S. GDP growth this year.

**2) Earnings leverage from AI is coming into view, elevating the value of monetization over mentions:**

According to Morgan Stanley Research’s [most recent mapping of 3,600 stocks for AI exposure](/content/dam/msdotcom/en/assets/pdfs/Research_AI-Rate-of-Change.pdf), 21% of S&P 500 companies mentioned at least one AI benefit (up from 10% in 2024). The catch: the market isn’t paying for “AI mentions” alone.

However, AI adopters are seeing results, with cash-flow margin expansion outpacing the global average by 2x. Morgan Stanley Investment Management found that [second-order AI beneficiaries show similar efficiency gains and margin expansion](/content/dam/msdotcom/en/assets/pdfs/IM_AI-Beneficiaries.pdf).

Markets are paying for evidence that adopters can monetize—and punishing uncertainty. That’s why Morgan Stanley Research flags the recent drawdown in software sector stock prices as a “peak uncertainty” moment, with group enterprise value/sales back near levels last seen during prior disruption scares.

**3) AI financing is reshaping markets—and rewarding discipline:**

AI’s scale means **balance sheets matter again**. As AI capex rises, Morgan Stanley Research expects **debt financing** to follow, especially for infrastructure-heavy projects. But markets are adapting. Issuers are increasingly diversifying their sources of capital. The full spectrum of credit markets—secured, unsecured, structured and securitized across both public and private realms—now play a role in financing AI-related infrastructure. This dynamic was on display in 2025, when Morgan Stanley advised Meta on the $27 billion structured JV for the U.S. AI data-center campus.

**4) AI is accelerating M&A and capital reallocation:**

Competitive pressure to leverage the benefits of AI is pulling forward strategic decisions. Morgan Stanley’s Investment Banking team notes that **[AI is increasingly driving M&A](/content/dam/msdotcom/en/assets/pdfs/IB_MA-2026-Outlook.pdf) as firms seek expertise and client base/market penetration.**

This acceleration isn’t limited to corporates. Demand from individuals and family offices to participate directly in the AI build-out has also been strong. Morgan Stanley has originated investment opportunities in private AI companies and data center assets—14 in 2025 alone — through its Wealth Management Private Markets platform.

**5) Risks are real—but they shape opportunity:**

AI’s upside is big—but markets are stress-testing who benefits, who gets disrupted and global strains along the way.

**[Debate on the nature of business model disruption](/content/dam/msdotcom/en/assets/pdfs/WM_Look-Back-Leap-Forward.pdf) will keep shifting investor preferences.** History suggests disruption cycles are volatile, not linear. In prior cycles, stocks perceived as disrupted experienced sharp drawdowns and rallies. The recent selloff in software and other services perceived as at-risk is emblematic.

**Geopolitical overreach is the macro overlay.** As the U.S. and China compete for AI leadership—across chips, compute, energy and data—tighter export controls, higher tariffs and localization pressures could fragment supply chains and raise costs. Those are risks to global growth even as they accelerate domestic buildout.

**Labor disruption adds another layer.** AI may impact the demand for existing work even as it creates new roles and productivity gains.

Each risk creates a response lever.

* The risk of AI-driven business model disruption drives faster AI adoption and **innovation, with a focus on assets that have enduring value in a world of more powerful AI capabilities.**
* The impact of geopolitics increases the value of **secure, domestic infrastructure**.
* Labor disruption increases returns for firms that **redeploy workers into higher-value roles**.

## Takeaways

1. ![](/content/dam/msdotcom/Insights/institute/icon-investor.png)

   For Investors:

   “GenAI ups the value of active portfolio positioning. Achieving portfolio diversification is increasingly difficult, given how correlated so many sector themes are to the scale and scope of the data center infrastructure build-out; but it is more necessary than ever, given how quickly things are changing. Don’t just chase broad tech exposure. Differentiate true AI winners.”

   *- Lisa Shalett, Chief Investment Officer for Wealth Management*

   “History shows that in major technology waves, equity value accrues not only to the technology suppliers, but to the companies that apply the technology most effectively. We believe investors should widen the aperture on AI returns - from AI services revenue to the broader operating leverage from AI-enabled productivity gains.”

   - *Thomas Kamei, Counterpoint Global, Morgan Stanley Investment Management*

   “Our recommended strategy for 2026: (1) focus on beneficiaries as nations pursue self-sufficiency in energy, critical materials, manufacturing capacity, and AI capabilities; (2) invest in AI infrastructure, given accelerating AI capabilities and the massive excess demand for compute relative to supply; (3) own AI adopters with pricing power, given the market fails to appreciate that the non-linear increase in AI capabilities magnifies adoption benefits; and (4) be positioned, both on offense and defense, for AI-driven disruptions including labor dislocation and life sciences advances.”

   *- Stephen Byrd, Head of Global Thematic Research, Morgan Stanley Research*

   “In fixed income, look for opportunities in structured credit and asset-backed financing tied to contracted AI infrastructure. In unsecured investment-grade credit, consider diversification benefits from highly rated, cash-rich hyperscalers.”

   *- Vishwanath Tirupattur, Chief Fixed-Income Strategist, Morgan Stanley Research*
2. ![](/content/dam/msdotcom/Insights/institute/icon-corporation.png)

   For Companies:

   “AI can move the needle both through adopting new tools and rethinking the art of the possible— so prioritize attacking the white space, not just the cost savings. I don’t believe anyone knows the ultimate answer, so do not wait around for the perfect use case to try something in AI. It’s clear some of the tools are about efficiency gains, but we’re also seeing an embrace of out-of-the-box thinking.”

   *– Simon Smith, Global Co-Head of Investment Banking*

   “Boards must help executives think through how to capitalize on AI’s potential while evaluating emerging risks such as data security and cyber exposure, model error, and bias. It’s also important to guide management on the right pace of AI adoption, while maintaining capital discipline with a focus on measurable ROI. This includes evaluating companies’ investments across data, talent, infrastructure, and build‑vs‑buy decisions.”

   *– Melissa James, Vice Chairman, Global Capital Markets*
