Speed of light

The **speed of light in vacuum**, often called simply the **speed of light** and commonly denoted **c**, is a universal [physical constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_constant "Physical constant") exactly equal to 299792458m⋅s−1.[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-physconst-c-2) It is exact because, by international agreement, a [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") is defined as the length of the path travelled by [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") in [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum") during a time interval of 1⁄299792458 [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second"). The value 299,792,458 metres per second is approximately 1billion kilometres per hour; 700million miles per hour. For other approximations of **c** valid for various units and size scales see the infobox.

All forms of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"), including [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light"), travel in vacuum at the speed _c_ as do [massless particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massless_particle "Massless particle") and [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)") perturbations, such as [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave"). The speed of light is [the same for all observers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(physics) "Invariant (physics)"), no matter their relative velocity. As a result, massless particles and waves travel at c in a vacuum regardless of the motion of the source or the inertial reference frame of the [observer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer_(special_relativity) "Observer (special relativity)"). The speed of light is the upper limit for the [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") at which [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information#Physics_and_determinacy "Information"), [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"), or [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") can travel through [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space#Relativity "Space").[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-6)[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-7)[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-8) Particles with nonzero [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") can be accelerated to approach c but can never reach it, regardless of the frame of reference in which their speed is measured.

For long distances and sensitive measurements, the finite speed of light has noticeable effects. Much [starlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlight "Starlight") viewed on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") is from the distant past, allowing humans to study the history of the universe by viewing distant objects. When [communicating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_communication "Data communication") with distant [space probes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_probe "Space probe"), it can take hours for signals to travel. In [computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing "Computing"), the speed of light fixes the ultimate minimum [communication delay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latency_(engineering) "Latency (engineering)"). The speed of light can be used in [time of flight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_of_flight "Time of flight") measurements to measure large distances to extremely high precision.

[Ole Rømer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer "Ole Rømer") first [demonstrated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%B8mer's_determination_of_the_speed_of_light "Rømer's determination of the speed of light") that light does not travel instantaneously by studying the apparent motion of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s moon [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon) "Io (moon)"). In an 1865 [paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Dynamical_Theory_of_the_Electromagnetic_Field "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field"), [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") proposed that light was an [electromagnetic wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") and, therefore, travelled at speed c.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-9) [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") postulated that the speed of light c with respect to any [inertial frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference") is a constant and is independent of the motion of the light source.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-stachel-10) He explored the consequences of that postulate by deriving the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"), and so showed that the parameter c had relevance outside of the context of light and [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"). In the theory of relativity, c interrelates [space and time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") and appears in the famous [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"), E = mc2.[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-11)

In some cases, objects or waves may appear to travel [faster than light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#Faster-than-light_observations_and_experiments). The [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") is understood to exceed the speed of light beyond [a certain boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_volume "Hubble volume").

The speed at which light propagates through [transparent materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency "Transparency and translucency"), such as glass or air, is less than c; similarly, the speed of [electromagnetic waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_waves "Electromagnetic waves") around wire cables (the [speed of electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_electricity "Speed of electricity")) is slower than c. The ratio between c and the speed v at which light travels in a material is called the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") n of the material (n = ⁠c/v⁠). For example, for visible light, the refractive index of glass is typically around 1.5, meaning that light in glass travels at ⁠c/1.5⁠ ≈ 200000km/s (124000mi/s); the [refractive index of air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index_of_air "Refractive index of air") for visible light is about 1.0003, so the speed of light in air is about 90km/s (56mi/s) slower than c.

## Notation

The speed of light in vacuum is usually denoted by a lowercase _c_. The origin of the letter choice is unclear, with guesses including "c" for "constant" or the Latin _[celeritas](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeritas "wikt:celeritas")_ (meaning 'swiftness, celerity').[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Mendelson-2006-12) The "c" was used for "celerity" meaning a velocity in books by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") and others, but this velocity was not specifically for light; [Isaac Asimov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Asimov "Isaac Asimov") wrote a popular science article, "C for Celeritas", but did not explain the origin.[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-13) In 1856, [Wilhelm Eduard Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Eduard_Weber "Wilhelm Eduard Weber") and [Rudolf Kohlrausch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Kohlrausch "Rudolf Kohlrausch") had used _c_ for a different constant that was later shown to equal √2 times the speed of light in vacuum. Historically, the symbol V was used as an alternative symbol for the speed of light, introduced by [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") in 1865. In 1903, [Max Abraham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Abraham "Max Abraham") used _c_ with its modern meaning in a widely read textbook on electromagnetism. [Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") used V in his [original German-language papers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annus_Mirabilis_papers "Annus Mirabilis papers") on special relativity in 1905, but in 1907 he switched to _c_, which by then had become the standard symbol for the speed of light.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Yc-14)[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Mendelson-2006-12)

Sometimes _c_ is used for the speed of waves in any material medium, and _c_0 for the speed of light in vacuum.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-handbook-15) This subscripted notation, which is endorsed in official SI literature,[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-BIPM_SI_units-16) has the same form as related electromagnetic constants: namely, _μ_ 0 for the [vacuum permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permeability "Vacuum permeability") or magnetic constant, _ε_ 0 for the [vacuum permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permittivity "Vacuum permittivity") or electric constant, and _Z_ 0 for the [impedance of free space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impedance_of_free_space "Impedance of free space"). This article uses _c_ exclusively for the speed of light in vacuum.

## In unit systems

Further information: [Metre §Speed of light definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre#Speed_of_light_definition "Metre")

Since 1983, the constant _c_ has been defined in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI) as _exactly_ 299792458m/s; this relationship is used to define the metre as exactly the distance that light travels in vacuum in 1⁄299792458 of a second. The second is, in turn, defined to be the length of time occupied by 9192631770cycles of the radiation emitted by a [caesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium "Caesium")-133 [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") in a transition between two specified [energy states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level").[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-nist-definitions-17) By using the value of _c_, as well as an accurate measurement of the second, one can establish a standard for the metre.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-fixes-18) The particular value chosen for the speed of light provided a more accurate definition of the metre that still agreed as much as possible with the definition used before 1983.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-nist-definitions-17)[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-penrose-19)

As a [dimensional physical constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_constant#Dimensional_and_dimensionless_physical_constants "Physical constant"), the numerical value of _c_ is different for different unit systems. For example, in [imperial units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units"), the speed of light is approximately 186282 miles per second. This value is less than 2% different from 1 billion feet per second or one [foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foot_(unit) "Foot (unit)") per nanosecond.[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-20) Naval officer and computer scientist [Grace Murray Hopper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Hopper "Grace Hopper") distributed foot-long wires to colleagues in the late 1960s to visually illustrate the importance of designing smaller components to increase computing speed.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-21)

In branches of physics in which _c_ appears often, such as in relativity, it is common to use systems of [natural units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_units "Natural units") of measurement or the [geometrized unit system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrized_unit_system "Geometrized unit system") where _c_ = 1.[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Lawrie-22)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hsu1-23) Using these units, _c_ does not appear explicitly because multiplication or division by1 does not affect the result. Its unit of [light-second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-second "Light-second") per second is still relevant, even if omitted.

## Fundamental role in physics

See also: [Special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity")

The speed at which light waves propagate in vacuum is independent both of the motion of the wave source and of the [inertial frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference") of the observer. This invariance of the speed of light was postulated by Einstein in 1905,[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-stachel-10) after being motivated by [Maxwell's theory of electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell's_theory_of_electromagnetism "Maxwell's theory of electromagnetism") and the lack of evidence for motion against the [luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether").[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-24) Experiments such as the [Kennedy–Thorndike experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kennedy%E2%80%93Thorndike_experiment "Kennedy–Thorndike experiment") and the [Ives–Stilwell experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ives%E2%80%93Stilwell_experiment "Ives–Stilwell experiment") have shown this postulate to match experimental observations.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-25)

The [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity "Special theory of relativity") explores the consequences of this invariance of c with the assumption that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference.[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-26)[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-27) One consequence is that c is the speed at which all massless particles and waves, including light, must travel in vacuum.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-:0-28)

[![γ starts at 1 when v equals zero and stays nearly constant for small v, then it sharply curves upwards and has a vertical asymptote, diverging to positive infinity as v approaches c.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Lorentz_factor.svg/500px-Lorentz_factor.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lorentz_factor.svg) The [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor") γ as a function of velocity. It starts at1 and approaches infinity as v approachesc.

Special relativity has many counterintuitive and experimentally verified implications.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-29) These include the [equivalence of mass and energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence") (E = mc2), [length contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_contraction "Length contraction") (moving objects shorten), [Terrell rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrell_rotation "Terrell rotation") (apparent rotation),[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-30)[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-31) and [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation") (moving clocks run more slowly). The factorγ by which lengths contract and times dilate is known as the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor") and is given by γ = (1 − v2/c2)−1/2, where v is the speed of the object. The difference of γ from1 is negligible for speeds much slower thanc, such as most everyday speeds–in which case special relativity is closely approximated by [Galilean relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_relativity "Galilean relativity")–but it increases at relativistic speeds and diverges to infinity as v approaches c. For example, a time dilation factor of γ = 2 occurs at a relative velocity of 86.6% of the speed of light (v = 0.866c). Similarly, a time dilation factor of γ = 10 occurs at 99.5% the speed of light (v = 0.995c).

The results of special relativity can be summarized by treating space and time as a unified structure known as [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") (withc relating the units of space and time), and requiring that physical theories satisfy a special [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_in_physics "Symmetry in physics") called [Lorentz invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_invariance "Lorentz invariance"), whose mathematical formulation contains the parameterc.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-32) Lorentz invariance is an almost universal assumption for modern physical theories, such as [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics"), [quantum chromodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics"), the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), and [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). As such, the parameterc is ubiquitous in modern physics, appearing in many contexts that are unrelated to light. For example, general relativity predicts thatc is also the [speed of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_gravity "Speed of gravity") and of [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves"),[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hartle-33) and observations of gravitational waves have been consistent with this prediction.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-34) In [non-inertial frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-inertial_frame "Non-inertial frame") of reference (gravitationally curved spacetime or [accelerated reference frames](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerated_reference_frame "Accelerated reference frame")), the _local_ speed of light is constant and equal toc, but the speed of light can differ fromc when measured from a remote frame of reference, depending on how measurements are extrapolated to the region.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Gibbs1997-35)

It is generally assumed that fundamental constants such asc have the same value throughout spacetime, meaning that they do not depend on location and do not vary with time. However, it has been suggested in various theories that the [speed of light may have changed over time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_speed_of_light "Variable speed of light").[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Ellis_Uzan-36)[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Mota-37) No conclusive evidence for such changes has been found, but they remain the subject of ongoing research.[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Uzan-38)[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Camelia-39)

It is generally assumed that the two-way speed of light is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy"), meaning that it has the same value regardless of the direction in which it is measured. Observations of the emissions from nuclear [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") as a function of the orientation of the emitting [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") in a magnetic field (see [Hughes–Drever experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hughes%E2%80%93Drever_experiment "Hughes–Drever experiment")), and of rotating [optical resonators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_resonator "Optical resonator") (see [Resonator experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment#Recent_experiments "Michelson–Morley experiment")) have put stringent limits on the possible two-way [anisotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy").[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Herrmann-40)[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Lang-41)

### Upper limit on speeds

An object with [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") m and speed v relative to a laboratory has [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy")(γ − 1)mc2 with respect to that lab, where γ is the Lorentz factor defined above. The γ factor approaches infinity as v approachesc, and it would take an infinite amount of energy to accelerate an object with mass to the speed of light.[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-42):13.3 The speed of light is the upper limit for the speeds of objects with positive rest mass. Analysis of individual photons confirm that information cannot travel faster than the speed of light.[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-43)[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-44) This is experimentally established in many [tests of relativistic energy and momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_relativistic_energy_and_momentum "Tests of relativistic energy and momentum").[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-45)

[![Three pairs of coordinate axes are depicted with the same origin A; in the green frame, the x-axis is horizontal and the ct-axis is vertical; in the red frame, the x′-axis is slightly skewed upwards, and the ct′-axis slightly skewed rightwards, relative to the green axes; in the blue frame, the x″-axis is somewhat skewed downwards, and the ct″-axis somewhat skewed leftwards, relative to the green axes. A point B on the green x-axis, to the left of A, has zero ct, positive ct′, and negative ct″.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Relativity_of_Simultaneity.svg/500px-Relativity_of_Simultaneity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relativity_of_Simultaneity.svg) EventA precedesB in the red frame, is simultaneous withB in the green frame, and followsB in the blue frame.

More generally, it is impossible for signals or energy to travel faster thanc. One argument for this is known as [causality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality_(physics) "Causality (physics)"). If the spatial distance between two eventsA andB is greater than the time interval between them multiplied byc then there are frames of reference in whichA precedesB, others in whichB precedesA, and others in which they are simultaneous. As a result, if something were travelling faster thanc relative to an inertial frame of reference, it would be travelling backwards in time relative to another frame, and causality would be violated.[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-46):497[\[45\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-47)[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Taylor_p74-48) In such a frame of reference, an "effect" could be observed before its "cause". Such a violation of causality has never been recorded,[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Zhang-49) and would lead to [paradoxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradox "Paradox") such as the [tachyonic antitelephone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tachyonic_antitelephone "Tachyonic antitelephone").[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-50)

In some theoretical treatments, the [Scharnhorst effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scharnhorst_effect "Scharnhorst effect") allows signals to travel faster thanc, by one part in 1036.[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-51) However other approaches to the same physical set up show no such effect.[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-52) and it appears the special conditions in which this effect might occur would prevent one from using it to violate causality.

### One-way speed of light

Main article: [One-way speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-way_speed_of_light "One-way speed of light")

It is only possible to verify experimentally that the two-way speed of light (for example, from a source to a mirror and back again) is frame-independent, because it is impossible to measure the [one-way speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-way_speed_of_light "One-way speed of light") (for example, from a source to a distant detector) without some convention as to how clocks at the source and at the detector should be synchronized. By adopting [Einstein synchronization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_synchronization "Einstein synchronization") for the clocks, the one-way speed of light becomes equal to the two-way speed of light by definition.[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hsu2-53)[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Zhang-49)

## Faster-than-light observations and experiments

See also: [Faster-than-light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faster-than-light "Faster-than-light") and [Superluminal motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superluminal_motion "Superluminal motion")

There are situations in which it may seem that matter, energy, or information-carrying signal travels at speeds greater thanc, but they do not. For example, as is discussed in the [propagation of light in a medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#In_a_medium) section below, many wave velocities can exceedc. The [phase velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_velocity "Phase velocity") of [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") through most glasses can routinely exceed c,[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-54) but phase velocity does not determine the velocity at which waves convey information.[\[53\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-55)

If a laser beam is swept quickly across a distant object, the spot of light can move faster thanc, although the initial movement of the spot is delayed because of the time it takes light to get to the distant object at the speedc. However, the only physical entities that are moving are the laser and its emitted light, which travels at the speedc from the laser to the various positions of the spot. Similarly, a shadow projected onto a distant object can be made to move faster thanc, after a delay in time.[\[54\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-56) In neither case does any matter, energy, or information travel faster than light.[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Gibbs-57)

The rate of change in the distance between two objects in a frame of reference with respect to which both are moving (their [closing speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faster-than-light#Closing_speeds "Faster-than-light")) may have a value in excess ofc. However, this does not represent the speed of any single object as measured in a single inertial frame.[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Gibbs-57)

Certain quantum effects appear to be transmitted instantaneously and therefore faster than c, as in the [EPR paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Podolsky%E2%80%93Rosen_paradox "Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen paradox"). An example involves the [quantum states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") of two particles that can be [entangled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement"). Until either of the particles is observed, they exist in a [superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition "Quantum superposition") of two quantum states. If the particles are separated and one particle's quantum state is observed, the other particle's quantum state is determined instantaneously. However, it is impossible to control which quantum state the first particle will take on when it is observed, so information cannot be transmitted in this manner.[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Gibbs-57)[\[56\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-58)

Another quantum effect that predicts the occurrence of faster-than-light speeds is called the [Hartman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hartman_effect "Hartman effect"): under certain conditions the time needed for a [virtual particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_particle "Virtual particle") to [tunnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_tunnelling "Quantum tunnelling") through a barrier is constant, regardless of the thickness of the barrier.[\[57\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Muga-59)[\[58\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Recami-60) This could result in a virtual particle crossing a large gap faster than light. However, no information can be sent using this effect.[\[59\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Wynne-61)

So-called [superluminal motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superluminal_motion "Superluminal motion") is seen in certain astronomical objects,[\[60\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-62) such as the [relativistic jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_jet "Relativistic jet") of [radio galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_galaxy "Radio galaxy") and [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"). However, these jets are not moving at speeds in excess of the speed of light: the apparent superluminal motion is a [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphical_projection "Graphical projection") effect caused by objects moving near the speed of light and approaching Earth at a small angle to the line of sight: since the light which was emitted when the jet was farther away took longer to reach the Earth, the time between two successive observations corresponds to a longer time between the instants at which the light rays were emitted.[\[61\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-63)

A 2011 experiment where [neutrinos were observed to travel faster than light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faster-than-light_neutrino_anomaly "Faster-than-light neutrino anomaly") turned out to be due to experimental error.[\[62\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-nature1204-64)[\[63\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-65)

In models of the [expanding universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe"), the farther galaxies are from each other, the faster they drift apart. For example, galaxies far away from Earth are inferred to be moving away from the Earth with speeds proportional to their distances. Beyond a boundary called the [Hubble sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_sphere "Hubble sphere"), the rate at which their distance from Earth increases becomes greater than the speed of light.[\[64\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Harrison-66) These recession rates, defined as the increase in [proper distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") per [cosmological time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_time "Cosmological time"), are not velocities in a relativistic sense. Faster-than-light cosmological recession speeds are only a [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_conditions "Coordinate conditions") artifact.

## Propagation of light

In [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics"), light is described as a type of [electromagnetic wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_wave "Electromagnetic wave"). The classical behaviour of the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") is described by [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell's_equations "Maxwell's equations"), which predict that the speedc with which electromagnetic waves (such as light) propagate in vacuum is related to the distributed capacitance and inductance of vacuum, otherwise respectively known as the [electric constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_constant "Electric constant") _ε_ 0 and the [magnetic constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_constant "Magnetic constant") _μ_ 0, by the equation[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-67)c=1ε0μ0.{\\displaystyle c={\\frac {1}{\\sqrt {\\varepsilon \_{0}\\mu \_{0}}}}.}![{\displaystyle c={\frac {1}{\sqrt {\varepsilon _{0}\mu _{0}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b468f8919e0ee4549d40346eeda5bef3761f0bfc)

In modern [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics"), the electromagnetic field is described by the theory of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") (QED). In this theory, light is described by the fundamental excitations (or quanta) of the electromagnetic field, called [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"). In QED, photons are [massless particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massless_particle "Massless particle") and thus, according to special relativity, they travel at the speed of light in vacuum.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-:0-28)

Extensions of QED in which the photon has a mass have been considered. In such a theory, its speed would depend on its frequency, and the invariant speedc of special relativity would then be the upper limit of the speed of light in vacuum.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Gibbs1997-35) No variation of the speed of light with frequency has been observed in rigorous testing, putting stringent limits on the mass of the photon.[\[66\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-68) The limit obtained depends on the model used: if the massive photon is described by [Proca theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proca_action "Proca action"),[\[67\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-adelberger-69) the experimental upper bound for its mass is about ×10−57 [grams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram "Gram");[\[68\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Sidharth-70) if photon mass is generated by a [Higgs mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_mechanism "Higgs mechanism"), the experimental upper limit is less sharp, m ≤ 10−14 [eV/ _c_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass "Electronvolt") (roughly 2×10−47g).[\[67\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-adelberger-69)

Another reason for the speed of light to vary with its frequency would be the failure of special relativity to apply to arbitrarily small scales, as predicted by some proposed theories of [quantum gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_gravity "Quantum gravity"). In 2009, the observation of [gamma-ray burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") [GRB 090510](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GRB_090510?action=edit&redlink=1 "GRB 090510") found no evidence for a dependence of photon speed on energy, supporting tight constraints in specific models of spacetime quantization on how this speed is affected by photon energy for energies approaching the [Planck scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_scale "Planck scale").[\[69\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-71)

### In a medium

See also: [Refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index")

In a medium, light usually does not propagate at a speed equal to c; further, different types of light wave will travel at different speeds. The speed at which the individual crests and troughs of a [plane wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_wave "Plane wave") (a wave filling the whole space, with only one [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency")) propagate is called the [phase velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_velocity "Phase velocity") _v_ p. A physical signal with a finite extent (a pulse of light) travels at a different speed. The overall [envelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Envelope_(waves) "Envelope (waves)") of the pulse travels at the [group velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_velocity "Group velocity") _v_ g, and its earliest part travels at the [front velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Front_velocity "Front velocity") _v_ f.[\[70\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Milonni-72)

[![A modulated wave moves from left to right. There are three points marked with a dot: A blue dot at a node of the carrier wave, a green dot at the maximum of the envelope, and a red dot at the front of the envelope.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Frontgroupphase.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Frontgroupphase.gif) The blue dot moves at the speed of the ripples, the phase velocity; the green dot moves with the speed of the envelope, the group velocity; and the red dot moves with the speed of the foremost part of the pulse, the front velocity.

The phase velocity is important in determining how a light wave travels through a material or from one material to another. It is often represented in terms of a _refractive index_. The refractive index of a material is defined as the ratio of c to the phase velocity_v_ p in the material: larger indices of refraction indicate lower speeds. The refractive index of a material may depend on the light's frequency, intensity, [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)"), or direction of propagation; in many cases, though, it can be treated as a material-dependent constant. The [refractive index of air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index_of_air "Refractive index of air") is approximately 1.0003.[\[71\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Podesta-73) Denser media, such as [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_properties_of_water_and_ice "Optical properties of water and ice"),[\[72\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-74) [glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glass "Glass"),[\[73\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-75) and [diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_properties_of_diamond#Optical_properties "Material properties of diamond"),[\[74\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-76) have refractive indexes of around 1.3, 1.5 and 2.4, respectively, for visible light.

In exotic materials like [Bose–Einstein condensates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate "Bose–Einstein condensate") near [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero"), the effective speed of light may be only a few metres per second. However, this represents absorption and re-radiation delay between atoms, as do all slower-than-c speeds in material substances. As an extreme example of light "slowing" in matter, two independent teams of physicists claimed to bring light to a "complete standstill" by passing it through a Bose–Einstein condensate of the element [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium"). The popular description of light being "stopped" in these experiments refers only to light being stored in the excited states of atoms, then re-emitted at an arbitrarily later time, as stimulated by a second laser pulse. During the time it had "stopped", it had ceased to be light. This type of behaviour is generally microscopically true of all transparent media which "slow" the speed of light.[\[75\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-77)

In transparent materials, the refractive index generally is greater than 1, meaning that the phase velocity is less than c. In other materials, it is possible for the refractive index to become smaller than1 for some frequencies; in some exotic materials it is even possible for the index of refraction to become negative.[\[76\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-78) The requirement that causality is not violated implies that the [real and imaginary parts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_and_imaginary_parts "Real and imaginary parts") of the [dielectric constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric_constant "Dielectric constant") of any material, corresponding respectively to the index of refraction and to the [attenuation coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attenuation_coefficient "Attenuation coefficient"), are linked by the [Kramers–Kronig relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kramers%E2%80%93Kronig_relation "Kramers–Kronig relation").[\[77\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-79)[\[78\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-80) In practical terms, this means that in a material with refractive index less than 1, the wave will be absorbed quickly.[\[79\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-81)

A pulse with different group and phase velocities (which occurs if the phase velocity is not the same for all the frequencies of the pulse) smears out over time, a process known as [dispersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_(optics) "Dispersion (optics)"). Certain materials have an exceptionally low (or even zero) group velocity for light waves, a phenomenon called [slow light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slow_light "Slow light").[\[80\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-82)
The opposite, group velocities exceeding c, was proposed theoretically in 1993 and achieved experimentally in 2000.[\[81\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-83) It should even be possible for the group velocity to become infinite or negative, with pulses travelling instantaneously or backwards in time.[\[70\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Milonni-72)

None of these options allow information to be transmitted faster than c. It is impossible to transmit information with a light pulse any faster than the speed of the earliest part of the pulse (the front velocity). It can be shown that this is (under certain assumptions) always equal to c.[\[70\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Milonni-72)

It is possible for a particle to travel through a medium faster than the phase velocity of light in that medium (but still slower than _c_). When a [charged particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle") does that in a [dielectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric "Dielectric") material, the electromagnetic equivalent of a [shock wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shock_wave "Shock wave"), known as [Cherenkov radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherenkov_radiation "Cherenkov radiation"), is emitted.[\[82\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-84)

## Practical effects of finiteness

The speed of light is of relevance to [telecommunications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications "Telecommunications"): the one-way and [round-trip delay time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Round-trip_delay_time "Round-trip delay time") are greater than zero. This applies from small to astronomical scales. On the other hand, some techniques depend on the finite speed of light, for example in distance measurements.

### Small scales

In [computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer "Computer"), the speed of light imposes a limit on how quickly data can be sent between [processors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_processing_unit "Central processing unit"). If a processor operates at 1 [gigahertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigahertz "Gigahertz"), a signal can travel only a maximum of about 30 centimetres (1ft) in a single clock cycle– in practice, this distance is even shorter since the [printed circuit board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printed_circuit_board "Printed circuit board") refracts and slows down signals. Processors must therefore be placed close to each other, as well as [memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory "Computer memory") chips, to minimize communication latencies, and care must be exercised when routing wires between them to ensure [signal integrity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_integrity "Signal integrity"). If clock frequencies continue to increase, the speed of light may eventually become a limiting factor for the internal design of single [chips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit").[\[83\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-processorlimit-85)[\[84\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-processorlimit2-86)

### Large distances on Earth

Acoustic representation of the speed of light: in the period between beeps, light travels the circumference of Earth at the equator.

Given that the equatorial circumference of the Earth is about 40075km and that c is about 300000km/s, the theoretical shortest time for a piece of information to travel half the globe along the surface is about 67 milliseconds. When light is traveling in [optical fibre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_fibre "Optical fibre") (a [transparent material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency "Transparency and translucency")) the actual transit time is longer, in part because the speed of light is slower by about 35% in optical fibre with an refractive index n around 1.52.[\[85\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Midwinter-87) Straight lines are rare in global communications and the travel time increases when signals pass through electronic switches or signal regenerators.[\[86\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-88)

Although this distance is largely irrelevant for most applications, latency becomes important in fields such as [high-frequency trading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-frequency_trading "High-frequency trading"), where traders seek to gain minute advantages by delivering their trades to exchanges fractions of a second ahead of other traders. For example, traders have been switching to [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") communications between trading hubs, because of the advantage which radio waves travelling at near to the speed of light through air have over comparatively slower [fibre optic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibre_optic "Fibre optic") signals.[\[87\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-89)[\[88\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-90)

### Spaceflight and astronomy

[![The diameter of the moon is about one quarter of that of Earth, and their distance is about thirty times the diameter of Earth. A beam of light starts from the Earth and reaches the Moon in about a second and a quarter.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9d/Earth_and_Moon_speed_of_light_by_James_O%27Donoghue.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Earth_and_Moon_speed_of_light_by_James_O'Donoghue.gif) A beam of light is depicted travelling between the Earth and the Moon in the time it takes a light pulse to move between them: 1.255 seconds at their mean orbital (surface-to-surface) distance. The relative sizes and separation of the Earth–Moon system are shown to scale.

Similarly, communications between the Earth and spacecraft are not instantaneous. There is a brief delay from the source to the receiver, which becomes more noticeable as distances increase. This delay was significant for communications between [ground control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mission_Control_Center "Mission Control Center") and [Apollo 8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_8 "Apollo 8") when it became the first crewed spacecraft to orbit the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"): for every question, the ground control station had to wait at least threeseconds for the answer to arrive.[\[89\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-91)

The communications delay between Earth and [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") can vary between five and twenty minutes depending upon the relative positions of the two planets. As a consequence of this, if a robot on the surface of Mars were to encounter a problem, its human controllers would not be aware of it until approximately 4–24 minutes later. It would then take a further 4–24 minutes for commands to travel from Earth to Mars.[\[90\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-92)[\[91\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-93)

Receiving light and other signals from distant astronomical sources takes much longer. For example, it takes 13billion (13×109) years for light to travel to Earth from the faraway galaxies viewed in the [Hubble Ultra-Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Ultra-Deep_Field "Hubble Ultra-Deep Field") images.[\[92\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hubble-94)[\[93\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-95) Those photographs, taken today, capture images of the galaxies as they appeared 13billion years ago, when the universe was less than a billion years old.[\[92\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hubble-94) The fact that more distant objects appear to be younger, due to the finite speed of light, allows astronomers to infer the [evolution of stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution_of_stars "Evolution of stars"), [of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution"), and [of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_universe "History of the universe") itself.[\[94\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-96)

Astronomical distances are sometimes expressed in [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year"), especially in [popular science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_science "Popular science") publications and media.[\[95\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-97) A light-year is the distance light travels in one [Julian year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_year_(astronomy) "Julian year (astronomy)"), around 9461billion kilometres, 5879billion miles, or 0.3066 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec"). In round figures, a light year is nearly 10trillion kilometres or nearly 6trillion miles. [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri"), the closest star to Earth after the Sun, is around 4.2 light-years away.[\[96\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-starchild-98)

### Distance measurement

Main article: [Distance measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_measurement "Distance measurement")

[Radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar "Radar") systems measure the distance to a target by the time it takes a radio-wave pulse to return to the radar antenna after being reflected by the target: the distance to the target is half the round-trip [transit time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar#Transit_time "Radar") multiplied by the speed of light. A [Global Positioning System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Positioning_System "Global Positioning System") (GPS) receiver measures its distance to [GPS satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GPS_satellites "GPS satellites") based on how long it takes for a radio signal to arrive from each satellite, and from these distances calculates the receiver's position. Because light travels about 300000kilometres (186000miles) in one second, these measurements of small fractions of a second must be very precise. The [Lunar Laser Ranging experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment "Lunar Laser Ranging experiment"), [radar astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar_astronomy "Radar astronomy") and the [Deep Space Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Space_Network "Deep Space Network") determine distances to the Moon,[\[97\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-science265_5171_482-99) planets[\[98\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-cm26_181-100) and spacecraft,[\[99\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-pieee95_11_2202-101) respectively, by measuring round-trip transit times.

## Determination

There are different ways to determine the value of c. One way is to measure directly the speed at which light waves propagate, which can be done in various astronomical and Earth-based setups. It is also possible to determine c from other physical laws where it appears, for example, by determining the values of the electromagnetic constants [_ε_ 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_permittivity "Relative permittivity") and [_μ_ 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permeability_(electromagnetism) "Permeability (electromagnetism)") and using their relation to c. Historically, the most accurate results have been obtained by separately determining the frequency and wavelength of a light beam, with their product equalling c. This is described in more detail in the ["Interferometry" section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#Interferometry) below.

In 1983 the [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") was defined as "the length of the path travelled by light in vacuum during a time interval of 1⁄299792458 of a second",[\[100\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Resolution_1-102) fixing the value of the speed of light at 299792458m/s by definition, as [described below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#Increased_accuracy_of_c_and_redefinition_of_the_metre_and_second). Consequently, accurate measurements of the speed of light yield an accurate realization of the metre rather than an accurate value of c.

### Astronomical measurements

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Io_eclipse_speed_of_light_measurement.svg/960px-Io_eclipse_speed_of_light_measurement.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Io_eclipse_speed_of_light_measurement.svg) Measurement of the speed of light from the time it takes Io to orbit Jupiter, using eclipses of Io by Jupiter's shadow to precisely measure its orbit.

[Outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space") is a convenient setting for measuring the speed of light because of its large scale and nearly perfect [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum"). Typically, one measures the time needed for light to traverse some reference distance in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), such as the [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") of the Earth's orbit. Historically, such measurements could be made fairly accurately, compared to how accurately the length of the reference distance is known in Earth-based units.

[Ole Rømer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer "Ole Rømer") used an astronomical measurement to make [the first quantitative estimate of the speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%B8mer's_determination_of_the_speed_of_light "Rømer's determination of the speed of light") in the year 1676.[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-cohen-103)[\[102\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-roemer-104) When measured from Earth, the periods of moons orbiting a distant planet are shorter when the Earth is approaching the planet than when the Earth is receding from it. The difference is small, but the cumulative time becomes significant when measured over months. The distance travelled by light from the planet (or its moon) to Earth is shorter when the Earth is at the point in its orbit that is closest to its planet than when the Earth is at the farthest point in its orbit, the difference in distance being the [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") of the Earth's orbit around the Sun. The observed change in the moon's orbital period is caused by the difference in the time it takes light to traverse the shorter or longer distance. Rømer observed this effect for [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter")'s innermost major moon Io and deduced that light takes 22 minutes to cross the diameter of the Earth's orbit.[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-cohen-103)

[![A star emits a light ray that hits the objective of a telescope. While the light travels down the telescope to its eyepiece, the telescope moves to the right. For the light to stay inside the telescope, the telescope must be tilted to the right, causing the distant source to appear at a different location to the right.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/SoL_Aberration.svg/500px-SoL_Aberration.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SoL_Aberration.svg) Aberration of light: light from a distant source appears to be from a different location for a moving telescope due to the finite speed of light.

Another method is to use the [aberration of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberration_of_light "Aberration of light"), discovered and explained by [James Bradley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Bradley "James Bradley") in the 18th century.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Bradley1729-105) This effect results from the [vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition") of the velocity of light arriving from a distant source (such as a star) and the velocity of its observer (see diagram on the right). A moving observer thus sees the light coming from a slightly different direction and consequently sees the source at a position shifted from its original position. Since the direction of the Earth's velocity changes continuously as the Earth orbits the Sun, this effect causes the apparent position of stars to move around. From the angular difference in the position of stars (maximally 20.5 [arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcsecond "Arcsecond"))[\[104\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-106) it is possible to express the speed of light in terms of the Earth's velocity around the Sun, which with the known length of a year can be converted to the time needed to travel from the Sun to the Earth. In 1729, Bradley used this method to derive that light travelled 10210 times faster than the Earth in its orbit (the modern figure is 10066 times faster) or, equivalently, that it would take light 8minutes 12seconds to travel from the Sun to the Earth.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Bradley1729-105)

#### Astronomical unit

Main article: [Astronomical unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit")

Historically the speed of light was used together with timing measurements to determine a value for the astronomical unit (AU).[\[105\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-107) The astronomical unit was redefined in 2012 as exactly 149597870700m.[\[106\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-108)[\[107\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-AU_redef-109) This redefinition is analogous to that of the metre and likewise has the effect of fixing the speed of light to an exact value in astronomical units per second (via the exact speed of light in metres per second).[\[108\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-110)

### Time of flight techniques

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/Michelson_speed_of_light_measurement_1930.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Michelson_speed_of_light_measurement_1930.jpg) One of the last and most accurate time of flight measurements, Michelson, Pease and Pearson's 1930–1935 experiment used a rotating mirror and a one-mile (1.6km) long vacuum chamber which the light beam traversed 10 times. It achieved accuracy of ±11km/s.[![A light ray passes horizontally through a half-mirror and a rotating cog wheel, is reflected back by a mirror, passes through the cog wheel, and is reflected by the half-mirror into a monocular.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Fizeau-int.svg/500px-Fizeau-int.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fizeau-int.svg) Diagram of the [Fizeau apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fizeau's_measurement_of_the_speed_of_light_in_air "Fizeau's measurement of the speed of light in air"):

1. Light source
2. Beam-splitting semi-transparent mirror
3. Toothed wheel-breaker of the light beam
4. Remote mirror
5. Telescopic tube

A method of measuring the speed of light is to measure the time needed for light to travel to a mirror at a known distance and back. This is the working principle behind experiments by [Hippolyte Fizeau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau "Hippolyte Fizeau") and [Léon Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault "Léon Foucault").

The [setup as used by Fizeau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fizeau's_measurement_of_the_speed_of_light_in_air "Fizeau's measurement of the speed of light in air") consists of a beam of light directed at a mirror 8 kilometres (5mi) away. On the way from the source to the mirror, the beam passes through a rotating cogwheel. At a certain rate of rotation, the beam passes through one gap on the way out and another on the way back, but at slightly higher or lower rates, the beam strikes a tooth and does not pass through the wheel. Knowing the distance between the wheel and the mirror, the number of teeth on the wheel, and the rate of rotation, the speed of light can be calculated.[\[109\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-How-111)

The [method of Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foucault's_measurements_of_the_speed_of_light "Foucault's measurements of the speed of light") replaces the cogwheel with a rotating mirror. Because the mirror keeps rotating while the light travels to the distant mirror and back, the light is reflected from the rotating mirror at a different angle on its way out than it is on its way back. From this difference in angle, the known speed of rotation and the distance to the distant mirror the speed of light may be calculated.[\[110\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-112) Foucault used this apparatus to measure the speed of light in air versus water, based on a suggestion by [François Arago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Arago "François Arago").[\[111\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hughes2012-113)

Today, using [oscilloscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscilloscopes "Oscilloscopes") with time resolutions of less than one nanosecond, the speed of light can be directly measured by timing the delay of a light pulse from a laser or an LED reflected from a mirror. This method is less precise (with errors of the order of 1%) than other modern techniques, but it is sometimes used as a laboratory experiment in college physics classes.[\[112\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-114)

### Electromagnetic constants

An option for deriving c that does not directly depend on a measurement of the propagation of electromagnetic waves is to use the relation between c and the [vacuum permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permittivity "Vacuum permittivity") _ε_ 0 and [vacuum permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permeability "Vacuum permeability") _μ_ 0 established by Maxwell's theory: c2 = 1/(_ε_ 0 _μ_ 0). The vacuum permittivity may be determined by measuring the [capacitance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitance "Capacitance") and dimensions of a [capacitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor "Capacitor"), whereas the value of the vacuum permeability was historically fixed at exactly 4π×10−7H⋅m−1 through the definition of the [ampere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ampere_(unit) "Ampere (unit)"). [Rosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Bennett_Rosa "Edward Bennett Rosa") and [Dorsey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noah_Ernest_Dorsey "Noah Ernest Dorsey") used this method in 1907 to find a value of 299710±22km/s. Their method depended upon having a standard unit of electrical resistance, the "international [ohm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohm "Ohm")", and so its accuracy was limited by how this standard was defined.[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Essen1948-115)[\[114\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-RosaDorsey-116)

### Cavity resonance

[![A box with three waves in it; there are one and a half wavelength of the top wave, one of the middle one, and a half of the bottom one.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Waves_in_Box.svg/500px-Waves_in_Box.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Waves_in_Box.svg) Electromagnetic [standing waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standing_waves "Standing waves") in a cavity

Another way to measure the speed of light is to independently measure the frequency f and wavelength λ of an electromagnetic wave in vacuum. The value of c can then be found by using the relation _c_ = _λf_. One option is to measure the resonance frequency of a [cavity resonator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavity_resonator "Cavity resonator"). If the dimensions of the resonance cavity are also known, these can be used to determine the wavelength of the wave. In 1946, [Louis Essen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Essen "Louis Essen") and A. C. Gordon-Smith established the frequency for a variety of [normal modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_mode "Normal mode") of microwaves of a [microwave cavity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_cavity "Microwave cavity") of precisely known dimensions. The dimensions were established to an accuracy of about ±0.8μm using gauges calibrated by interferometry.[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Essen1948-115) As the wavelength of the modes was known from the geometry of the cavity and from [electromagnetic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_theory "Electromagnetic theory"), knowledge of the associated frequencies enabled a calculation of the speed of light.[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Essen1948-115)[\[115\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-117)

The Essen–Gordon-Smith result, 299792±9km/s, was substantially more precise than those found by optical techniques.[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Essen1948-115) By 1950, repeated measurements by Essen established a result of 299792.5±3.0km/s.[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Essen1950-118)

A household demonstration of this technique is possible, using a [microwave oven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_oven "Microwave oven") and food such as marshmallows or margarine: if the turntable is removed so that the food does not move, it will cook the fastest at the [antinodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antinode "Antinode") (the points at which the wave amplitude is the greatest), where it will begin to melt. The distance between two such spots is half the wavelength of the microwaves; by measuring this distance and multiplying the wavelength by the microwave frequency (usually displayed on the back of the oven, typically 2450MHz), the value of c can be calculated, "often with less than 5% error".[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-119)[\[118\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-120)

### Interferometry

[![Schematic of the working of a Michelson interferometer.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Interferometer_sol.svg/500px-Interferometer_sol.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Interferometer_sol.svg) An interferometric determination of length. Left: [constructive interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructive_interference "Constructive interference"); Right: [destructive interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destructive_interference "Destructive interference").

[Interferometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interferometry "Interferometry") is another method to find the wavelength of electromagnetic radiation for determining the speed of light.[\[119\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Vaughan-121) A [coherent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(physics) "Coherence (physics)") beam of light (e.g. from a [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser")), with a known frequency f, is split to follow two paths and then recombined. By adjusting the path length while observing the [interference pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)") and carefully measuring the change in path length, the wavelength of the light λ can be determined. The speed of light is then calculated using the equation _c_ = _λf_.

Before the advent of laser technology, coherent [radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiowave "Radiowave") sources were used for interferometry measurements of the speed of light.[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Froome1858-122) Interferometric determination of wavelength becomes less precise with wavelength and the experiments were thus limited in precision by the long wavelength (~4mm\[0.16in\]) of the radiowaves. The precision can be improved by using light with a shorter wavelength, but then it becomes difficult to directly measure the frequency of the light.[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-NIST_pub-123)

One way around this problem is to start with a low frequency signal of which the frequency can be precisely measured, and from this signal progressively synthesize higher frequency signals whose frequency can then be linked to the original signal. A laser can then be locked to the frequency, and its wavelength can be determined using interferometry.[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-NIST_pub-123) This technique was due to a group at the National Bureau of Standards (which later became the [National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology")). They used it in 1972 to measure the speed of light in vacuum with a [fractional uncertainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_uncertainty "Measurement uncertainty") of 3.5×10−9.[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-NIST_pub-123)[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-NIST_heterodyne-124)

## History

Until the [early modern period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_modern_period "Early modern period"), it was not known whether light travelled instantaneously or at a very fast finite speed. The first extant recorded examination of this subject was in [ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece"). The ancient Greeks, Arabic scholars, and classical European scientists long debated this until Rømer provided the first calculation of the speed of light. Einstein's theory of special relativity postulates that the speed of light is constant regardless of one's frame of reference. Since then, scientists have provided increasingly accurate measurements.

History of measurements of c (in m/s)

### Early history

[Empedocles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empedocles "Empedocles") (c. 490–430 BCE) was the first to propose a theory of light[\[131\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-133) and claimed that light has a finite speed.[\[132\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-134) He maintained that light was something in motion, and therefore must take some time to travel. [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") argued, to the contrary, that "light is due to the presence of something, but it is not a movement".[\[133\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Statistics-135) [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") and [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy") advanced Empedocles' [emission theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_theory_(vision) "Emission theory (vision)") of vision, where light is emitted from the eye, thus enabling sight. Based on that theory, [Heron of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron_of_Alexandria "Heron of Alexandria") argued that the speed of light must be [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity") because distant objects such as stars appear immediately upon opening the eyes.[\[134\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-136)

[Early Islamic philosophers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Islamic_philosophy "Early Islamic philosophy") initially agreed with the [Aristotelian view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotelian_physics "Aristotelian physics") that light had no speed of travel. In 1021, [Alhazen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhazen "Alhazen") (Ibn al-Haytham) published the _[Book of Optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Optics "Book of Optics")_, in which he presented a series of arguments dismissing the emission theory of [vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception "Visual perception") in favour of the now accepted intromission theory, in which light moves from an object into the eye.[\[135\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-137) This led Alhazen to propose that light must have a finite speed,[\[133\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Statistics-135)[\[136\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Hamarneh-138)[\[137\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Lester-139) and that the speed of light is variable, decreasing in denser bodies.[\[137\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Lester-139)[\[138\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-140) He argued that light is substantial matter, the propagation of which requires time, even if this is hidden from the senses.[\[139\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-141) Also in the 11th century, [Abū Rayhān al-Bīrūnī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Biruni "Al-Biruni") agreed that light has a finite speed, and observed that the speed of light is much faster than the speed of sound.[\[140\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-142)

In the 13th century, [Roger Bacon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Bacon "Roger Bacon") argued that the speed of light in air was not infinite, using philosophical arguments backed by the writing of Alhazen and Aristotle.[\[141\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Lindberg-143)[\[142\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-144) In the 1270s, [Witelo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Witelo "Witelo") considered the possibility of light travelling at infinite speed in vacuum, but slowing down in denser bodies.[\[143\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Marshall-145)

In the early 17th century, [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") believed that the speed of light was infinite since empty space presents no obstacle to it. [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") argued that if the speed of light were to be finite, the Sun, Earth, and Moon would be noticeably out of alignment during a [lunar eclipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_eclipse "Lunar eclipse"). Although this argument fails when aberration of light is taken into account, the latter was not recognized until the following century.[\[144\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-146) Since such misalignment had not been observed, Descartes concluded the speed of light was infinite. Descartes speculated that if the speed of light were found to be finite, his whole system of philosophy might be demolished.[\[133\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Statistics-135) Despite this, in his derivation of [Snell's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snell's_law "Snell's law"), Descartes assumed that some kind of motion associated with light was faster in denser media.[\[145\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-147)[\[146\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-148) [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat") derived Snell's law using the opposing assumption, the denser the medium the slower light travelled. Fermat also argued in support of a finite speed of light.[\[147\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-149)

### First measurement attempts

In 1629, [Isaac Beeckman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Beeckman "Isaac Beeckman") proposed an experiment in which a person observes the flash of a cannon reflecting off a mirror about one mile (1.6km) away. In 1638, [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") proposed an experiment, with an apparent claim to having performed it some years earlier, to measure the speed of light by observing the delay between uncovering a lantern and its perception some distance away. He was unable to distinguish whether light travel was instantaneous or not, but concluded that if it were not, it must nevertheless be extraordinarily rapid.[\[123\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-2newsciences-125)[\[124\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-boyer-126) According to Galileo, the lanterns he used were "at a short distance, less than a mile". Assuming the distance was not too much shorter than a mile, and that "about a thirtieth of a second is the minimum time interval distinguishable by the unaided eye", Boyer notes that Galileo's experiment could at best be said to have established a lower limit of about 60 miles per second for the velocity of light.[\[124\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-boyer-126) In 1667, the [Accademia del Cimento](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accademia_del_Cimento "Accademia del Cimento") of Florence reported that it had performed Galileo's experiment, with the lanterns separated by about onemile, but no delay was observed.[\[148\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-150) The actual delay in this experiment would have been about 11 [microseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsecond "Microsecond").

[![A diagram of a planet's orbit around the Sun and of a moon's orbit around another planet. The shadow of the latter planet is shaded.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Illustration_from_1676_article_on_Ole_R%C3%B8mer%27s_measurement_of_the_speed_of_light.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Illustration_from_1676_article_on_Ole_R%C3%B8mer's_measurement_of_the_speed_of_light.png) Rømer's observations of the occultations of Io from Earth

[The first quantitative estimate of the speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%B8mer's_determination_of_the_speed_of_light "Rømer's determination of the speed of light") was made in 1676 by Ole Rømer.[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-cohen-103)[\[102\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-roemer-104) From the observation that the periods of Jupiter's innermost moon [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon) "Io (moon)") appeared to be shorter when the Earth was approaching Jupiter than when receding from it, he concluded that light travels at a finite speed, and estimated that it takes light 22 minutes to cross the diameter of Earth's orbit. [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens") combined this estimate with an estimate for the diameter of the Earth's orbit to obtain an estimate of speed of light of 220000km/s, which is 27% lower than the actual value.[\[127\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Huygens_1690_8%E2%80%939-129)

In his 1704 book _[Opticks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opticks "Opticks")_, [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") reported Rømer's calculations of the finite speed of light and gave a value of "seven or eight minutes" for the time taken for light to travel from the Sun to the Earth (the modern value is 8minutes 19seconds).[\[149\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-151) Newton queried whether Rømer's eclipse shadows were coloured. Hearing that they were not, he concluded the different colours travelled at the same speed. In 1729, [James Bradley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Bradley "James Bradley") discovered [stellar aberration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberration_of_light "Aberration of light").[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Bradley1729-105) From this effect he determined that light must travel 10,210 times faster than the Earth in its orbit (the modern figure is 10,066 times faster) or, equivalently, that it would take light 8minutes 12seconds to travel from the Sun to the Earth.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Bradley1729-105)

### Connections with electromagnetism

See also: [History of electromagnetic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_electromagnetic_theory "History of electromagnetic theory") and [History of special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_special_relativity "History of special relativity")

In the 19th century [Hippolyte Fizeau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippolyte_Fizeau "Hippolyte Fizeau") developed a method to determine the speed of light based on time-of-flight measurements on Earth and reported a value of 315000km/s.[\[150\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-guarnieri_7-1-152) His method was improved upon by [Léon Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault "Léon Foucault") who obtained a value of 298000km/s in 1862.[\[109\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-How-111) In the year 1856, [Wilhelm Eduard Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Eduard_Weber "Wilhelm Eduard Weber") and [Rudolf Kohlrausch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Kohlrausch "Rudolf Kohlrausch") measured the ratio of the electromagnetic and electrostatic units of charge, 1/√_ε_ 0 _μ_ 0, by discharging a [Leyden jar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leyden_jar "Leyden jar"), and found that its numerical value was very close to the speed of light as measured directly by Fizeau. The following year [Gustav Kirchhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff "Gustav Kirchhoff") calculated that an electric signal in a [resistanceless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistance "Electrical resistance") wire travels along the wire at this speed.[\[151\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-153)

In the early 1860s, Maxwell showed that, according to the theory of electromagnetism he was working on, electromagnetic waves propagate in empty space[\[152\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-154) at a speed equal to the above Weber/Kohlrausch ratio, and drawing attention to the numerical proximity of this value to the speed of light as measured by Fizeau, he proposed that light is in fact an electromagnetic wave.[\[153\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-maxwellbio-155) Maxwell backed up his claim with his own experiment published in the 1868 Philosophical Transactions which determined the ratio of the electrostatic and electromagnetic units of electricity.[\[154\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-156)

### "Luminiferous aether"

Main article: [Luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether")

The wave properties of light were well known since [Thomas Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist) "Thomas Young (scientist)"). In the 19th century, physicists believed light was propagating in a medium called aether (or ether). After Maxwell's theory unified light and electric and magnetic waves, it was favored that both light and electric magnetic waves propagate in the same aether medium (or called the [luminiferous aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminiferous_aether "Luminiferous aether")).[\[155\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-157)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Einstein_en_Lorentz.jpg/500px-Einstein_en_Lorentz.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Einstein_en_Lorentz.jpg) Hendrik Lorentz (right) with Albert Einstein (1921)

Some physicists thought that this aether acted as a [preferred frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferred_frame "Preferred frame") of reference for the propagation of light and therefore it should be possible to measure the motion of the Earth with respect to this medium, by measuring the [isotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropy "Isotropy") of the speed of light. Beginning in the 1880s several experiments were performed to try to detect this motion, the most famous of which is [the experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment "Michelson–Morley experiment") performed by [Albert A. Michelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_A._Michelson "Albert A. Michelson") and [Edward W. Morley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_W._Morley "Edward W. Morley") in 1887.[\[156\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-158)[\[157\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-159) The detected motion was found to always be nil (within observational error). Modern experiments indicate that the two-way speed of light is [isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotropic "Isotropic") (the same in every direction) to within 6 nanometres per second.[\[158\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-160)

Because of Michelson-Morley experiment [Hendrik Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz") proposed that the motion of the apparatus through the aether may cause the apparatus to [contract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_contraction "Lorentz contraction") along its length in the direction of motion, and he further assumed that the time variable for moving systems must also be changed accordingly ("local time"), which led to the formulation of the [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation"). Based on [Lorentz's aether theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_ether_theory "Lorentz ether theory"), [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") (1900) showed that this local time (to first order in _v_/ _c_) is indicated by clocks moving in the aether, which are synchronized under the assumption of constant light speed. In 1904, he speculated that the speed of light could be a limiting velocity in dynamics, provided that the assumptions of Lorentz's theory are all confirmed. In 1905, Poincaré brought Lorentz's aether theory into full observational agreement with the [principle of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_relativity "Principle of relativity").[\[159\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-161)[\[160\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-162)

### Special relativity

In 1905 Einstein postulated from the outset that the speed of light in vacuum, measured by a non-accelerating observer, is independent of the motion of the source or observer. Using this and the principle of relativity as a basis he derived the [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity "Special theory of relativity"), in which the speed of light in vacuum c featured as a fundamental constant, also appearing in contexts unrelated to light. This made the concept of the stationary aether (to which Lorentz and Poincaré still adhered) useless and revolutionized the concepts of space and time.[\[161\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-163)[\[162\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-164)

### Increased accuracy of c and redefinition of the metre and second

See also: [History of the metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_metre "History of the metre")

In the second half of the 20th century, much progress was made in increasing the accuracy of measurements of the speed of light, first by cavity resonance techniques and later by laser interferometer techniques. These were aided by new, more precise, definitions of the metre and second. In 1950, [Louis Essen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Essen "Louis Essen") determined the speed as 299792.5±3.0km/s, using cavity resonance.[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Essen1950-118) This value was adopted by the 12th General Assembly of the Radio-Scientific Union in 1957. In 1960, the [metre was redefined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_metre#Krypton_standard "History of the metre") in terms of the wavelength of a particular spectral line of [krypton-86](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krypton-86 "Krypton-86"), and, in 1967, the second was redefined in terms of the hyperfine transition frequency of the ground state of [caesium-133](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-133 "Caesium-133").[\[163\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-13thCGPMr1-165)

In 1972, using the laser interferometer method and the new definitions, a group at the US [National Bureau of Standards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology") in [Boulder, Colorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado "Boulder, Colorado") determined the speed of light in vacuum to be c = 299792456.2±1.1m/s. This was 100 times less uncertain than the previously accepted value. The remaining uncertainty was mainly related to the definition of the metre.[\[164\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-11thCGPM-166)[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-NIST_heterodyne-124) As similar experiments found comparable results for c, the 15th [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures") in 1975 recommended using the value 299792458m/s for the speed of light.[\[165\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-15thCGPM-167)

### Defined as an explicit constant

In 1983 the 17th meeting of the General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM) found that wavelengths from frequency measurements and a given value for the speed of light are more [reproducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproducibility "Reproducibility") than the previous standard. They kept the 1967 definition of second, so the [caesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium "Caesium") [hyperfine frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure#Use_in_defining_the_SI_second_and_meter "Hyperfine structure") would now determine both the second and the metre. To do this, they redefined the metre as "the length of the path traveled by light in vacuum during a time interval of 1/299792458 of a second".[\[100\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Resolution_1-102)

As a result of this definition, the value of the speed of light in vacuum is exactly 299792458m/s[\[166\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Wheeler-168)[\[167\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-timeline-169) and has become a defined constant in the SI system of units.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-fixes-18) Improved experimental techniques that, prior to 1983, would have measured the speed of light no longer affect the known value of the speed of light in SI units, but instead allow a more precise realization of the metre by more accurately measuring the wavelength of krypton-86 and other light sources.[\[168\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-Adams-170)[\[169\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-W_Rindler-171)

In 2011, the CGPM stated its intention to redefine all seven SI base units using what it calls "the explicit-constant formulation", where each "unit is defined indirectly by specifying explicitly an exact value for a well-recognized fundamental constant", as was done for the speed of light. It proposed a new, but completely equivalent, wording of the metre's definition: "The metre, symbol m, is the unit of length; its magnitude is set by fixing the numerical value of the speed of light in vacuum to be equal to exactly 299792458 when it is expressed in the SI unit m s−1."[\[170\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-172) This was one of the changes that was incorporated in the [2019 revision of the SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_revision_of_the_SI "2019 revision of the SI"), also termed the _New SI_.[\[171\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light#cite_note-173)

     "The origins of the letter c being used for the speed of light can be traced back to a paper of 1856 by Weber and Kohlrausch \[...\] Weber apparently meant c to stand for 'constant' in his force law, but there is evidence that physicists such as Lorentz and Einstein were accustomed to a common convention that c could be used as a variable for velocity. This usage can be traced back to the classic Latin texts in which c stood for 'celeritas', meaning 'speed'."
