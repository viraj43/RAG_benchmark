Title: State of Venture Q1’26 - CB Insights Research
Key Highlights:
In Q1’26, quarterly funding hit a record high, but beneath the surface, venture continued a quiet contraction: fewer firms writing checks, fewer deals getting completed, and a growing share of liquidity happening entirely outside the public markets.

### 2. Venture momentum builds in AI infra and hard tech, moving beyond software
[...]
AI infrastructure and hard tech markets — specifically silicon, defense, and space — are showing the strongest potential in Q1’26, based on CB Insights’ Mosaic scores(our predictive measure of private-company success).
[...]
Transformer-optimized ASICs(chips designed specifically to accelerate training and/or inference for transformer-based models, which underpin many modern LLMs) rank first by average Mosaic. While the market is small with only 11 companies, total headcount increased 352% over the past two years. Demand is structural: every major AI model requires specialized silicon to run efficiently at scale. Companies developing vision-language models (VLMs) and large vision models (LVMs) follow closely behind.
[...]
Hard tech markets in areas like defense, space, and quantum fill many of the remaining top spots, including defense & national security AI copilots, counter-space weapons, and neutral-atom quantum computing. Recent government contracts and partnerships reflect that momentum:
[...]

- Ursa Major closed a partnership with Teledyne to supply propulsion for hypersonic vehicles.
- Pascal and Welinq expanded their quantum computing collaboration to develop interconnected neutral-atom processors, targeting data center deployment.
[...]
As launch costs fall, defense startup funding rises — and as AI expands into hardware, capital is moving toward the physical layer, from chips to rockets to defense systems. The companies gaining ground are the ones building the infrastructure for the next wave.

The US is home to a disproportionate share of the most valuable AI companies — and that concentration is reflected in where exits are happening.

### 4. Private-company secondaries are filling the exit gap
[...]
As companies stay private longer, they are increasingly engineering their own liquidity.
[...]
Private-market secondary activity— where existing shareholders sell stakes to new buyers rather than waiting for an exit — has grown steadily for a decade. Q1’26 reached 134 secondary deals, putting the full year on an annualized pace of 536, equaling the record set last year.
[...]
Private-market secondaries remain highly concentrated among certain firms, with 34% of the top 100 most valuable private companies conducting secondary rounds, compared to just 1.1% of all VC-backed companies. They are tools used by the most valuable companies to remain private longer at record valuations.
[...]
For venture investors, the rise of private-market secondaries changes the calculus. Capital no longer needs to wait for an IPO or acquisition to generate a return. The traditional exit-driven venture model is giving way to something more continuous.
[...]
Private companies are raising capital, distributing it, and staying private on their own terms. With global M&A and IPO activity at multi-year lows, secondaries offer a meaningful alternative.
[...]
### 5. The active investor pool is quietly collapsing
[...]
The number of active investors fell globally to 10K in Q1’26, a 10% decline from last quarter and the lowest level since Q3’20.
[...]
- US active investors fell to 3.5K — well below the peak of 5.6K in Q1’22.
- At 3.3K, the number of active investors in Asia has dropped by 41% since its peak.
- Europe’s investor count is now around 2.5K, down by one-third from its peak.
[...]
The US is consolidating around established firms with the ability to invest from early stages through IPO. As capital concentrates among them, smaller investors find fewer opportunities to participate, making it harder to raise new funds and remain active.
[...]
As the investor base shrinks, deal activity is concentrating among top firms. CB Insights Smart Money VC firms— the top 25 best-performing venture capital firms over the past decade — are closing more deals, not fewer. The cohort did 331 equity deals in Q1’26 (5% more than the prior quarter) including 80 mega-rounds, with AI companies representing two-thirds of target investments.
[...]
Venture has contracted before and recovered, but recoveries do not reset the market to its prior state. The firms positioned to lead the next cycle are already visible. They are the ones making deals now, while many others in the market cannot.

Title: Tech Trends 2026 | Deloitte Insights
Key Highlights:
## As technology innovation and adoption accelerate, five trends reveal how successful organizations are moving from experimentation to impact
[...]
The question used to be “What can we do with AI?” Now it’s “How do we move from experimentation to impact?” The focus has moved from endless pilots to real business value, and there’s a sense of urgency behind it all. Not because the technology is getting better—though it is—but because the pace of change itself has accelerated.
[...]
But rapid adoption is only the surface. Innovation is compounding; forces aren’t simply additive, but multiplicative. Think of it as a flywheel: Better technology enables more applications. More applications generate more data. More data attracts more investment. More investment builds better infrastructure. Better infrastructure reduces costs. Lower costs enable more experimentation. Each improvement simultaneously accelerates all the others.
[...]
It’s why AI startups scale from US$1 million to US$30 million in revenue five times faster than SaaS companies did.3 It’s why the knowledge half-life in AI has shrunk to months from years.4 And it’s why one chief information officer (CIO) told me, “The time it takes us to study a new technology now exceeds that technology’s relevance window.”
[...]
For 17 years, Tech Trends has explored emerging technologies poised to reshape business in the next 18 to 24 months. Our research is based on trend sensing from conversations with Deloitte subject matter experts and external technology leaders, as well as Deloitte’s proprietary research on emerging technologies. This year, the data reveals five interconnected forces.
[...]
## AI goes physical: Navigating the convergence of AI and robotics
[...]
Amazon deployed its millionth robot, and its DeepFleet AI coordinates the entire robot fleet, improving travel efficiency within warehouses by 10%.5 BMW’s factories have cars driving themselves through kilometer-long production routes.6 Intelligence isn’t confined to screens anymore; it’s embodied, autonomous, and solving real problems in the physical world.
[...]
## The agentic reality check: Preparing for a silicon-based workforce
[...]
Only 11% of organizations have agents in production, despite 38% piloting them. The gap between pilot to production tells you everything. Forty-two percent are still developing their strategy, while 35% have no strategy at all.7 Gartner predicts that 40% of agentic projects will fail by 20278—not because the technology doesn’t work, but because organizations are automating broken processes instead of redesigning operations. HPE’s chief financial officer captured what works: “We wanted to select an end-to-end process where we could truly transform, not just solve for a single pain point.”9 Redesign, don’t automate. That’s the pattern separating success from failure.
[...]
## The AI infrastructure reckoning: Optimizing compute strategy in the age of inference economics
[...]
Token costs have dropped 280-fold in two years;10 yet some enterprises are seeing monthly bills in the tens of millions. Usage exploded faster than costs declined. Organizations are discovering their existing infrastructure strategies aren't designed to scale AI to production-scale deployment. They're shifting from cloud-first to strategic hybrid: cloud for elasticity, on-premises for consistency, and edge for immediacy.
[...]
## The great rebuild: Architecting an AI-native tech organization
[...]
AI is restructuring tech organizations, making them leaner, faster, and more strategic. Only 1% of IT leaders surveyed by Deloitte reported that no major operating model changes were underway.11 Leaders are shifting from incremental IT management to orchestrating human-agent teams, with CIOs becoming AI evangelists. Success requires bold reimagination: modular architectures, embedded governance, and perpetual evolution as core capabilities.
[...]
## The AI dilemma: Securing and leveraging AI for cyber defense
[...]
The technology meant to give businesses an advantage is becoming the target used against them. AT&T’s chief information security officer captured the challenge: “What we’re experiencing today is no different than what we’ve experienced in the past. The only difference with AI is speed and impact.”12 Organizations must secure AI across four domains—data, models, applications, and infrastructure—but they also have the opportunity to use AI-powered defenses to fight threats operating at machine speed.
[...]
- They lead with problems, not technology. Broadcom’s CIO: “Without focusing on a specific business problem and the value you want to derive, it could be easy to invest in AI and receive no return.”13
[...]
- Specifically, their biggest problems. UiPath CEO: “Rather than getting stuck in a cycle of perpetual proofs of concept, consider attacking your biggest problem and going for a big outcome.”14
[...]
- They prioritize velocity over perfection. Western Digital’s CIO: “We’d rather fail fast on small pilots than miss the wave entirely.”15
[...]
- They design with people, not just for them. Walmart involved store associates in building its scheduling app, which includes shift swapping, schedule visibility, and employee control. The result: Scheduling time dropped from 90 minutes to 30 minutes, and people actually used the app.16
[...]
- They treat change as continuous. Coca-Cola’s CIO described their journey as moving from “What can we do?” to “What should we do?”17 That shift—from capability-first to need-first—is what separates productive experimentation from pilot purgatory.
[...]
It’s not just that AI is powerful. It’s that the S-curves are compressing. The distance between emerging and mainstream is collapsing.
[...]
Organizations built for sequential improvement can’t compete with those operating in continuous learning loops. The traditional playbook assumed you had time to get it right. That assumption no longer holds.
[...]
The organizations that succeed will probably not be those with the most sophisticated technology. They’ll be those with the courage to redesign rather than automate, the discipline to connect every investment to business outcomes, and the velocity to execute before the window closes.
[...]
Innovation compounds. The gap between laggards and leaders grows exponentially. How you respond determines which side of that gap you’re on.

Title: State of AI Q1’26 Report - CB Insights Research
Key Highlights:
Private AI companies raised $226B in Q1’26, surpassing the full-year total for 2025 in just a single quarter.
[...]
A single round, OpenAI‘s $122B corporate minority, comprised 54% of funding. Outside of that, Q1’26 would have seen $104B in funding, a 45% QoQ increase in funding.
[...]
Mega-rounds dominated: $100M+ deals accounted for 94% of total funding (up from 80% last quarter), pushing the year-to-date average deal size to $160M — more than 4x the full-year $38M average in 2025.
[...]
Besides the dominance from LLMs (outside of OpenAI, both Anthropic and xAI raised multi-billion-dollar rounds), physical AI emerged as a key theme, with 11% of all AI deals going to companies building robotics, defense tech, and autonomous systems.
[...]
M&A activity remained elevated in Q1’26 with 266 deals closed, up 90% YoY.
[...]
- Quarterly funding outpaces 2025’s annual total, driven by model developers
- Physical AI hits its stride
- Big tech M&A activity highlights race to AI capabilities
[...]
Globally, private AI companies raised over $226B in Q1’26, up 216% QoQ from approximately $72B in Q4’25, and surpassing the $217B that 2025 produced. But that headline figure was driven largely by a single transaction: OpenAI’s $122B raise accounted for over half of AI funding during the quarter.
[...]
Still, even without that financing, Q1’26 would have seen $104B in funding, a 45% QoQ increase. Mega-rounds ($100M+ deals) accounted for the highest share on record: 94% of total Q1’26 funding, bringing the year-to-date average deal size to $160M — more than 4x the full-year $38M average in 2025.
[...]
With funding soaring even as deal count fell 5% QoQ, the AI market is becoming increasingly top-heavy, with capital concentrated in fewer, larger rounds.
[...]
LLM developers drove the surge. The top 3 deals by model developers alone totaled $160B, or roughly 71% of Q1’26 funding:
[...]
- OpenAI’s $122B corporate minority round at an $840B valuation
- Anthropic’s $30B Series G at $380B
- xAI’s $7.5B Series E at $230B
[...]
These companies are racing to cover the compute, talent, and energy costs required to stay at the frontier of model development. In fact, OpenAI is projecting $600B in compute spending by 2030.
[...]
Q1’26 funding highlights how investment is increasingly concentrating around leading model developers as training costs rise. For startups outside the frontier tier, the window for differentiation is narrowing as the largest model developers continue to pull ahead.
[...]
## Physical AI hits its stride
[...]
Physical AI & robotics led all sectors at 11% share of AI deals this quarter, with the largest bets spanning defense, industrial, and mobility.
[...]
Within the space, industrial humanoid robot developers led with 17 deals, as investment shifts from R&D toward commercial deployment. Humanoid robot companies are on pace for a record $10B in 2026 funding. While mass commercialization remains years away, structured environments remain crucial for the initial deployment phase. Pilot programs are already running:
[...]
- Boston Dynamics’ Atlas robot will be deployed at Hyundai facilities this year
- Airbus is using UBTECH’s Walker S2 humanoid for aviation manufacturing
- BMW became the first automaker to utilize humanoid robots in production in Germany
[...]
Beyond the headline LLM rounds, autonomous driving was another significant investment theme this quarter. Notably, Waymo, Wayve, and Waabi together raised $18B and are already operating at commercial scale. Waymo now operates 3K+ robotaxis completing 500K+ weekly paid rides.
[...]
The longer-term bet is on physical AI’s orchestration layer. AI systems are evolving from controlling single robots to coordinating robots, factory systems, and equipment across connected workflows. Robot foundation model developers are emerging as a key battleground, as industrial incumbents, tech giants, and startups compete to power that coordination.
[...]
## Big tech M&A activity reflects the race for AI capabilities
[...]
AI M&A surged 90% YoY in Q1’26, with 266 deals, even as the quarter posted a modest dip from the record pace of the prior three quarters. Big tech is driving some of that activity and moving faster than the market norm to do it.
[...]
In Q1’26, big tech acquired companies with an average time to exit of 4.5 years, versus 7.6 years across all AI M&A this quarter. At that stage, many targets are still built around a single core capability, making them easier for incumbents to integrate into existing product stacks.
[...]
Google emerges as the most active dealmaker, with notable deals focused on acquiring ready-made capabilities — all targeting companies founded 4-6 years ago.
[...]
- Intrinsic and Common Sense Machines bolster its physical AI and spatial reasoning stack
- Hume AI brings emotional voice intelligence into DeepMind
- ProducerAI brings a ready-made user base onto Google’s Lyria 3 music model
- Wiz, which officially closed this quarter, gives Google enterprise security credibility across multicloud environments
[...]
While Google leads the pack, earlier-stage AI-native startups are also becoming acquirers. This quarter, Harvey bought AI tool developer Hexus and Sword bought AI care platform Kaia Health, signaling the next consolidation wave will come from vertical AI leaders locking down their own categories.

Title: Sector Snapshot: Venture Funding To Foundational AI Startups In Q1 Was Double All Of 2025
Key Highlights:
Funding to foundational AI startups, also known as generative AI companies or frontier labs, has doubled in the first quarter of 2026 so far compared to all of 2025, Crunchbase data shows.
[...]
That funding is increasingly concentrated in a handful of foundational giants, including OpenAI, Anthropic and xAI. In 2025 and early 2026, the market saw a shift to a small number of companies capturing a disproportionate share of global capital.
[...]
The broad trend: After three years of declining or flat venture investment, global startup overall funding grew year over year in 2025, Crunchbase data shows. Notably, year-over-year funding growth concentrated in the largest rounds and in the AI sector. Roughly 50% of all global venture funding in 2025 went to companies in AI-related fields, making artificial intelligence the leading sector for funding, as it was for the past three years.
[...]
Venture funding to AI overall reached $211 billion — up 85% year over year from $114 billion in 2024 — Crunchbase data shows. Funding to the AI sector in 2025 surpassed every year in the past decade, including the peak global funding year of 2021.
[...]
The numbers: As of March 31, foundational AI startups had raised $178 billion across 24 deals, compared with $88.9 billion across 66 deals in all of 2025 in a 100% increase. That’s also significantly higher — 466.9% higher to be exact — than the $31.4 billion raised across 52 deals in 2024.
