The **history of programming languages** spans from documentation of early mechanical computers to modern tools for [software development](/wiki/Software_development "Software development"). Early programming languages were highly specialized, relying on [mathematical notation](/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation") and similarly obscure [syntax](/wiki/Syntax_(programming_languages) "Syntax (programming languages)").[[1]](#cite_note-1) Throughout the 20th century, research in [compiler](/wiki/Compiler "Compiler") theory led to the creation of [high-level programming languages](/wiki/High-level_programming_language "High-level programming language"), which use a more accessible syntax to communicate instructions.

The first high-level programming language was [Plankalkül](/wiki/Plankalk%C3%BCl "Plankalkül"), created by [Konrad Zuse](/wiki/Konrad_Zuse "Konrad Zuse") between 1942 and 1945.[[2]](#cite_note-2) The first high-level language to have an associated [compiler](/wiki/Compiler "Compiler") was created by [Corrado Böhm](/wiki/Corrado_B%C3%B6hm "Corrado Böhm") in 1951, for his [PhD](/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy "Doctor of Philosophy") thesis.[[3]](#cite_note-3) The first commercially available language was [FORTRAN](/wiki/FORTRAN "FORTRAN") (FORmula TRANslation), developed in 1956 (first manual appeared in 1956, but first developed in 1954) by a team led by [John Backus](/wiki/John_Backus "John Backus") at [IBM](/wiki/IBM "IBM").

## Early history

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=History_of_programming_languages&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early history")]

During 1842–1849, [Ada Lovelace](/wiki/Ada_Lovelace "Ada Lovelace") translated the memoir of Italian mathematician [Luigi Menabrea](/wiki/Luigi_Menabrea "Luigi Menabrea") about [Charles Babbage](/wiki/Charles_Babbage "Charles Babbage")'s newest proposed machine: the [Analytical Engine](/wiki/Analytical_Engine "Analytical Engine"); she supplemented the memoir with notes that specified in detail a method for calculating [Bernoulli numbers](/wiki/Bernoulli_number "Bernoulli number") with the engine, recognized by most historians as the world's first published computer program.[[4]](#cite_note-4)

[Jacquard Looms](/wiki/Jacquard_Loom "Jacquard Loom") and Charles Babbage's [Difference Engine](/wiki/Difference_engine "Difference engine") both were designed to utilize [punched cards](/wiki/Punched_card "Punched card"),[[5]](#cite_note-Bales-5)[[6]](#cite_note-Swade-6) which would describe the sequence of operations that their programmable machines should perform.

The first computer [codes](/wiki/Source_code "Source code") were specialized for their applications: e.g., [Alonzo Church](/wiki/Alonzo_Church "Alonzo Church") was able to express the [lambda calculus](/wiki/Lambda_calculus "Lambda calculus") in a formulaic way, and the [Turing machine](/wiki/Turing_machine "Turing machine") was an abstraction of the operation of a tape-marking machine.

## First programming languages

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=History_of_programming_languages&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: First programming languages")]

In the 1940s, the first recognizably modern electrically powered computers were created. The limited speed and [memory capacity](/wiki/Computer_data_storage "Computer data storage") forced programmers to write hand-tuned [assembly language](/wiki/Assembly_language "Assembly language") programs. It was eventually realized that programming in assembly language required a great deal of intellectual effort.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

An early proposal for a [high-level programming language](/wiki/High-level_programming_language "High-level programming language") was [Plankalkül](/wiki/Plankalk%C3%BCl "Plankalkül"), developed by [Konrad Zuse](/wiki/Konrad_Zuse "Konrad Zuse") for his [Z1 computer](/wiki/Z1_(computer) "Z1 (computer)") between 1942 and 1945, but not implemented at the time.[[7]](#cite_note-7)

The first functioning programming languages designed to communicate instructions to a computer were written in the early 1950s. [John Mauchly](/wiki/John_Mauchly "John Mauchly")'s [Short Code](/wiki/Short_Code_(computer_language) "Short Code (computer language)"), proposed in 1949, was one of the first high-level languages ever developed for an [electronic computer](/wiki/Electronic_computer "Electronic computer").[[8]](#cite_note-Sebesta-8) Unlike [machine code](/wiki/Machine_code "Machine code"), Short Code statements represented [mathematical expressions](/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)") in an understandable form. However, the program had to be [interpreted](/wiki/Interpreter_(computing) "Interpreter (computing)") into machine code every time it ran, making the process much slower than running the equivalent machine code.

In the early 1950s, [Alick Glennie](/wiki/Alick_Glennie "Alick Glennie") developed [Autocode](/wiki/Autocode "Autocode"), possibly the first compiled programming language, at the [University of Manchester](/wiki/University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester"). In 1954, a second iteration of the language, known as the "Mark 1 Autocode", was developed for the [Mark 1](/wiki/Harvard_Mark_I "Harvard Mark I") by [R. A. Brooker](/wiki/Tony_Brooker "Tony Brooker"). Brooker, with the University of Manchester, also developed an autocode for the [Ferranti Mercury](/wiki/Ferranti_Mercury "Ferranti Mercury") in the 1950s. The version for the [EDSAC](/wiki/EDSAC "EDSAC") 2 was devised by [Douglas Hartree](/wiki/Douglas_Hartree "Douglas Hartree") of the [University of Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory](/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Mathematical_Laboratory "University of Cambridge Mathematical Laboratory") in 1961. Known as EDSAC 2 Autocode, it was a straight development from Mercury Autocode, adapted for local circumstances, and was noted for its [object code](/wiki/Object_code "Object code") optimization and source-language diagnostics, which were advanced for the time. A contemporary but separate thread of development, [Atlas Autocode](/wiki/Atlas_Autocode "Atlas Autocode") was developed for the [University of Manchester](/wiki/University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester") [Atlas 1](/wiki/Atlas_Computer_(Manchester) "Atlas Computer (Manchester)") machine.

In 1954, [FORTRAN](/wiki/FORTRAN "FORTRAN") was invented at IBM by a team led by [John Backus](/wiki/John_Backus "John Backus"); it was the first widely used high-level general-purpose language to have a functional implementation, in contrast to only a design on paper.[[9]](#cite_note-9)[[10]](#cite_note-10) When FORTRAN was first introduced, it was viewed with skepticism due to bugs, delays in development, and the comparative efficiency of "hand-coded" programs written in assembly.[[11]](#cite_note-11) However, in a hardware market that was rapidly evolving, the language eventually became known for its efficiency. It is still a popular language for [high-performance computing](/wiki/High-performance_computing "High-performance computing")[[12]](#cite_note-hpc-12) and is used for programs that benchmark and rank the world's [TOP500](/wiki/TOP500 "TOP500") fastest supercomputers.[[13]](#cite_note-13)

Another early programming language was devised by [Grace Hopper](/wiki/Grace_Hopper "Grace Hopper") in the US, named [FLOW-MATIC](/wiki/FLOW-MATIC "FLOW-MATIC"). It was developed for the [UNIVAC I](/wiki/UNIVAC_I "UNIVAC I") at [Remington Rand](/wiki/Remington_Rand "Remington Rand") during the period from 1955 until 1959. Hopper found that business [data processing](/wiki/Data_processing "Data processing") customers were uncomfortable with [mathematical notation](/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation"), and in early 1955, she and her team wrote a specification for an [English language](/wiki/English_language "English language") programming language and implemented a prototype.[[14]](#cite_note-14) The FLOW-MATIC compiler became publicly available in early 1958 and was substantially complete in 1959.[[15]](#cite_note-15) Flow-Matic was a major influence in the design of [COBOL](/wiki/COBOL "COBOL"), since only it and its direct descendant [AIMACO](/wiki/AIMACO "AIMACO") were in use at the time.[[16]](#cite_note-16)

Other languages still in use today include [LISP](/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language) "Lisp (programming language)") (1958), invented by [John McCarthy](/wiki/John_McCarthy_(computer_scientist) "John McCarthy (computer scientist)"), and [COBOL](/wiki/COBOL "COBOL") (1960), created by the Short Range Committee. Another milestone in the late 1950s was the publication, by a committee of American and European computer scientists, of "a new language for algorithms"; the *[ALGOL](/wiki/ALGOL "ALGOL") 60 Report* (the "**ALGO**rithmic **L**anguage"). This report consolidated many ideas circulating at the time and featured three key language innovations:

* nested block structure: code sequences and associated declarations could be grouped into [blocks](/wiki/Block_(programming) "Block (programming)") without having to be turned into separate, explicitly named procedures;
* [lexical scoping](/wiki/Scope_(programming) "Scope (programming)"): a block could have its own private variables, procedures, and functions, invisible to code outside that block, that is, [information hiding](/wiki/Information_hiding "Information hiding").

Another innovation, related to this, was in how the language was described:

* a mathematically exact notation, [Backus–Naur form](/wiki/Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form "Backus–Naur form") (BNF), was used to describe the language's syntax. Nearly all subsequent programming languages have used a variant of BNF to describe the [context-free](/wiki/Context-free_grammar "Context-free grammar") portion of their syntax.

[ALGOL 60](/wiki/ALGOL_60 "ALGOL 60") was particularly influential in the design of later languages, some of which soon became more popular. The [Burroughs large systems](/wiki/Burroughs_large_systems "Burroughs large systems") were designed to be programmed in an extended subset of ALGOL.

ALGOL's key ideas were continued, producing [ALGOL 68](/wiki/ALGOL_68 "ALGOL 68"):

* syntax and [semantics](/wiki/Semantics_(computer_science) "Semantics (computer science)") became even more orthogonal, with anonymous routines, a recursive typing system with higher-order functions, etc.;
* not only the context-free part, but the full language syntax and semantics were defined formally, in terms of [Van Wijngaarden grammar](/wiki/Van_Wijngaarden_grammar "Van Wijngaarden grammar"), a formalism designed specifically for this purpose.

ALGOL 68's many little-used language features (for example, concurrent and parallel blocks) and its complex system of syntactic shortcuts and automatic type coercions made it unpopular with implementers and gained it a reputation for being difficult. [Niklaus Wirth](/wiki/Niklaus_Wirth "Niklaus Wirth") actually walked out of the design committee to create the simpler [Pascal](/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language) "Pascal (programming language)") language.

The period from the late 1960s to the late 1970s brought a major flowering of programming languages. Most of the major language [paradigms](/wiki/Programming_paradigm "Programming paradigm") now in use were invented in this period:[*[original research?](/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research "Wikipedia:No original research")*]

* **[Speakeasy](/wiki/Speakeasy_(computational_environment) "Speakeasy (computational environment)")**, developed in 1964 at [Argonne National Laboratory](/wiki/Argonne_National_Laboratory "Argonne National Laboratory") (ANL) by [Stanley Cohen](/wiki/Stanley_Cohen_(physicist) "Stanley Cohen (physicist)"), is an [object-oriented programming](/wiki/Object-oriented_programming "Object-oriented programming") system (OOPS), much like the later [MATLAB](/wiki/MATLAB "MATLAB"), [IDL](/wiki/IDL_(programming_language) "IDL (programming language)") and [Mathematica](/wiki/Mathematica "Mathematica") numerical package. Speakeasy has a clear [Fortran](/wiki/Fortran "Fortran") foundation syntax. It first addressed efficient physics computing internally at ANL, was modified for research use (as "Modeleasy") for the [Federal Reserve Board](/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Board "Federal Reserve Board") in the early 1970s and then was made available commercially; Speakeasy and Modeleasy are still in use.
* **[Simula](/wiki/Simula "Simula")**, invented in the late 1960s by [Nygaard](/wiki/Kristen_Nygaard "Kristen Nygaard") and [Dahl](/wiki/Ole-Johan_Dahl "Ole-Johan Dahl") as a superset of ALGOL 60, was the first language designed to support [object-oriented programming](/wiki/Object-oriented_programming "Object-oriented programming").
* **[FORTH](/wiki/Forth_(programming_language) "Forth (programming language)")**, the earliest [concatenative programming language](/wiki/Concatenative_programming_language "Concatenative programming language") was designed by Charles Moore in 1969 as a personal development system while at the [National Radio Astronomy Observatory](/wiki/National_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory "National Radio Astronomy Observatory") (NRAO).
* **[C](/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)")**, an early [systems programming](/wiki/System_programming "System programming") language, was developed by [Dennis Ritchie](/wiki/Dennis_Ritchie "Dennis Ritchie") and [Ken Thompson](/wiki/Ken_Thompson "Ken Thompson") at [Bell Labs](/wiki/Bell_Labs "Bell Labs") between 1969 and 1973.
* **[Smalltalk](/wiki/Smalltalk "Smalltalk")** (mid-1970s) provided a complete ground-up design of an object-oriented language.
* **[Prolog](/wiki/Prolog "Prolog")**, designed in 1972 by [Alain Colmerauer](/wiki/Alain_Colmerauer "Alain Colmerauer"), Phillipe Roussel, and [Robert Kowalski](/wiki/Robert_Kowalski "Robert Kowalski"), was the first [logic programming](/wiki/Logic_programming "Logic programming") language.
* **[ML](/wiki/ML_(programming_language) "ML (programming language)")** built a polymorphic type system (invented by [Robin Milner](/wiki/Robin_Milner "Robin Milner") in 1973) on Lisp,[[17]](#cite_note-Gordon1996-17) pioneering [statically typed](/wiki/Type_system "Type system") [functional programming](/wiki/Functional_programming "Functional programming") languages.

Each of these languages spawned an entire family of descendants, and most modern languages count at least one of them in their ancestry.

The 1960s and 1970s also saw considerable debate over the merits of "[structured programming](/wiki/Structured_programming "Structured programming")", which essentially meant programming without the use of `goto`. A significant fraction of programmers believed that, even in languages that provide `goto`, it is bad [programming style](/wiki/Programming_style "Programming style") to use it except in rare circumstances. This debate was closely related to language design: some languages had no `goto`, which forced the use of structured programming.

To provide even faster compile times, some languages were structured for "[one-pass compilers](/wiki/One-pass_compiler "One-pass compiler")" which expect subordinate routines to be defined first, as with [Pascal](/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language) "Pascal (programming language)"), where the main routine, or driver function, is the final section of the program listing.

Some notable languages that were developed in this period include:

* 1967 – [BCPL](/wiki/BCPL "BCPL") (forerunner to B)
* 1967 – [Logo](/wiki/Logo_(programming_language) "Logo (programming language)")
* 1969 – [B](/wiki/B_(programming_language) "B (programming language)") (forerunner to C)
* 1970 – [Pascal](/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language) "Pascal (programming language)")
* 1970 – [Forth](/wiki/Forth_(programming_language) "Forth (programming language)")
* 1972 – [C](/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)")
* 1972 – [Smalltalk](/wiki/Smalltalk "Smalltalk")
* 1972 – [Prolog](/wiki/Prolog "Prolog")
* 1973 – [ML](/wiki/ML_(programming_language) "ML (programming language)")
* 1975 – [Scheme](/wiki/Scheme_(programming_language) "Scheme (programming language)")
* 1978 – [SQL](/wiki/SQL "SQL") (a query language, later extended)

C++

The 1980s were years of relative consolidation in [imperative languages](/wiki/Imperative_language "Imperative language"). Rather than inventing new paradigms, all of these movements elaborated upon the ideas invented in the prior decade. [C++](/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++") combined object-oriented and systems programming. The United States government standardized [Ada](/wiki/Ada_(programming_language) "Ada (programming language)"), a systems programming language intended for use by defense contractors. In Japan and elsewhere, vast sums were spent investigating so-called [fifth-generation programming languages](/wiki/Fifth-generation_programming_language "Fifth-generation programming language") that incorporated logic programming constructs. The functional languages community moved to standardize ML and Lisp. Research in [Miranda](/wiki/Miranda_(programming_language) "Miranda (programming language)"), a functional language with [lazy evaluation](/wiki/Lazy_evaluation "Lazy evaluation"), began to take hold in this decade.

One important new trend in language design was an increased focus on programming for large-scale systems through the use of *modules*, or large-scale organizational units of code. [Modula](/wiki/Modula "Modula"), Ada, and ML all developed notable module systems in the 1980s. Module systems were often wedded to [generic programming](/wiki/Generic_programming "Generic programming") constructs: generics being, in essence, parametrized modules[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] (see also [Polymorphism (computer science)](/wiki/Polymorphism_(computer_science) "Polymorphism (computer science)")).

Although major new paradigms for imperative programming languages did not appear, many researchers expanded on the ideas of prior languages and adapted them to new contexts. For example, the languages of the [Argus](/wiki/Argus_(computer_system) "Argus (computer system)") and Emerald systems adapted object-oriented programming to [distributed computing](/wiki/Distributed_computing "Distributed computing") systems.

The 1980s also brought advances in programming language implementation. The [reduced instruction set computer](/wiki/Reduced_instruction_set_computer "Reduced instruction set computer") (RISC) movement in [computer architecture](/wiki/Computer_architecture "Computer architecture") postulated that hardware should be designed for [compilers](/wiki/Compiler "Compiler") rather than for human assembly programmers. Aided by [central processing unit](/wiki/Central_processing_unit "Central processing unit") (CPU) speed improvements that enabled increasingly aggressive compiling methods, the RISC movement sparked greater interest in compiler technology for high-level languages.

Language technology continued along these lines well into the 1990s.

Some notable languages that were developed in this period include:

* 1984 – [Common Lisp](/wiki/Common_Lisp "Common Lisp")
* 1984 – [MATLAB](/wiki/MATLAB "MATLAB")
* 1984 – dBase III, dBase III Plus ([Clipper](/wiki/Clipper_(programming_language) "Clipper (programming language)") and [FoxPro](/wiki/FoxPro "FoxPro") as [FoxBASE](/wiki/FoxBASE "FoxBASE"))
* 1985 – [Eiffel](/wiki/Eiffel_(programming_language) "Eiffel (programming language)")
* 1986 – [Objective-C](/wiki/Objective-C "Objective-C")
* 1986 – [LabVIEW](/wiki/LabVIEW "LabVIEW") ([visual programming language](/wiki/Visual_programming_language "Visual programming language"))
* 1986 – [Erlang](/wiki/Erlang_(programming_language) "Erlang (programming language)")
* 1987 – [Perl](/wiki/Perl "Perl")
* 1988 – [PIC (markup language)](/wiki/PIC_(markup_language) "PIC (markup language)")
* 1988 – [Tcl](/wiki/Tcl_(programming_language) "Tcl (programming language)")
* 1988 – [Wolfram Language](/wiki/Wolfram_Language "Wolfram Language") (as part of [Mathematica](/wiki/Mathematica "Mathematica"), only got a separate name in June 2013)

The rapid growth of the Internet in the mid-1990s was the next major historic event in programming languages. By opening up a radically new platform for computer systems, the Internet created an opportunity for new languages to be adopted. The [JavaScript](/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript") language rose rapidly to popularity because of its early integration with the Netscape Navigator web browser. Various other scripting languages achieved widespread use in developing customized applications for web servers such as PHP. The 1990s saw no fundamental novelty in [imperative programming](/wiki/Imperative_programming "Imperative programming") languages, but much recombining and maturing of old ideas. This era began the spread of [functional programming](/wiki/Functional_programming "Functional programming") languages. A big driving philosophy was programmer productivity. Many [rapid application development](/wiki/Rapid_application_development "Rapid application development") (RAD) languages emerged, which usually came with an [integrated development environment](/wiki/Integrated_development_environment "Integrated development environment") (IDE), [garbage collection](/wiki/Garbage_collection_(computer_science) "Garbage collection (computer science)"), and were descendants of older languages. All such languages were [object-oriented](/wiki/Object-oriented_programming "Object-oriented programming"). These included [Object Pascal](/wiki/Object_Pascal "Object Pascal"), Objective Caml (renamed [OCaml](/wiki/OCaml "OCaml")), [Visual Basic](/wiki/Visual_Basic_(classic) "Visual Basic (classic)"), and [Java](/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)"). Java received much attention.

More radical and innovative than the RAD languages were the new [scripting languages](/wiki/Scripting_language "Scripting language"). These did not directly descend from other languages and featured new syntaxes and more liberal incorporation of features. Many consider these scripting languages to be more productive than even the RAD languages, but often because of choices that make small programs simpler but large programs more difficult to write and maintain.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] Nevertheless, scripting languages came to be the most prominent ones used relative to the Web.

Some programming languages included other languages in their distribution to save the development time. For example, both of [Python](/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)") and [Ruby](/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) "Ruby (programming language)") included [Tcl](/wiki/Tcl_(programming_language) "Tcl (programming language)") to support [graphical user interface](/wiki/Graphical_user_interface "Graphical user interface") (GUI) programming through libraries like [Tkinter](/wiki/Tkinter "Tkinter").

Programming language evolution continues with the rise of new programming domains.

* Increased interest in distribution and mobility.
* Integration with databases, including [XML](/wiki/XML "XML") and [relational databases](/wiki/Relational_database "Relational database").
* [Open source](/wiki/Open_source "Open source") as a developmental philosophy for languages, including the [GNU Compiler Collection](/wiki/GNU_Compiler_Collection "GNU Compiler Collection") (GCC) and languages such as [PHP](/wiki/PHP "PHP"), [Python](/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)"), [Ruby](/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) "Ruby (programming language)"), and [Scala](/wiki/Scala_(programming_language) "Scala (programming language)").
* Massively parallel languages for [graphics processing units](/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit "Graphics processing unit") (GPUs) and [supercomputer](/wiki/Supercomputer "Supercomputer") arrays, including [OpenCL](/wiki/OpenCL "OpenCL")
* Early research into [quantum computing](/wiki/Quantum_computing "Quantum computing") and [quantum programming](/wiki/Quantum_programming "Quantum programming") languages
* Multiple new programming languages tried to provide a modern replacement for the C programming language.
* Many new programming languages are influenced by the popular dynamic languages and promised adding type safety without decreasing the productivity.
* Many new programming languages uses [LLVM](/wiki/LLVM "LLVM") in their implementation.

Many [Big Tech](/wiki/Big_Tech "Big Tech") companies continued introducing new programming languages that are designed to serve their needs and provides first-class support for their platforms. for example:

* [Microsoft](/wiki/Microsoft "Microsoft") introduced [TypeScript](/wiki/TypeScript "TypeScript"), [Q#](/wiki/Q_Sharp "Q Sharp") and [Bosque](/wiki/Bosque_(programming_language) "Bosque (programming language)")
* [Google](/wiki/Google "Google") introduced [Dart](/wiki/Dart_(programming_language) "Dart (programming language)")
* [Apple](/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.") introduced [Swift](/wiki/Swift_(programming_language) "Swift (programming language)").
* [Meta](/wiki/Meta_Platforms "Meta Platforms") introduced [Hack](/wiki/Hack_(programming_language) "Hack (programming language)").

Some notable languages developed during this period include:[[18]](#cite_note-TIOBE_Index-18)[[19]](#cite_note-GitHub's_Octoverse_2018-19)

* 2011 – [Dart](/wiki/Dart_(programming_language) "Dart (programming language)")
* 2011 – [Kotlin](/wiki/Kotlin "Kotlin")
* 2012 – [Julia](/wiki/Julia_(programming_language) "Julia (programming language)")
* 2012 – [TypeScript](/wiki/TypeScript "TypeScript")
* 2012 – [Elixir](/wiki/Elixir_(programming_language) "Elixir (programming language)")
* 2014 – [Swift](/wiki/Swift_(programming_language) "Swift (programming language)")
* 2014 – [Hack](/wiki/Hack_(programming_language) "Hack (programming language)")
* 2015 – [Rust](/wiki/Rust_(programming_language) "Rust (programming language)")
* 2015 – [Raku](/wiki/Raku_(programming_language) "Raku (programming language)")
* 2016 – [Zig](/wiki/Zig_(programming_language) "Zig (programming language)")

Other new programming languages include [Elm](/wiki/Elm_(programming_language) "Elm (programming language)"), [Ballerina](/wiki/Ballerina_(programming_language) "Ballerina (programming language)"), [Red](/wiki/Red_(programming_language) "Red (programming language)"), [Crystal](/wiki/Crystal_(programming_language) "Crystal (programming language)"), [V (Vlang)](/wiki/V_(programming_language) "V (programming language)"), [Reason](/wiki/Reason_(programming_language) "Reason (programming language)").

Some key people who helped develop programming languages:

* [Ada Lovelace](/wiki/Ada_Lovelace "Ada Lovelace"), published first computer program
* [Alan Cooper](/wiki/Alan_Cooper_(software_designer) "Alan Cooper (software designer)"), developer of [Visual Basic](/wiki/Visual_Basic_(classic) "Visual Basic (classic)").
* [Alan Kay](/wiki/Alan_Kay "Alan Kay"), pioneering work on object-oriented programming, and originator of [Smalltalk](/wiki/Smalltalk "Smalltalk").
* [Anders Hejlsberg](/wiki/Anders_Hejlsberg "Anders Hejlsberg"), developer of [Turbo Pascal](/wiki/Turbo_Pascal "Turbo Pascal"), [Delphi](/wiki/Delphi_(software) "Delphi (software)"), [C#](/wiki/C_Sharp_(programming_language) "C Sharp (programming language)"), and [TypeScript](/wiki/TypeScript "TypeScript").
* [Arthur Whitney](/wiki/Arthur_Whitney_(computer_scientist) "Arthur Whitney (computer scientist)"), developer of [A+](/wiki/A%2B_(programming_language) "A+ (programming language)"), [k](/wiki/K_(programming_language) "K (programming language)"), and [q](/wiki/Q_(programming_language_from_Kx_Systems) "Q (programming language from Kx Systems)").
* [Bertrand Meyer](/wiki/Bertrand_Meyer "Bertrand Meyer"), inventor of [Eiffel](/wiki/Eiffel_(programming_language) "Eiffel (programming language)").
* [Bjarne Stroustrup](/wiki/Bjarne_Stroustrup "Bjarne Stroustrup"), developer of [C++](/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++").
* [Brad Cox](/wiki/Brad_Cox "Brad Cox"), co-creator of [Objective-C](/wiki/Objective-C "Objective-C").
* [Brendan Eich](/wiki/Brendan_Eich "Brendan Eich"), developer of [JavaScript](/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript").
* [Brian Kernighan](/wiki/Brian_Kernighan "Brian Kernighan"), co-author of the first book on the [C](/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)") programming language with [Dennis Ritchie](/wiki/Dennis_Ritchie "Dennis Ritchie"), coauthor of the [AWK](/wiki/AWK "AWK") and [AMPL](/wiki/AMPL_(programming_language) "AMPL (programming language)") programming languages.
* [Chuck Moore](/wiki/Charles_H._Moore "Charles H. Moore"), inventor of [Forth](/wiki/Forth_(programming_language) "Forth (programming language)"), the first concatenative programming language, and a prominent name in [stack machine](/wiki/Stack_machine "Stack machine") microprocessor design.
* [Chris Lattner](/wiki/Chris_Lattner "Chris Lattner"), creator of [Swift](/wiki/Swift_(programming_language) "Swift (programming language)"), [Mojo](/wiki/Mojo_(programming_language) "Mojo (programming language)") and [Clang](/wiki/Clang "Clang")/[LLVM](/wiki/LLVM "LLVM").
* [Cleve Moler](/wiki/Cleve_Moler "Cleve Moler"), creator of [MATLAB](/wiki/MATLAB "MATLAB").
* [Dennis Ritchie](/wiki/Dennis_Ritchie "Dennis Ritchie"), inventor of [C](/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)"). Unix Operating System, Plan 9 Operating System.
* [Douglas McIlroy](/wiki/Douglas_McIlroy "Douglas McIlroy"), influenced and designed such languages as [SNOBOL](/wiki/SNOBOL "SNOBOL"), [TRAC](/wiki/TRAC_(programming_language) "TRAC (programming language)"), [PL/I](/wiki/PL/I "PL/I"), [ALTRAN](/wiki/ALTRAN "ALTRAN"), [TMG](/wiki/TMG_(language) "TMG (language)") and [C++](/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++").
* [Grace Hopper](/wiki/Grace_Hopper "Grace Hopper"), first to use the term [compiler](/wiki/Compiler "Compiler") and developer of [FLOW-MATIC](/wiki/FLOW-MATIC "FLOW-MATIC"), influenced development of [COBOL](/wiki/COBOL "COBOL"). Popularized machine-independent programming languages and the term "[debugging](/wiki/Debugging "Debugging")".
* [Guido van Rossum](/wiki/Guido_van_Rossum "Guido van Rossum"), creator of [Python](/wiki/Python_(programming_language) "Python (programming language)").
* [James Gosling](/wiki/James_Gosling "James Gosling"), lead developer of [Java](/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)") and its precursor, [Oak](/wiki/Oak_(programming_language) "Oak (programming language)").
* [Jean Ichbiah](/wiki/Jean_Ichbiah "Jean Ichbiah"), chief designer of [Ada](/wiki/Ada_(programming_language) "Ada (programming language)"), [Ada 83](/wiki/Ada_83 "Ada 83").
* [Jean-Yves Girard](/wiki/Jean-Yves_Girard "Jean-Yves Girard"), co-inventor of the [polymorphic lambda calculus](/wiki/Polymorphic_lambda_calculus "Polymorphic lambda calculus") (System F).
* [Jeff Bezanson](/wiki/Jeff_Bezanson "Jeff Bezanson"), main designer, and one of the core developers of [Julia](/wiki/Julia_(programming_language) "Julia (programming language)").
* [Jeffrey Snover](/wiki/Jeffrey_Snover "Jeffrey Snover"), inventor of [PowerShell](/wiki/PowerShell "PowerShell").
* [Joe Armstrong](/wiki/Joe_Armstrong_(programming) "Joe Armstrong (programming)"), creator of [Erlang](/wiki/Erlang_(programming_language) "Erlang (programming language)").
* [John Backus](/wiki/John_Backus "John Backus"), inventor of [Fortran](/wiki/Fortran "Fortran"), cooperated in designing [ALGOL 58](/wiki/ALGOL_58 "ALGOL 58") and [ALGOL 60](/wiki/ALGOL_60 "ALGOL 60").
* [John C. Reynolds](/wiki/John_C._Reynolds "John C. Reynolds"), co-inventor of the polymorphic lambda calculus (System F).
* [John McCarthy](/wiki/John_McCarthy_(computer_scientist) "John McCarthy (computer scientist)"), inventor of [LISP](/wiki/Lisp_(programming_language) "Lisp (programming language)"), design committee of [ALGOL 60](/wiki/ALGOL_60 "ALGOL 60").
* [John von Neumann](/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann"), originator of the [operating system](/wiki/Operating_system "Operating system") concept.
* [Graydon Hoare](/wiki/Rust_(programming_language) "Rust (programming language)"), inventor of [Rust](/wiki/Rust_(programming_language) "Rust (programming language)").
* [Ken Thompson](/wiki/Ken_Thompson "Ken Thompson"), inventor of [B](/wiki/B_(programming_language) "B (programming language)") and [Go](/wiki/Go_(programming_language) "Go (programming language)").
* [Kenneth E. Iverson](/wiki/Kenneth_E._Iverson "Kenneth E. Iverson"), developer of [APL](/wiki/APL_(programming_language) "APL (programming language)"), co-developer of [J](/wiki/J_(programming_language) "J (programming language)") with [Roger Hui](/wiki/Roger_Hui "Roger Hui").
* [Konrad Zuse](/wiki/Konrad_Zuse "Konrad Zuse"), designed the first [high-level programming language](/wiki/High-level_programming_language "High-level programming language"), [Plankalkül](/wiki/Plankalk%C3%BCl "Plankalkül") (which influenced [ALGOL 58](/wiki/ALGOL_58 "ALGOL 58")[[20]](#cite_note-20)).
* [Kristen Nygaard](/wiki/Kristen_Nygaard "Kristen Nygaard"), pioneered object-oriented programming, co-invented [Simula](/wiki/Simula "Simula").
* [Larry Wall](/wiki/Larry_Wall "Larry Wall"), creator of the Perl programming language (see [Perl](/wiki/Perl "Perl") and [Raku](/wiki/Raku_(programming_language) "Raku (programming language)")).
* [Martin Odersky](/wiki/Martin_Odersky "Martin Odersky"), creator of [Scala](/wiki/Scala_(programming_language) "Scala (programming language)"), and previously a contributor to the design of [Java](/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)").
* [Martin Richards](/wiki/Martin_Richards_(computer_scientist) "Martin Richards (computer scientist)") developed the [BCPL](/wiki/BCPL "BCPL") programming language, forerunner of the [B](/wiki/B_(programming_language) "B (programming language)") and [C](/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)") languages.
* [Nathaniel Rochester](/wiki/Nathaniel_Rochester_(computer_scientist) "Nathaniel Rochester (computer scientist)"), inventor of first [assembler](/wiki/Assembler_(computing) "Assembler (computing)") (IBM 701).
* [Niklaus Wirth](/wiki/Niklaus_Wirth "Niklaus Wirth"), inventor of [Pascal](/wiki/Pascal_(programming_language) "Pascal (programming language)"), [Modula](/wiki/Modula "Modula") and [Oberon](/wiki/Oberon_(programming_language) "Oberon (programming language)").
* [Ole-Johan Dahl](/wiki/Ole-Johan_Dahl "Ole-Johan Dahl"), pioneered object-oriented programming, co-invented [Simula](/wiki/Simula "Simula").
* [Rasmus Lerdorf](/wiki/Rasmus_Lerdorf "Rasmus Lerdorf"), creator of [PHP](/wiki/PHP "PHP").
* [Rich Hickey](/wiki/Rich_Hickey "Rich Hickey"), creator of [Clojure](/wiki/Clojure "Clojure").
* [Robert Gentleman](/wiki/Robert_Gentleman_(statistician) "Robert Gentleman (statistician)"), co-creator of [R](/wiki/R_(programming_language) "R (programming language)").
* [Robert Griesemer](/wiki/Robert_Griesemer "Robert Griesemer"), co-creator of [Go](/wiki/Go_(programming_language) "Go (programming language)").
* [Robin Milner](/wiki/Robin_Milner "Robin Milner"), inventor of [ML](/wiki/ML_(programming_language) "ML (programming language)"), and sharing credit for [Hindley–Milner](/wiki/Hindley%E2%80%93Milner "Hindley–Milner") [polymorphic](/wiki/Parametric_polymorphism "Parametric polymorphism") [type inference](/wiki/Type_inference "Type inference").
* [Rob Pike](/wiki/Rob_Pike "Rob Pike"), co-creator of [Go](/wiki/Go_(programming_language) "Go (programming language)"), [Inferno (operating system)](/wiki/Inferno_(operating_system) "Inferno (operating system)") and [Plan 9 (operating system)](/wiki/Plan_9_(operating_system) "Plan 9 (operating system)") Operating System co-author.
* [Ross Ihaka](/wiki/Ross_Ihaka "Ross Ihaka"), co-creator of [R](/wiki/R_(programming_language) "R (programming language)").
* [Stanley Cohen](/wiki/Stanley_Cohen_(physicist) "Stanley Cohen (physicist)"), inventor of [Speakeasy](/wiki/Speakeasy_(computational_environment) "Speakeasy (computational environment)"), which was created with an OOPS, object-oriented programming system, the first instance, in 1964.
* [Stephen Wolfram](/wiki/Stephen_Wolfram "Stephen Wolfram"), creator of [Mathematica](/wiki/Mathematica "Mathematica").
* [Walter Bright](/wiki/Walter_Bright "Walter Bright"), creator of [D](/wiki/D_(programming_language) "D (programming language)").
* [Yukihiro Matsumoto](/wiki/Yukihiro_Matsumoto "Yukihiro Matsumoto"), creator of [Ruby](/wiki/Ruby_(programming_language) "Ruby (programming language)").

# Computer History: A Timeline of Computer Programming Languages

August 15, 2023

In today's world, computer programming is required to keep the systems and devices we use every day operating smoothly. Programming languages enable humans to interact with machines and make them perform necessary operations. Humans and machines process information differently, and programming languages are the key to bridging the gap between people and computers.

**1883:** The first [programming language](https://devskiller.com/history-of-programming-languages/) was developed in 1883 when [Ada Lovelace](https://www.sdsc.edu/ScienceWomen/lovelace.html) and Charles Babbage worked together on the Analytical Engine, which was a primitive mechanical computer. Lovelace was able to discern the importance of numbers, realizing that they could represent more than just numerical values of things. Lovelace wrote an algorithm for the Analytical Engine, the first computer program, to compute Bernoulli numbers.

* [Ada and the First Computer](http://www.cs.virginia.edu/~robins/Ada_and_the_First_Computer.pdf)

**1949**: Assembly language was first used as a type of computer programming language that was able to simplify machine code language, which is necessary for telling a computer what to do.

**1952**: Alick Glennie developed Autocode, which some consider to be the first compiled computer programming language. This means it could be translated directly into machine code.

**1957**: [John Backus](http://www.columbia.edu/cu/computinghistory/backus.html) created FORTRAN, which is a computer programming language for working with scientific, mathematical, and statistical projects.

* [John Backus](https://lemelson.mit.edu/resources/john-backus)

**1958**: Algol was created as an algorithmic language. It was also a precursor to programming languages such as Java and C.

**1959:** [COBOL](https://americanhistory.si.edu/cobol/introduction) was created by Dr. Grace Murray Hopper to be a language that could operate on all types of computers.

* [Grace Murray Hopper](https://news.yale.edu/2017/02/10/grace-murray-hopper-1906-1992-legacy-innovation-and-service)

**1959**: [John McCarthy created LISP](http://jmc.stanford.edu/articles/lisp/lisp.pdf), which is still used today. This programming language was designed for use in artificial intelligence research, and today, it can be used with Python and Ruby.

* [The Roots of LISP](http://languagelog.ldc.upenn.edu/myl/llog/jmc.pdf)

**1964**: John G. Kemeny and Thomas E. Kurtz developed [BASIC](https://www.uopeople.edu/blog/6-reasons-why-you-should-learn-basic-programming/) for students without a strong background in technology and math, enabling them to still use computers.

**1970**: Niklaus Wirth developed [Pascal](http://p.web.umkc.edu/pynzpf/CS%20441/proj%201/history_of_pascal.htm), naming it after Blaise Pascal. This language is easy to learn and was the main language used by Apple for early software development.

* [The Pascal Language Page](http://groups.umd.umich.edu/cis/course.des/cis400/pascal/pascal.html)

**1972**: Alan Kay, Adele Goldberg, and Dan Ingalls developed Smalltalk, which enabled computer programmers to change code quickly.

**1972**: Dennis Ritchie developed C, generally regarded as the first high-level programming language. This means that it's closer to human language and less like machine code.

* [The C Programming Language](http://users.ece.utexas.edu/~valvano/Volume1/E-Book/C5_IntroductionToC.htm)

**1972**: Donald D. Chamberlin and Raymond F. Boyce developed [SQL](https://www.codecademy.com/articles/sql-commands?r=master) for IBM. This language was used for viewing and changing data stored in databases.

* [Introduction to SQL](https://www.w3schools.com/sql/sql_intro.asp)

# The Evolution of Programming Languages

Last Updated : 11 Jul, 2022

Programming Language is indeed the fundamental unit of today's tech world. It is considered as the set of commands and instructions that we give to the machines to perform a particular task. For example, if you give some set of instructions to add two numbers then the machine will do it for you and tell you the correct answer accordingly. But do you know that Programming Languages are having a long and rich history of their evolution? And with a similar concern, here in this article, we'll take a look at the evolution of Programming Languages over the period.

In the computer world, we have about 500+ programming languages with having their own syntax and features. And if you type who's the father of the computer, then the search engine will show you the result as to Charles Babbage but the father of the computer didn't write the first code. It was Ada Lovelace who has written the first-ever computer programming language and the year was 1883.

**1883: The Journey starts from here...!!**

* In the early days, Charles Babbage had made the device, but he was confused about how to give instructions to the machine, and then Ada Lovelace wrote the instructions for the analytical engine.
* The device was made by Charles Babbage and the code was written by Ada Lovelace for computing Bernoulli's number.
* First time in history that the capability of computer devices was judged.

**1949: Assembly Language**

* It is a type of low-level language.
* It mainly consists of instructions (kind of symbols) that only machines could understand.
* In today's time also assembly language is used in real-time programs such as simulation flight navigation systems and medical equipment eg - Fly-by-wire (FBW) systems.
* It is also used to create computer viruses.

**1952: Autocode**

* Developed by Alick Glennie.
* The first compiled computer programming language.
* COBOL and FORTRAN are the languages referred to as Autocode.

**1957: FORTRAN**

* Developers are John Backus and IBM.
* It was designed for numeric computation and scientific computing.
* Software for NASA probes voyager-1 (space probe) and voyager-2 (space probe) was originally written in FORTRAN 5.

**1958: ALGOL**

* ALGOL stands for **ALGO**rithmic **L**anguage.
* The initial phase of the most popular programming languages of C, C++, and JAVA.
* It was also the first language implementing the nested function and has a simple syntax than FORTRAN.
* The first programming language to have a code block like "begin" that indicates that your program has started and "end" means you have ended your code.

**1959: COBOL**

* It stands for **CO**mmon **B**usiness-**O**riented **L**anguage.
* In 1997, 80% of the world's business ran on Cobol.
* The US internal revenue service scrambled its path to COBOL-based IMF (individual master file) in order to pay the tens of millions of payments mandated by the coronavirus aid, relief, and economic security.

**1964: BASIC**

* It stands for beginners All-purpose symbolic instruction code.
* In 1991 Microsoft released Visual Basic, an updated version of Basic
* The first microcomputer version of Basic was co-written by Bill Gates, Paul Allen, and Monte Davidoff for their newly-formed company, Microsoft.

**1972: C**

* It is a general-purpose, procedural programming language and the most popular programming language till now.
* All the code that was previously written in assembly language gets replaced by the C language like operating system, kernel, and many other applications.
* It can be used in implementing an operating system, embedded system, and also on the website using the Common Gateway Interface (CGI).
* C is the mother of almost all higher-level programming languages like C#, D, Go, Java, JavaScript, Limbo, LPC, Perl, PHP, Python, and Unix's C shell.

# Coding From 1849 to 2022: a Guide to The Timeline of Programming Languages

It starts with surprising and remarkable people, long before the birth of other types of commonplace technology like cars and telephones. It even predates modern medical treatments we take for granted, such as insulin and antibiotics. In fact, coding dates back to the 1840s.

Let’s take a closer look at the history of coding and the timeline of programming languages.

## Origins

### 1843 Ada Lovelace

![Ada Lovelace](/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fmain-cdn.computer.org%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2022%2F06%2FTimeline-of-Programming-Languages-2-200x300.png&w=640&q=75)Ada Lovelace was a British aristocrat; unusually for a woman of her time, she was educated in mathematics and science. However, her contribution to the development of coding and computer languages is not that she invented a device that was the breakthrough of Charles Babbage in the 1820s with his Difference Machine. Instead, Lovelace created the very first machine algorithm in 1843.

![Ada Lovelace](/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fmain-cdn.computer.org%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2022%2F06%2FTimeline-of-Programming-Languages-2-200x300.png&w=640&q=75)

She realized that Babbage’s machine could do more than just calculate; it could be used as a general-purpose machine. Lovelace made the leap that numbers could represent other things, and the idea of programming languages was born. Lovelace’s groundbreaking work was the first step in being able to [design a language](/csdl/magazine/co/2012/02/mco2012020007/13rRUy08MzA) capable of giving computers instructions.

## The 20th Century

### 1936 Alan Turing

Today, Alan Turing is famous for his work on cracking Nazi Germany’s Enigma code machine in 1939. But in 1936, Turing published a paper regarded as the founding piece on computer science. Turing came up with the concept of a universal machine. A machine that could follow instructions. He eventually turned this groundbreaking idea into a plan for a computer powered by electricity that could run programs.

### 1940s Konrad Zuse

Konrad Zuse created what is considered the first programming language for computers in the early 1940s. It was called Plankalkul, and it could store codes, enabling engineers to carry out routine, repetitive tasks far more efficiently and quickly.

### 1949 Assembly Language and Shortcode

The next big development was Assembly language, a computer programming language that simplified the instructions to make a computer function. Also, in 1949 came Shortcode, used by

### 1952 Autocode

In 1952 Autocode was developed for the Mark 1 computer at the University of Manchester. It was the first language that could be translated into machine code.

### 1957 Fortran

John Backus created FORmula TRANslation or FORTRAN back in 1957. Incredibly, this programming language from the 1950s is still used today in supercomputers and scientific and mathematical computations.

### 1958 ALGOL and LISP

An algorithmic language created by American and European scientists, ALGOL became the point of origin for world-renowned programming languages such as Pascal, Java, C, and C++.

In the same year, John McCarthy invented the List processor or LISP. Intended for AI, companies like Boeing and Genworks are still using it.

### 1959 COBOL

COBOL was developed by a team led by Dr. Grace Murray Hopper; COBOL stands for Common Business Oriented Language. It was designed for credit card transaction processors, traffic signals, and phone calls, and today is used in banking and gaming. Today’s [virtual PBX system](https://www.ringcentral.co.uk/office/features/virtual-pbx/overview.html) and other modern solutions to communication rest on the shoulders of programming languages like COBOL.

### 1964 BASIC

Students developed BASIC at Dartmouth College. It stands for Beginners All-Purpose Symbolic Instruction Code. BASIC was then further developed and marketed as a product by Bill Gates.

### 1970 PASCAL

Niklaus Wirth developed this programming language and named it after Blaise Pascal, the French mathematician. So easy to learn, it was used by Apple early on in its history.

### 1972 Smalltalk, C, and SQL

Alan Kay, Adele Goldberg, and Dan Ingalls developed Smalltalk at the Xerox Palo Alto Research Centre. It was designed to modify code. Its principles are present in today’s Java, Python, and Ruby and are used by companies like CrowdStrike and Logitech.

C was also developed in 1972 by Dennis Ritchie at the Bell Telephone Laboratories. It was designed to be used with the Unix operating system. Based on its forerunner B, languages like C#, Java, JavaScript, Perl, PHP, and Python are all derived from C.

In what could be called a vintage year for coding and programming languages, Raymond Boyce and Donald Chamberlain developed SQL (Structured Query Language) the same year. It was first called SEQUEL, and it was used for viewing and altering information in databases.

These three languages represent a significant leap forward in programming, which has impacted some of the largest tech companies in existence today. Our whole way of using the digital world, from social media to how a business’s [lead referral program](https://affise.com/blog/referral-program-examples-to-fuel-your-customer-acquisition/) functions, owes a debt to these developments.

### 1980s ADA

![1980s ADA](/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fmain-cdn.computer.org%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2022%2F06%2FTimeline-of-Programming-Languages-4-300x196.png&w=640&q=75)Named after Ada Lovelace, the computing pioneer, ADA was designed by Jean Ichbiah’s team at CUU Honeywell Bull. Ada is a high-level programming language, extended from other popular programming languages such as Pascal. ADA is used for air-traffic control in various European countries and space programs.

![1980s ADA](/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fmain-cdn.computer.org%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2022%2F06%2FTimeline-of-Programming-Languages-4-300x196.png&w=640&q=75)

### 1983 C++, Objective C

Bjarne Stroustrup modified the C language at Bell Labs. The result was C++, which had some augmentations such as classes, templates, and virtual functions. C++ is now used in MS Office, Adobe Photoshop, and various types of high-performance software.

Brad Cox and Tom Love then developed Objective-C, the programming language used for Apple’s operating systems.

### 1987 Perl

Larry Wall developed PERL as a general-purpose, high-level language. He designed it for text editing, but today its primary use is in database applications, [data processing](/csdl/proceedings-article/icde/2019/747400b980/1aDSRSNEWQM), graphic programming, and system administration.

### 1990 Haskell

Named after the American mathematician Haskell Brooks Curry. Haskell is a primarily mathematical programming language. Used in various industries that need to make complicated calculations, crunch numbers, and keep records.

### 1991 Python and Visual Basic

The iconic British comedy act Monty Python inspired Python’s name. Guido Van Rossum developed this general-purpose, high-level language. Today Python is one of the most popular programming languages worldwide. Giants like Google and Spotify use it.

Visual Basic allows a user to drag and drop sections of code via a user interface (GUI). Parts of Visual Basic are used in applications like Word and Excel.

### 1993 Ruby

Yukihiro Matsumoto created Ruby as a high-level programming language. Used for web applications development, today it’s used by Groupon.

### 1995 Java, JavaScript, PHP

Created by James Gosling in 1995, Java is one of the world's most famous and popular programming languages. It’s a general-purpose, high-level language used in cell phones and parking machines.

![Modern Java](/_next/image?url=https%3A%2F%2Fmain-cdn.computer.org%2Fwp-content%2Fuploads%2F2022%2F06%2FTimeline-of-Programming-Languages-5-300x141.png&w=640&q=75)

JavaScript was created by Brendan Eich. Used for web development, PDF documents, and desktop widgets, it’s on almost every major website. Some famous examples are Adobe, Gmail, and Mozilla Firefox.

PHP was known as ‘Personal Home Page’ but now stands for ‘Hypertext Preprocessor.’ It was developed by Rasmus Lerdorf. It’s used for building and maintaining web pages and server-side development. Huge companies like Facebook, Wikipedia, and WordPress use PHP.

## The 21st Century

### 2000 C#

Microsoft developed C# in an attempt to combine elements of Visual Basic and C. It’s now used in all the company’s products.

### 2003 Scala and Groovy

In 2003 Martin Odersky created Scala. Its ability to work with Java gives it an advantage over Android development. Scala is used by social media and entertainment giants like Twitter, Linkedin, Twitter, and Netflix.

Groovy is an easy-to-learn language developed by James Strachan and Bob McWhirter. Starbucks, Craftbase, and Transferwise all use Groovy.

### 2009 Go (Google)

Large software systems face various challenges, and Google developed Go to resolve them.

Go is popular with big tech companies, such as Uber, Dropbox, and of course, Google. If you need to find out [what is vishing](https://www.ringcentral.co.uk/gb/en/blog/definitions/vishing-voice-or-voip-phishing/)? In part, you can thank Go for finding the answer.

### 2014 Swift

Apple developed Swift to replace C, C++, and Objective-C. The idea was that Swift would be easier and more versatile than these languages. Swift can be used for cloud applications, mobile, and desktop. Language platform Duolingo used it recently to launch a new app. Making apps that customers love using isn’t easy; these days, many companies use a [mobile app flowchart](https://www.globalapptesting.com/blog/how-to-build-a-mobile-app-that-customers-love).

## Today and Tomorrow

Since the early days of 19th-century pioneers and 20th-century groundbreakers, the world of computing has expanded into cloud computing, a dizzying array of apps, new technology, and devices.

Some programming languages like COBOL and Fortran are still used today, while others have been through various incarnations or fallen out of use.

Coding and computer languages will continue to develop and evolve, but just as Ada Lovelace probably couldn’t have imagined precisely what her ideas would mean for the centuries to come, who can say exactly what the next 180 years will hold.
