Key Points

About This Summary

- The AI chip market has significantly broadened beyond Nvidia, now including Intel, AMD, Arm, Marvell, and others, with new entrants like Qualcomm and Cerebras.

- Investors are rewarding the expanding AI chip sector, with the PHLX Semiconductor Index climbing almost 25% in April.

- The shift from AI model training to inference is driving explosive demand, particularly benefiting Intel, whose stock more than tripled.


[Nvidia](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/nvda?mod=article_chiclet) used to be the artificial-intelligence chip winner. Then it was Nvidia and [Broadcom](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/avgo?mod=article_chiclet). Now it feels like everyone has an AI chip, and they’re all being rewarded by the stock market.

Nvidia’s graphics-processing units (GPUs) dominated the early stages of the AI revolution, as they proved ideal for training the models behind chatbots like ChatGPT. Gradually, [custom chips](https://www.barrons.com/articles/google-tpu-nvidia-gpu-ai-chip-stocks-93dc908f?mod=article_inline) from the likes of Google—made in collaboration with Broadcom—began to make inroads.

But this year has seen a significant broadening of the market. Shares of [Marvell Technology](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/mrvl?mod=article_chiclet), [Intel](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/intc?mod=article_chiclet), [Arm Holdings](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/arm?mod=article_chiclet), and [Advanced Micro Devices](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/amd?mod=article_chiclet) have all posted strong gains in recent months. A bewildering mix of GPUs, CPUs, XPUs, TPUs, and NPUs are now powering AI for big technology companies. That’s not even including memory chips from [Micron Technology](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/mu?mod=article_chiclet) and its South Korean peers, or networking chips from the likes of [Cisco Systems](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/csco?mod=article_chiclet).

Investors appear to like the widening pool of AI players. The [PHLX Semiconductor Index](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/indexes/sox?mod=article_chiclet) rose 2.1% Friday, putting it on track to extend its winning streak to 13 days—its longest streak since 2014, according to Dow Jones Market Data.

All told, the PHLX has climbed more than 25% in April. If those gains hold, they would represent the chip index’s best month since November 2002.

The most significant driver of the change in the AI chip market has been the shift from [training to inference](https://www.barrons.com/articles/nvidia-stock-ai-chips-chatgpt-claude-inference-67bb3638?eafs_enabled=false), the process of running models. As has long been predicted, the growth of AI agents, or programs that can act autonomously, has led to an explosive increase in demand for inference computing.

The inference pivot has been particularly good news for Intel, which has gone from being seen as an AI laggard to seeing its stock more than triple over 12 months, driven by projected demand for its central-processing units in AI servers. Intel is a recent _Barron’s_ [stock pick](https://www.barrons.com/articles/buy-intel-stock-price-pick-b6f38b94?mod=article_inline).

“Commentary suggesting agentic AI could drive ~4x higher CPU needs per gigawatts vs prior architectures helps frame the magnitude of the CPU demand increase we are seeing today,” wrote Jefferies equity sales specialist William Beavington in a recent research note.

Even more players look set to join the market soon. [Qualcomm](https://www.barrons.com/market-data/stocks/qcom?mod=article_chiclet) has been a conspicuous laggard in the chip sector due to concerns about its core smartphone market, but has [unveiled](https://www.barrons.com/articles/qualcomm-ai-stock-price-news-d4e24986?eafs_enabled=false) its AI200 and AI250 rack servers for inference.

Chip start-up Cerebras Systems looks set to potentially list this year after delaying a planned initial public offering. ChatGPT developer OpenAI has already struck a multibillion-dollar agreement to buy computing capacity from Cerebras, according to The Wall Street Journal.
