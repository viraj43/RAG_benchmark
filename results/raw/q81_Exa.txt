# How to decide which skills to learn next for career growth
[...]
Developing in-demand skills can improve your career trajectory. Explore how you can learn skills for a new job or career advancement.
[...]
Your education does not end when you get your first job. Employers expect you to stay on top of the latest knowledge and skills you need for professional success. Developing these abilities may require extra college courses, a certificate, or an advanced degree.
[...]
## How to decide which skills to build next
[...]
### 1. Clarify your career direction
[...]
Choosing the skills for a job you need starts with identifying your short- and long-term goals. These goals may include:
[...]
- A promotion with your current employer.
- Changing employers.
- Changing career fields.
- Starting over with your career.
[...]
Once you've chosen one or more goals, start researching:
[...]
- Potential job titles, industries, and employers.
[...]
latest economic trends impacting these industries and employers.
[...]
This research may reveal the most in-demand skills for a job in your field. You can also determine which skills may best help you weather economic downturns, such as a recession. You may need to adjust your goals based on what your research reveals. To stay competitive in a changing job market, learn how to future-proof your skills.
[...]
### 2. Assess your current skills and experience
[...]
Start assessing your current skills and experience by identifying your career stage. Early-career (i.e., 1–10 years of experience) professionals may need to build hard and soft skills for a job. More experienced workers may need to focus solely on hard skills related to the latest developments in their industry.
[...]
Before you identify high-impact skills for your goals, make sure you have a clear picture of your existing strengths and gaps. This will help you focus your learning where it matters most. No matter your career stage, you can assess how your skills stack up through feedback, self-assessments, and skill surveys.
[...]
Review the results to understand where you're strong and where there's room to grow. Then, compare those insights with the skills most commonly emphasized in job postings within your target field.
[...]
### 3. Identify high-impact skills for your goals
[...]
Whether you need to develop a few or many skills to reach your goals, focus on high-impact skills first. High-impact skills are those that can best improve your career trajectory.
[...]
Identify whether high-impact skills for your industry are soft or hard (or a combination of both). Soft skills (e.g., communication, time management) are transferable, while hard skills (e.g., domain-specific knowledge) relate to your chosen field. You may need to focus solely on the latter if you have many years of professional experience.
[...]
Beyond hard and soft skills, high-impact skills relate to other factors, including your long-term career goals. Explore the responsibilities sections in job postings to identify which skills employers consider high impact. This information can tell you whether you need to upskill or reskill.
[...]
Top trending skills to learn in 2025:
[...]
- Adaptability and flexibility: Successful professionals are open to learning new methods, mastering new technologies, and taking on extra responsibilities.
- Artificial intelligence: With many businesses embracing AI, workers need to know how to use the technology to keep their jobs relevant.
[...]
- Critical thinking: Employers in all fields want job applicants who can analyze information and draw conclusions others may not have considered.
[...]
- Emotional intelligence: Also known as emotional awareness, this skill helps you build positive professional relationships with coworkers, managers, and outside stakeholders.
- Leadership and management: Leadership and management skills show that you can mentor less-experienced employees, affect organizational change, and make sound decisions.

### 4. Prioritize what to learn next
[...]
It's time to rank the skills you need to learn or improve, from most to least important. Prioritize skills that can help you in your current career or job hunt. To learn these skills as quickly as possible, consider an edX online course, such as:

- Communication skills

- Manage team performance
- Public speaking
[...]
Starting with stand-alone courses can help you make measurable gains and short-term wins. Mastering more challenging skills may require earning a professional certificate. At the end of each course or certificate program, reflect on your progress and adjust your priorities, if necessary.

[...]
edX's focus on asynchronous and self-paced learning makes it a great starting point for exploring courses and programs that emphasize hard and soft skills for a job. No matter which course or program you choose, make sure it allows you to balance work, family, and other responsibilities.

# How you should choose which technology to learn next
[...]
Keeping up with the growing software ecosystem — new databases, new programming languages, new web frameworks — becomes harder and harder every year as more and more software is written. It is impossible to learn all existing technologies, let alone the new ones being released every day.
[...]
If you want to learn another programming language you can choose from Dart, Swift, Go, Idris, Futhark, Ceylon, Zimbu, Elm, Elixir, Vala, OCaml, LiveScript, Oz, R, TypeScript, PureScript, Haskell, F#, Scala, Dylan, Squeak, Julia, CoffeeScript… and about a thousand more, if you’re still awake. This stream of new technologies can be overwhelming, a constant worry that your skills are getting rusty and out of date.
[...]
Luckily you don’t need to learn all technologies, and you are likely to use only a small subset during your tenure as a programmer. Instead your goal should be to maximize your return on investment: learn the most useful tools, with the least amount of effort. How then should you choose which technologies to learn?
[...]
choosing a technology
[...]
Don’t spend too much time on technologies which are either too close or too far from your current set of knowledge. If you are an expert on PostgreSQL then learning another relational database like MySQL won’t teach you much. Your existing knowledge is transferable for the most part, and you’d have no trouble applying for a job requiring MySQL knowledge. On the other hand a technology that is too far from your current tools will be much more difficult to learn, e.g. switching from web development to real-time embedded devices.
[...]
Focus on technologies that can build on your existing knowledge while still being different enough to teach you something new. Learning these technologies provides multiple benefits:
[...]
- Since you have some pre-existing knowledge you can learn them faster.
- They can help you with your current job by giving you a broader but still relevant set of tools.
- They can make it easier to expand the scope of a job search because they relate to your existing experience.
[...]
## Three paths to choose from
[...]
There are three ways you can build on your existing knowledge of tools and technologies:
[...]
1. Branch out to nearby technologies: If you’re a backend web developer you are interacting with a database, with networking via the HTTP protocol, with a browser running Javascript. You will end up knowing at least a little bit about these technologies, and you have some sense of how they interact with the technology you already know well. These are all great candidates for a new technology to learn next.
[...]
2. Alternative solutions for a problem you understand: If you are an expert on the PostgreSQL database you might want to learn MongoDB. It’s still a database, solving a problem whose parameters you already understand: how to store and search structured data. But the way MongoDB solves this problem is fundamentally different than PostgreSQL, which means you will learn a lot.
[...]
3. Enhance your usage of existing tools: Tools for testing your existing technology stack can make you a better programmer by providing faster feedback and a broader view of software quality and defects. Learning how to better use a sophisticated text editor like Emacs/Vim or an IDE like Eclipse with your programming language of choice can make you a more productive programmer.
[...]
Neither you nor any other programmer will ever be able to learn all the technologies in use today: there are just too many. What you can and should do is learn those that will help with your current projects, and those that you can learn more easily. The more technologies you know, the broader the range of technologies you have at least partial access to, and the easier it will be to learn new ones.
