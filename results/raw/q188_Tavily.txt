![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/96/Symbol_category_class.svg/20px-Symbol_category_class.svg.png)

**Gold** is a [chemical element](/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"); its [chemical symbol](/wiki/Chemical_symbol "Chemical symbol") is **Au** (from [Latin](/wiki/Latin "Latin") *aurum*) and [atomic number](/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") 79. In its pure form, it is a [bright](/wiki/Brightness "Brightness")-metallic-[yellow](/wiki/Yellow "Yellow"), dense, soft, [malleable](/wiki/Malleable "Malleable"), and [ductile](/wiki/Ductile "Ductile") [metal](/wiki/Metal "Metal"). Chemically, gold is a [transition metal](/wiki/Transition_metal "Transition metal"), a [group 11 element](/wiki/Group_11_element "Group 11 element"), and one of the [noble metals](/wiki/Noble_metal "Noble metal"). It is one of the least [reactive](/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry) "Reactivity (chemistry)") chemical elements, being the second lowest in the [reactivity series](/wiki/Reactivity_series "Reactivity series"), with only [platinum](/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") ranked as less reactive.[[10]](#cite_note-10) Gold is solid under [standard conditions](/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure").

Gold often occurs as the [free element](/wiki/Free_element "Free element") ([native state](/wiki/Native_state_(metallurgy) "Native state (metallurgy)")), as [nuggets](/wiki/Gold_nugget "Gold nugget") or grains, in [rocks](/wiki/Rock_(geology) "Rock (geology)"), [veins](/wiki/Vein_(geology) "Vein (geology)"), and [alluvial deposits](/wiki/Alluvial_deposit "Alluvial deposit"). It occurs in a [solid solution](/wiki/Solid_solution "Solid solution") series with the native element [silver](/wiki/Silver "Silver") (as in [electrum](/wiki/Electrum "Electrum")), naturally [alloyed](/wiki/Alloy "Alloy") with other metals such as [copper](/wiki/Copper "Copper"), platinum, and [palladium](/wiki/Palladium "Palladium"), as well as [mineral inclusions](/wiki/Mineral_inclusion "Mineral inclusion") such as within [pyrite](/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite"). Less commonly, it occurs in minerals as gold compounds, often with [tellurium](/wiki/Tellurium "Tellurium") ([gold tellurides](/wiki/Gold_telluride "Gold telluride")).

Gold is resistant to most acids, though it does dissolve in [aqua regia](/wiki/Aqua_regia "Aqua regia") (a mixture of [nitric acid](/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid") and [hydrochloric acid](/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid")), forming a soluble [tetrachloroaurate](/wiki/Tetrachloroaurate "Tetrachloroaurate") [anion](/wiki/Anion "Anion"). Gold is insoluble in nitric acid alone, which dissolves silver and [base metals](/wiki/Base_metal "Base metal"), a property long used to [refine](/wiki/Refining_(metallurgy) "Refining (metallurgy)") gold and confirm the presence of gold in metallic substances, giving rise to the term "[acid test](/wiki/Acid_test_(gold) "Acid test (gold)")". Gold dissolves in [alkaline](/wiki/Alkaline "Alkaline") solutions of [cyanide](/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide"), which are used in [mining](/wiki/Gold_cyanidation "Gold cyanidation") and [electroplating](/wiki/Electroplating "Electroplating"). Gold also dissolves in [mercury](/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)"), forming [amalgam](/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry) "Amalgam (chemistry)") alloys, and as the gold acts simply as a solute, this is not a [chemical reaction](/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction").

A relatively rare element when compared to silver[[11]](#cite_note-11)[[12]](#cite_note-12) (though thirty times more common than platinum),[[13]](#cite_note-13) gold is a [precious metal](/wiki/Precious_metal "Precious metal") that has been used for [coinage](/wiki/Gold_coin "Gold coin"), [jewelry](/wiki/Jewelry "Jewelry"), and other [works of art](/wiki/Works_of_art "Works of art") throughout [recorded history](/wiki/Recorded_history "Recorded history"). In the past, a [gold standard](/wiki/Gold_standard "Gold standard") was often implemented as a [monetary policy](/wiki/Monetary_policy "Monetary policy"). Most gold coins ceased to be minted as a circulating currency in the 1930s, and the world gold standard was abandoned for a [fiat currency](/wiki/Fiat_currency "Fiat currency") system after the [Nixon shock](/wiki/Nixon_shock "Nixon shock") measures of 1971.

In 2023, the [world's largest gold producer](/wiki/List_of_countries_by_gold_production "List of countries by gold production") was China, followed by Russia and Australia.[[14]](#cite_note-Gold_Production-2023-14) As of 2020[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gold&action=edit), a total of around 201,296 [tonnes](/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") of gold exist above ground.[[15]](#cite_note-15) If all of this gold were put together into a cube shape, each of its sides would measure 21.7 meters (71 ft). The world's consumption of new gold produced is about 50% in jewelry, 40% in [investments](/wiki/Gold_as_an_investment "Gold as an investment"), and 10% in [industry](/wiki/Manufacturing "Manufacturing").[[16]](#cite_note-Soos-2011-16) Gold's high malleability, ductility, resistance to corrosion and most other chemical reactions, as well as conductivity of electricity have led to its continued use in corrosion-resistant [electrical connectors](/wiki/Electrical_connector "Electrical connector") in all types of computerized devices (its chief industrial use). Gold is also used in [infrared](/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") shielding, the production of [colored glass](/wiki/Colored_glass "Colored glass"), [gold leafing](/wiki/Gold_leaf "Gold leaf"), and [tooth restoration](/wiki/Restorative_dentistry "Restorative dentistry"). [Auranofin](/wiki/Auranofin "Auranofin") is a gold-containing drug used to treat [rheumatoid arthritis](/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis "Rheumatoid arthritis").

## Etymology

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d5/Beowulf_-_gold.jpg)

*Gold* is [cognate](/wiki/Cognate "Cognate") with similar words in many [Germanic languages](/wiki/Germanic_languages "Germanic languages"), deriving via [Proto-Germanic](/wiki/Proto-Germanic "Proto-Germanic") [\**gulþą*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/gul%C3%BE%C4%85 "wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/gulþą") from [Proto-Indo-European](/wiki/Proto-Indo-European "Proto-Indo-European") [\**ǵʰelh₃-*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/%C7%B5%CA%B0elh%E2%82%83- "wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/ǵʰelh₃-") 'to shine, to gleam; to be yellow or green'.[[17]](#cite_note-17)[[18]](#cite_note-18)

The symbol *Au* is from the [Latin](/wiki/Latin "Latin") *[aurum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aurum "wikt:aurum")* 'gold'.[[19]](#cite_note-19) The Proto-Indo-European ancestor of *aurum* was *\*h₂é-h₂us-o-*, meaning 'glow'. This word is derived from the same [root](/wiki/Root_(linguistics) "Root (linguistics)") (Proto-Indo-European *\*h₂u̯es-* 'to dawn') as [*\*h₂éu̯sōs*](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/h%E2%82%82%C3%A9ws%C5%8Ds "wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/h₂éwsōs"), the ancestor of the Latin word *[aurora](/wiki/Aurora "Aurora")* 'dawn'.[[20]](#cite_note-20) This etymological relationship is presumably behind the frequent claim in scientific publications that *aurum* meant 'shining dawn'.[[21]](#cite_note-Christie-2011-21)

## Characteristics

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Small_gold_nugget_5mm_dia_and_corresponding_foil_surface_of_half_sq_meter.jpg/250px-Small_gold_nugget_5mm_dia_and_corresponding_foil_surface_of_half_sq_meter.jpg)

Gold is the most [malleable](/wiki/Malleable "Malleable") metal. It can be drawn into a [wire of single-atom width](/wiki/Nanowire "Nanowire"), and then stretched considerably before it breaks.[[22]](#cite_note-Kizuka-2008-22) Such nanowires distort via the formation, reorientation, and migration of [dislocations](/wiki/Dislocation "Dislocation") and [crystal twins](/wiki/Crystal_twinning "Crystal twinning") without noticeable hardening.[[23]](#cite_note-23) A single gram of gold can be beaten into a sheet of 1 square meter (11 sq ft), and an [avoirdupois ounce](/wiki/Avoirdupois_ounce "Avoirdupois ounce") into 28 square meters (300 sq ft). Gold leaf can be beaten thin enough to become semi-transparent. Light transmitted through gold appears greenish-blue, because gold strongly reflects yellow and red.[[24]](#cite_note-24) Such semi-transparent sheets also strongly reflect [infrared](/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") light, making them useful as infrared (radiant heat) shields in the visors of heat-resistant suits and in sun visors for [spacesuits](/wiki/Spacesuit "Spacesuit").[[25]](#cite_note-25) Gold is a good [conductor of heat](/wiki/Conduction_(heat) "Conduction (heat)") and [electricity](/wiki/Electrical_conductor "Electrical conductor").

Gold has a density of 19.3 g/cm3, almost identical to that of [tungsten](/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten") at 19.25 g/cm3; as such, tungsten has been used in the [counterfeiting](/wiki/Counterfeiting "Counterfeiting") of [gold bars](/wiki/Gold_bar "Gold bar"), such as by plating a tungsten bar with gold.[[26]](#cite_note-Gray-2008-26)[[27]](#cite_note-27)[[28]](#cite_note-28)[[29]](#cite_note-29) By comparison, the density of [lead](/wiki/Lead "Lead") is 11.34 g/cm3, and that of the densest element, [osmium](/wiki/Osmium "Osmium"), is 22.588±0.015 g/cm3.[[30]](#cite_note-Arblaster-1995-30)

### Color

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Gold_bullion_ap_001.JPG/250px-Gold_bullion_ap_001.JPG)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Ag-Au-Cu-colours-english.svg/250px-Ag-Au-Cu-colours-english.svg.png)

Whereas most metals are gray or silvery white, gold is slightly reddish-yellow.[[31]](#cite_note-Lippincott-1880-31) This color is a well-known example of [relativistic quantum chemistry](/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_chemistry "Relativistic quantum chemistry"). The 5d-6s band gap is greatly reduced when relativity is included in theoretical calculations, and this is thought to account for the yellow color, although a full comparison of the absorption spectrum between the relativistic and non-relativistic cases has not been performed as of 2004.[[32]](#cite_note-32) Similar effects impart a golden hue to metallic [caesium](/wiki/Caesium "Caesium").

Common colored gold alloys include the distinctive eighteen-karat [rose gold](/wiki/Rose_gold "Rose gold") created by the addition of copper. Alloys containing [palladium](/wiki/Palladium "Palladium") or [nickel](/wiki/Nickel "Nickel") are also important in commercial jewelry as these produce white gold alloys. Fourteen-karat gold-copper alloy is nearly identical in color to certain [bronze](/wiki/Bronze "Bronze") alloys, and both may be used to produce police and other [badges](/wiki/Badge "Badge"). Fourteen- and eighteen-karat gold alloys with silver alone appear greenish-yellow and are referred to as [green gold](/wiki/Green_gold "Green gold"). Blue gold can be made by alloying with [iron](/wiki/Iron "Iron"), and purple gold can be made by alloying with [aluminium](/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium"). Less commonly, addition of [manganese](/wiki/Manganese "Manganese"), [indium](/wiki/Indium "Indium"), and other elements can produce more unusual colors of gold for various applications.[[33]](#cite_note-WorldGoldCouncil-33)

[Colloidal gold](/wiki/Colloidal_gold "Colloidal gold"), used by electron-microscopists, is red if the particles are small; larger particles of colloidal gold are blue.[[34]](#cite_note-34)

Thirty-six [radioisotopes](/wiki/Radioisotopes "Radioisotopes") have been synthesized, ranging in [atomic mass](/wiki/Atomic_mass "Atomic mass") from 169 to 205. The most stable of these is 195

#### Synthesis

The possible production of gold from a more common element, such as [lead](/wiki/Lead "Lead"), has long been a subject of human inquiry, and the ancient and medieval discipline of [alchemy](/wiki/Alchemy "Alchemy") often focused on it; however, the transmutation of the chemical elements did not become possible until the understanding of [nuclear physics](/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics") in the 20th century. The first synthesis of gold was conducted by Japanese physicist [Hantaro Nagaoka](/wiki/Hantaro_Nagaoka "Hantaro Nagaoka"), who synthesized gold from [mercury](/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") in 1924 by neutron bombardment.[[37]](#cite_note-37) An American team, working without knowledge of Nagaoka's prior study, conducted the same experiment in 1941, achieving the same result and showing that the [isotopes of gold](/wiki/Isotopes_of_gold "Isotopes of gold") produced by it were all [radioactive](/wiki/Radioactive "Radioactive").[[38]](#cite_note-38) In 1980, [Glenn Seaborg](/wiki/Glenn_T._Seaborg "Glenn T. Seaborg") transmuted several thousand atoms of bismuth into gold at the Lawrence Berkeley Laboratory.[[39]](#cite_note-39)[[40]](#cite_note-40) Gold can be manufactured in a nuclear reactor, but doing so is highly impractical and would cost far more than the value of the gold that is produced.[[41]](#cite_note-41)

## Chemistry

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Gold%28III%29_chloride_solution.jpg/250px-Gold%28III%29_chloride_solution.jpg)

Although gold is the most noble of the noble metals,[[42]](#cite_note-42)[[43]](#cite_note-43) it still forms many diverse compounds. The [oxidation state](/wiki/Oxidation_state "Oxidation state") of gold in its compounds ranges from −1 to +5, but Au(I) and Au(III) dominate its chemistry. Au(I), referred to as the aurous ion, is the most common oxidation state with soft [ligands](/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") such as [thioethers](/wiki/Thioether "Thioether"), [thiolates](/wiki/Thiolate "Thiolate"), and [organophosphines](/wiki/Organophosphine "Organophosphine"). Au(I) compounds are typically linear. A good example is [Au(CN)−2](/wiki/Gold_cyanidation "Gold cyanidation"), which is the soluble form of gold encountered in mining. The binary [gold halides](/wiki/Gold_halide "Gold halide"), such as [AuCl](/wiki/Gold(I)_chloride "Gold(I) chloride"), form zigzag polymeric chains, again featuring linear coordination at Au. Most [drugs based on gold](/wiki/Gold-containing_drugs "Gold-containing drugs") are Au(I) derivatives.[[44]](#cite_note-44)

Au(III) (referred to as auric) is a common oxidation state, and is illustrated by [gold(III) chloride](/wiki/Gold(III)_chloride "Gold(III) chloride"), Au2Cl6. The gold atom centers in Au(III) complexes, like other d8 compounds, are typically [square planar](/wiki/Square_planar "Square planar"), with [chemical bonds](/wiki/Chemical_bond "Chemical bond") that have both [covalent](/wiki/Covalent "Covalent") and [ionic](/wiki/Ion "Ion") character. [Gold(I,III) chloride](/wiki/Gold(I,III)_chloride "Gold(I,III) chloride") is also known, an example of a [mixed-valence complex](/wiki/Mixed-valence_complex "Mixed-valence complex").

Gold does not react with oxygen at any temperature[[45]](#cite_note-45) and, up to 100 °C, is resistant to attack from ozone:[[46]](#cite_note-46)

Gold does not react with sulfur directly,[[50]](#cite_note-Emery-1961-50) but [gold(III) sulfide](/wiki/Gold(III)_sulfide "Gold(III) sulfide") can be made by passing [hydrogen sulfide](/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide") through a dilute solution of gold(III) chloride or [chlorauric acid](/wiki/Chlorauric_acid "Chlorauric acid").

Unlike sulfur, phosphorus reacts directly with gold at elevated temperatures to produce [gold phosphide](/wiki/Gold_phosphide "Gold phosphide") (Au2P3).[[51]](#cite_note-51)

Gold readily dissolves in [mercury](/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") at room temperature to form an [amalgam](/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry) "Amalgam (chemistry)"), and forms [alloys](/wiki/Alloy "Alloy") with many other metals at higher temperatures. These alloys can be produced to modify the hardness and other metallurgical properties, to control [melting point](/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") or to create exotic colors.[[33]](#cite_note-WorldGoldCouncil-33)

Gold is unaffected by most acids. It does not react with [hydrofluoric](/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid "Hydrofluoric acid"), [hydrochloric](/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"), [hydrobromic](/wiki/Hydrobromic_acid "Hydrobromic acid"), [hydriodic](/wiki/Hydriodic_acid "Hydriodic acid"), [sulfuric](/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid"), or [nitric acid](/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid"). It does react with [selenic acid](/wiki/Selenic_acid "Selenic acid"), and is dissolved by [aqua regia](/wiki/Aqua_regia "Aqua regia"), a 1:3 mixture of nitric acid and hydrochloric acid. Nitric acid oxidizes the metal to +3 ions, but only in minute amounts, typically undetectable in the pure acid because of the chemical equilibrium of the reaction. However, the ions are removed from the equilibrium by hydrochloric acid, forming AuCl−4 ions, or [chloroauric acid](/wiki/Chloroauric_acid "Chloroauric acid"), thereby enabling further oxidation:

Gold is similarly unaffected by most bases. It does not react with [aqueous](/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution"), [solid](/wiki/Solid "Solid"), or [molten](/wiki/Molten "Molten") [sodium](/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide") or [potassium hydroxide](/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide"). It does, however, react with [sodium](/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide") or [potassium cyanide](/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide") under alkaline conditions when [oxygen](/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") is present to form soluble complexes.[[50]](#cite_note-Emery-1961-50)

Common [oxidation states](/wiki/Oxidation_state "Oxidation state") of gold include +1 (gold(I) or aurous compounds) and +3 (gold(III) or auric compounds). Gold ions in solution are readily [reduced](/wiki/Reduction_(chemistry) "Reduction (chemistry)") and [precipitated](/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry) "Precipitation (chemistry)") as metal by adding any other metal as the [reducing agent](/wiki/Reducing_agent "Reducing agent"). The added metal is [oxidized](/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") and dissolves, allowing the gold to be displaced from solution and be recovered as a solid precipitate.

### Rare oxidation states

Less common oxidation states of gold include −1, +2, and +5.

The −1 oxidation state occurs in aurides, compounds containing the Au− [anion](/wiki/Anion "Anion"). [Caesium auride](/wiki/Caesium_auride "Caesium auride") (CsAu), for example, crystallizes in the [caesium chloride](/wiki/Caesium_chloride "Caesium chloride") motif;[[52]](#cite_note-Jansen-2005-52) rubidium, potassium, and [tetramethylammonium](/wiki/Tetramethylammonium "Tetramethylammonium") aurides are also known.[[53]](#cite_note-Holleman-2001-53) Gold has the highest [electron affinity](/wiki/Electron_affinity "Electron affinity") of any metal, at 222.8 kJ/mol, making Au− a stable species,[[54]](#cite_note-Jansen-2008-54) analogous to the [halides](/wiki/Halide "Halide").

Gold also has a –1 oxidation state in covalent complexes with the [group 4](/wiki/Group_4_element "Group 4 element") transition metals, such as in titanium tetraauride and the analogous zirconium and hafnium compounds. These chemicals are expected to form gold-bridged [dimers](/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") in a manner similar to [titanium(IV) hydride](/wiki/Titanium(IV)_hydride "Titanium(IV) hydride").[[55]](#cite_note-55)

Gold(II) compounds are usually [diamagnetic](/wiki/Diamagnetic "Diamagnetic") with Au–Au bonds such as [Au(CH2)2P(C6H5)2]2Cl2. The evaporation of a solution of Au(OH)3 in concentrated H2SO4 produces red crystals of [gold(II) sulfate](/wiki/Gold(II)_sulfate "Gold(II) sulfate"), Au2(SO4)2. Originally thought to be a mixed-valence compound, it has been shown to contain Au4+2 cations, analogous to the better-known [mercury(I)](/wiki/Mercury(I) "Mercury(I)") ion, Hg2+2.[[56]](#cite_note-56)[[57]](#cite_note-57) A gold(II) complex, the [tetraxenonogold(II)](/wiki/Tetraxenonogold(II) "Tetraxenonogold(II)") cation, which contains [xenon](/wiki/Xenon "Xenon") as a ligand, occurs in [AuXe4](Sb2F11)2.[[58]](#cite_note-58) In September 2023, a novel type of [metal-halide perovskite material](/wiki/Perovskite_(structure) "Perovskite (structure)") consisting of Au3+ and Au2+ cations in its crystal structure has been found.[[59]](#cite_note-59) It has been shown to be unexpectedly stable at normal conditions.

[Gold pentafluoride](/wiki/Gold_pentafluoride "Gold pentafluoride"), along with its derivative anion, AuF−6, and its [difluorine complex](/wiki/Difluorine_complex "Difluorine complex"), [gold heptafluoride](/wiki/Gold_heptafluoride "Gold heptafluoride"), is the sole example of gold(V), the highest verified oxidation state.[[60]](#cite_note-60)

Some gold compounds exhibit *[aurophilic bonding](/wiki/Aurophilicity "Aurophilicity")*, which describes the tendency of gold ions to interact at distances that are too long to be a conventional Au–Au bond but shorter than [van der Waals bonding](/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force "Van der Waals force"). The interaction is estimated to be comparable in strength to that of a [hydrogen bond](/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond").

Well-defined cluster compounds are numerous.[[53]](#cite_note-Holleman-2001-53) In some cases, gold has a fractional oxidation state. A representative example is the octahedral species {Au([P(C6H5)3](/wiki/Triphenylphosphine "Triphenylphosphine"))}2+6.

## Origin

### Gold production in the universe

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Vredefort_crater_cross_section_2.png/500px-Vredefort_crater_cross_section_2.png)

Gold in the universe is produced through several cosmic processes and was present in the [dust](/wiki/Solar_nebula "Solar nebula") from which the [Solar System](/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") formed.[[61]](#cite_note-61) Scientists have identified three main cosmic sources for gold formation: [supernova nucleosynthesis](/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis "Supernova nucleosynthesis"), [neutron star collisions](/wiki/Neutron_star_merger "Neutron star merger"),[[62]](#cite_note-62) and magnetar flares.

All three sources involve a process called the [r-process](/wiki/R-process "R-process") (rapid neutron capture), which forms elements heavier than [iron](/wiki/Iron "Iron").[[63]](#cite_note-63) For decades, scientists believed [supernova nucleosynthesis](/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis "Supernova nucleosynthesis") was the primary mechanism for gold formation. More recently, research has shown that [neutron star collisions](/wiki/Neutron_star_merger "Neutron star merger") produce significant quantities of gold through the r-process.[[64]](#cite_note-64)

In August 2017, the spectroscopic signatures of heavy elements, including gold, were directly observed by electromagnetic observatories during the [GW170817](/wiki/GW170817 "GW170817") neutron star merger event.[[65]](#cite_note-65) This confirmed neutron star mergers as a source of gold, after years of only indirect detection.[[66]](#cite_note-66) This single event generated between 3 and 13 [Earth masses](/wiki/Earth_mass "Earth mass") of gold, suggesting that neutron star mergers might produce enough gold to account for most of this element in the universe.[[67]](#cite_note-67)

However, neutron star mergers alone cannot explain all cosmic gold, particularly in older stars, because these mergers occur relatively late in galactic history and are infrequent (approximately once every 100,000 years).[[68]](#cite_note-68) This created a timing paradox in explaining the presence of gold in stars formed early in the universe.

In 2025, researchers resolved this paradox by confirming that giant flares from [magnetars](/wiki/Magnetar "Magnetar") (highly magnetic neutron stars) are also a significant source of gold formation.[[69]](#cite_note-69) Analysis of a 2004 magnetar flare showed these events produce heavy elements through the same r-process as neutron star mergers. The amount of heavy elements created in a single magnetar flare can exceed the mass of Mars.[[70]](#cite_note-70) Since magnetars existed earlier in cosmic history and flare more frequently than neutron star mergers occur, they help explain gold's presence in older stars. Scientists estimate magnetar flares may contribute approximately 1–10% of all elements heavier than iron in our galaxy, including gold.[[71]](#cite_note-71)

### Asteroid origin theories

Because the Earth was molten [when it was formed](/wiki/History_of_Earth "History of Earth"), almost all of the gold present in the [early Earth](/wiki/Early_Earth "Early Earth") probably sank into the [planetary core](/wiki/Core_(geology) "Core (geology)"). Therefore, as hypothesized in one model, most of the gold in the Earth's [crust](/wiki/Crust_(geology) "Crust (geology)") and [mantle](/wiki/Mantle_(geology) "Mantle (geology)") is thought to have been delivered to Earth by [asteroid impacts](/wiki/Asteroid_impact "Asteroid impact") during the [Late Heavy Bombardment](/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment "Late Heavy Bombardment"), about 4 billion years ago.[[72]](#cite_note-Willbold-2011-72)[[73]](#cite_note-Battison-2011-73)

Gold which is reachable by humans has, in one case, been associated with a particular asteroid impact. The asteroid that formed [Vredefort impact structure](/wiki/Vredefort_impact_structure "Vredefort impact structure") 2.020 billion years ago is often credited with seeding the [Witwatersrand basin](/wiki/Witwatersrand_basin "Witwatersrand basin") in [South Africa](/wiki/South_Africa "South Africa") with the richest gold deposits on earth.[[74]](#cite_note-74)[[75]](#cite_note-75)[[76]](#cite_note-76)[[77]](#cite_note-77) However, this scenario is now questioned. The gold-bearing [Witwatersrand](/wiki/Witwatersrand "Witwatersrand") rocks were laid down between 700 and 950 million years before the Vredefort impact.[[78]](#cite_note-McCarthy-2005-78)[[79]](#cite_note-Norman-2006-79) These gold-bearing rocks had furthermore been covered by a thick layer of Ventersdorp lavas and the [Transvaal Supergroup](/wiki/Transvaal_Basin "Transvaal Basin") of rocks before the meteor struck, and thus the gold did not actually arrive in the asteroid/meteorite. What the Vredefort impact achieved, however, was to distort the Witwatersrand basin in such a way that the gold-bearing rocks were brought to the present [erosion surface](/wiki/Erosion_surface "Erosion surface") in [Johannesburg](/wiki/Johannesburg "Johannesburg"), on the Witwatersrand, just inside the rim of the original 300 km (190 mi) diameter crater caused by the meteor strike. The discovery of the deposit in 1886 launched the [Witwatersrand Gold Rush](/wiki/Witwatersrand_Gold_Rush "Witwatersrand Gold Rush"). Some 22% of all the gold that is ascertained to exist today on Earth has been extracted from these Witwatersrand rocks.[[79]](#cite_note-Norman-2006-79)

### Mantle return theories

Much of the rest of the gold on Earth is thought to have been incorporated into the planet since its very beginning, as [planetesimals](/wiki/Planetesimals "Planetesimals") formed the [mantle](/wiki/Mantle_(geology) "Mantle (geology)"). In 2017, an international group of scientists established that gold "came to the Earth's surface from the deepest regions of our planet",[[80]](#cite_note-80) the mantle, as evidenced by their findings at [Deseado Massif](/wiki/Deseado_Massif "Deseado Massif") in the [Argentinian Patagonia](/wiki/Argentinian_Patagonia "Argentinian Patagonia").[[81]](#cite_note-81)[*[clarification needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")*]

## Occurrence

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Gold_nugget_%28Australia%29_4_%2816848647509%29.jpg/250px-Gold_nugget_%28Australia%29_4_%2816848647509%29.jpg)

On Earth, gold is found in [ores](/wiki/Ore "Ore") in rock formed from the [Precambrian](/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian") time onward.[[82]](#cite_note-La_Niece-2009-82) It most often occurs as a [native metal](/wiki/Native_metal "Native metal"), typically in a metal [solid solution](/wiki/Solid_solution "Solid solution") with silver (i.e. as a gold/silver [alloy](/wiki/Alloy "Alloy")). Such alloys usually have a silver content of 8–10%. [Electrum](/wiki/Electrum "Electrum") is elemental gold with more than 20% silver, and is commonly known as [white gold](/wiki/White_gold "White gold"). Electrum's color runs from golden-silvery to silvery, dependent upon the silver content. The more silver, the lower the [specific gravity](/wiki/Specific_gravity "Specific gravity").

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Gold-Pyrite-263192.jpg/250px-Gold-Pyrite-263192.jpg)

Native gold occurs as very small to microscopic particles embedded in rock, often together with [quartz](/wiki/Quartz "Quartz") or [sulfide minerals](/wiki/Sulfide_mineral "Sulfide mineral") such as "[fool's gold](/wiki/Fool%27s_gold "Fool's gold")", which is a [pyrite](/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite").[[83]](#cite_note-83) These are called [lode](/wiki/Lode "Lode") deposits. The metal in a native state is also found in the form of free flakes, grains or larger [nuggets](/wiki/Gold_nugget "Gold nugget")[[82]](#cite_note-La_Niece-2009-82) that have been eroded from rocks and end up in [alluvial](/wiki/Alluvial "Alluvial") deposits called [placer deposits](/wiki/Placer_deposit "Placer deposit"). Such free gold is always richer at the exposed surface of gold-bearing veins, owing to the [oxidation](/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") of accompanying minerals followed by weathering; and by washing of the dust into streams and rivers, where it collects and can be welded by water action to form nuggets.

Gold sometimes occurs combined with [tellurium](/wiki/Tellurium "Tellurium") as the [minerals](/wiki/Mineral "Mineral") [calaverite](/wiki/Calaverite "Calaverite"), [krennerite](/wiki/Krennerite "Krennerite"), [nagyagite](/wiki/Nagyagite "Nagyagite"), [petzite](/wiki/Petzite "Petzite") and [sylvanite](/wiki/Sylvanite "Sylvanite") (see [telluride minerals](/wiki/Telluride_mineral "Telluride mineral")), and as the rare bismuthide maldonite (Au2Bi) and antimonide [aurostibite](/wiki/Aurostibite "Aurostibite") (AuSb2). Gold also occurs in rare alloys with [copper](/wiki/Copper "Copper"), [lead](/wiki/Lead "Lead"), and [mercury](/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)"): the minerals [auricupride](/wiki/Auricupride "Auricupride") (Cu3Au), novodneprite (AuPb3) and weishanite ((Au,Ag)3Hg2).

A 2004 research paper suggests that microbes can sometimes play an important role in forming gold deposits, transporting and precipitating gold to form grains and nuggets that collect in alluvial deposits.[[84]](#cite_note-84)

A 2013 study has claimed water in faults vaporizes during an earthquake, depositing gold. When an earthquake strikes, it moves along a [fault](/wiki/Fault_(geology) "Fault (geology)"). Water often lubricates faults, filling in fractures and jogs. About 10 kilometers (6.2 mi) below the surface, under very high temperatures and pressures, the water carries high concentrations of carbon dioxide, silica, and gold. During an earthquake, the fault jog suddenly opens wider. The water inside the void instantly vaporizes, flashing to steam and forcing silica, which forms the mineral quartz, and gold out of the fluids and onto nearby surfaces.[[85]](#cite_note-85)

### Seawater

The world's [oceans](/wiki/Ocean "Ocean") contain gold. Measured concentrations of gold in the Atlantic and Northeast Pacific are 50–150 [femtomol](/wiki/Femtomolar "Femtomolar")/L or 10–30 parts per [quadrillion](/wiki/Quadrillion "Quadrillion") (about 10–30 g/km3). In general, gold concentrations for south Atlantic and central Pacific samples are the same (~50 femtomol/L) but less certain. Mediterranean deep waters contain slightly higher concentrations of gold (100–150 femtomol/L), which is attributed to wind-blown dust or rivers. At 10 parts per quadrillion, the Earth's [oceans](/wiki/Oceans "Oceans") would hold 15,000 tonnes of gold.[[86]](#cite_note-86) These figures are three orders of magnitude less than reported in the literature prior to 1988, indicating contamination problems with the earlier data.

A number of people have claimed to be able to economically recover gold from [sea water](/wiki/Sea_water "Sea water"), but they were either mistaken or acted in an intentional deception. [Prescott Jernegan](/wiki/Prescott_Jernegan "Prescott Jernegan") ran a gold-from-seawater swindle in the [United States](/wiki/United_States "United States") in the 1890s, as did an English fraudster in the early 1900s.[[87]](#cite_note-87) [Fritz Haber](/wiki/Fritz_Haber "Fritz Haber") did research on the extraction of gold from sea water in an effort to help pay [Germany](/wiki/Germany "Germany")'s reparations following [World War I](/wiki/World_War_I "World War I").[[88]](#cite_note-88) Based on the published values of 2 to 64 ppb of gold in seawater, a commercially successful extraction seemed possible. After analysis of 4,000 water samples yielding an average of 0.004 ppb, it became clear that extraction would not be possible, and he ended the project.[[89]](#cite_note-89)

## History

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Indian_gold_tribute_donor_Apadana.jpg/250px-Indian_gold_tribute_donor_Apadana.jpg)

The earliest recorded metal employed by humans appears to be gold. Small amounts of natural gold have been found in Spanish caves used during the late [Paleolithic](/wiki/Paleolithic "Paleolithic") period, c. 40,000 BC.[[91]](#cite_note-91)

The oldest gold artifacts in the world are from [Bulgaria](/wiki/Bulgaria "Bulgaria") and are dating back to the 5th millennium BC, such as those found in the [Varna Necropolis](/wiki/Varna_Necropolis "Varna Necropolis") near Lake Varna and the [Black Sea](/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") coast, thought to be the earliest "well-dated" finding of gold artifacts in history.[[92]](#cite_note-92)[[82]](#cite_note-La_Niece-2009-82)[[93]](#cite_note-93)

Gold artifacts probably made their appearance in Ancient Egypt at the beginning of the pre-dynastic period, at the end of the fifth millennium BC and the start of the fourth, and smelting was developed during the course of the 4th millennium; gold artifacts appear in the archeology of Lower Mesopotamia during the early 4th millennium.[[94]](#cite_note-94) As of 1990, gold artifacts found at the [Wadi Qana](/wiki/Wadi_Qana "Wadi Qana") cave cemetery of the [4th millennium BC](/wiki/4th_millennium_BC "4th millennium BC") in [West Bank](/wiki/West_Bank "West Bank") were the earliest from the Levant.[[95]](#cite_note-Gopher-1990-95) Gold artifacts such as the [golden hats](/wiki/Golden_hats "Golden hats") and the [Nebra disk](/wiki/Nebra_disk "Nebra disk") appeared in Central Europe from the 2nd millennium BC [Bronze Age](/wiki/European_Bronze_Age "European Bronze Age").

Exploitation of gold in the south-east corner of the [Black Sea](/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea") is said to date from the time of [Midas](/wiki/Midas "Midas"), and this gold was important in the establishment of what is probably the world's earliest coinage in [Lydia](/wiki/Lydia "Lydia") around 610 BC.[[96]](#cite_note-Lion-2003-96)

During [Mansa Musa](/wiki/Mansa_Musa "Mansa Musa")'s (ruler of the [Mali Empire](/wiki/Mali_Empire "Mali Empire") from 1312 to 1337) [hajj](/wiki/Hajj "Hajj") to [Mecca](/wiki/Mecca "Mecca") in 1324, he passed through [Cairo](/wiki/Cairo "Cairo") in July 1324, and was reportedly accompanied by a [camel train](/wiki/Camel_train "Camel train") that included thousands of people and nearly a hundred camels where he gave away so much gold that it depressed the price in Egypt for over a decade, causing high [inflation](/wiki/Inflation "Inflation").[[97]](#cite_note-97)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg/250px-Monnaie_de_Bactriane%2C_Eucratide_I%2C_2_faces.jpg)

The European exploration of the Americas was fueled in no small part by reports of the gold ornaments displayed in great profusion by [Native American](/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Indigenous peoples of the Americas") peoples, especially in [Mesoamerica](/wiki/Mesoamerica "Mesoamerica"), [Peru](/wiki/Peru "Peru"), [Ecuador](/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador") and [Colombia](/wiki/Colombia "Colombia"). The [Aztecs](/wiki/Aztec "Aztec") regarded gold as the product of the gods, calling it literally "god excrement" (*teocuitlatl* in [Nahuatl](/wiki/Nahuatl "Nahuatl")), and after [Moctezuma II](/wiki/Moctezuma_II "Moctezuma II") was killed, most of this gold was shipped to Spain.[[99]](#cite_note-99) However, for the [indigenous peoples of North America](/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_North_America "Indigenous peoples of North America") gold was considered useless and they saw much greater value in other [minerals](/wiki/Minerals "Minerals") which were directly related to their utility, such as [obsidian](/wiki/Obsidian "Obsidian"), [flint](/wiki/Flint "Flint"), and [slate](/wiki/Slate "Slate").[[100]](#cite_note-100)

Beginning in the [early modern period](/wiki/Early_modern_period "Early modern period"), European [exploration](/wiki/Age_of_Discovery "Age of Discovery") and [colonization](/wiki/Colonisation_of_Africa "Colonisation of Africa") of [West Africa](/wiki/West_Africa "West Africa") was driven in large part by reports of gold deposits in the region, which was eventually referred to by Europeans as the "[Gold Coast](/wiki/Gold_Coast_(region) "Gold Coast (region)")".[[101]](#cite_note-101) From the late 15th to early 19th centuries, European trade in the region was primarily focused in gold, along with [ivory](/wiki/Ivory "Ivory") and [slaves](/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade "Atlantic slave trade").[[102]](#cite_note-102) The gold trade in West Africa was dominated by the [Ashanti Empire](/wiki/Ashanti_Empire "Ashanti Empire"), who initially traded with the [Portuguese](/wiki/Portuguese_Empire "Portuguese Empire") before branching out and trading with [British](/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire"), [French](/wiki/French_colonial_empire "French colonial empire"), [Spanish](/wiki/Spanish_Empire "Spanish Empire") and [Danish](/wiki/Danish_colonial_empire "Danish colonial empire") merchants.[[103]](#cite_note-Green-2019-103) British desires to secure control of West African gold deposits played a role in the [Anglo-Ashanti wars](/wiki/Anglo-Ashanti_wars "Anglo-Ashanti wars") of the late 19th century, which saw the Ashanti Empire [annexed by Britain](/wiki/Gold_Coast_(British_colony) "Gold Coast (British colony)").[[104]](#cite_note-104)

One main goal of the [alchemists](/wiki/Alchemy "Alchemy") was to produce gold from other substances, such as [lead](/wiki/Lead "Lead") — presumably by the interaction with a mythical substance called the [philosopher's stone](/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone "Philosopher's stone"). Trying to produce gold led the alchemists to systematically find out what can be done with substances, and this laid the foundation for today's [chemistry](/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), which can produce gold (albeit uneconomically) by using [nuclear transmutation](/wiki/Nuclear_transmutation "Nuclear transmutation").[[105]](#cite_note-105)

## Production

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Gold_-_world_production_trend.svg/250px-Gold_-_world_production_trend.svg.png)

According to the [United States Geological Survey](/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey "United States Geological Survey") in 2016, about 5,726,000,000 troy ounces (178,100 t) of gold has been accounted for, of which 85% remains in active use.[[106]](#cite_note-106)

### Mining and prospecting

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Miner_underground_at_Pumsaint_gold_mine_%281294028%29.jpg/250px-Miner_underground_at_Pumsaint_gold_mine_%281294028%29.jpg)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Grasberg_mine.jpg/250px-Grasberg_mine.jpg)

Since the 1880s, South Africa has been the source of a large proportion of the world's gold supply, and about 22% of the gold presently accounted is from South Africa. Production in 1970 accounted for 79% of the world supply, about 1,480 tonnes. In 2007 China (with 276 tonnes) overtook South Africa as the world's largest gold producer, the first time since 1905 that South Africa had not been the largest.[[107]](#cite_note-107)

In 2023, [China](/wiki/Gold_mining_in_China "Gold mining in China") was the world's leading gold-mining country, followed in order by Russia, Australia, Canada, the United States and Ghana.[[14]](#cite_note-Gold_Production-2023-14)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Gold_30g_for_a_860kg_rock.jpg/250px-Gold_30g_for_a_860kg_rock.jpg)

In South America, the controversial project [Pascua Lama](/wiki/Pascua_Lama "Pascua Lama") aims at exploitation of rich fields in the high mountains of [Atacama Desert](/wiki/Atacama_Desert "Atacama Desert"), at the border between [Chile](/wiki/Chile "Chile") and [Argentina](/wiki/Argentina "Argentina").

It has been estimated that up to one-quarter of the yearly global gold production originates from artisanal or small scale mining.[[108]](#cite_note-108)[[109]](#cite_note-109)[[110]](#cite_note-110)

The city of [Johannesburg](/wiki/Johannesburg "Johannesburg") located in South Africa was founded as a result of the [Witwatersrand Gold Rush](/wiki/Witwatersrand_Gold_Rush "Witwatersrand Gold Rush") which resulted in the discovery of some of the largest natural gold deposits in recorded history. The gold fields are confined to the northern and north-western edges of the [Witwatersrand basin](/wiki/Witwatersrand_basin "Witwatersrand basin"), which is a 5–7 km (3.1–4.3 mi) thick layer of [archean](/wiki/Archean "Archean") rocks located, in most places, deep under the [Free State](/wiki/Free_State_(South_African_province) "Free State (South African province)"), [Gauteng](/wiki/Gauteng "Gauteng") and surrounding provinces.[[111]](#cite_note-Truswell-1977-111) These Witwatersrand rocks are exposed at the surface on the [Witwatersrand](/wiki/Witwatersrand "Witwatersrand"), in and around Johannesburg, but also in isolated patches to the south-east and south-west of Johannesburg, as well as in an arc around the [Vredefort Dome](/wiki/Vredefort_Dome "Vredefort Dome") which lies close to the center of the Witwatersrand basin.[[78]](#cite_note-McCarthy-2005-78)[[111]](#cite_note-Truswell-1977-111) From these surface exposures the basin [dips](/wiki/Strike_and_dip "Strike and dip") extensively, requiring some of the mining to occur at depths of nearly 4,000 m (13,000 ft), making them, especially the [Savuka](/w/index.php?title=Savuka_Mine&action=edit&redlink=1 "Savuka Mine (page does not exist)") and [TauTona](/wiki/TauTona "TauTona") mines to the south-west of Johannesburg, the deepest mines on Earth. The gold is found only in six areas where archean rivers from the north and north-west formed extensive pebbly [Braided river](/wiki/Braided_river "Braided river") deltas before draining into the "Witwatersrand sea" where the rest of the Witwatersrand sediments were deposited.[[111]](#cite_note-Truswell-1977-111)

The [Second Boer War](/wiki/Second_Boer_War "Second Boer War") of 1899–1901 between the [British Empire](/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") and the [Afrikaner](/wiki/Afrikaner "Afrikaner") [Boers](/wiki/Boer "Boer") was at least partly over the rights of miners and possession of the gold wealth in South Africa.

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Kullanhuuhdontaa_Ivalossa.jpg/250px-Kullanhuuhdontaa_Ivalossa.jpg)

During the 19th century, [gold rushes](/wiki/Gold_rush "Gold rush") occurred whenever large gold deposits were discovered. The first documented discovery of gold in the United States was at the [Reed Gold Mine](/wiki/Reed_Gold_Mine "Reed Gold Mine") near Georgeville, North Carolina in 1803.[[112]](#cite_note-112) The first major gold strike in the United States occurred in a small north Georgia town called [Dahlonega](/wiki/Dahlonega,_Georgia "Dahlonega, Georgia").[[113]](#cite_note-113) Further gold rushes occurred in [California](/wiki/California_Gold_Rush "California Gold Rush"), [Colorado](/wiki/Pike%27s_Peak_Gold_Rush "Pike's Peak Gold Rush"), the [Black Hills](/wiki/Black_Hills_Gold_Rush "Black Hills Gold Rush"), [Otago](/wiki/Otago_gold_rush "Otago gold rush") in New Zealand, a number of locations across [Australia](/wiki/Australian_gold_rushes "Australian gold rushes"), [Witwatersrand](/wiki/Witwatersrand_Gold_Rush "Witwatersrand Gold Rush") in South Africa, and the [Klondike](/wiki/Klondike_Gold_Rush "Klondike Gold Rush") in Canada.

[Grasberg mine](/wiki/Grasberg_mine "Grasberg mine") located in [Papua](/wiki/Papua_(province) "Papua (province)"), [Indonesia](/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia") is the largest [gold mine](/wiki/Gold_mining "Gold mining") in the world.[[114]](#cite_note-114)

### Extraction and refining

[Gold extraction](/wiki/Gold_extraction "Gold extraction") is most economical in large, easily mined deposits. Ore grades as little as 0.5 parts per million (ppm) can be economical. Typical ore grades in [open-pit](/wiki/Open-pit_mining "Open-pit mining") mines are 1–5 ppm; ore grades in underground or [hard rock](/wiki/Underground_mining_(hard_rock) "Underground mining (hard rock)") mines are usually at least 3 ppm. Because ore grades of 30 ppm are usually needed before gold is visible to the naked eye, in most gold mines the gold is invisible.

The average gold mining and extraction costs were about $317 per troy ounce in 2007 (equivalent to $492 in 2025), but these can vary widely depending on mining type and ore quality; global mine production amounted to 2,471.1 tonnes.[[115]](#cite_note-115)

After initial production, gold is often subsequently refined industrially by the [Wohlwill process](/wiki/Wohlwill_process "Wohlwill process") which is based on [electrolysis](/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") or by the [Miller process](/wiki/Miller_process "Miller process"), that is chlorination in the melt. The Wohlwill process results in higher purity, but is more complex and is only applied in small-scale installations.[[116]](#cite_note-116)[[117]](#cite_note-117) Other methods of assaying and purifying smaller amounts of gold include parting and inquartation as well as [cupellation](/wiki/Cupellation "Cupellation"), or refining methods based on the dissolution of gold in aqua regia.[[118]](#cite_note-118)

### Recycling

In 1997, recycled gold accounted for approximately 20% of the 2700 tons of gold supplied to the market.[[119]](#cite_note-119) Jewelry companies such as Generation Collection and computer companies including [Dell](/wiki/Dell "Dell") conduct recycling.[[120]](#cite_note-120)

As of 2020, the amount of [carbon dioxide](/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") CO2 produced in mining a kilogram of gold is 16 tonnes, while recycling a kilogram of gold produces 53 kilograms of CO2 equivalent. Approximately 30 percent of the global gold supply is recycled and not mined as of 2020.[[121]](#cite_note-121)

### Pollution

Gold production is associated with contribution to hazardous [pollution](/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_mining "Environmental effects of mining").[[124]](#cite_note-124)

Low-grade gold ore may contain less than one [ppm](/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") gold metal; such ore is [ground](/wiki/Milling_(grinding) "Milling (grinding)") and mixed with [sodium cyanide](/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide") to dissolve the gold. Cyanide is a highly poisonous chemical, which can kill living creatures when exposed in minute quantities. Many [cyanide spills](/wiki/List_of_gold_mining_disasters "List of gold mining disasters")[[125]](#cite_note-125) from gold mines have occurred in both developed and developing countries which killed aquatic life in long stretches of affected rivers. Environmentalists consider these events major environmental disasters.[[126]](#cite_note-126)[[127]](#cite_note-127) Up to thirty tons of used ore can be dumped as waste for producing one troy ounce of gold.[[128]](#cite_note-NYT-2005-128) Gold ore dumps are the source of many heavy elements such as cadmium, lead, zinc, copper, [arsenic](/wiki/Arsenic "Arsenic"), [selenium](/wiki/Selenium "Selenium") and mercury. When sulfide-bearing minerals in these ore dumps are exposed to air and water, the sulfide transforms into [sulfuric acid](/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") which in turn dissolves these heavy metals facilitating their passage into surface water and ground water. This process is called [acid mine drainage](/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage "Acid mine drainage"). These gold ore dumps contain long-term, highly [hazardous waste](/wiki/Hazardous_waste "Hazardous waste").[[128]](#cite_note-NYT-2005-128)

It was once common to use mercury to recover gold from ore, but today the use of mercury is largely limited to small-scale individual miners.[[129]](#cite_note-129) Minute quantities of mercury compounds can reach water bodies, causing heavy metal contamination. Mercury can then enter into the human food chain in the form of [methylmercury](/wiki/Methylmercury "Methylmercury"). [Mercury poisoning](/wiki/Mercury_poisoning "Mercury poisoning") in humans can cause severe brain damage.[[130]](#cite_note-130)

Gold extraction is also a highly energy-intensive industry — extracting ore from deep mines and grinding the large quantity of ore for further chemical extraction requires nearly 25 [kWh](/wiki/Kilowatt-hour "Kilowatt-hour") of electricity per gram of gold produced.[[131]](#cite_note-131)

## Monetary use

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Two_20kr_gold_coins.png/250px-Two_20kr_gold_coins.png)

Gold has been [widely used](/wiki/History_of_money "History of money") throughout the world as [money](/wiki/Money "Money"),[[132]](#cite_note-132) for efficient indirect exchange (versus [barter](/wiki/Barter "Barter")), and to store wealth in [hoards](/wiki/Hoard "Hoard"). For exchange purposes, [mints](/wiki/Mint_(coin) "Mint (coin)") produce standardized [gold bullion](/wiki/Bullion "Bullion") [coins](/wiki/Coins "Coins"), [bars](/wiki/Gold_bar "Gold bar") and [other units](/wiki/Good_delivery "Good delivery") of fixed weight and purity.

The first known coins containing gold were struck in Lydia, Asia Minor, around 600 BC.[[96]](#cite_note-Lion-2003-96) The *[talent](/wiki/Talent_(measurement) "Talent (measurement)")* coin of gold in use during the periods of Grecian history both before and during the time of the life of Homer weighed between 8.42 and 8.75 grams.[[133]](#cite_note-133) From an earlier preference in using silver, European economies re-established the minting of gold as coinage during the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries.[[134]](#cite_note-Postan-1967-134)

[Bills](/wiki/Real_bills_doctrine "Real bills doctrine") (that mature into gold coin) and [gold certificates](/wiki/Gold_certificates "Gold certificates") (convertible into gold coin at the issuing bank) added to the circulating stock of [gold standard](/wiki/Gold_standard "Gold standard") money in most 19th century industrial economies. In preparation for [World War I](/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") the warring nations moved to fractional gold standards, inflating their currencies to finance the war effort. Post-war, the victorious countries, most notably Britain, gradually restored gold-convertibility, but international flows of gold via bills of exchange remained embargoed; international shipments were made exclusively for bilateral trades or to pay war reparations.

After [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") gold was replaced by a system of nominally [convertible currencies](/wiki/Convertible_currency "Convertible currency") related by fixed exchange rates following the [Bretton Woods system](/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system "Bretton Woods system"). [Gold standards](/wiki/Gold_standard "Gold standard") and the direct convertibility of currencies to gold have been abandoned by world governments, led in 1971 by the United States' refusal to redeem its dollars in gold. [Fiat currency](/wiki/Fiat_currency "Fiat currency") now fills most monetary roles. [Switzerland](/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland") was the last country to tie its currency to gold; this was ended by a referendum in 1999.[[135]](#cite_note-135)

Central banks continue to keep a portion of their liquid reserves as gold in some form, and metals exchanges such as the [London Bullion Market Association](/wiki/London_Bullion_Market_Association "London Bullion Market Association") still clear transactions denominated in gold, including future delivery contracts. Today, [gold mining](/wiki/Gold_mining "Gold mining") output is declining.[[136]](#cite_note-136) With the sharp growth of economies in the 20th century, and increasing foreign exchange, the world's [gold reserves](/wiki/Gold_reserve "Gold reserve") and their trading market have become a small fraction of all markets and fixed exchange rates of currencies to gold have been replaced by floating prices for gold and gold [future contract](/wiki/Futures_contract "Futures contract"). Though the gold stock grows by only 1% or 2% per year, very little metal is irretrievably consumed. Inventory above ground would satisfy many decades of industrial and even artisan uses at current prices.

The gold proportion ([fineness](/wiki/Fineness "Fineness")) of alloys is measured by [karat](/wiki/Fineness#karat "Fineness") (k). Pure gold (commercially termed *fine* gold) is designated as 24 karat, abbreviated 24k. English gold coins intended for circulation from 1526 into the 1930s were typically a standard 22k alloy called [crown gold](/wiki/Crown_gold "Crown gold"),[[137]](#cite_note-137) for hardness (American gold coins for circulation after 1837 contain an alloy of 0.900 fine gold, or 21.6 kt).[[138]](#cite_note-138)

Often the prices of various [platinum](/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") group metals can be much higher than gold, although gold has been used as a standard for [currencies](/wiki/Currency "Currency") to a greater degree than the platinum group metals. Gold has been used as a symbol for purity, value, royalty, and particularly roles that combine these properties. Gold as a sign of wealth and prestige was ridiculed by [Thomas More](/wiki/Thomas_More "Thomas More") in his treatise *[Utopia](/wiki/Utopia_(More_book) "Utopia (More book)")*. On that imaginary island, gold is so abundant that it is used to make chains for slaves, tableware, and lavatory seats. When ambassadors from other countries arrive, dressed in ostentatious gold jewels and badges, the Utopians mistake them for menial servants, paying homage instead to the most modestly dressed of their party.

The [ISO 4217](/wiki/ISO_4217 "ISO 4217") currency code of gold is XAU.[[139]](#cite_note-139) Many holders of gold store it in form of [bullion](/wiki/Bullion "Bullion") coins or [bars](/wiki/Gold_bar "Gold bar") as a hedge against [inflation](/wiki/Inflation "Inflation") or other economic disruptions. A paper by the [National Bureau of Economic Research](/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Economic_Research "National Bureau of Economic Research") found that gold may be reliable as an inflation hedge over long timescales (centuries) but not over practical timescales.[[140]](#cite_note-140) Modern [bullion coins](/wiki/Bullion_coin "Bullion coin") for investment or collector purposes do not require good mechanical wear properties; they are typically fine gold at 24k, although the [American Gold Eagle](/wiki/American_Gold_Eagle "American Gold Eagle") and the British [gold sovereign](/wiki/Sovereign_(British_coin) "Sovereign (British coin)") continue to be minted in 22k (0.92) metal in historical tradition, and the South African [Krugerrand](/wiki/Krugerrand "Krugerrand"), first released in 1967, is also 22k (0.92).[[141]](#cite_note-141)

The *special issue* [Canadian Gold Maple Leaf](/wiki/Canadian_Gold_Maple_Leaf "Canadian Gold Maple Leaf") coin contains the highest purity gold of any [bullion coin](/wiki/Bullion_coin "Bullion coin"), at 99.999% or 0.99999, while the *popular issue* Canadian Gold Maple Leaf coin has a purity of 99.99%. In 2006, the [United States Mint](/wiki/United_States_Mint "United States Mint") began producing the [American Buffalo](/wiki/American_Buffalo_(coin) "American Buffalo (coin)") gold bullion coin with a purity of 99.99%. The [Australian](/wiki/Australia "Australia") Gold Kangaroos were first coined in 1986 as the [Australian Gold Nugget](/wiki/Australian_Gold_Nugget "Australian Gold Nugget") but changed the reverse design in 1989. Other modern coins include the [Austrian](/wiki/Austria "Austria") [Vienna Philharmonic](/wiki/Vienna_Philharmonic_(coin) "Vienna Philharmonic (coin)") bullion coin and the [Chinese Gold Panda](/wiki/Chinese_Gold_Panda "Chinese Gold Panda").[[142]](#cite_note-142)

### Price

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Gold_price_in_USD.png/500px-Gold_price_in_USD.png)

Like other precious metals, gold is measured by [troy weight](/wiki/Troy_weight "Troy weight") and by grams. The proportion of gold in the alloy is measured by *[karat](/wiki/Fineness#karat "Fineness")* (k), with 24 karat (24k) being pure gold (100%), and lower karat numbers proportionally less (18k = 75%). The purity of a [gold bar](/wiki/Gold_bar "Gold bar") or coin can also be expressed as a decimal figure ranging from 0 to 1, known as the [millesimal fineness](/wiki/Fineness#millesimal_fineness "Fineness"), such as 0.995 being nearly pure.

The price of gold is determined through trading in the gold and [derivatives](/wiki/Derivative_(finance) "Derivative (finance)") markets, but a procedure known as the [Gold Fixing](/wiki/Gold_Fixing "Gold Fixing") in [London](/wiki/London "London"), originating in September 1919, provides a daily benchmark price to the industry. The afternoon fixing was introduced in 1968 to provide a price when US markets are open.[[143]](#cite_note-143)

## Other applications

### Jewelry

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/CairoEgMuseumTaaMaskMostlyPhotographed.jpg/250px-CairoEgMuseumTaaMaskMostlyPhotographed.jpg)

Pure (24k) gold is often [alloyed](/wiki/Alloy "Alloy") with other metals for use in jewelry, altering its hardness and ductility, melting point, color and other properties. Alloys with lower [karat rating](/wiki/Fineness#Karat "Fineness"), typically 22k, 18k, 14k or 10k, contain higher percentages of copper, silver, or other base metals in the alloy.[[33]](#cite_note-WorldGoldCouncil-33) Nickel is toxic, and its release from nickel white gold is controlled by legislation in Europe.[[33]](#cite_note-WorldGoldCouncil-33) Palladium-gold alloys are more expensive than those using nickel.[[144]](#cite_note-144) High-karat white gold alloys are more resistant to corrosion than are either pure silver or [sterling silver](/wiki/Sterling_silver "Sterling silver"), though not as corrosion-proof as [platinum](/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") jewelry. The Japanese craft of [Mokume-gane](/wiki/Mokume-gane "Mokume-gane") exploits the color contrasts between laminated colored gold alloys to produce decorative wood-grain effects.

Gold [solder](/wiki/Solder "Solder") is used for joining the components of gold jewelry by high-temperature hard soldering or [brazing](/wiki/Brazing "Brazing"). If the work is to be of [hallmarking](/wiki/Hallmark "Hallmark") quality, the gold solder alloy must match the fineness of the work, and alloy formulas are manufactured to color-match yellow and white gold. Gold solder is usually made in at least three melting-point ranges referred to as Easy, Medium and Hard. By using the hard, high-melting point solder first, followed by solders with progressively lower melting points, goldsmiths can assemble complex items with several separate soldered joints. Gold can also be made into [thread](/wiki/Gold_thread "Gold thread") and used in [embroidery](/wiki/Embroidery "Embroidery").

### Electronics

Only 10% of the world consumption of new gold produced goes to industry,[[16]](#cite_note-Soos-2011-16) but by far the most important industrial use for new gold is in fabrication of corrosion-free [electrical connectors](/wiki/Electrical_connectors "Electrical connectors") in computers and other electrical devices. For example, according to the World Gold Council, a typical cell phone may contain 50 mg of gold, worth about three dollars. But since nearly one billion cell phones are produced each year, a gold value of US$2.82 (equivalent to $3 in 2025) in each phone adds to US$2.82 billion in gold from just this application (equivalent to $3,102,513,077 in 2025).[[145]](#cite_note-145)

Though gold is attacked by free chlorine, its good conductivity and general resistance to oxidation and corrosion in other environments (including resistance to non-chlorinated acids) has led to its widespread industrial use in the electronic era as a thin-layer coating on [electrical connectors](/wiki/Electrical_connector "Electrical connector"), thereby ensuring good connection. For example, gold is used in the connectors of the more expensive electronics cables, such as audio, video and [USB](/wiki/USB "USB") cables. The benefit of using gold over other connector metals such as [tin](/wiki/Tin "Tin") in these applications has been debated; gold connectors are often criticized by audio-visual experts as unnecessary for most consumers and seen as simply a marketing ploy. However, the use of gold in other applications in electronic sliding contacts in highly humid or corrosive atmospheres, and in use for contacts with a very high failure cost (certain [computers](/wiki/Computer "Computer"), communications equipment, [spacecraft](/wiki/Spacecraft "Spacecraft"), [jet aircraft](/wiki/Jet_aircraft "Jet aircraft") engines) remains very common.[[146]](#cite_note-146)

Besides sliding electrical contacts, gold is also used in [electrical contacts](/wiki/Electrical_contacts "Electrical contacts") because of its resistance to [corrosion](/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion"), [electrical conductivity](/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity"), [ductility](/wiki/Ductile "Ductile") and lack of [toxicity](/wiki/Toxicity "Toxicity").[[147]](#cite_note-147) Switch contacts are generally subjected to more intense corrosion stress than are sliding contacts. Fine gold wires are used to connect [semiconductor devices](/wiki/Semiconductor_device "Semiconductor device") to their packages through a process known as [wire bonding](/wiki/Wire_bonding "Wire bonding").

The concentration of free electrons in gold metal is 5.91×1022 cm−3.[[148]](#cite_note-148) Gold is highly [conductive](/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity") to electricity and has been used for [electrical wiring](/wiki/Electrical_wiring "Electrical wiring") in some high-energy applications (only silver and copper are more conductive per volume, but gold is the only of these three with zero corrosion). For example, gold electrical wires were used during some of the [Manhattan Project](/wiki/Manhattan_Project "Manhattan Project")'s atomic experiments, but large high-current silver wires were used in the [calutron](/wiki/Calutron "Calutron") isotope separator magnets in the project.

It is estimated that 16% of the world's presently-accounted-for gold and 22% of the world's silver is contained in electronic technology in Japan.[[149]](#cite_note-149)

### Medicine

There are only two gold compounds currently employed as pharmaceuticals in modern medicine ([sodium aurothiomalate](/wiki/Sodium_aurothiomalate "Sodium aurothiomalate") and [auranofin](/wiki/Auranofin "Auranofin")), used in the treatment of arthritis and other similar conditions in the US due to their [anti-inflammatory](/wiki/Anti-inflammatory "Anti-inflammatory") properties. These drugs have been explored as a means to help to reduce the pain and swelling of [rheumatoid arthritis](/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis "Rheumatoid arthritis"), and also (historically) against [tuberculosis](/wiki/Tuberculosis "Tuberculosis") and some parasites.[[150]](#cite_note-Messori-2004-150)[[151]](#cite_note-mech08-151)

Historically, metallic and gold compounds have long been used for medicinal purposes. Gold, usually as the metal, is perhaps the most anciently administered medicine (apparently by shamanic practitioners)[[151]](#cite_note-mech08-151) and known to [Dioscorides](/wiki/Dioscorides "Dioscorides").[[152]](#cite_note-152)[[153]](#cite_note-153) In medieval times, gold was often seen as beneficial for the health, in the belief that something so rare and beautiful could not be anything but healthy.

In the 19th century gold had a reputation as an [anxiolytic](/wiki/Anxiolytic "Anxiolytic"), a therapy for nervous disorders. [Depression](/wiki/Depression_(mood) "Depression (mood)"), [epilepsy](/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy"), [migraine](/wiki/Migraine "Migraine"), and glandular problems such as [amenorrhea](/wiki/Amenorrhea "Amenorrhea") and [impotence](/wiki/Impotence "Impotence") were treated, and most notably [alcoholism](/wiki/Alcoholism "Alcoholism") (Keeley, 1897).[[154]](#cite_note-154)

The apparent paradox[*[further explanation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")*] of the actual toxicology of the substance suggests the possibility of serious gaps in the understanding of the action of gold in physiology.[[155]](#cite_note-155) Only salts and radioisotopes of gold are of pharmacological value, since elemental (metallic) gold is inert to all chemicals it encounters inside the body (e.g., ingested gold cannot be attacked by stomach acid).

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Gold255.jpg/250px-Gold255.jpg)

Gold alloys are used in [restorative dentistry](/wiki/Restorative_dentistry "Restorative dentistry"), especially in tooth restorations, such as [crowns](/wiki/Crown_(dental_restoration) "Crown (dental restoration)") and permanent [bridges](/wiki/Bridge_(dentistry) "Bridge (dentistry)"). The gold alloys' slight malleability facilitates the creation of a superior molar mating surface with other teeth and produces results that are generally more satisfactory than those produced by the creation of porcelain crowns. The use of gold crowns in more prominent teeth such as incisors is favored in some cultures and discouraged in others.

[Colloidal gold](/wiki/Colloidal_gold "Colloidal gold") preparations (suspensions of [gold nanoparticles](/wiki/Gold_nanoparticle "Gold nanoparticle")) in water are intensely red-[colored](/wiki/Color "Color"), and can be made with tightly controlled particle sizes up to a few tens of nanometers across by reduction of gold chloride with [citrate](/wiki/Citrate "Citrate") or [ascorbate](/wiki/Ascorbate "Ascorbate") ions. Colloidal gold is used in research applications in medicine, biology and [materials science](/wiki/Materials_science "Materials science"). The technique of [immunogold labeling](/wiki/Immunogold_labeling "Immunogold labeling") exploits the ability of the gold particles to adsorb protein molecules onto their surfaces. Colloidal gold particles coated with specific antibodies can be used as probes for the presence and position of antigens on the surfaces of cells.[[156]](#cite_note-156) In ultrathin sections of tissues viewed by [electron microscopy](/wiki/Electron_microscope "Electron microscope"), the immunogold labels appear as extremely dense round spots at the position of the [antigen](/wiki/Antigen "Antigen").[[157]](#cite_note-157)

Gold, or alloys of gold and [palladium](/wiki/Palladium "Palladium"), are applied as conductive coating to biological specimens and other non-conducting materials such as plastics and glass to be viewed in a [scanning electron microscope](/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope "Scanning electron microscope"). The coating, which is usually applied by [sputtering](/wiki/Sputtering "Sputtering") with an [argon](/wiki/Argon "Argon") [plasma](/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"), has a triple role in this application. Gold's very high electrical conductivity drains [electrical charge](/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") to earth, and its very high density provides stopping power for electrons in the [electron beam](/wiki/Electron_beam "Electron beam"), helping to limit the depth to which the electron beam penetrates the specimen. This improves definition of the position and topography of the specimen surface and increases the [spatial resolution](/wiki/Angular_resolution "Angular resolution") of the image. Gold also produces a high output of [secondary electrons](/wiki/Secondary_emission "Secondary emission") when irradiated by an electron beam, and these low-energy electrons are the most commonly used signal source used in the scanning electron microscope.[[158]](#cite_note-158)

The isotope [gold-198](/wiki/Gold-198 "Gold-198") ([half-life](/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") 2.7 days) is used in [nuclear medicine](/wiki/Nuclear_medicine "Nuclear medicine"), in some [cancer](/wiki/Cancer "Cancer") treatments and for treating other diseases.[[159]](#cite_note-159)[[160]](#cite_note-160)

### Cuisine

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Dessert_%2810938449815%29.jpg/330px-Dessert_%2810938449815%29.jpg)

Gold can be used in food and has the [E number](/wiki/E_number "E number") 175.[[161]](#cite_note-FSA-2007-161) It can be applied as [gold leaf](/wiki/Gold_leaf "Gold leaf"), flake or dust for decorative purposes. Since metallic gold is generally considered inert to all body chemistry, it has no taste, provides no nutrition, and leaves the body unaltered.[[162]](#cite_note-KingUses-162) In 2016, the [European Food Safety Authority](/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority "European Food Safety Authority") published an opinion on the re-evaluation of gold as a food additive. Concerns included the possible presence of minute amounts of gold [nanoparticles](/wiki/Nanoparticle "Nanoparticle") in the food additive, and that gold nanoparticles have been shown to be [genotoxic](/wiki/Genotoxic "Genotoxic") in mammalian cells [in vitro](/wiki/In_vitro "In vitro").[[163]](#cite_note-163)

Decorative use of gold flake goes back to [medieval Europe](/wiki/Medieval_Europe "Medieval Europe") as a decoration in food and drinks among nobility.[[164]](#cite_note-164) Leaf or flakes are used today in sweets and drinks.[[165]](#cite_note-165) [Vark](/wiki/Vark "Vark") is a foil or leaf composed of a pure metal that can include gold,[[166]](#cite_note-166) and is used for [garnishing](/wiki/Garnish_(food) "Garnish (food)") sweets in South Asian cuisine.

Danziger [Goldwasser](/wiki/Goldwasser "Goldwasser") (English: Goldwater) is a traditional German herbal [liqueur](/wiki/Liqueur "Liqueur")[[167]](#cite_note-167) produced in what is today [Gdańsk](/wiki/Gda%C5%84sk "Gdańsk"), [Poland](/wiki/Poland "Poland"), and [Schwabach](/wiki/Schwabach "Schwabach"), Germany, and contains flakes of gold leaf. There are also some expensive (~ $1,000, equivalent to $1,501 in 2025) cocktails which contain flakes of gold leaf.[[162]](#cite_note-KingUses-162)[*[verification needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")*]

## Toxicity

Pure metallic (elemental) gold is non-toxic and non-irritating when ingested[[173]](#cite_note-173) and is sometimes used as a food decoration in the form of [gold leaf](/wiki/Gold_leaf "Gold leaf").[[174]](#cite_note-174) Metallic gold is also a component of the alcoholic drinks [Goldschläger](/wiki/Goldschl%C3%A4ger "Goldschläger"), [Gold Strike](/wiki/Gold_Strike_(drink) "Gold Strike (drink)"), and [Goldwasser](/wiki/Goldwasser "Goldwasser"). Metallic gold is approved as a [food additive](/wiki/Food_additive "Food additive") in the EU ([E175](/wiki/E_number "E number") in the [Codex Alimentarius](/wiki/Codex_Alimentarius "Codex Alimentarius")). Although the gold ion is toxic, the acceptance of metallic gold as a food additive is due to its relative chemical inertness, and resistance to being corroded or transformed into soluble salts (gold compounds) by any known chemical process which would be encountered in the human body.

Soluble compounds ([gold salts](/wiki/Gold_salts "Gold salts")) such as [gold chloride](/wiki/Gold(I,III)_chloride "Gold(I,III) chloride") are toxic to the liver and kidneys. Common [cyanide](/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") salts of gold such as potassium gold cyanide, used in gold [electroplating](/wiki/Electroplating "Electroplating"), are toxic by virtue of both their cyanide and gold content. There are rare cases of lethal gold poisoning from [potassium gold cyanide](/wiki/Potassium_gold_cyanide "Potassium gold cyanide").[[175]](#cite_note-175)[[176]](#cite_note-176) Gold toxicity can be ameliorated with [chelation therapy](/wiki/Chelation_therapy "Chelation therapy") with an agent such as [dimercaprol](/wiki/Dimercaprol "Dimercaprol").

Gold metal was voted [Allergen of the Year](/wiki/Allergen_of_the_Year "Allergen of the Year") in 2001 by the American Contact Dermatitis Society; gold contact allergies affect mostly women.[[177]](#cite_note-Tsuruta-2001-177) Despite this, gold is a relatively non-potent contact allergen, in comparison with metals like [nickel](/wiki/Nickel "Nickel").[[178]](#cite_note-178)

A sample of the fungus *[Aspergillus niger](/wiki/Aspergillus_niger "Aspergillus niger")* was found growing from gold mining solution; and was found to contain cyano metal complexes, such as gold, silver, copper, iron and zinc. The fungus also plays a role in the solubilization of heavy metal sulfides.[[179]](#cite_note-179)

## Chemical Symbol for Gold: Why the Atomic Number is 79?

## Chemical Symbol for Gold: Why the Atomic Number is 79?

Gold is a chemical element with the symbol Au and atomic number 79. The name is from the Latin: aurum, meaning "shiny dawn". Gold is a dense, soft, shiny solid metal. It is a chemical element of the group of elements known as transition metals.

The red-orange metal can be found in nature as nuggets or grains. But it is usually mixed with other metals because pure gold is too soft to work easily. Gold being soft allows you to make gold jewellery, by mixing other metals such as silver or copper to make what are called 'gold alloys'.

Gold is not just used to make coins, [gold bars](https://bullionjoy.com/product-category/gold-bars/), and jewellery but can also be used to coat electrical devices in order to prevent corrosion. Gold is a good conductor of electricity so it can be used for connectors on instruments with electric circuitry as well.

![Chemical symbol for gold](https://cdn.shopify.com/s/files/1/0678/0534/8136/t/3/assets/Chemical-symbol-for-gold.png?v=1671637143)

## Why is Atomic Chemical Symbol for Gold 79?

The reason why Chemical Symbol for gold 79 is because if gold was to be placed under a microscope, we would see electrons orbiting around a nucleus that contains 79 protons. Hence why Scientific Chemical Symbol for Gold is Au because its Atomic Number (a number that represents the number of protons in an atom) is 79.

### Why is Gold a Precious Metal?

Gold is one of the most sought-after metals in the world and there's no wonder why. Not only does gold has an attractive colour, but also has many qualities that make it desirable like durability to corrosion and oxidation which makes it a noble metal. Gold shares qualities with other precious metals like silver, platinum, or palladium; they don't rust or corrode either! (But not all precious metals are noble metals)
