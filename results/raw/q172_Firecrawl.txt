**Silicon Valley** is a region in [Northern California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_California "Northern California") that is a global center for [high technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_technology "High technology") and [innovation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innovation "Innovation"). Located in the southern part of the [San Francisco Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area "San Francisco Bay Area"), it corresponds roughly to the geographical area of the [Santa Clara Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_Valley "Santa Clara Valley").[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Malone_Page_xix-1)[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Matthews_Page_2-2)[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Shueh_Page_8-3)

The cities of [Sunnyvale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California "Sunnyvale, California"), [Mountain View](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_View,_California "Mountain View, California"), [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California") and [Menlo Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California "Menlo Park, California") are frequently cited as the birthplace of Silicon Valley. Other major Silicon Valley cities are [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California"), [Santa Clara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California "Santa Clara, California"), [Redwood City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redwood_City,_California "Redwood City, California") and [Cupertino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cupertino,_California "Cupertino, California").\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] As of 2015, the San Jose Metropolitan Area has the third-highest GDP per capita in the world (after [Zurich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zurich "Zurich") and [Oslo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oslo "Oslo")), according to the [Brookings Institution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brookings_Institution "Brookings Institution").[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-4) As of June 2021, it also had the highest percentage of homes valued at $1 million or more in the United States.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NYT-20210617-5)

Silicon Valley is home to many of the world's largest [high-tech corporations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology_company "Technology company"), including the headquarters of more than 30 businesses in the [_Fortune_ 1000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fortune_1000 "Fortune 1000"), and thousands of [startup companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Startup_company "Startup company").\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] The Bay Area also accounts for one-third of all of the [venture capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venture_capital "Venture capital") investment in the United States, which has helped it to become a leading hub and [startup ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Startup_ecosystem "Startup ecosystem") for high-tech innovation, although the tech ecosystem has recently become more geographically dispersed.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NewYorkCloserThanEvertoBayAreaTech-6)[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-ManhattanNowBiggestTechHub-7) It was in Silicon Valley that the silicon-based [integrated circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit"), the [microprocessor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microprocessor "Microprocessor"), and the [microcomputer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcomputer "Microcomputer"), among other technologies, were developed.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]As of 2021[\[update\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Silicon_Valley&action=edit), Santa Clara and San Mateo Counties employed about a half million [information technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_technology "Information technology") workers.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-8)

As more high-tech companies were established across San Jose and the Santa Clara Valley, and then north towards the Bay Area's two other major cities, [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco "San Francisco") and [Oakland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland,_California "Oakland, California"), the term "Silicon Valley" came to have two definitions: a narrower geographic one, referring to [Santa Clara County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Clara_County,_California "Santa Clara County, California") and southeastern [San Mateo County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Mateo_County,_California "San Mateo County, California"), and a [metonymical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metonymy "Metonymy") definition referring to high-tech businesses in the entire Bay Area.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] The name also became a global synonym for leading high-tech research and enterprises, and thus inspired [similarly named locations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_places_with_%22Silicon%22_names "List of places with \"Silicon\" names"), as well as [research parks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_research_parks "List of research parks") and [technology centers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_technology_centers "List of technology centers") with comparable structures all around the world. Many headquarters of tech companies in Silicon Valley have become hotspots for [tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism "Tourism").[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-9)[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-10)[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-11)

## Etymology

"Silicon" refers to the [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") used in [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon")-based [transistors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor") and [integrated circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit") chips, which is the focus of a large number of [computer hardware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_hardware "Computer hardware") and [software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software "Software") innovators and manufacturers in the region.

The popularization of the name is often credited to [Don Hoefler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Hoefler "Don Hoefler"), the first journalist to use the term in a news story.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Malone_Page_xix-1) His article "Silicon Valley U.S.A." was published in the January 11, 1971, issue of the weekly trade newspaper _[Electronic News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_News "Electronic News")_. In preparation for this report, during a lunch meeting with marketing people who were visiting the area, he heard them use the term.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-DH-71-12) Earlier uses outside journalism exist; for example, a May 1970 advertisement in the _[Peninsula Times Tribune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peninsula_Times_Tribune "Peninsula Times Tribune")_ described a [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto "Palo Alto") company that "helps production people in Silicon Valley."[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-13) It is possible that the nickname was coined by Washington, D.C.–based Department of Defense people who did business with California chip makers.[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-14) However, the term did not gain widespread use until the early 1980s,[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Malone_Page_xix-1) at the time of the introduction of the [IBM PC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer "IBM Personal Computer") and numerous related hardware and software products to the consumer market.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/NatSem_INS8080AJ_1.jpg/500px-NatSem_INS8080AJ_1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NatSem_INS8080AJ_1.jpg) Silicon Valley derives its name from the [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") used in [transistors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor") and [computer chips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit"), pioneered in the region in the 20th century.

The urbanized area is built upon an [alluvial plain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alluvial_plain "Alluvial plain")[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-15) within a [longitudinal valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitudinal_valley "Longitudinal valley") formed by roughly parallel earthquake faults. The area between the faults subsided into a [graben](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graben "Graben") or dropped valley.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-TimelineWater-16)[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-17) Hoefler defined Silicon Valley as the urbanized parts of "the San Francisco Peninsula and Santa Clara Valley".[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-DH-71-12) Before the expansive growth of the tech industry, the region had been the largest fruit-producing and packing region in the world up through the 1960s, with 39 fruit canneries.[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-18)[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-19) The nickname it had been known as during that period was "the Valley of Heart’s Delight".[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-20)[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-21)

## History

Silicon Valley was born through the intersection of several contributing factors, including a skilled science research base housed in area universities, plentiful [venture capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venture_capital "Venture capital"), permissive government regulation, and steady [U.S. Department of Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense "United States Department of Defense") spending. [Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University")’s leadership was especially important in the Valley's early development.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-22) Together these elements formed the basis of its growth and success.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Castells2011p52-23) The [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") was more friendly than other countries to business investment, charging much lower taxes on [capital gains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_gains "Capital gains") since the [Revenue Act of 1921](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revenue_Act_of_1921 "Revenue Act of 1921"), and featuring particularly loose [free market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_market "Free market") controls over new business. In 1953, the [Small Business Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Business_Administration "Small Business Administration") was created to foster [startups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Startup_company "Startup company"), giving a boost to entrepreneurs. [Northern California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_California "Northern California") was even more welcoming, with a group of venture capitalists actively seeking high-tech business ideas, clustered on [Sand Hill Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_Hill_Road "Sand Hill Road") in [Menlo Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California "Menlo Park, California") and [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California"). [California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California "California")'s civil code undermined the usual [non-compete clauses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-compete_clause "Non-compete clause") that effectively tied employees to their companies in other states, allowing California workers to freely apply the knowledge they gained from their previous employer. This gave Silicon Valley an advantage over other American [tech hubs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tech_hub "Tech hub") such as [Massachusetts Route 128](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_128 "Massachusetts Route 128") curving around [Boston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston "Boston").[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-24)

### Early military origins

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Moffett_Field_circa_1934.jpg/500px-Moffett_Field_circa_1934.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Moffett_Field_circa_1934.jpg) Established in 1931, [Moffett Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moffett_Federal_Airfield "Moffett Federal Airfield") in [Sunnyvale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California "Sunnyvale, California")/ [Mountain View](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_View,_California "Mountain View, California") has played a strategic role in Silicon Valley's evolution, researching and developing key technologies, first for the [U.S. military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._military "U.S. military") and then for [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA"). Today it hosts the [Ames Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Ames_Research_Center "NASA Ames Research Center").

The [San Francisco Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area "San Francisco Bay Area") had long been a major site of [United States Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Navy "United States Navy") research and technology. In 1909, [Charles Herrold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Herrold "Charles Herrold") started the first [radio station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_station "Radio station") in the United States with regularly scheduled programming in [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California"). Later that year, Stanford University graduate [Cyril Elwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyril_Frank_Elwell "Cyril Frank Elwell") purchased the U.S. patents for [Poulsen arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_converter "Arc converter") radio transmission technology and founded the Federal Telegraph Corporation (FTC) in [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California"). Over the next decade, the FTC created the world's first global radio communication system, and signed a contract with the Navy in 1912.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Sturgeon-25)

In 1933, [Air Base Sunnyvale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moffett_Federal_Airfield "Moffett Federal Airfield"), California, was commissioned by the [United States Government](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Government "United States Government") for use as a Naval Air Station (NAS) to house the airship _[USS Macon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Macon_(ZRS-5) "USS Macon (ZRS-5)")_ in [Hangar One](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangar_One_(Mountain_View,_California) "Hangar One (Mountain View, California)"). The station was renamed NAS [Moffett Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moffett_Field "Moffett Field"), and between 1933 and 1947, U.S. Navy blimps were based there.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-26) A number of technology firms had set up shop in the area around Moffett Field to serve the Navy. When the Navy gave up its airship ambitions and moved most of its west coast operations to [San Diego](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Diego "San Diego"), the [National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics "National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics") (NACA, forerunner of [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA")) took over portions of Moffett Field for [aeronautics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeronautics "Aeronautics") research. Many of the original companies stayed, while new ones moved in. The immediate area was soon filled with [aerospace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerospace "Aerospace") firms, such as [Lockheed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation "Lockheed Corporation"), which was the area's largest employer from the 1950s into 1980s.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-27)

### Role of Stanford University

See also: [Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University") and [Stanford Research Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Research_Park "Stanford Research Park")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Stanford_University_Main_Quad_May_2011_001_%28cropped%29.jpg/500px-Stanford_University_Main_Quad_May_2011_001_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stanford_University_Main_Quad_May_2011_001_(cropped).jpg) [Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University") played the central role in the emergence of Silicon Valley, both through its academic programs and through its real investments into the local tech ecosystem, such as with the [Stanford Research Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Research_Park "Stanford Research Park").[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Markoff-28)

[Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University"), its affiliates, and graduates have played a major role in the development of the culture of collaboration among high-tech companies.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Markoff-28) A powerful sense of regional solidarity shaped the outlook of inventors and engineers in California; contrasting markedly from the insular and competitive environment of engineering firms on the [East Coast of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States "East Coast of the United States").[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Sturgeon-25)[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-29) From the 1890s, Stanford University's [leaders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leader "Leader") saw its mission as service to the (American) [West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_United_States "Western United States") and shaped the school accordingly. At the same time, the perceived exploitation of the West at the hands of eastern interests fueled [booster-like](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boosterism "Boosterism") attempts to build self-sufficient local industry. Thus regionalism helped align Stanford's interests with those of the area's high-tech firms.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-30)

[Frederick Terman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Terman "Frederick Terman"), as Stanford University's dean of the school of engineering from 1946,[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-31) encouraged faculty and graduates to start their own companies. In 1951 Terman spearheaded the formation of Stanford Industrial Park (now [Stanford Research Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Research_Park "Stanford Research Park"), an area surrounding [Page Mill Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Page_Mill_Road "Page Mill Road"), south west of [El Camino Real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Camino_Real_(California) "El Camino Real (California)") and extending beyond [Foothill Expressway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_county_routes_in_zone_G#G5 "California county routes in zone G") to Arastradero Road), where the university leased portions of its land to high-tech firms.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-32) Terman nurtured companies like [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard"), [Varian Associates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varian_Associates "Varian Associates"), [Eastman Kodak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastman_Kodak "Eastman Kodak"), [General Electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Electric "General Electric"), [Lockheed Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation "Lockheed Corporation"), and other high-tech firms, until what would become Silicon Valley grew up around the Stanford University campus.

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Hewlett-Packard_garage_and_house_exterior_in_Palo_Alto%2C_Silicon_Valley%2C_California_%28_HP_garage_%29.jpg/500px-Hewlett-Packard_garage_and_house_exterior_in_Palo_Alto%2C_Silicon_Valley%2C_California_%28_HP_garage_%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hewlett-Packard_garage_and_house_exterior_in_Palo_Alto,_Silicon_Valley,_California_(_HP_garage_).jpg) The [HP Garage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HP_Garage "HP Garage"), dubbed the "Birthplace of Silicon Valley", where [Bill Hewlett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Hewlett "Bill Hewlett") and [David Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Packard "David Packard") (founders of [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard")) began developing their [audio oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_oscillator "Electronic oscillator") in 1938. Their business philosophy, called [The HP Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_HP_Way "The HP Way"), soon spread throughout Silicon Valley.[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-33)

In 1951, to address the financial demands of Stanford's growth requirements, and to provide local employment-opportunities for graduating students, [Frederick Terman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Terman "Frederick Terman") proposed leasing Stanford's lands for use as an [office park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Office_park "Office park") named the [Stanford Industrial Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Industrial_Park "Stanford Industrial Park") (later [Stanford Research Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Research_Park "Stanford Research Park")). Terman invited only high-technology companies. The first tenant was [Varian Associates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varian_Associates "Varian Associates"), founded by Stanford alumni in the 1930s to build military-radar components. Terman also found [venture capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venture_capital "Venture capital") for civilian-technology start-ups. [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard") became one of the major success-stories. Founded in 1939 in [Packard's garage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Packard's_garage "Packard's garage") by Stanford graduates [Bill Hewlett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Hewlett "Bill Hewlett") and [David Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Packard "David Packard"), Hewlett-Packard moved its offices into the Stanford Research Park shortly after 1953. In 1954 Stanford originated the Honors Cooperative Program to allow full-time employees of the companies to pursue graduate degrees from the university on a part-time basis. The initial companies signed five-year agreements in which they would pay double the tuition for each student in order to cover the costs. Hewlett-Packard has become the largest personal-computer manufacturer in the world, and transformed the home-printing market when it released the first thermal drop-on-demand [ink-jet printer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ink-jet_printer "Ink-jet printer") in 1984.[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-34) Other early tenants included [Eastman Kodak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastman_Kodak "Eastman Kodak"), [General Electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Electric "General Electric"), and [Lockheed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation "Lockheed Corporation").[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-35)

### Rise of Silicon

Main articles: [Silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") and [History of the transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_transistor "History of the transistor")

See also: [Invention of the integrated circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invention_of_the_integrated_circuit "Invention of the integrated circuit") and [Fairchildren companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor#Fairchildren_companies "Fairchild Semiconductor")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/ShockleyBldg_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ShockleyBldg_(cropped).jpg) Plaque commemorating [Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shockley_Semiconductor_Laboratory "Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory") as the first high-tech company in what would become Silicon Valley

In 1956, [William Shockley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Shockley "William Shockley"), the co-inventor of the first working [transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor") (with [John Bardeen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Bardeen "John Bardeen") and [Walter Houser Brattain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Houser_Brattain "Walter Houser Brattain")), moved from [New Jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Jersey "New Jersey") to [Mountain View, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_View,_California "Mountain View, California"), to start [Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shockley_Semiconductor_Laboratory "Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory") to live closer to his ailing mother [May](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_Bradford_Shockley "May Bradford Shockley") in [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California"). Shockley's work served as the basis for many electronic developments for decades.[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-36)[\[37\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-37) Both Frederick Terman and [William Shockley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Shockley "William Shockley") are often called "the father of Silicon Valley".[\[38\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-38)[\[39\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-39) Unlike many other researchers who used germanium as the semiconductor material, Shockley believed that [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") was the better material for making transistors. Shockley intended to replace the current transistor with a new three-element design (today known as the [Shockley diode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shockley_diode "Shockley diode")), but the design was considerably more difficult to build than the "simple" transistor. In 1957, Shockley decided to end research on the silicon transistor. As a result of Shockley's abusive management style, eight engineers left the company to form [Fairchild Semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor "Fairchild Semiconductor"); Shockley referred to them as the " [traitorous eight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traitorous_eight "Traitorous eight")". Two of the original employees of Fairchild Semiconductor, [Robert Noyce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Noyce "Robert Noyce") and [Gordon Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Moore "Gordon Moore"), would go on to found [Intel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel "Intel").[\[40\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-40)[\[41\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-SV110-41)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/1st_IBM_Plant_in_Silicon_Valley_%2816th_%26_St._John_in_San_Jose%29.jpg/500px-1st_IBM_Plant_in_Silicon_Valley_%2816th_%26_St._John_in_San_Jose%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:1st_IBM_Plant_in_Silicon_Valley_(16th_&_St._John_in_San_Jose).jpg) The first [IBM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM "IBM") plant in California, established in [San Jose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose,_California "San Jose, California") in 1943

Following the 1959 inventions of the monolithic [integrated circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit") (IC) chip by [Robert Noyce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Noyce "Robert Noyce") at Fairchild, the first commercial MOS IC was introduced by [General Microelectronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Microelectronics "General Microelectronics") in 1964.[\[42\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-42) The first single-chip microprocessor was the [Intel 4004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_4004 "Intel 4004"),[\[43\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-43) designed and realized by [Federico Faggin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federico_Faggin "Federico Faggin") along with [Ted Hoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcian_Hoff "Marcian Hoff"), [Masatoshi Shima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masatoshi_Shima "Masatoshi Shima") and [Stanley Mazor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Mazor "Stanley Mazor") at Intel in 1971.[\[44\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-computerhistory1971-44)[\[45\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-ieee-45) In April 1974, [Intel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel "Intel") released the [Intel 8080](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_8080 "Intel 8080"), the second [8-bit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-bit_computing "8-bit computing") microprocessor designed and manufactured by [Intel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel "Intel").[\[46\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Electronic_News_Apr_1974-46)

### Origins of the Internet

Main articles: [ARPANET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET "ARPANET") and [History of the Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Internet "History of the Internet")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Arpanet_logical_map%2C_march_1977.png/500px-Arpanet_logical_map%2C_march_1977.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Arpanet_logical_map,_march_1977.png) [ARPANET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET "ARPANET"), the predecessor to the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet"), began to be developed in 1966 by the [U.S. Department of Defense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense "United States Department of Defense") and four research universities in California, including Stanford.

On April 23, 1963, [J. C. R. Licklider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._C._R._Licklider "J. C. R. Licklider"), the first director of the [Information Processing Techniques Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_Processing_Techniques_Office "Information Processing Techniques Office") (IPTO) at [The Pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Pentagon "The Pentagon")'s [ARPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DARPA "DARPA") issued an office memorandum addressed to [Members and Affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergalactic_Computer_Network "Intergalactic Computer Network"). It rescheduled a meeting in [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California") regarding his vision of a computer network, which he imagined as an electronic commons open to all, the main and essential medium of informational interaction for governments, institutions, corporations, and individuals.[\[47\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-47)[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-48)[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Garreau2006-49)[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-britannica-50) As head of IPTO from 1962 to 1964, "Licklider initiated three of the most important developments in information technology: the creation of computer science departments at several major universities, time-sharing, and networking."[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-britannica-50) In 1969, the [Stanford Research Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SRI_International "SRI International") (now SRI International), operated one of the four original nodes that comprised [ARPANET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET "ARPANET"), predecessor to the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet").[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-51)

### Emergence of venture capital

Further information: [Venture capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venture_capital "Venture capital") and [Sand Hill Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_Hill_Road "Sand Hill Road")

By the early 1970s, there were many [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") companies in the area, [computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer "Computer") firms using their devices, and programming and service companies serving both. Industrial space was plentiful and housing was still inexpensive. Growth during this era was fueled by the emergence of [venture capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venture_capital "Venture capital") on [Sand Hill Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_Hill_Road "Sand Hill Road"), beginning with [Kleiner Perkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleiner_Perkins "Kleiner Perkins") and [Sequoia Capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequoia_Capital "Sequoia Capital") in 1972; the availability of venture capital exploded after the successful $1.3billion [IPO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_public_offering "Initial public offering") of [Apple Computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Computer "Apple Computer") in December 1980. Since the 1980s, Silicon Valley has been home to the largest concentration of venture capital firms in the world.[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-WRichardScott-52)

In 1971, [Don Hoefler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Hoefler "Don Hoefler") traced the origins of Silicon Valley firms, including via investments from [Fairchild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor "Fairchild Semiconductor")'s eight co-founders.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-DH-71-12)[\[53\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NetValley-53) The key investors in Kleiner Perkins and Sequoia Capital were from the same group, directly leading to _[Tech Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tech_Crunch "Tech Crunch")_ 2014 estimate of 92 public firms of 130 related listed firms then worth over US$2.1 trillion with over 2,000 firms traced back to them.[\[54\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Morris-2014-54)

### Banking

Another important pillar of the Valley's success was [Silicon Valley Bank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley_Bank "Silicon Valley Bank") (SVB), founded in 1983 by a group of former [Bank of America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_of_America "Bank of America") executives.[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-WRichardScott-52) Before its [2023 collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collapse_of_Silicon_Valley_Bank "Collapse of Silicon Valley Bank"), SVB specialized in providing banking services to Silicon Valley entrepreneurs and their startup firms. SVB's original primary commercial lending product was a [working capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Working_capital "Working capital") [line of credit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_of_credit "Line of credit"), secured by a startup's [accounts receivable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounts_receivable "Accounts receivable").[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Dean_Page_314-55) In contrast to traditional banks, who focused their commercial lending on already-established businesses, SVB specialized in lending money to small startup companies in the "preprofit" stage.[\[55\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Dean_Page_314-55)

### Lawyers and law firms

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/855_Main_Street.jpg/500px-855_Main_Street.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:855_Main_Street.jpg) As of 2025, this [Redwood City, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redwood_City,_California "Redwood City, California") office building was home to the Silicon Valley branch offices of large law firms [Hogan Lovells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hogan_Lovells "Hogan Lovells") and [Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freshfields_Bruckhaus_Deringer "Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer")

Prior to 1970, most Northern California [lawyers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawyer "Lawyer") were based in San Francisco, especially the experienced [patent attorneys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patent_attorney "Patent attorney") whom the high-tech industry needed to protect its intellectual property. During the 1970s, lawyers began to follow venture capitalists down the [Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Peninsula "San Francisco Peninsula") to serve the booming high-tech industry in Silicon Valley.[\[56\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-56) As of 1999, there were 2,400 lawyers practicing law in Palo Alto, a city of only 50,000 people, "the densest concentration of lawyers" in the United States outside of [Washington, D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.")[\[57\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Friedman_Page_470-57)

By the year 2000, large [law firms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_firm "Law firm") from all over the world were rushing to establish offices in the mid-Peninsula region on or near Sand Hill Road, and Silicon Valley law firms had become global trendsetters in that they were the first legal services employers to adopt [business casual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_casual "Business casual") apparel (in imitation of their startup clients).[\[58\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Baker-58) During this era, lawyers evolved from their relatively narrow conventional role as protectors of intellectual property into business advisers, intermediaries, and dealmakers, and thereby acquired great prominence in Silicon Valley.[\[58\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Baker-58)[\[59\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Suchman_Page_71-59) For young entrepreneurs new to the Valley's mysterious ways, their lawyer often served as their first coach, mentor, teacher, friend, and cheerleader who helped connect them to the Valley's startup ecosystem.[\[60\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Johnson_Page_325-60)

As of 2023, the San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara metropolitan area had the highest average wage for lawyers in the United States, at $268,570.[\[61\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-ABA_Wages-61) _[Above the Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Above_the_Law_(website) "Above the Law (website)")_ occasionally publishes lists of top-performing Silicon Valley law firms, which it calls the "white sandal elite", a parody of the " [white-shoe firm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White-shoe_firm "White-shoe firm")" descriptor traditionally applied to certain [East Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States "East Coast of the United States") professional services firms.[\[62\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-62)[\[63\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-63)

### Rise of computer culture

Main articles: [Microcomputer revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcomputer_revolution "Microcomputer revolution") and [Homebrew Computer Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Invitation_to_First_Homebrew_Computer_Club_meeting.jpg/500px-Invitation_to_First_Homebrew_Computer_Club_meeting.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Invitation_to_First_Homebrew_Computer_Club_meeting.jpg) The [Homebrew Computer Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club") was a highly influential computer hobbyist group in the 1970s and 80s that produced many influential tech founders, like [Steve Jobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs") and [Steve Wozniak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Wozniak "Steve Wozniak"). Pictured is the invitation to its first meeting in 1975.

The [Homebrew Computer Club](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club") was an informal group of electronic enthusiasts and technically minded hobbyists who gathered to trade parts, [circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_circuit "Electronic circuit"), and information pertaining to [DIY](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DIY "DIY") construction of computing devices.[\[64\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-64) It was started by [Gordon French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_French "Gordon French") and [Fred Moore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fred_Moore_(activist) "Fred Moore (activist)") who met at the Community Computer Center in [Menlo Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California "Menlo Park, California"). They both were interested in maintaining a regular, open forum for people to get together to work on making computers more accessible to everyone.[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Dormouse-65)

The first meeting was held as of March 1975 at French's garage in [Menlo Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menlo_Park,_California "Menlo Park, California"), [San Mateo County, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Mateo_County,_California "San Mateo County, California"); which was on occasion of the arrival of the [MITS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro_Instrumentation_and_Telemetry_Systems "Micro Instrumentation and Telemetry Systems") [Altair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altair_8800 "Altair 8800") microcomputer, the first unit sent to the area for review by [People's Computer Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Computer_Company "People's Computer Company"). [Steve Wozniak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Wozniak "Steve Wozniak") and [Steve Jobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs") credit that first meeting with inspiring them to design the original [Apple I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_I "Apple I") and (successor) [Apple II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_II "Apple II") computers. As a result, the first preview of the [Apple I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_I "Apple I") was given at the Homebrew Computer Club.[\[66\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-66) Subsequent meetings were held at an auditorium at the [Stanford Linear Accelerator Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SLAC_National_Accelerator_Laboratory "SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory").[\[67\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-67)

### Advent of software

Although semiconductors are still a major component of the area's economy, Silicon Valley has been most famous in recent years for innovations in software and [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet") services. Silicon Valley has significantly influenced computer operating systems, [software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software "Software"), and user interfaces. Using money from [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA"), the [US Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Air_Force "United States Air Force"), and [ARPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DARPA "DARPA"), [Douglas Engelbart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Engelbart "Douglas Engelbart") invented the [mouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_mouse "Computer mouse") and hypertext-based collaboration tools in the mid-1960s and 1970s while at [Stanford Research Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_Research_Institute "Stanford Research Institute") (now SRI International), first publicly demonstrated in 1968 in what is now known as [The Mother of All Demos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mother_of_All_Demos "The Mother of All Demos").

Engelbart's [Augmentation Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augmentation_Research_Center "Augmentation Research Center") at SRI was also involved in launching the [ARPANET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET "ARPANET") (precursor to the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet")) and starting the [Network Information Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InterNIC "InterNIC") (now [InterNIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InterNIC "InterNIC")). [Xerox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xerox "Xerox") hired some of Engelbart's best researchers beginning in the early 1970s. In turn, in the 1970s and 1980s, Xerox's [Palo Alto Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PARC_(company) "PARC (company)") (PARC) played a pivotal role in [object-oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Object-oriented_programming "Object-oriented programming") programming, [graphical user interfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphical_user_interface "Graphical user interface") (GUIs), [Ethernet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethernet "Ethernet"), [PostScript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PostScript "PostScript"), and [laser printers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_printer "Laser printer").

While Xerox marketed equipment using its technologies, for the most part its technologies flourished elsewhere. The diaspora of Xerox inventions led directly to [3Com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3Com "3Com") and [Adobe Systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adobe_Inc. "Adobe Inc."), and indirectly to [Cisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisco "Cisco"), [Apple Computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc."), and [Microsoft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft "Microsoft"). Apple's [Macintosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mac_(computer) "Mac (computer)") GUI was largely a result of [Steve Jobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs")' visit to PARC and the subsequent hiring of key personnel.[\[68\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-68) Cisco's impetus stemmed from the need to route a variety of protocols over [Stanford University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanford_University "Stanford University")'s [Ethernet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethernet "Ethernet") [campus network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campus_network "Campus network").[\[69\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-69)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Steve_Jobs_presents_iPhone_%28cropped%29.jpg/500px-Steve_Jobs_presents_iPhone_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Steve_Jobs_presents_iPhone_(cropped).jpg) [Apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.") founder [Steve Jobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs") introducing the [iPhone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPhone_(1st_generation) "IPhone (1st generation)") in 2007

### Internet age

Main article: [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet")

See also: [dot-com bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot-com_bubble "Dot-com bubble")

Commercial use of the Internet became practical and grew slowly throughout the early 1990s. In 1995, commercial use of the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet") grew substantially and the initial wave of internet startups, [Amazon.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazon.com "Amazon.com"), [eBay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EBay "EBay"), and the predecessor to [Craigslist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craigslist "Craigslist") began operations.[\[70\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-N1995-70) Silicon Valley is generally considered to have been the center of the [dot-com bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot-com_bubble "Dot-com bubble"), which started in the mid-1990s and collapsed after the [NASDAQ stock market](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASDAQ_stock_market "NASDAQ stock market") began to decline dramatically in April 2000. During the bubble era, real estate prices reached unprecedented levels. For a brief time, [Sand Hill Road](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand_Hill_Road "Sand Hill Road") was home to the most expensive commercial real estate in the world, and the booming economy resulted in severe [traffic congestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traffic_congestion "Traffic congestion").

The [PayPal Mafia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PayPal_Mafia "PayPal Mafia") is sometimes credited with inspiring the re-emergence of consumer-focused Internet companies after the [dot-com bust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot-com_bust "Dot-com bust") of 2001.[\[71\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-71) After the dot-com crash, Silicon Valley continues to maintain its status as one of the top research and development centers in the world. A 2006 _[The Wall Street Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal "The Wall Street Journal")_ story found that 12 of the 20 most inventive towns in America were in California, and 10 of those were in Silicon Valley.[\[72\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-72) San Jose led the list with 3,867 utility patents filed in 2005, and number two was Sunnyvale, at 1,881 utility patents.[\[73\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-73) Silicon Valley is also home to a significant number of " [Unicorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicorn_(finance) "Unicorn (finance)")" ventures, referring to [startup companies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Startup_company "Startup company") whose [valuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valuation_(finance) "Valuation (finance)") has exceeded $1billion [dollars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_dollar "United States dollar").[\[74\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-74)

## Economy

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Map_of_High_Tech_Employment_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area_in_2022.svg/500px-Map_of_High_Tech_Employment_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area_in_2022.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Map_of_High_Tech_Employment_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area_in_2022.svg) Map of High-Tech Employment in the Bay Area

A world-renowned technology hub, the [San Francisco Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area "San Francisco Bay Area") has the largest concentration of [high-tech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-tech "High-tech") companies in the United States.[\[75\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-75)[\[76\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-76)[\[77\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-77)\[ _[bettersourceneeded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Questionable_sources "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_\] Largely a result of the high technology sector, the San Jose-Sunnyvale-Santa Clara Metropolitan Statistical Area has the most millionaires and the most billionaires in the United States per capita, although the venture capital ecosystem has grown more geographically decentralized over time.[\[78\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-78)

Silicon Valley was the biggest high-tech manufacturing center in the United States as of 2008.[\[79\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-79)[\[80\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-80) The [unemployment rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unemployment_rate "Unemployment rate") of the region was 9.4% in January 2009 and has decreased to a record low of 2.7% as of August 2019.[\[81\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-81) Silicon Valley received 41% of all U.S. venture investment in 2011, and 46% in 2012.[\[82\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-82) During the period from 2019 to 2021, the Bay Area's share of U.S. venture capital investment dropped to 35.9%, but had surged back to 41% as of the first quarter of 2023.[\[83\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Florida-83)

More traditional industries also recognize the potential of high-tech development, and several car manufacturers have opened offices in Silicon Valley to capitalize on its [entrepreneurial ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entrepreneurial_ecosystem "Entrepreneurial ecosystem").[\[84\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-84) Manufacture of [transistors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transistor "Transistor") was for a long time the core industry in Silicon Valley. The workforce was for the most part composed of Asian and Latino immigrants who were paid low wages and worked in hazardous conditions due to the chemicals used in the manufacture of [integrated circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit"). Technical, engineering, design, and administrative staffs were in large part well compensated.[\[85\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-SVWCD154-56-85)[\[86\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-BLSSemiconductor-86)

### Housing

Silicon Valley has a [severe housing shortage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_housing_shortage "California housing shortage"), caused by the market imbalance between jobs created and housing units built: from 2010 to 2015, many more jobs have been created than housing units built. (400,000 jobs, 60,000 housing units)[\[87\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-WSJ6716-87) This shortage has driven [home prices extremely high, far out of the range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affordable_housing_in_Silicon_Valley "Affordable housing in Silicon Valley") of production workers.[\[88\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-SVWCD233-88) As of 2016 a two-bedroom apartment rented for about $2,500 while the median home price was about $1million.[\[87\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-WSJ6716-87) The _[Financial Post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_Post "Financial Post")_ called Silicon Valley the most expensive U.S. housing region.[\[89\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-89) [Homelessness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homelessness "Homelessness") is a problem with housing beyond the reach of middle-income residents; there is little [shelter space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeless_shelter "Homeless shelter") other than in San Jose which, as of 2015, was making an effort to develop shelters by renovating old hotels.[\[90\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NR121415-90)

_[The Economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist "The Economist")_ also attributes the high cost of living to the success of the industries in this region. Although, this rift between high and low salaries is driving many residents out who can no longer afford to live there. In the [Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area "San Francisco Bay Area"), the number of residents planning to leave within the next several years has had an increase of 35% since 2016, from 34% to 46%.[\[91\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-91)[\[92\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-92)

### Wealth

The wealth inequality in Silicon Valley is more pronounced than in any other region of the United States. A 2023 report found that the aggregate household wealth of Silicon Valley (including [ultra-high net worth individuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-net-worth_individual "High-net-worth individual")) was nearly $1.1 trillion, and less than 1% of the Valley's population held 36% of the wealth.[\[93\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Lazo-93) Conversely, as of 2021, 23% of residents were [living below the poverty line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poverty_in_the_United_States "Poverty in the United States").[\[93\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Lazo-93) However, the meaning of the term "poverty" is dependent upon context; in Silicon Valley, it means something different because of the region's severe housing shortage and high housing prices.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] As of 2023, the low-income [poverty threshold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poverty_threshold "Poverty threshold") set by the [California Department of Housing and Community Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Department_of_Housing_and_Community_Development "California Department of Housing and Community Development") for single-person households in the counties of San Francisco, San Mateo, and Marin was $104,400, followed by $96,000 for the county of Santa Clara.[\[94\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Echeverria-94)[\[95\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Kirkeby-95) In contrast, the 2023 national low-income poverty threshold set by the [U.S. Census Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau "U.S. Census Bureau") for a single-person household was $14,891.[\[96\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Olya-96)

A 1999 study by [AnnaLee Saxenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AnnaLee_Saxenian "AnnaLee Saxenian") for the [Public Policy Institute of California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_Policy_Institute_of_California "Public Policy Institute of California") reported that a third of Silicon Valley scientists and engineers were immigrants and that nearly a quarter of Silicon Valley's high-technology firms since 1980 were run by [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_American "Chinese American") (17 percent) or [Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_American "Indian American") descent CEOs (7 percent).[\[97\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-97) In addition, Saxenian's study found that successful immigrant entrepreneurs relied heavily on ethnic resources as they integrated into the Silicon Valley work culture and American society.[\[98\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-98) There is a stratum of well-compensated technical employees and managers, including tens of thousands of "single-digit millionaires". This income and range of assets will support a middle-class lifestyle in Silicon Valley.[\[99\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NYT8507-99)

### Gender

See also: [Sexism in the technology industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexism_in_the_technology_industry "Sexism in the technology industry")

In November 2006, the [University of California, Davis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis "University of California, Davis") released a report analyzing business leadership by women within the state.[\[100\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-UCD-PR2006-100) The report showed that although 103 of the 400 largest public companies headquartered in California were located in Santa Clara County (the most of all counties), only 8.8% of Silicon Valley companies had women CEOs.[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Ellis2006-101):4,7 This was the lowest percentage in the state.[\[102\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Zee061116-102) (San Francisco County had 19.2% and Marin County had 18.5%.)[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Ellis2006-101)

Silicon Valley tech leadership positions are occupied almost exclusively by men.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-WP11911-103) This is also represented in the number of new companies founded by women as well as the number of women-lead startups that receive venture capital funding. Wadhwa said he believes that a contributing factor is a lack of parental encouragement to study science and engineering.[\[104\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-2014MomDad-104) He also cited a lack of women role models and noted that most famous tech leaders—like [Bill Gates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Gates "Bill Gates"), [Steve Jobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs"), and [Mark Zuckerberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Zuckerberg "Mark Zuckerberg")—are men.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-WP11911-103)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Silicon_Valley_Pride_Parade_2016_%28cropped%29.jpg/500px-Silicon_Valley_Pride_Parade_2016_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Silicon_Valley_Pride_Parade_2016_(cropped).jpg) [Silicon Valley Pride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley_Pride "Silicon Valley Pride") in San Jose

As of October 2014, some high-profile Silicon Valley firms were working actively to prepare and recruit women. _[Bloomberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloomberg_News "Bloomberg News")_ reported that Apple, Facebook, Google, and Microsoft attended the 20th annual [Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grace_Hopper_Celebration_of_Women_in_Computing "Grace Hopper Celebration of Women in Computing") conference to actively recruit and potentially hire female engineers and technology experts.[\[105\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Burrows141008-105) The same month, the second annual Platform Summit was held to discuss increasing racial and gender diversity in tech.[\[106\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Porter141029-106) As of April 2015 experienced women were engaged in creation of venture capital firms which leveraged women's perspectives in funding of startups.[\[107\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NYT4115-107)

After UC Davis published its _Study of California Women Business Leaders_ in November 2006,[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Ellis2006-101) some _[San Jose Mercury News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose_Mercury_News "San Jose Mercury News")_ readers dismissed the possibility that sexism contributed in making Silicon Valley's leadership gender gap the highest in the state.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] A January 2015 issue of _[Newsweek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newsweek "Newsweek")_ magazine featured an article detailing reports of sexism and misogyny in Silicon Valley.[\[108\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Burleigh150128-108) The article's author, [Nina Burleigh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nina_Burleigh "Nina Burleigh"), asked, "Where were all these offended people when women like [Heidi Roizen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heidi_Roizen "Heidi Roizen") published accounts of having a venture capitalist stick her hand in his pants under a table while a deal was being discussed?"[\[109\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Tam150130-109) As of 2014, the board of directors of the top 15 companies in Santa Clara, San Mateo, San Francisco, Alameda, and Contra Costa Counties were composed of 15.7% women, and the top 150 were composed of just 10% women, compared with 20.9% in the S&P 100.[\[110\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-110)[\[111\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-111)

The 2012 lawsuit _[Pao v. Kleiner Perkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pao_v._Kleiner_Perkins "Pao v. Kleiner Perkins")_ was filed in [San Francisco County Superior Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco_County_Superior_Court "San Francisco County Superior Court") by executive [Ellen Pao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellen_Pao "Ellen Pao") for [gender discrimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender_discrimination "Gender discrimination") against her employer, [Kleiner Perkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleiner_Perkins "Kleiner Perkins").[\[112\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Complaint-112) The case went to trial in February 2015. On March 27, 2015, the jury found in favor of Kleiner Perkins on all counts.[\[113\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Recodeverdict-113) Nevertheless, the case, which had wide press coverage, resulted in major advances in consciousness of gender discrimination on the part of venture capital and technology firms and their women employees.[\[114\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-RecodeDecker-114)[\[115\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-NYTDisrupter-115) Two other cases have been filed against Facebook and Twitter.[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Streitfeld150327-116)

### Race

A 2017 study showed that [white](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Americans "White Americans") males made up the majority of higher positions in Silicon Valley tech firms, with 58.7% of executive positions and 46.5% of managers. The second highest position holders were [Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asian_Americans "Asian Americans") men, with 16.3% of executive positions and 17.9% of managers. [African/Black](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Americans "African Americans") and [Hispanic/Latino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans "Hispanic and Latino Americans") people had the lowest percentages in all categories.[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-117)

_[Harvard Business Review](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harvard_Business_Review "Harvard Business Review")_ published an article in 2018 discussing diversity and inclusion and gave statistics on black employees along with advice to future black technicians. LeRon L. Barton, a black man who spent over two decades in Tech, gave an insight on his work experiences. He said he saw no one who looked like him in his profession and said he received many comments that he believed disregarded his skill such as being called the diversity hire. He described being isolated from his team, and constantly having to prove he could do the job he was hired for.[\[118\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-118)

Some of the most successful Black Silicon Valley tech figures include Roy Clay, the founder of Hewlett Packard's computing division who is considered the "Godfather of Silicon Valley";[\[119\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-119) Ken Coleman, the first Black recruiter at Hewlett and later an executive at Activision and Silicon Graphics;[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-120) John W. Thompson, the first Black CEO of a major tech company, the enterprise security software provider Symantec, and the chairman of computing giant Microsoft replacing the founder Bill Gates;\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] Robert F. Smith, former Goldman Sachs technology M&A banker and founder of software private equity firm Vista Equity Partners;\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] Charles Phillips, President of Oracle and CEO of Infor who coined the term "enterprise software" during his time as a technology banker at Morgan Stanley with Frank Quattrone and Mary Meeker;[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-121)[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-122) and Stacy Brown-Philpot, the first Black female tech CEO at online marketplace TaskRabbit. [\[123\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-123)

Late 2024 and early 2025 marked a shift in the discourse around DEI or diversity equity and inclusion initiatives in tech companies. As many tech companies faced economic headwinds and pressure to reduce costs in the midst of falling valuations and rising interest rates, leading to widespread layoffs and budget scrutiny by investors, DEI programs were often the first thing out the door. [\[124\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-124)

### Statistics

In 2014, tech companies [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google"), [Yahoo!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo! "Yahoo!"), [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook"), [Apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc."), and others, released corporate transparency reports that offered detailed employee breakdowns. In May, [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google") said 17% of its tech employees worldwide were women, and, in the U.S., 1% of its tech workers were [black](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_Americans "African Americans") and 2% were [Hispanic/Latino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans "Hispanic and Latino Americans").[\[125\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Musil140528-125) June 2014 brought reports from Yahoo! and Facebook. [Yahoo!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo! "Yahoo!") said that 15% of its tech jobs were held by women, 2% of its tech employees were black and 4% Hispanic.[\[126\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Levy140617-126) [Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") reported that 15% of its tech workforce was female, and 3% was Hispanic and 1% was black.[\[127\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Williams140625-127)

In August 2014, [Apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.") reported that 80% of its global tech staff was male and that, in the U.S., 54% of its tech jobs were staffed by Caucasians and 23% by Asians.[\[128\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-128) Soon after, _[USA Today](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USA_Today "USA Today")_ published an article about Silicon Valley's lack of tech-industry diversity, pointing out that it is largely white or Asian, and male. "Blacks and Hispanics are largely absent," it reported, "and women are underrepresented in Silicon Valley—from giant companies to start-ups to venture capital firms."[\[129\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Guynn-Weise-129) Civil rights activist [Jesse Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesse_Jackson "Jesse Jackson") said of improving diversity in the tech industry, "This is the next step in the civil rights movement"[\[130\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Koch140815-130) while [T. J. Rodgers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T._J._Rodgers "T. J. Rodgers") has argued against Jackson's assertions.

The geographical boundaries of Silicon Valley have changed over the years. Historically, the term Silicon Valley was treated as synonymous with Santa Clara Valley[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Malone_Page_xix-1)[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Matthews_Page_2-2)[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-Shueh_Page_8-3) and adjacent regions in southern San Mateo County and southern Alameda County.[\[134\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-:1-134) However, the metonym "Silicon Valley" has also been used to refer to technology companies based in other Northern California locales, such as San Francisco, Contra Costa County, Santa Cruz County, and the northern parts of Alameda County and San Mateo County.[\[134\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-:1-134)[\[135\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-135)

### Secondary education

Funding for public schools in upscale Silicon Valley communities such as [Woodside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woodside,_California "Woodside, California") is often supplemented by grants from private foundations set up for that purpose and funded by local residents. Schools in less affluent areas such as [East Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Palo_Alto,_California "East Palo Alto, California") must depend on state funding.[\[138\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-TNY52713-138)

## Media

Main article: [Media in the San Francisco Bay Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Media_in_the_San_Francisco_Bay_Area "Media in the San Francisco Bay Area")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/McKinley_memorial%2C_St._James_Park%2C_San_Jose%2C_California_%28cropped%29.jpg/500px-McKinley_memorial%2C_St._James_Park%2C_San_Jose%2C_California_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:McKinley_memorial,_St._James_Park,_San_Jose,_California_(cropped).jpg) Headquarters of _[The Mercury News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mercury_News "The Mercury News")_, Silicon Valley's largest newspaper, in Downtown San Jose[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Charles_Herrold_laboratory_circa_1912.jpg/500px-Charles_Herrold_laboratory_circa_1912.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Charles_Herrold_laboratory_circa_1912.jpg) 50 W. San Fernando Street in downtown San Jose is the site of the world's first [radio broadcasting station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_broadcasting_station "Radio broadcasting station"), created in 1909 by [Charles Herrold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Herrold "Charles Herrold"), the "Father of Broadcasting".[\[139\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-139)

In 1980, _Intelligent Machines Journal_ changed its name to _[InfoWorld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InfoWorld "InfoWorld")_, and, with offices in [Palo Alto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California"), began covering the emergence of the microcomputer industry in the valley.[\[140\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Valley#cite_note-FireForward-140)

Local and national media cover Silicon Valley and its companies. CNN, _[The Wall Street Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal "The Wall Street Journal")_, and Bloomberg News operate Silicon Valley bureaus out of Palo Alto. Public broadcaster [KQED (TV)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KQED_(TV) "KQED (TV)") and [KQED-FM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KQED-FM "KQED-FM"), as well as the Bay Area's local [ABC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company "American Broadcasting Company") station [KGO-TV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KGO-TV "KGO-TV"), operate bureaus in San Jose. [KNTV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KNTV "KNTV"), [NBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBC "NBC")'s local Bay Area affiliate "NBC Bay Area", is located in San Jose. Produced from this location is the nationally distributed TV Show "Tech Now" as well as the CNBC Silicon Valley bureau. San Jose-based media serving Silicon Valley include the _[San Jose Mercury News](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Jose_Mercury_News "San Jose Mercury News")_ daily and the _[Metro Silicon Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metro_Silicon_Valley "Metro Silicon Valley")_ weekly.

Specialty media include _[El Observador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Observador_(San_Jose) "El Observador (San Jose)")_ and the _San Jose / Silicon Valley Business Journal_. Most of the Bay Area's other major TV stations, newspapers, and media operate in [San Francisco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Francisco "San Francisco") or [Oakland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oakland,_California "Oakland, California"). [Patch.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patch.com "Patch.com") operates various web portals, providing local news, discussion and events for residents of Silicon Valley. Mountain View has a public nonprofit station, KMVT-15. KMVT-15's shows include Silicon Valley Education News (EdNews)-Edward Tico Producer.

_Read Part I [here](https://steveblank.substack.com/p/if-i-told-you-id-have-to-kill-you), Part II [here](https://steveblank.substack.com/p/the-story-behind-the-secret-history), Part III [here](http://steveblank.com/category/secret-history-of-silicon-valley/), Part IVa [here](https://steveblank.substack.com/p/story-behind-the-secret-history-part-c60) and Part IVb [here](https://steveblank.substack.com/p/the-secret-history-of-silicon-valley)._

_When the legend becomes fact, print the legend._

_– The Man Who Shot Liberty Valance_

I always had been curious about how Silicon Valley, a place I had lived and worked in, came to be.  And throughout my career as an entrepreneur I kept asking questions of my VC investors and friends; Where did the valleys entrepreneurship culture come from?  How did Silicon Valley start? Why here?  Why now? How did this culture of “make it happen” emerge, etc.  And the answer came back much as it did in my past jobs; Who cares, get back to work.

After I retired, and before I started teaching at Stanford, [Jerry Engel](http://www.haas.berkeley.edu/faculty/engel.html), director of the [Lester Center on Entrepreneurship](http://entrepreneurship.berkeley.edu/main/index.html), at U.C. Berkeley [Haas Business School](http://www.haas.berkeley.edu/) was courageous enough to give me a forum teach the [Customer Development](http://www.slideshare.net/venturehacks/customer-development-methodology-presentation) Methodology. As I was researching my [class text](http://www.amazon.com/Four-Steps-Epiphany-Steven-Blank/dp/0976470705), I thought it would be simple enough to read up on a few histories of the valley and finally get my questions about the genesis of entrepreneurship answered.

[Share](https://steveblank.substack.com/p/the-secret-history-of-silicon-valley-155?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email&utm_content=share&action=share)

**The Legend: HP, Intel and Apple** I read all the popular books about the valley and they all told a variant of the same story; “entrepreneurs as heroes” building the Semiconductor and Personal Computer companies: [Bill Hewlett and David Packard](http://www.amazon.com/Bill-Dave-Hewlett-Packard-Greatest/dp/1591841526) at HP, Bob Taylor and the team at [Xerox PARC](http://www.amazon.com/Dealers-Lightning-Xerox-PARC-Computer/dp/0887309895), Steve Jobs and Wozniak at [Apple](http://www.amazon.com/Fire-Valley-Making-Personal-Computer/dp/0071358927), Gordon Moore and [Bob Noyce](http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/tg/detail/-/0195163435) at Intel, etc.  These were inspiring stories, but I realized that, no surprise, the popular press were writing books that had mass appeal. They were all fun reads about plucky entrepreneurs who start from nothing and against all odds, build a successful company.

[![popular-view-of-silicon-valley-history1](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rCpv!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe9b24c1c-ed45-442c-b55c-546d3bb8e7dd_468x307.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rCpv!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe9b24c1c-ed45-442c-b55c-546d3bb8e7dd_468x307.jpeg)

But no one was writing about where the entrepreneurial _culture_ had come from.  Where were the books explaining why were all these chip and computer companies started here?  Why not elsewhere in the country or the world?  With the exception of [one great book](http://www.amazon.com/Regional-Advantage-Culture-Competition-Silicon/dp/0674753402), no one was writing about our regional advantage. Was it because entrepreneurs keep moving forward and rarely look back? I needed to dig deeper.

**The Facts: Vacuum Tube Valley – Our 100th Anniversary**

To my surprise, I discovered that yes, Silicon Valley did start in a garage in Palo Alto, but _it didn’t start in the Hewlett Packard garage_. The first electronics company in Silicon Valley was [Federal Telegraph](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uPzXYCqjtn8), a vaccum tube company [started in 1909](http://books.google.com/books?hl=en&lr=&id=CLxzUW4V_2cC&oi=fnd&pg=PA19) in Palo Alto as [Poulsen Wireless](https://earlyradiohistory.us/1910poul.htm).  (The 100th anniversary of Silicon Valley in 2009 went unnoticed and unmentioned by anyone.)  By 1912, [Lee Deforest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lee_de_Forest) working at Federal Telegraph would invent the [Triode](https://davidsarnoff.tcnj.edu/2018/03/18/item-of-the-week-audion/), (a tube amplifier) and would go on to become the Steve Jobs of his day – visionary, charismatic and controversial.

_\\* Federal Telegraph and Lee Deforest in Palo Alto are the first major events in what would become Silicon Valley. We need to reset our Silicon Valley birthday calendars to here._

By 1937, when Bill Hewlett and David Packard left Stanford to start HP, the agricultural fields outside of Stanford had already become “Vacuum Tube Valley.” HP was a supplier of electronic test equipment and joined a small but  [thriving valley electronics industry](http://www.amazon.com/Making-Silicon-Valley-Innovation-Technology/dp/0262122812) with companies like Litton and [Eitel and McCollough](http://www.ieeeghn.org/wiki/index.php/Eimac).

\* _By the late 1930’s when HP started, a small group (measured in hundreds) of engineers who made radio tubes were building the valleys’ ecosystem for electronics manufacturing, product engineering and technology management._

Who would have known?

**Microwave Valley – the 1950’s and ’60’s**

There isn’t much written about Silicon Valley during and after World War II.  The story of the valley post war, through the 1950’s, is mostly about the growth of the tube companies and [the rise of Hewlett Packard](https://www.hpmemoryproject.org/wa_pages/wall_a_page_08.htm) and the birth of [Fairchild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor).  The popular literature has the valley springing to life in the 1960’s with the semiconductor revolution started by [Shockley](http://www.amazon.com/Broken-Genius-William-Shockley-Electronic/dp/1403988153), Fairchild, Signetics, National and Intel, followed by the emergence of the personal computer in the mid 1970’s.

But the more I read, the more I realized that the public history’s of the valley in the 1950’s and ’60’s _were incomplete and just plain wrong._ The truth was that huge dollars were spent on a large number of companies that never made the press or into the history books. Companies specializing in components and systems that operated in the [microwave](http://www.wa1mba.org/micros.htm) portion of the electromagnetic spectrum sprouted faster than fruit trees in the valley orchards. In ten years, from the early 1950’s to the early 1960’s, the valley went through a hiring frenzy as jobs in microwave companies went from [700 to 7,000](https://faculty.fiu.edu/~revellk/pad2011/heinrich.pdf).

This wave of 1950’s/’60’s startups ( [Watkins-Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watkins-Johnson_Company), Varian, Huggins Labs, MEC, Stewart Engineering, etc.) were making  dizzying array of new [microwave components](https://ethw.org/Microwave_Circuits); [power grid tubes](http://www.thevalvepage.com/valvetek/McCullo/McCullo.htm), [klystrons](http://www.slac.stanford.edu/pubs/slacpubs/7500/slac-pub-7731.pdf), [magnetrons](http://www.radartutorial.eu/08.transmitters/tx08.en.html),  [backward wave oscillators,](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backward_wave_oscillator) [traveling wave tubes (TWT’s)](https://www.radartutorial.eu/08.transmitters/Traveling%20Wave%20Tube.en.html), cross-field amplifiers, [gyrotrons](http://tmo.jpl.nasa.gov/progress_report2/42-52/52C.PDF), and on, on…  And literally across the valley, these microwave devices were being built into complete systems for the U.S. military by other new startups;  Sylvania Electronics Defense Laboratory, Granger Associates, Philco, Dalmo Victor, ESL (my first startup in the valley) and Argosystems. In the 1950’s and ’60’s more money was pouring into these companies than on the fledgling chip and computer companies.

_\\* The 10x expansion in the number of engineers in the valley in the 1950’s came from the military and microwaves – before the semiconductor boom. And these microwave engineers were working at startups – not large companies. You never heard of them because their customers were the department of defense, the intelligence community and most often their devices were embedded in classified systems._

When I read the funny names of these microwaves devices… Backward wave oscillators, TWT’s, Magnetrons…long silent memories came back. These components were the heart of the electronic warfare equipment I had worked on; including [Wild Weasels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wild_Weasel) and fighter planes in [Thailand](http://steveblank.com/2009/03/23/if-i-told-you-i%E2%80%99d-have-to-kill-you-the-story-behind-the-secret-history-of-silicon-valley/) and on [B-52 bombers](http://steveblank.com/2009/03/29/the-story-behind-the-secret-history-part-ii-getting-b-52s-through-the-soviet-air-defense-system/).  After decades, the story started coming home for me.

**The Revolution Wasn’t Televised** What the heck happened here to create this burst of innovation?  What created this microwave startup culture in the 1950’s? And since there was no Venture Capital in the 1950’s/’60’s where was the money coming from? This startup boom seemed to come out of nowhere.  Why was it occurring here? And why on earth the sudden military interest in microwaves?

[![the-real-story-of-silicon-valley1](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qdww!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3e7a0e32-028f-47fd-988e-e48714c54a4d_468x307.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qdww!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3e7a0e32-028f-47fd-988e-e48714c54a4d_468x307.jpeg)

Part of the answer was that these companies and the military had forged some type of relationship.  And it appeared that Stanford University’s engineering department was in middle of all this. The formation of the [military/industrial/university relationships during the Cold War](http://www.amazon.com/Creating-Cold-War-University-Transformation/dp/0520205413) and the relationship [between Stanford](http://www.amazon.com/Cold-War-American-Science-Military-Industrial-Academic/dp/0231079591) and the intelligence community in particular, went on untold and out of sight.

While nothing I read described the specific products being worked on, or what specifically was Stanford’s contribution, there were some really tantalizing pointers to who the real customers were (hint, it wasn’t just the “military,”) or why was this work was being done at Stanford.

Few knew that the answers to all these questions pointed to just one guy at the center of it all –  _Fred Terman of Stanford_ University.

\* _Stanford, the military and our intelligence agencies started the wave of entrepreneurial culture that today’s Silicon Valley takes for granted._

## From the Field: A Short History of Silicon Valley

## Francine Hardaway

What made Silicon Valley a Hub for entrepreneurs? And why has that become as much of a liability as an advantage?

Silicon Valley grew up in the area between San Jose, California, and San Francisco as a result of Frederick Terman, the legendary dean of Stanford engineering school during the 1940s and 1950s. He created the tradition of Stanford faculty starting their own companies. A number of companies we still reference came out of Stanford during those years, especially Hewlett Packard and Varian Associates.

The transistor was invented and manufactured in Silicon Valley, which gave the area a leg up in the radio and telegraph industries. And the Navy had a base in Sunnyvale. By 1957, Russia’s success with Sputnik unleashed a big space competition, and the U.S. government founded NASA. At the time NASA opened at Moffett Field near San Jose, the only company able to build electronics for the space capsule was Fairchild Semiconductor.

The legend of Silicon Valley from the beginning of the transistor industry is well-known and has been written about and [documented.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pirates_of_Silicon_Valley) [\[1\]](https://press.rebus.community/media-innovation-and-entrepreneurship/chapter/from-the-field-a-short-history-of-silicon-valley/#footnote-600-1 "\"Pirates of Silicon Valley,\" Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pirates_of_Silicon_Valley.")  Most of the Valley’s early success came from either components like Silicon chips (from which the Valley got its name) or the hardware into which the chips were placed.

It’s actually fun to learn about the Valley’s history, because the lesson is that the beginning of Silicon Valley was all about men and women, engineers, who had ideas out of science fiction about what technology could enable.  Many of those ideas have now come true, like the personal computer and the iPhone, both envisioned by Steve Jobs. And we now wear what was once envisioned in a comic book as the “Dick Tracy wrist radio”–the Apple Watch.

Early engineers were not after money, they were about making things possible that had never been possible before, such as space travel. As a result, they received both government grants and venture capital. Before venture capital became a “thing,” it originated from the financial centers like New York. Arthur Rock, for example, was an early investor in Intel, Apple, and Scientific Data Systems. The first Bay Area venture capital firms, who located on Sand Hill Road adjacent to the Stanford campus, were Kleiner, Perkins Caulfield and Byers, and Sequoia Capital.

If you are interested in the history of Silicon Valley, you might want to watch the [HBO show Silicon Valley](http://www.imdb.com/title/tt2575988/), which tells you everything you need to know about where Silicon Valley is in the present, and also the fourth season of [AMC’s Halt and Catch Fire](https://www.theverge.com/2017/6/28/15885634/halt-and-catch-fire-season-4-premiere-when-amc), which can take you from the 1980s to the rise of the World Wide Web.

We have now reached the point where Silicon Valley is a rich ecosystem where everyone with an idea eventually shows up because it’s possible to sit in a coffee shop and form a team, raise some money, and get started. Numerous accelerators, such as Y Combinator make it easy to get a start, and the cost of starting a company has come down significantly due to Amazon Web Services (cloud-based infrastructure) and software development tools and frameworks.

However, we’re also reached the point where Silicon Valley is a self-parody, with all the downsides of its own success: [race, age, and gender discrimination,](http://mashable.com/2017/06/08/silicon-valley-harassment/#0mmwSdHUjEqP) [drug and alcohol abuse,](https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/health-science/tweaking-brains-with-smart-drugs-to-get-ahead-in-silicon-valley/2017/06/09/5bc9c064-0b35-11e7-93dc-00f9bdd74ed1_story.html?utm_term=.506ab42c00a4) [suicide and depression.](http://www.cnn.com/videos/cnnmoney/2017/03/08/mostly-human-silicon-valleys-secret.cnnmoney) [\[2\]](https://press.rebus.community/media-innovation-and-entrepreneurship/chapter/from-the-field-a-short-history-of-silicon-valley/#footnote-600-2 "Why Silicon Valley is Going to Keep Having Sexual Harassment Lawsuits, Mashable, http://mashable.com/2017/06/08/silicon-valley-harassment/#sRm_EoiXdEqf.") Its “win at all costs mentality” has spawned excesses of every variety.

Not to mention the fact that it is now possible to start a company anywhere, especially near a university. Let’s give Silicon Valley credit for establishing a culture of entrepreneurship, which is really a culture of resilience, and then understand that for digital media, it’s not Paradise.

_As the founder of Stealthmode Partners, **Francine Hardaway** is a nineteen-year advocate and resource for entrepreneurs and intrapreneurs. She has consulted with more than 1,000 startups and blogs about technology, entrepreneurship, and health-care policy issues at_ Huffington Post, Medium, _and [http://blog.stealthmode.com](http://stealthmode.com/) . Reach her on Twitter at [@hardaway](https://twitter.com/hardaway)._

Title: The Origins of Silicon Valley: Why and How It Happened
# The Origins of Silicon Valley: Why and How It Happened

## Abstract/Contents

AbstractSilicon Valley is known to be unique, with its wealth of technology companies and different style of management. Why did Silicon Valley come into being? According to Paul Wesling, the story goes back to local Hams (amateur radio operators) trying to break RCA's tube patents, early Stanford engineers, the sinking of the Titanic, Naval ship communications requirements, early “angel” investments, Fred Terman and Stanford University, local invention of high-power tubes (gammatron, klystron), WWII and radar, new approaches to running companies, and the San Francisco Bay Area infrastructure. These factors pretty much determined that the semiconductor and integrated circuit industries would be located in the Santa Clara Valley.As we explore this exciting history of device technology development and innovation from 1909 through 1960, you will "meet" some of the colorful characters--Cyril Elwell, Lee DeForest, Bill Eitel, Charles Litton, Fred Terman, David Packard, Bill Hewlett and others--who set leadership patterns for the worldwide electronics industries through their inventions, process development, and allied management techniques. You will also learn about some current local organizations and movements, including Maker Faire and MeetUps, that keep alive the spirit of the radio Hams and the Homebrew Computer Club, where geeks gather to start new companies that invent the future.

## Description

```
Why did Silicon Valley come into being? In this talk, Paul Wesling brings you back to early Stanford engineers, campus faculty kids around 1915, local Hams (amateur radio operators) trying to break RCA's tube patents, the sinking of the Titanic, angel investments, Fred Terman and Stanford University, local invention of high-power tubes (gammatron, klystron), WWII and radar, new approaches to running companies, and the San Francisco Bay Area infrastructure. These factors led to the semiconductor and integrated circuit industries being located here, and earned it the designation as “Silicon Valley”. In this exciting history of device technology development and innovation from 1909 through 1960, you will "meet" some of the colorful characters -- Cyril Elwell, Leonard Fuller, Lee DeForest, Bill Eitel, Charles Litton, Russell Varian, Fred Terman, David Packard, Bill Hewlett and others -- who set leadership patterns for the worldwide electronics industries through their collaboration, inventions, process development, and allied management techniques.  Here is where geeks now gather to start new companies that invent the future. This edited lecture was given in Cubberley Auditorium, Stanford University, on April 19, 2017.

Paul Wesling's presentation slides can be found via this link: https://drive.google.com/file/d/0B8jA-AzRHKeMMkdQX1VNTnNNWTQ/view
```
