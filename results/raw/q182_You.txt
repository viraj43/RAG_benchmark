Pi is the ratio of the circumference of a circle to its diameter. The value of pi is approximately 3.14, or 22/7. Pi is an irrational number, which means it is not equal to the ratio of any two whole numbers. This also means pi can be expressed as an infinite decimal expansion with no regularly repeating digits. Rhind papyrusThe Rhind papyrus is a document that has been central to understanding ancient Egyptian mathematics. It also represents one of the earliest documented references to puzzles, in the form of dozens of mathematical problems.(more) The oldest surviving evidence of pi comes from Babylonia (present-day Iraq). Old Babylonian cuneiform tablets (c. 1900–1600 bce) give a practical value for pi derived from hexagon geometry: They treated the ratio of an inscribed regular hexagon’s perimeter to the circle’s circumference as 24/25, giving the value of pi as 25/8, or about 3.125. In the 3rd century bce Greek mathematician and engineer Archimedes developed a geometric method to calculate pi. By comparing perimeters of polygons inscribed in and circumscribed around a circle (up to 96 sides), he proved 223/71 < π < 22/7, or an average value of about 3.1418. Archimedes also proved that the ratio of the area of a circle to the square of its radius is the same constant. Its decimal expansion goes on forever without repeating. The square root of 2 and the constant e are examples. Pi is irrational because no fraction can capture its exact value.
