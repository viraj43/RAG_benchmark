# What Should I Learn? 28 Useful Skills to Learn in 2023

What should you learn to stay relevant in 2023 and beyond?

It’s a tricky question to answer. Technologies change all the time, companies come and go, and predictions turn out to be wrong. Still, it’s worth thinking about — both for the professional benefits and the sheer joy of learning something new.

This guide, then, is our attempt to answer the question, “What should I learn?” Some of the skills will be more useful to certain jobs than others, but all of them will benefit you.

In addition to work-related skills, we’ve also included some skills that will make your life better no matter what you do for a living. Some of these you can learn in an afternoon, while others can take years to grasp (most fall somewhere in between).

Here’s a look at some skills we’re fairly sure will be relevant for years to come (and don’t worry, it’s more than programming and computer skills).

## Digital Literacy

A lot of guides to what you should learn will say, “learn to program.” But, quite honestly, [not everyone should code](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EFwa5Owp0-k).

While coding is an extremely useful skill, the world also needs people who can manage companies, come up with business ideas, and sell/market the products that programmers help produce.

However, I do think that everyone can benefit from a certain degree of digital literacy. This means knowing how to use your computer to its fullest potential.

Here are a few computer skills that I consider essential for the modern knowledge worker:

If these seem like elementary skills to you, great! But if you’re not sure about some (or are a bit rusty), then use some of the links above to learn more.

Basic digital literacy will make you a more effective employee and free up time to focus on work tasks (instead of constantly struggling to use your tools).

## Written Communication

Even if you don’t consider yourself a writer, you still have to write.

Whether it’s an email to your boss, a cover letter for a new job, or even a text to a friend, learning to communicate your ideas in writing will make life a lot easier. And you don’t have to get a degree in English or take some kind of “business communication” class to learn these skills.

Instead, start with our [guide to professional communication](https://collegeinfogeek.com/professional-communication-guide/). And for more specific advice, check out our guides to [writing a cover letter](https://collegeinfogeek.com/cover-letter/) and [creating a resume](https://collegeinfogeek.com/how-to-write-a-resume/). Also, install the [Grammarly app](https://collegeinfogeek.com/grammarly/), which will catch and fix most spelling and grammar mistakes.

## Public Speaking

I know it may be strange to write about public speaking in a time when many in-person events are canceled or postponed, but it’s still a valuable skill even if you’re communicating via video calls or phone.

Great public speaking means communicating your ideas to a group in a clear, compelling way. That’s true whether you’re speaking to an audience of thousands or a handful of people in a conference room.

How to manage others is a valuable skill (especially as you advance in your career), but we often overlook the skill of self-management. Managing yourself is at the core of this website, and it’s what much of “productivity” advice is really about.

## Web Development

While I don’t think *everyone* should code (or wants to), web development is a very valuable skill to learn if you’re so inclined.

Obviously, you can use it to make money through a job or [freelancing](https://collegeinfogeek.com/ultimate-guide-freelancing-college/). But it also makes it much easier to create and market your projects online. And, it can be extremely fun and exciting (especially with new advancements happening each day). Finally, it’s a great way to get into other more technical (but useful) forms of programming.

To start learning web development, I recommend these resources:

Also, if learning to program intimidates you, I encourage you to *at least* [learn the basics of HTML](https://www.w3schools.com/html/). This can make it much easier to maintain your own website (see the next section for more on that).

## How to Make a Website

A website is a powerful tool for showcasing your work and impressing prospective employers. It takes more effort to make than a resume, but we think it’s worth it for improving your job prospects.

Plus, it will teach you lots of valuable skills in the process, such as how to buy a domain, maintain a website with WordPress, and even publish your own blog posts.

To get started creating a website, check out our [free, comprehensive guide](https://collegeinfogeek.com/the-ultimate-guide-to-building-a-personal-website/).

## How to Cook

Being able to cook food (that you actually enjoy eating) is an underrated skill. There are the obvious benefits of saving money and eating healthier. But cooking can also be tremendously rewarding and fun. It’s a great way to relax, entertain friends, and even impress a date.

If cooking has always eluded you, I recommend these resources to get started:

## How to Exercise and Be Fit

Unless you played lots of sports or came from a particularly fit family, you may never have learned to exercise as a kid. It sounds silly, but I found myself in that situation when I started college. Thankfully, I ended up learning how to create (and stick to) a regular exercise routine.

If you haven’t done the same, I highly recommend it. Being fit makes life a lot more enjoyable, and it’s also a chance to learn some new skills.

Not sure where to start? [Nerd Fitness](https://www.nerdfitness.com/) was the website that taught me how to exercise, but you can also just pick an activity you enjoy and do it regularly. Try different things and see what you enjoy.

And remember that there’s no such thing as the “best” exercise program. The best program is the one you’ll actually stick with.

## Design

Even if you aren’t a professional designer, learning some basic principles of design can be very useful. You’ll be able to design better-looking presentations…or just more interesting Instagram posts. And if you work with designers in your job, it will make communication much easier.

## How to View the World Through Mental Models

Mental models are a concept I discovered back when I was in college (thanks to the Farnam Street blog).

Mental models are different ways of understanding a complex, unpredictable world. They draw from a variety of disciplines (chemistry, physics, statistics, economics, etc.) in an effort to arrive at a more complete, accurate understanding of reality. Being aware of them can help you make better decisions and work more productively.

To start learning about mental models, I recommend [this master post from Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/mental-models/). For a deeper dive, read *[Thinking, Fast and Slow](https://www.amazon.com/dp/0374533555?tag=colinfgee-20).*

## Logic and Reasoning

Logical thinking can be extremely powerful for making better decisions and solving problems. However, most of us receive little training in this area in school. So it’s worth spending some time on your own to learn how to think logically and avoid certain cognitive biases.

To be clear, I’m not recommending you become a robot or a “pure logic” Vulcan. That’s a great way to alienate yourself and lose your friends. But having logical thinking in your mental “toolbox” can be tremendously useful.

## Statistics

Even if you took a statistics class in high school or college, you may not have learned how to *think* statistically.

Far more important than being able to calculate the standard deviation is learning how to interpret statistics you encounter in everyday life, such as in online articles or even ads.

Because while statistics can be a powerful tool for making sense of the world, they’re also a great tool for manipulating unsuspecting people. If you don’t want to be one of those people, understanding statistics is key.

The best introduction we’ve found to this topic is *[How to Lie With Statistics](https://www.amazon.com/dp/0393310728?tag=colinfgee-20).* It explains how people misuse statistics (intentionally or not), and how you can spot shoddy statistical thinking.

## How to Give a Great Job Interview

Job interviews are something all of us have to do in one form or another. While they may not be fun, it is possible to make them less stressful and more positive experiences.

Foremost, remember that a job interview is *not* an interrogation. It’s just a conversation to help people decide if they can work together in a mutually beneficial way.

## How to Network

Networking gets a bad rap. People will call it sleazy, fake, or even manipulative. But if you do it in an authentic way, it can open all kinds of doors that pure skill cannot. Plus, the relationships you make networking can enrich your life and lead to friendships that go beyond the workplace.

In fact, we prefer to think of it not as networking, but as [making friends](https://collegeinfogeek.com/make-friends-college/) (thanks to [Stephen Warley](https://lifeskillsthatmatter.com/) for this idea). Many of those will be friends who share your professional interests and can even help you advance your career. But the focus is on connections first, professional benefits later.

To learn how to network, check out these resources:

## How to Dress Professionally

I spent the first 13 years of my education attending private schools with a strict dress code. This reduced decision fatigue (I never had to think about what I was going to wear), but it also meant that I didn’t learn how to buy quality clothes and put together outfits until I was in college.

The unfortunate reality is that people *do* pay attention to what you wear and make judgments based on it. So you might as well do your best to dress in a way that gives the impression you want.

For advice on what to wear in a variety of business and professional settings, [check out this guide](https://www.thebalancecareers.com/business-casual-attire-2061335).

## How to Play a Musical Instrument

Some people would argue that this isn’t an “essential” skill, but I would disagree. While you may never play professionally, playing music still has benefits. It’s relaxing, challenging, and fun. And it will teach you a lot about how to learn (and practice) new skills.

For those of us who work with computers all day, playing an instrument is also refreshingly analog, physical, and disconnected. Much like exercise, it reminds you that you’re a physical being existing in space, not an abstract mind.

I can’t give you specific resources for learning, as that depends on the instrument and your overall musical knowledge. But YouTube is definitely the place to start. If you can afford it, I’d also encourage you to take a few lessons with a teacher so that you can learn proper technique and avoid bad habits.

## How to Speak a New Language

If your only experiences with language learning have been in high school or college, then you may mistakenly believe that you’re “too old” or “not smart enough” to learn a new language as an adult.

However, I challenge you to question those assumptions. You absolutely can learn to fluently speak new languages, and you can do so in less time (and for less money) than you might think.

To start, you need to pick a language. While you could pick a language based on its “usefulness” (such as one of the [official languages of the United Nations](https://www.un.org/en/sections/about-un/official-languages/index.html)), it’s better to pick a language you feel a connection to and are interested in.

This could mean learning a language that your ancestors spoke, a language your friends or family members speak, or the language of a country you’ve always wanted to visit. You’re more likely to stick with a language you care about than one you feel obligated to learn.

To start your language learning journey, [read this guide from Martin](https://collegeinfogeek.com/how-to-learn-a-language/) (our web developer and resident polyglot).

## How to Make and Use Spreadsheets

Spreadsheets used to intimidate me. I assumed I needed some kind of advanced math or programming knowledge to use them, and I never thought about creating my own.

Once I learned how to use spreadsheets, however, I found myself unable to live without them. They’re a great tool for organizing information, automating calculations, and even making data-driven decisions. I use them to plan complex trips, manage my personal finances, and track certain projects at work.

To get started learning, I encourage you to find a project that a spreadsheet could help you organize. Making something to model your expenses and budget is a good start (see Thomas’ [budgeting spreadsheet](https://collegeinfogeek.com/budget-tracker-2020/) for some inspiration).

Unless you have a reason to learn Excel specifically, [Google Sheets](https://www.google.com/sheets/about/) is our recommendation for beginners (it’s free). When you get stuck, just google your question — there’s almost certainly an answer out there.

## How to Make Good Coffee (or Tea)

We drink our fair share of caffeine here at College Info Geek, and that habit would get pretty expensive if we were buying all our coffee from Starbucks. Luckily, we’ve learned how to make great caffeinated beverages at home, and you can too.

For coffee, the best thing you can do is buy freshly roasted beans and grind them yourself. They don’t have to be fancy; we drink a lot of bulk beans from Costco and they taste just fine.

But grinding the coffee fresh makes a big difference in the taste ([this hand grinder](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B013R3Q7B2?tag=colinfgee-20) is an affordable, quality option). If you want to make fancier, single cup coffee, then get an [Aeropress](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0047BIWSK?tag=colinfgee-20).

Making great tea at home is even simpler. Buy loose leaf tea and some kind of [infuser](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B000FPN8TK?tag=colinfgee-20), and you’re good to go. If you’re tired of waiting for your water to boil on the stove, get an [electric kettle](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07TZ5YHJN?tag=colinfgee-20).

I buy most of my tea from [Adagio](https://collegeinfogeek.com/adagio-teas/). But if you have a local tea shop, be sure to check that out as well.

## How to Ask for a Raise

It’s easy to feel intimidated when discussing money with your boss. But if you don’t ask, you’re unlikely to get a raise (or, at least, as much of a raise as you deserve).

Remember that your company has an incentive to keep you, and giving you a raise is much less expensive than finding, hiring, and training a new employee.

Obviously, you need the [job performance](https://collegeinfogeek.com/ways-to-improve-work-performance/) to merit a raise, but you also need to make a good case for it. For advice on doing that, [read this excellent guide from The Cut](https://www.thecut.com/article/how-to-ask-for-a-raise.html).

## The Fundamentals of Business

Most of us work for businesses, but how many of us understand how they really work? Of course, you understand the parts of the business that relate to your job duties, but do you really know what they do in HR or accounting?

Even though you’re unlikely to work in every aspect of your company’s business, it’s still useful to understand how the business as a whole works. It will help you collaborate more effectively with other members of your organization, as well as help you sound more intelligent and informed during company meetings. Plus, it’s useful to know if you ever decide to [start your own business](https://collegeinfogeek.com/starting-your-own-business/).

*[The Personal MBA](https://www.amazon.com/dp/1591843529?tag=colinfgee-20)* remains our favorite introduction to the fundamentals of business. It’s concise, full of useful examples, and easy to reference.

## How Taxes Work

Unless you went to a particularly practical school, you probably didn’t learn how to do your taxes. But everyone has to do them, so you might as well learn how they work.

I’m not saying that you should manually fill out and file every form of your return — that’s a waste of time.

But it is worth understanding some of what’s going on in the background of your tax preparation software. And if you ever decide to hire an accountant, knowing how to do your own taxes will help you choose a good one.

Taxes can get quite complex (especially if you’re self-employed or own property), but the basics for most employees are simple enough. For a general overview, check out our [guide to taxes for college students](https://collegeinfogeek.com/student-tax-return/).

*(I recognize this section is U.S.-centric. If you’re from a different country, I’m sure there are similar resources for understanding your country’s tax system, which is probably less complex than that of the United States).*

## How to Budget and Save Money

In addition to not teaching how to do your taxes, most schools do a poor job of teaching you how to manage your money. But unless you’re living off the grid and producing everything you need to live, you have to deal with money. So you might as well learn how to manage it responsibly.

To learn how to budget and save money, [check out our detailed guide](https://collegeinfogeek.com/budgeting-for-college-students/) (even if you aren’t in college, the advice still applies). I also recommend signing up for an account with [Mint](https://collegeinfogeek.com/mint-com-automated-budgeting/), which makes it much easier to see and manage all your different financial accounts.

## Basic Investing

If you can learn to budget and save money, you’ll already be ahead of most people. The next step, however, is putting your money to work and saving for retirement.

Investing seems like an extremely complex topic that only Wall Street pros understand, but that’s largely an illusion. Investing is not complicated, everyone should be doing it, and you don’t need a lot of money to get started.

If you’re looking for an easy place to open an investment account, then we recommend [Betterment](https://collegeinfogeek.com/betterment-automated-investing/) (they also offer a great [online checking account](https://collegeinfogeek.com/betterment-checking/)). For guidance on how investing works, read our guide to [investing for beginners](https://collegeinfogeek.com/how-to-start-investing/).

## How to Perform Basic Home Repairs

Knowing how to fix things around your house or apartment is empowering. Instead of having to wait hours (or days) for your landlord or property manager to fix it, you can do so immediately. And once you own your home, doing your own repairs can save you lots of money.

To get started, [buy a basic tool kit](https://collegeinfogeek.com/tool-set/). And when you encounter some kind of problem with your place, see if you can figure out how to fix it using YouTube and some google searches. [This guide from DIYNetwork](https://www.diynetwork.com/how-to/maintenance-and-repair/repairing/simple-repairs-for-the-home) is also a useful reference for quick fixes.

***Caveat:** Please don’t electrocute yourself or flood your house. I’m encouraging you to learn how to make basic, minor repairs. If you have any doubt, call a professional (especially when it comes to electrical or plumbing work). And be sure any work you do is okay with your landlord.*

## How to Buy a Car

While I wish it weren’t so, owning a car is still a necessity for many people. Therefore, it’s worth understanding how to intelligently buy one.

Mainly, I recommend that you do a lot of research and don’t rush into anything. Shop around (for cars and loans, if you need one). If you can, get a mechanic to look at any used car *before* you buy it. And be sure to stay within your budget.

For more advice on buying a car, listen to these podcast episodes:

Also, if you’re not familiar with Car Talk, do yourself a favor and [give it a listen](https://www.cartalk.com/radio/our-show). Even if you don’t care about cars, the show is immensely entertaining (not to mention educational).

## How to Buy a House

Besides your car, a home is the largest purchase you’re likely to make. Plus, you’re not just purchasing new shoes; you’re buying something that will affect many aspects of your life, every day. So you should give it lots of thought and learn how to do it correctly.

To start, figure out if you can even afford to buy a house. And, if it makes financial sense to do so (if you’re planning to stay somewhere less than five years, for instance, it’s often cheaper to rent). [This calculator from The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2014/upshot/buy-rent-calculator.html) is very helpful for deciding if you should buy a house in your area and financial situation.

# Top 11 Future Professions in High Demand

The world of work is changing fast. To succeed, leading organizations and future-ready professionals are making a commitment to upskilling and lifelong learning to prepare for new jobs and skills requirements of the future.

[Maria Valero](/en-us/authors/maria-valero.html)

The job market is currently being redefined by business priorities and skill sets that didn’t exist just a few years ago. From AI breakthroughs to the continued rise of remote and hybrid workplaces to a growing focus on sustainability, forces of change are reshaping entire industries.

To keep pace, forward-looking organizations are treating skills as their most strategic asset. [Workday research](https://forms.workday.com/en-gb/reports/the-global-state-of-skills-report/form.html) found more than half (55%) of organizations have already shifted to skills-based approaches to become more adaptable and resilient. ## What Future Professions Are in Demand?

The future of work is already taking shape in today’s labor market, and continuing to evolve. ### 1. AI Specialists and Machine Learning Engineers

AI specialists and machine learning engineers design and refine systems that increasingly shape our everyday experiences—think recommendation engines, fraud detection, personalized medicine, and intelligent automation. Their work is at the core of business transformation, enabling organizations to scale insights and automate complex processes once thought impossible.

As AI adoption accelerates, these roles will expand beyond tech companies into every sector, from retail and finance to manufacturing and agriculture. The future of competitive advantage rests heavily on how well businesses integrate AI into products, services, and operations—and data and AI specialists make that happen.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Machine learning frameworks
* Deep learning
* Python or R
* Natural language processing
* Cloud platforms
* Model ethics and governance
* Bias mitigation testing

### 2. Data Scientists

[Handling large data](/en-us/big-data-and-data-mining.html) is a challenge for every business today. Data scientists specialize in transforming raw data into actionable insights. They create predictive models, run simulations, and reveal patterns that inform strategy and innovation. In an era of data abundance, their ability to distill clarity from complexity is invaluable.

As organizations push for measurable business outcomes to prove ROI from AI, the role is shifting. Tomorrow’s data scientists will need to combine technical mastery with sharp communication skills to influence leaders and help to shape more intelligent, data-driven decisions.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Statistical modeling
* Python or R
* SQL
* Data visualization
* Applied machine learning
* Business acumen

### 3. Cybersecurity Analysts and Digital Trust Officers

As digital systems become more interconnected, the risk of cyberattacks grows exponentially. Cybersecurity analysts protect networks, infrastructure, and intellectual property, while digital trust officers take a broader view, safeguarding brand reputation, data, and compliance in a world where security *equals* trust.

With cyberthreats increasingly becoming complex, these professionals will be on the front lines of defending organizations. Their expertise ensures continuity, protects sensitive information, and maintains the confidence of customers and partners.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Threat detection
* Risk assessment
* Cloud and network security
* Ethical hacking
* Incident response
* Knowledge of AI-enabled defense tools

### 4. Nurse Practitioners and Physician Assistants

An aging population and broader access to healthcare are driving rapid growth in advanced practice providers. Nurse practitioners and physicians’ assistants deliver frontline care, often serving as primary providers in communities where physicians are limited.

As rates of chronic illness climb and health systems face mounting pressure, their role has become central to ensuring patients receive high-quality and accessible care.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Clinical expertise
* Diagnostic proficiency
* Patient communication
* Emotional intelligence
* Adaptability to evolving healthcare technologies
* Collaboration with multidisciplinary teams

### 5. Medical and Health Services Managers

Behind every efficient healthcare system are medical and health services managers. They oversee operations in hospitals, clinics, and long-term care facilities and ensure clinical staff and resources align to delivering quality care.

As digital tools like electronic health records (EHRs), telehealth, and predictive analytics become standard, managers who can integrate these systems into workflows will be in high demand.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Health informatics
* Regulatory compliance
* Leadership skills
* Budget management
* Strategic planning in hybrid and digital care models

### 6. Sustainability and Climate Solutions Specialists

Organizations are under increasing pressure to reduce emissions, manage resources responsibly, and meet ESG goals. Sustainability specialists guide these transitions by developing climate strategies, measuring impact, and ensuring compliance with regulations and stakeholder expectations.

These roles are largely still emerging, but will play a central role in ensuring climate action shifts from ambition to execution. Specialists in this space will help organizations not just “go green,” but embed sustainability into the core of business strategy.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* ESG reporting
* Carbon accounting
* Sustainability assessment
* Regulatory knowledge
* Systems thinking for circular economy practices

### 7. Green Energy Engineers

The global energy system is in the midst of profound change, shifting away from fossil fuels toward renewables and electrification. Green energy engineers design, develop, and deploy technologies such as solar, wind, energy storage, and smart grids that make this transition possible.

Professionals in this role sit at the intersection of engineering and climate solutions, tasked with scaling infrastructure that is both reliable and sustainable. Their expertise is vital to energy security and the long-term resilience of industries and communities.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Renewable energy system design
* Grid integration
* Energy storage technologies
* Data-driven energy modeling
* Knowledge of environmental standards

### 8. Human-AI Interaction Designers

As AI becomes a collaborator rather than just a tool, human-AI interaction designers will create the interfaces, workflows, and experiences that allow people and intelligent systems to work together effectively. This role is part designer, part behavioral scientist and part technologist.

It requires understanding both human needs and machine capabilities, ensuring that AI solutions are not only usable but ethical and aligned with how people *actually* work.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* UX/UI design
* Human-centered design
* AI system literacy
* Behavioral psychology
* Ethics in human-computer interaction

### 9. Operations Research and Optimization Analysts

Complexity is the new normal in global supply chains and resource management. Operations research analysts help organizations navigate this by applying modeling, optimization, and simulation techniques to improve efficiency and resilience.

As disruptions from climate, geopolitical shifts, and market volatility increase, these analysts will be key to designing systems that adapt under pressure. Their work ensures that businesses can keep goods moving and manage inventory well—even in uncertain conditions.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Data modeling
* Simulations
* Optimization methods
* Scenario planning and forecasting
* Advanced analytics

### 10. Full-Stack Software Developers

Software developers have long been essential to business innovation, but their role is evolving. Today, full-stack developers build applications that span cloud infrastructure, mobile, and web, integrating AI into the products and platforms people use every day.

As industries continue to digitize, full-stack developers will be needed to create scalable, secure, connected, and intuitive systems.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Full-stack programming
* Cloud computing
* Agile development
* Performance optimization
* Integration of AI and automation tools

### 11. Digital Learning and Skills Development Specialists

In a technology [augmented workforce](/en-us/using-ai-empower-augmented-workforce.html), learning itself is becoming an industry. Digital learning specialists are designing platforms and programs that help workers continually upskill and reskill. They blend instructional design with emerging technologies—VR, AI, adaptive learning—to make development more effective and personalized.

These roles will grow as organizations increasingly recognize that [talent is their most strategic asset](/en-us/crosschqs-brilliant-500-ranks-workday-n1-in-software-quality-of-hire.html). The future of work depends on how well people can adapt, and specialists in digital learning will help make that possible.

**Skills You’ll Need**

* Instructional design
* Learning technologies
* Data-driven learning analytics
* Curriculum development
* Organizational psychology

## Macrotrends Changing the Workplace

Work today is being reshaped by the convergence of a few major shifts. Advances in AI and automation are accelerating alongside demographic change, the green transition, and persistent global economic and geopolitical uncertainty. Together, these trends are disrupting industries and redefining the skills employers value most to stay successful.

According to the World Economic Forum’s [Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/), more than 60% of employers expect AI and digital access to transform their businesses by 2030, while nearly half anticipate climate adaptation and mitigation will be equally disruptive.

Among these forces, AI and related technologies such as information processing, robotics, and automation are the top reported accelerants of change, with renewable energy innovations following close behind.

Meanwhile, aging populations in advanced economies, growing youth populations in emerging markets, and increasingly [multigenerational workforces](/en-us/top-7-tips-managing-multigenerational-workforces.html) are reshaping demand for healthcare, education, and workforce development.

In parallel, rising living costs and slower growth compound the complexity, shaping both job creation and displacement. By 2030, these forces are expected to create 170 million new jobs and displace 92 million, resulting in a net gain of 78 million roles worldwide.

For organizations and professionals alike, the challenge is to translate these forces into clear skills priorities that keep them competitive as work evolves.

> By 2030, disruptive trends are expected to create 170 million new jobs and displace 92 million, resulting in a net gain of 78 million roles worldwide.

In a job market defined by rapid change, the most valuable investment professionals can make is in themselves. Upskilling, reskilling, and lifelong learning keeps people adaptable and ready for career growth. Employers are looking for professionals who show initiative and commit to lifelong learning.

The good news? Access to learning has never been easier. Whether you’re exploring an emerging field or deepening expertise in your current role, today’s tools make skill development flexible and practical.

Some of the most effective ways to skill up include:

* **Online learning platforms** such as [Workday](https://www.workday.com/en-us/services/learn-with-workday.html), Coursera, edX, and LinkedIn Learning that deliver high-quality, flexible courses.
* **Certifications and micro-credentials** that validate specialized skills in areas like data analytics, cloud computing, or project management.
* **A lifelong learning mindset**, with the discipline of continually seeking opportunities to learn, experiment, and grow, both inside and outside of work.

By combining structured training with a proactive approach to growth, you’ll build the resilience and versatility needed to advance your career in a world where skills are the true currency of opportunity.

## 12 high-income technical skills to learn

Below, you’ll find 12 high-income [technical skills](https://www.coursera.org/articles/what-are-technical-skills) useful for specific careers or to strengthen your performance in your current role. For instance, a marketing professional may want to enhance their data skills, not to switch into a data role, but to uncover insights and create more effective campaigns.

To build this list, we referenced reports and platforms forecasting the most current in-demand skills from Pearson [[1](https://futureskills.pearson.com/research/assets/pdfs/technical-report.pdf)], LinkedIn [[2](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/5-most-in-demand-skills-2025-bernard-marr-xny2e/)], Glassdoor, Coursera [[3](https://www.coursera.org/skills-reports/job-skills#form)], and the World Economic Forum [[4](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/01/future-of-jobs-report-2025-jobs-of-the-future-and-the-skills-you-need-to-get-them/)].

### 1. Generative AI (GenAI)

[Generative AI (GenAI)](https://www.coursera.org/articles/what-is-generative-ai) is a transformative technology that's becoming a requirement across many roles. A growing number of businesses already rely on AI tools to increase their productivity, with even more businesses interested in finding ways to integrate AI into their workstream, business model, products, or services.
