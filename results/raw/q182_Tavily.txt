Top Questions

### What is pi?

Pi is the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) of the circumference of a [circle](https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics) to its diameter.

### What is the value of pi?

The value of pi is approximately 3.14, or 22/7. Pi is an [irrational number](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number), which means it is not equal to the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) of any two whole numbers. This also means pi can be expressed as an infinite decimal expansion with no regularly repeating digits. Pi has been calculated to trillions of decimal places.

### What is the symbol for pi?

The symbol for pi is π. It was first used by British mathematician William Jones in 1706 to represent the ratio and was later popularized by the Swiss mathematician [Leonhard Euler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonhard-Euler).

### What are the uses of pi?

Pi is used for solving problems involving the lengths of arcs or other [curves](https://www.britannica.com/science/curve), the areas of [ellipses](https://www.britannica.com/science/ellipse), sectors, and other curved surfaces, and the volumes of many solids. It is also used in various formulas of [physics](https://www.britannica.com/science/physics-science) and [engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/engineering) to describe the motion of [pendulums](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendulum), the vibration of strings, and [alternating electric currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/alternating-current).

**pi (π)**, in [mathematics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics), the [ratio](https://www.britannica.com/science/ratio) of the circumference of a [circle](https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics) to its [diameter](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/diameter). The [Greek letter](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Greek-alphabet) **π** was first used by [British](https://www.britannica.com/place/Great-Britain-island-Europe) [mathematician](https://www.britannica.com/science/mathematics) William Jones in 1706 to represent the ratio and was later popularized by [Swiss](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland) mathematician [Leonhard Euler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonhard-Euler). Because pi is [irrational](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number) (not equal to the ratio of any two whole numbers), its digits do not repeat, and an approximation such as 3.14 or 22/7 is often used for everyday calculations. To 39 decimal places, pi is 3.141592653589793238462643383279502884197.

## History

## Early approximations

A Biblical Approximation

The [Hebrew Bible](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hebrew-Bible) (1 Kings 7:23) describes the molten sea (a metal basin) of Solomon’s Temple as 10 cubits across and 30 cubits in circumference—an implied pi of 3. Though imprecise, it reflects an awareness of a fixed circle ratio.

The oldest surviving evidence of pi comes from [Babylonia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Babylonia) (present-day [Iraq](https://www.britannica.com/place/Iraq)). Old Babylonian [cuneiform](https://www.britannica.com/topic/cuneiform) tablets (c. 1900–1600 bce) give a practical [value](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/value) for pi derived from [hexagon](https://www.britannica.com/science/hexagon) [geometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry): They treated the ratio of an inscribed regular [hexagon](https://www.britannica.com/science/hexagon)’s perimeter to the circle’s circumference as 24/25, giving the value of pi as 25/8, or about 3.125. In [Egypt](https://www.britannica.com/place/Egypt) the [Rhind papyrus](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rhind-papyrus) (c. 1650 bce)—a scroll on [arithmetic](https://www.britannica.com/science/arithmetic) and geometry—uses the rule “subtract one-ninth of the diameter and square the remainder,” yielding 256/81, or about 3.16045. These figures show how scribes and builders treated pi as a stable operational [constant](https://www.britannica.com/topic/constant) long before formal proofs.

## Classical geometry

In the 3rd century bce [Greek](https://www.britannica.com/place/Greece) mathematician and engineer [Archimedes](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Archimedes) developed a geometric method to calculate pi. By comparing perimeters of polygons inscribed in and circumscribed around a circle (up to 96 sides), he proved 223/71 < π < 22/7, or an average value of about 3.1418. Archimedes also proved that the ratio of the area of a circle to the square of its radius is the same constant. In the 2nd century ce, [Claudius Ptolemy](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ptolemy), a Greco-Roman [astronomer](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy) and geographer working in [Alexandria](https://www.britannica.com/place/Alexandria-Egypt), used trigonometric chord tables in his astronomical [treatise](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatise) *[Almagest](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Almagest)*—a [comprehensive](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/comprehensive) guide to the motions of the [Sun](https://www.britannica.com/place/Sun), [Moon](https://www.britannica.com/place/Moon), and [planets](https://www.britannica.com/science/planet)—to obtain a value of pi of about 3.1416.

## Advances in [Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Asia)

In [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China), [Liu Hui](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Liu-Hui) (3rd century ce), noted for his commentary on *The Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art*, refined polygonal methods to reach pi of about 3.14159. Two centuries later mathematician and astronomer [Zu Chongzhi](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Zu-Chongzhi) (429–500 ce) achieved a record level of accuracy with the [fraction](https://www.britannica.com/science/fraction) 355/113 and proved that pi lies between 3.1415926 and 3.1415927—known as the *milü* (“close ratio”). In [India](https://www.britannica.com/place/India), [Aryabhata](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Aryabhata-I) (born 476 ce), an early astronomer-mathematician, used pi as 62,832/20,000 (about 3.1416) in the “Ganita” (“Mathematics”) section of his treatise *Aryabhatiya*, where he presented rules for arithmetic, square and cubic roots, and geometric measurements. Mathematician and astronomer Madhava of Sangamagrama, who led the Kerala school of mathematics and astronomy (14th–16th century), calculated pi to 11 decimal places. In [Central Asia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Central-Asia), [Jamshid al-Kāshī](https://www.britannica.com/biography/al-Kashi) (c. 1380–1429), an astronomer at the [Samarkand](https://www.britannica.com/place/Samarkand-Uzbekistan) observatory, calculated 2π to 9 sexagesimal places, equivalent to about 16 correct decimal digits, giving the most precise known value of pi in the [medieval](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/medieval) world.

## Early modern analysis

During the 16th and 17th centuries, [European](https://www.britannica.com/place/Europe) mathematicians developed new ways to calculate pi with precision. [French](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) mathematician [François Viète](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francois-Viete-seigneur-de-la-Bigotiere) was the first to express pi as an endless product of numbers, introducing an approach that linked [geometric](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry) ideas with [algebraic](https://www.britannica.com/science/algebra) calculation. In 1655–56 English mathematician [John Wallis](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Wallis) introduced an [infinite](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/infinite) product formula for pi in his book *Arithmetica Infinitorum* (“The Arithmetic of Infinitesimals”), representing the constant as an unending sequence of ratios built from whole numbers—a formulation that later reappeared unexpectedly in quantum-mechanical calculations of the [hydrogen](https://www.britannica.com/science/hydrogen) atom.

In the early 1670s [James Gregory](https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Gregory) in [Scotland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Scotland) and [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gottfried-Wilhelm-Leibniz) in [Germany](https://www.britannica.com/place/Germany) each presented a series for pi divided by 4 (1 – 1/3 + 1/5 – 1/7 + …), a method earlier anticipated by Madhava. In 1706 [John Machin](https://www.britannica.com/biography/John-Machin) of [England](https://www.britannica.com/place/England) used a faster arctangent formula to compute pi by hand to 100 decimal places. That same year William Jones of [Wales](https://www.britannica.com/place/Wales) adopted the symbol π to [denote](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/denote) the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter—a notation popularized by the [Swiss](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland) mathematician [Leonhard Euler](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leonhard-Euler) a few decades later.

## Mathematical proofs and expanding precision

What Is an [Irrational Number](https://www.britannica.com/science/irrational-number)?

An irrational number is a number that cannot be expressed as a fraction of two [integers](https://www.britannica.com/science/integer). Its decimal expansion goes on forever without repeating. The [square root](https://www.britannica.com/science/square-root) of 2 and the constant *e* are examples. Pi is irrational because no fraction can capture its exact value. The digits of pi continue endlessly without settling into a repeating pattern, which is why such approximations as 3.14 or 22/7 are used in everyday work.

The nature of pi became clearer in the 18th and 19th centuries. In 1768 Swiss mathematician [Johann Heinrich Lambert](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Johann-Heinrich-Lambert) proved that pi is irrational—it cannot be expressed as a ratio of whole numbers. [Ferdinand von Lindemann](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ferdinand-von-Lindemann), a mathematician from Germany who was influenced by the work of French mathematician [Charles Hermite](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Charles-Hermite), proved in 1882 that pi is [transcendental](https://www.britannica.com/science/transcendental-number), meaning it cannot be the root of any [algebraic equation](https://www.britannica.com/science/algebraic-equation). His proof, based partly on Hermite’s demonstration that the constant *e* is transcendental and the identity [e](https://www.britannica.com/science/e-mathematics)iπ = −1, settled the ancient Greek problem of “squaring the circle,” showing it impossible to construct a square equal in area to a given circle using only a straightedge and [compass](https://www.britannica.com/technology/compass-navigational-instrument).

In 1873 English mathematician William Shanks computed 707 digits by hand, though later analysis showed errors beyond the 527th place. His results went unchallenged for 75 years, until [American](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States) D.F. Ferguson, working with a mechanical desk [calculator](https://www.britannica.com/technology/calculator) in 1946, identified the mistake and recomputed pi correctly to 808 digits. In 1914 Indian mathematician [Srinivasa Ramanujan](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Srinivasa-Ramanujan) presented 17 formulas for calculating pi, including a series for its [reciprocal](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/reciprocal). Each term in the series produced several correct digits of pi, making his methods far more efficient than earlier approaches. His work later inspired the Ramanujan-Sato series and provided the mathematical foundation for modern computer [algorithms](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/algorithms) that have computed pi to trillions of digits.

Celebrating Pi Day

Pi Day, observed on March 14 (3/14), honors the mathematical constant π and its significance across [science](https://www.britannica.com/science/science) and engineering. The date reflects the first three digits of pi, 3.14, and coincides with [Albert Einstein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Albert-Einstein)’s birthday. The tradition began in 1988, when physicist Larry Shaw organized the first celebration at a [San Francisco](https://www.britannica.com/place/San-Francisco-California) science museum; it was officially recognized by the U.S. Congress in 2009.

## Computational era

In 1949, the [Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer](https://www.britannica.com/technology/ENIAC) (ENIAC) became the first electronic machine used to calculate pi, producing 2,037 digits in about 70 hours. In the late 1980s, American mathematicians Gregory and David Chudnovsky, working from an apartment in [New York City](https://www.britannica.com/place/New-York-City), used a custom-built parallel supercomputer to compute pi to more than one billion digits; a few years later, they extended their result to more than four billion digits. In October 1999, Yasumasa Kanada and his assistant, Daisuke Takahasi, at the [University of Tokyo](https://www.britannica.com/topic/University-of-Tokyo), [Japan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Japan), achieved a new record with 206,158,430,000 digits, using a [Hitachi](https://www.britannica.com/place/Hitachi) SR8000 [supercomputer](https://www.britannica.com/technology/supercomputer). In the early 21st century, Japanese mathematician Shigeru Kondo, working with Alexander Yee in the [United States](https://www.britannica.com/place/United-States), used modern algorithms and [distributed computing](https://www.britannica.com/technology/distributed-computing) to calculate more than 10 trillion digits of pi.

## Applications

Pi appears throughout mathematics, science, and engineering wherever circular, periodic, or wavelike phenomena arise. It connects geometry, analysis, and the [physical sciences](https://www.britannica.com/science/physical-science) in equations describing [motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/motion-mechanics), [symmetry](https://www.britannica.com/science/symmetry-physics), and change.

* **[Geometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/geometry) and [trigonometry](https://www.britannica.com/science/trigonometry):** Pi is fundamental to formulas involving [circles](https://www.britannica.com/science/circle-mathematics), [spheres](https://www.britannica.com/science/sphere), and related figures. The circumference of a circle is given by 2πr and its area by πr2, where *r* is the radius of the circle. Pi also appears in the surface area and volume formulas for spheres, [cylinders](https://www.britannica.com/technology/cylinder-engineering), and [cones](https://www.britannica.com/science/cone-mathematics). In trigonometry, pi underlies the measurement of angles in radians, where π radians equals 180 degrees.
* **Analysis and [number theory](https://www.britannica.com/science/number-theory):** The constant appears in [infinite series](https://www.britannica.com/science/infinite-series), [integrals](https://www.britannica.com/science/integral-mathematics), and special functions. The [Fourier transform](https://www.britannica.com/science/Fourier-transform), which represents periodic phenomena as sums of [sines](https://www.britannica.com/science/sine) and [cosines](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosine), depends directly on pi. [Probabilistic](https://www.britannica.com/science/probability-theory) results such as Buffon’s needle problem also connect pi to geometric probability and randomness.
* **Physics:** Pi occurs in the description of [wave motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/wave-motion), oscillations, and [resonance](https://www.britannica.com/science/resonance-particle-physics). It is present in formulas for [pendulums](https://www.britannica.com/technology/pendulum) and vibrating strings in [simple harmonic motion](https://www.britannica.com/science/simple-harmonic-motion), as well as [alternating electrical currents](https://www.britannica.com/science/alternating-current). More advanced theories, such as [quantum mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/quantum-mechanics-physics) and [relativity](https://www.britannica.com/science/relativity), also employ pi in their fundamental equations.
* **[Engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/engineering) and [technology](https://www.britannica.com/technology/technology):** Engineers use pi in applications that involve circular, rotating, or curved components. It appears in formulas for signal processing, electrical [circuits](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/circuits), and electromagnetic waves, where it helps relate [frequency](https://www.britannica.com/science/frequency-physics), rotation, and [phase](https://www.britannica.com/science/phase-mechanics). In [mechanical](https://www.britannica.com/technology/mechanical-engineering) and [civil engineering](https://www.britannica.com/technology/civil-engineering), pi is essential for calculating areas and volumes of cylinders, tanks, and pipes and for analyzing stresses and rotational motion in [machines](https://www.britannica.com/technology/machine) and structures. It also underlies calculations used in satellite communications, actuator control, and the design of storage vessels and materials systems.
* **[Astronomy](https://www.britannica.com/science/astronomy) and [cosmology](https://www.britannica.com/science/cosmology-astronomy):** Scientists and engineers use pi to explore and measure the [universe](https://www.britannica.com/science/universe). It is essential in orbital [mechanics](https://www.britannica.com/science/mechanics) for calculating spacecraft trajectories, orbital paths, and gravitational forces. On missions such as [Mars](https://www.britannica.com/place/Mars-planet) landings, pi helps determine parachute size, descent rates, and landing zones. Astronomers use pi to map planetary surfaces, measure [craters](https://www.britannica.com/science/crater-geology) and planetary volumes, and identify exoplanets by analyzing changes in starlight during transits. It also helps determine the sizes and [densities](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/densities) of planets, [asteroids](https://www.britannica.com/science/asteroid), and [binary stars](https://www.britannica.com/science/binary-star) such as Alpha Draconis. Beyond the [solar system](https://www.britannica.com/science/solar-system), pi appears in equations describing [elliptical](https://www.britannica.com/science/ellipse) orbits and the geometry of space, helping model the structure and motion of the universe.

Pi - which is written as the Greek letter for p, or - is the ratio of the circumference of any circle to the diameter of that circle. Regardless of the circle's size, this ratio will always equal pi. In decimal form, the value of pi is approximately 3.14. But pi is an irrational number, meaning that its decimal form neither ends (like 1/4 = 0.25) nor becomes repetitive (like 1/6 = 0.166666...). To only 18 decimal places, pi is 3.141592653589793238.

It is useful to have shorthand for this ratio of circumference to diameter. According to Petr Beckmann's *A History of Pi*, the Greek letter was first used for this purpose by William Jones in 1706, probably as an abbreviation of periphery, and became standard mathematical notation roughly 30 years later.

Pi is most commonly used in certain computations regarding circles. Pi not only relates circumference and diameter. Amazingly, it also connects the diameter or radius of a circle with the area of that circle by the formula: the area is equal to pi times the radius squared. The importance of pi has been recognized for at least 4,000 years. *A History of Pi* notes that by 2000 B.C., "the Babylonians and the Egyptians (at least) were aware of the existence and significance of the constant ," recognizing that every circle has the same ratio of circumference to diameter. Both the Babylonians and Egyptians had rough numerical approximations to the value of pi, and later mathematicians in ancient Greece, particularly Archimedes, improved on those approximations. By the start of the 20th century, about 500 digits of pi were known. With computation advances, Pi fanatics have recently been able to calculate Pi to [12.1 trillion digits](https://www.numberworld.org/misc_runs/pi-12t/)!

According to Steven Strogatz, professor of mathematics at Cornell, in his March 13, 2015 article in the New Yorker, the beauty of pi, in part, is that it puts infinity within reach. Even young children get this. The digits of pi never end and never show a pattern. They go on forever, seemingly at random—except that they can’t possibly be random, because they embody the order inherent in a perfect circle. This tension between order and randomness is one of the most tantalizing aspects of pi".

But there’s still more to pi. After all, other famous irrational numbers, like *e* (the base of natural logarithms) and the square root of two, bridge different areas of mathematics, and they, too, have never-ending, seemingly random sequences of digits.

Pi is inescapable because cycles are the temporal cousins of circles; they are to time as circles are to space. Pi is at the heart of both.

For this reason, pi is intimately associated with waves, from the ebb and flow of the ocean’s tides to the electromagnetic waves that let us communicate wirelessly. # **What is Pi?**

***“Probably no symbol in mathematics has evoked as much mystery, romanticism, misconception and human interest as the number pi”***

**~William L. Schaaf, *Nature and History of Pi***

Pi (often represented by the lower-case Greek letter π), one of the most well-known mathematical constants, is the ratio of a circle’s circumference to its diameter.  For any circle, the distance around the edge is a little more than three times the distance across.

Typing π into a calculator and pressing ENTER will yield the result 3.141592654, not because this value is exact, but because a calculator’s display is often limited to 10 digits.  Pi is actually an irrational number (a decimal with no end and no repeating pattern) that is most often approximated with the decimal 3.14 or the fraction \(\frac{22}{7}\).

This brings up a rather interesting question: *If pi is the number of diameter lengths that fit around a circle, how can it have no end*?

## **Pi: A Perennial Puzzle**

Pi has interested people around the world for over 4,000 years.  Many mathematicians – from famous ones such as Fibonacci, Newton, Leibniz, and Gauss, to lesser well-known mathematical minds – have toiled over pi, calculated its digits, and applied it in numerous areas of mathematics.  Some spent the better parts of their lives calculating just a few digits. Here is a sampling of the many milestones in the life of pi.

Early decimal approximations for pi were obtained in a number of different ways.  For example, in ancient Babylon, rope stretchers marking the locations of buildings and boundaries estimated pi to be \(\frac{25}{8}\) = 3.125.  The ancient Egyptians determined the ratio to be \((\frac{16}{9})^{2}\) ≈ 3.16. The earliest calculations of pi were largely based on measurement.

Archimedes, a Greek mathematician, was the first to use an algorithmic approach to calculate pi.  He drew a polygon inside a circle and drew a second polygon outside of the circle. Then he continuously added more and more sides of both polygons, getting closer and closer to the shape of the circle.  Having reached 96-sided polygons, he proved that \(\frac{223}{71}\) < pi < \(\frac{22}{7}\).

From Archimedes’ time (about 250 B.C.E.) to the early 1600s mathematicians in countries around the world used methods similar to Archimedes’ to estimate pi, with increasingly efficient and accurate results.  In 1630, Austrian astronomer Christoph Grienberger calculated 38 digits of pi using polygons with 1040 sides, which remains the best calculation of pi using this polygonal method.

The Renaissance saw many developments and work on pi, including the creation of the name pi.  Until 1647, it didn’t have a universal name or symbol. English mathematician William Oughtred began calling it pi in his publication *Clavis Mathematicae*, but it wasn’t until Leonhard Euler used the symbol in 1737 that it became widely embraced.  The reason for adopting this particular Greek letter is because it is the first letter of the Greek word, perimetros, which loosely translates to “circumference.”  
 [![](data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns=%22http://www.w3.org/2000/svg%22%20viewBox=%220%200%20728%2090%22%3E%3C/svg%3E)](https://www.mometrix.com/university/mathcr/)  
 In 1767, Swiss mathematician Johann Heinrich Lambert proved pi is irrational and in 1882 Ferdinand von Lindemann proved pi is transcendental, which means π cannot be a solution to a polynomial equation with rational coefficients.  This finding is significant because, until this point, it was believed that one could construct a square and a circle with equal area, known as “squaring the circle”. Proving the transcendence of pi showed this is not possible and the phrase “squaring the circle” is now used as a metaphor for trying to do something that is impossible.

With modern technological advances, pi has now been calculated to [31 trillion digits](https://www.theverge.com/2019/3/14/18265358/pi-calculation-record-31-trillion-google).  However, only the first 39 or so are needed to be able to perform all calculations in our observable universe with virtually no error.  Though it is news every time the digit record is broken, we can now use technology to explore other aspects of pi. One example from the Chudnovsky brothers, a pair of American mathematicians:

***“We are looking for the appearance of some rules that will distinguish the digits of pi from other numbers. If you see a Russian sentence that extends for a whole page, with hardly a comma, it is definitely Tolstoy. If someone gave you a million digits from somewhere in pi, could you tell it was from pi? We don’t really look for patterns; we look for rules”***

Take some time to research and explore this unique number.  It has a long and very detailed history that shows the field of mathematics as a living, breathing subject, not as a collection of rules and formulas.

## **Where does pi occur?**

Pi occurs in many areas of mathematics, far too many to list here.

The study of pi begins around middle school, when students learn about the circumference and area of circles. 

### **CIRCUMFERENCE and AREA of CIRCLES**

The definition of pi gives us a way to calculate **circumference**. The circumference of a circle is the distance around a circle. If π = \(\frac{C}{d}\), then *C* = π*d*. You can also calculate the circumference of a circle with *C* = 2π*r*.  

The **area** of a circle is *A* = π\(r^{2}\).

This learning progresses as students study cylinders, spheres, and cones.

### **CYLINDERS, SPHERES, and CONES**

The **surface area of a cylinder** is 2π\(r^{2}\) + *h*(2π*r*).

The **volume of a cylinder** is π\(r^{2}\)*h*.

The **surface area of a sphere** is 4π\(r^{2}\).

The **volume of a sphere** is \(\frac{4}{3}\)π\(r^{3}\).

The **surface area of a cone** is π*rl* + π\(r^{2}\).

The **volume of a cone** is \(\frac{1}{3}\)π\(r^{2}\)*h*.

In high school, students study circles more in-depth and also study unit-circle trigonometry.

### **RADIANS and DEGREES**

Angles can be measured in both degrees and radians.  A radian is defined as an arc that has the same measure as the radius of a circle.  Since π diameters equal circumference, 2π radius lengths also equals circumference. Therefore, 360 degrees is the same as 2π radians, 180 degrees equals π radians, 90 degrees equals \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) radians, etc.

### **ARCS**

An arc created by a central angle, θ, is a fraction of the circumference of a circle: arc length = \(\theta \frac{C}{2\pi}\). The equation for circumference can be substituted in, then the whole equation can be simplified to: arc length = \(\theta \frac{\pi d}{2\pi}=\theta \frac{d}{2}=\theta r\).

### **SECTORS**

The area of a sector created by a central angle θ is a fraction of the area of a circle: area of sector = \(\theta \frac{A}{2\pi}\). The equation for area can be substituted in, then the whole equation can be simplified to: area of sector = \(\theta \frac{\pi r^{2}}{2\pi}=\theta \frac{r^{2}}{2}\).

### **CALCULATING VOLUMES of SOLIDS**

In calculus, students learn methods to calculate the volume of solids formed by rotating 2-dimensional surfaces around different axes.

These just scratch the surface!  Pi exists in many mathematical and scientific applications.  Technological advancements over the last 60 years or so will allow us to explore the famous number even more deeply than ever before!  If you’re interested in learning more about pi, there’s no better time to see what’s out there. Have fun!

# A Brief History of Pi (π)

A Brief History of Pi (π)

Pi (π) has been known for almost 4000 years—but even if we calculated the number of seconds in those 4000 years and calculated π to that number of places, we would still only be approximating its actual value. Here’s a brief history of finding π.

The ancient Babylonians calculated the area of a circle by taking 3 times the square of its radius, which gave a value of pi = 3. One Babylonian tablet (ca. 1900–1680 BC) indicates a value of 3.125 for π, which is a closer approximation.

The *Rhind Papyrus* (ca.1650 BC) gives us insight into the mathematics of ancient Egypt. The Egyptians calculated the area of a circle by a formula that gave the approximate value of 3.1605 for π.

The first calculation of π was done by Archimedes of Syracuse (287–212 BC), one of the greatest mathematicians of the ancient world. Archimedes approximated the area of a circle by using the Pythagorean Theorem to find the areas of two regular polygons: the polygon inscribed within the circle and the polygon within which the circle was circumscribed. Since the actual area of the circle lies between the areas of the inscribed and circumscribed polygons, the areas of the polygons gave upper and lower bounds for the area of the circle. Archimedes knew that he had not found the value of π but only an approximation within those limits. In this way, Archimedes showed that π is between 3 1/7 and 3 10/71.

A similar approach was used by Zu Chongzhi (429–501), a brilliant Chinese mathematician and astronomer. Zu Chongzhi would not have been familiar with Archimedes’ method—but because his book has been lost, little is known of his work. He calculated the value of the ratio of the circumference of a circle to its diameter to be 355/113. To compute this accuracy for π, he must have started with an inscribed regular *24,576-gon* and performed lengthy calculations involving hundreds of square roots carried out to 9 decimal places.

Mathematicians began using the Greek letter π in the 1700s. Introduced by William Jones in 1706, use of the symbol was popularized by Leonhard Euler, who adopted it in 1737.

An eighteenth-century French mathematician named Georges Buffon devised a way to calculate π based on probability. You can try it yourself at the Exploratorium's Pi Toss exhibit.
