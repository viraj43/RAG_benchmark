[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Continental_models-Australia.gif/500px-Continental_models-Australia.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Continental_models-Australia.gif) Animated, colour-coded map showing four to seven continents – depending on the [convention and model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundaries_between_the_continents "Boundaries between the continents"), some continents may be consolidated or subdivided.

A **continent** is any of several large terrestrial geographical regions. Continents are generally identified by [convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_(norm) "Convention (norm)") rather than any strict criteria. A continent could be a single large [landmass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landmass "Landmass"), a landmass and nearby islands either on or beyond its [continental shelf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"), or a part of a larger landmass, as in the case of Asia and Europe within [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia"). Due to these varying definitions, the number of continents varies, up to seven or as few as four. Most [English-speaking countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English-speaking_world "English-speaking world") recognize seven continents. In order from largest to smallest in area, these seven are [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia"), [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America"), [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"), [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica"), [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), and [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)") (or sometimes [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania") or [Australasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australasia "Australasia")). Different variations with fewer continents merge some of these regions; examples of this are merging Asia and Europe into [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia"),[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-NatlGeo1-1) North America and South America into the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas") (or simply America), and Africa, Asia, and Europe into [Afro-Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro-Eurasia "Afro-Eurasia").

[Oceanic islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island#Oceanic_islands "Island") are occasionally grouped with a nearby continent to divide all the world's land into geographical regions. Under this scheme, most of the island countries and territories in the [Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean") are grouped together with the continent of Australia to form the geographical region of [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania").[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-2)

In [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), a continent is defined as "one of [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth")'s major landmasses, including both dry land and [continental shelves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf")".[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Glossary2005-3) The [geological continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#Geological_continents) correspond to seven large areas of continental crust that are found on the [tectonic plates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_tectonic_plates "List of tectonic plates"), but exclude small [continental fragments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment") such as [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar") that are generally referred to as [microcontinents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment"). [Continental crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_crust "Continental crust") is only known to exist on Earth.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-4)

The idea of [continental drift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_drift "Continental drift") gained recognition in the 20th century. It postulates that the current continents formed from the breaking up of a supercontinent ( [Pangaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea")) that formed hundreds of millions of years ago.

## Etymology

From the 16th century the English noun _continent_ was derived from the term _continent land_, meaning continuous or connected land[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-oed-5) and translated from the Latin _terra continens_.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-6) The noun was used to mean "a connected or continuous tract of land" or [mainland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland "Mainland").[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-oed-5) It was not applied only to very large areas of land—in the 17th century, references were made to the _continents_ (or mainlands) of the [Isle of Man](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isle_of_Man "Isle of Man"), [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ireland "Ireland") and [Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wales "Wales") and in 1745 to [Sumatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumatra "Sumatra").[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-oed-5) The word _continent_ was used in translating Greek and Latin writings about the three "parts" of the world, although in the original languages no word of exactly the same meaning as _continent_ was used.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199729-7)

While _continent_ was used on the one hand for relatively small areas of continuous land, on the other hand geographers again raised Herodotus's query about why a single large landmass should be divided into separate continents. In the mid-17th century, [Peter Heylin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Heylin "Peter Heylin") wrote in his _Cosmographie_ that "A Continent is a great quantity of Land, not separated by any Sea from the rest of the World, as the whole Continent of Europe, Asia, Africa." In 1727, [Ephraim Chambers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ephraim_Chambers "Ephraim Chambers") wrote in his _Cyclopædia,_ "The world is ordinarily divided into two grand continents: the [Old](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_World "Old World") and the [New](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_World "New World")." And in his 1752 atlas, [Emanuel Bowen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emanuel_Bowen "Emanuel Bowen") defined a continent as "a large space of dry land comprehending many countries all joined together, without any separation by water. Thus Europe, Asia, and Africa is one great continent, as America is another."[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-8) However, the old idea of Europe, Asia and Africa as "parts" of the world ultimately persisted with these being regarded as separate continents.

## Definitions and application

By convention, continents "are understood to be large, continuous, discrete masses of land, ideally separated by expanses of water".[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199721-9) By this definition, all continents have to be an island of some metric. In modern schemes with five or more recognized continents, at least one pair of continents is joined by land in some fashion. The criterion "large" leads to arbitrary classification: [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"), with a surface area of 2,166,086 square kilometres (836,330 sq mi), is only considered the world's largest island, while [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)"), at 7,617,930 square kilometres (2,941,300 sq mi), is deemed the smallest continent.

Earth's major landmasses all have coasts on a single, continuous [World Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean#World_Ocean "Ocean"), which is divided into several principal oceanic components by the continents and various geographic criteria.[\[10\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-10)[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-UNAoO-11)

The geological definition of a continent has four criteria: high elevation relative to the ocean floor; a wide range of igneous, metamorphic and sedimentary rocks rich in silica; a crust thicker than the surrounding oceanic crust; and well-defined limits around a large enough area.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-nytimes.com-12)

### Extent

The most restricted meaning of _continent_ is that of a continuous[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-13)\[ _[non-tertiary source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources "Wikipedia:No original research")_\] area of land or mainland, with the coastline and any land boundaries forming the edge of the continent. In this sense, the term _[continental Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_Europe "Continental Europe")_ (sometimes referred to in Britain as "the Continent") is used to refer to mainland Europe, excluding islands such as [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Britain "Great Britain"), [Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland "Iceland"), [Ireland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ireland "Ireland"), and [Malta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malta "Malta"), while the term _continent of Australia_ may refer to the [mainland of Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia"), excluding [New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Guinea "New Guinea"), [Tasmania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tasmania "Tasmania"), and other nearby islands. Similarly, the _[continental United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contiguous_United_States#Continental_and_mainland_United_States "Contiguous United States")_ refers to "the 49 States (including [Alaska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska "Alaska") but excluding [Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaii "Hawaii")) located on the continent of North America, and the [District of Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.")."[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-14)

From the perspective of geology or [physical geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_geography "Physical geography"), _continent_ may be extended beyond the confines of continuous dry land to include the shallow, submerged adjacent area (the [continental shelf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"))[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-15) and the islands on the shelf ( [continental islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island#Continental_islands "Island")), as they are structurally part of the continent.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-16)

From this perspective, the edge of the continental shelf is the true edge of the continent, as shorelines vary with changes in sea level.[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Ollier-17) In this sense the islands of Great Britain and Ireland are part of Europe, while Australia and the island of New Guinea together form a continent. Taken to its limit, this view could support the view that there are only three continents: Antarctica, Australia-New Guinea, and a single mega-continent which joins Afro-Eurasia and America via the contiguous continental shelf in and around the Bering Sea. The vast size of the latter compared to the first two might even lead some to say it is the only continent, the others being more comparable to Greenland or New Zealand.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-nytimes.com-12)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Island_nations.svg/500px-Island_nations.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Island_nations.svg) Map of [island countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_island_countries "List of island countries"): these states are often grouped geographically with a neighboring continental landmass.

As a [cultural construct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_constructionism "Social constructionism"), the concept of a continent may go beyond the continental shelf to include [oceanic islands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island#Oceanic_islands "Island") and continental fragments. In this way, Iceland is considered a part of Europe, and [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar") a part of Africa. Extrapolating the concept to its extreme, some geographers group the [Australian continental landmass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia") with other islands in the Pacific Ocean into [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania"), which is usually considered a region rather than a continent. This divides the entire land surface of Earth into continents, regions, or [quasi-continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quasi-continents&action=edit&redlink=1 "Quasi-continents (page does not exist)").[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-18)

### Separation

Main article: [Boundaries between the continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundaries_between_the_continents "Boundaries between the continents")

See also: [List of transcontinental countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_transcontinental_countries "List of transcontinental countries")

The criterion that each continent is a discrete landmass is commonly relaxed due to historical conventions and practical use. Of the seven most globally recognized continents, only Antarctica and Australia are completely separated from other continents by the ocean. Several continents are defined not as absolutely distinct bodies but as " _more or less_ discrete masses of land".[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199735-19) Africa and Asia are joined by the [Isthmus of Suez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isthmus_of_Suez "Isthmus of Suez"), and North America and South America by the [Isthmus of Panama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama "Isthmus of Panama"). In both cases, there is no complete separation of these landmasses by water (disregarding the [Suez Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal") and the [Panama Canal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panama_Canal "Panama Canal"), which are both narrow and shallow, as well as human-made). Both of these [isthmuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isthmus "Isthmus") are very narrow compared to the bulk of the landmasses they unite.

North America and South America are treated as separate continents in the seven-continent model. However, they may also be viewed as a single continent known as [America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas"). This viewpoint was common in the United States until [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and remains prevalent in some Asian and most Latin American six-continent models.[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-20)

The criterion of a discrete landmass is completely disregarded if the continuous landmass of [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia") is classified as two separate continents (Asia and Europe). [Physiographically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_geography "Physical geography"), Europe and the [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent") are large peninsulas of the Eurasian landmass. However, Europe is considered a continent with its comparatively large land area of 10,180,000 square kilometres (3,930,000 sq mi), while the Indian subcontinent, with less than half that area, is considered a subcontinent. The alternative view—in geology and geography—that Eurasia is a single continent results in a six-continent view of the world. Some view the separation of Eurasia into Asia and Europe as a residue of [Eurocentrism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurocentrism "Eurocentrism"): "In physical, cultural and historical diversity, [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") and [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") are comparable to the entire European landmass, not to a single European country. \[...\]."[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents1997104%E2%80%93123-21) However, for historical and cultural reasons, the view of Europe as a separate continent continues in almost all categorizations.

If continents are defined strictly as discrete landmasses, embracing all the contiguous land of a body, then Africa, Asia, and Europe form a single continent which may be referred to as [Afro-Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro-Eurasia "Afro-Eurasia").[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-McColl-22) Combined with the consolidation of the Americas, this would produce a four-continent model consisting of Afro-Eurasia, America, Antarctica, and Australia.

When sea levels were lower during the [Pleistocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleistocene "Pleistocene") [ice ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_age "Ice age"), greater areas of the continental shelf were exposed as dry land, forming [land bridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_bridge "Land bridge") between [Tasmania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tasmania "Tasmania") and the [Australian mainland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia").[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-23) At those times, Australia and New Guinea were a single, continuous continent known as [Sahul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahul "Sahul"). Likewise, Afro-Eurasia and the Americas were joined by the [Bering Land Bridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beringia "Beringia"). Other islands, such as [Great Britain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Britain "Great Britain"), were joined to the mainlands of their continents. At that time, there were just three discrete landmasses in the world: [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa")- [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia")- [America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas"), [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica"), and [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia")- [New Guinea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Guinea "New Guinea") ( [Sahul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sahul "Sahul")).

In the English-speaking countries, geographers often use the term [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania") to denote a geographical region which includes most of the [island countries and territories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_island_countries "List of island countries") in the [Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean"), as well as the [continent of Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)").[\[48\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-worldatlas-48) Outside of the [English-speaking world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English-speaking_world "English-speaking world"), Oceania is generally considered a continent, while [Mainland Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia") is regarded as its continental [landmass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landmass "Landmass").[\[49\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-49)[\[50\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-50)[\[51\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-51)[\[52\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-52)

## Other divisions

### Supercontinents

Main article: [Supercontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercontinent "Supercontinent")

Further information: [Geological history of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geological_history_of_Earth "Geological history of Earth")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Pangaea_200Ma.jpg/500px-Pangaea_200Ma.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pangaea_200Ma.jpg) Reconstruction of the supercontinent [Pangaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea") approximately 200 million years ago

Apart from the current continents, the scope and meaning of the term _continent_ includes past geological ones. [Supercontinents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercontinent "Supercontinent"), largely in evidence earlier in the geological record, are landmasses that comprise most of the world's [cratons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craton "Craton") or continental cores.[\[64\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Evans_2013-65) These have included [Vaalbara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaalbara "Vaalbara"), [Kenorland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenorland "Kenorland"), [Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_(supercontinent) "Columbia (supercontinent)"), [Rodinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodinia "Rodinia"), [Pannotia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pannotia "Pannotia"), and [Pangaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea"). Over time, these supercontinents broke apart into large landmasses which formed the present continents.

### Subcontinents

Further information: [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent") and [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/India_78.40398E_20.74980N.jpg/250px-India_78.40398E_20.74980N.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:India_78.40398E_20.74980N.jpg) The [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent")

Certain parts of continents are recognized as subcontinents, especially the large peninsulas separated from the main continental landmass by geographical features. The most widely recognized example is the [Indian subcontinent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent").[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Baldwin-66) The [Arabian Peninsula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula"), [Southern Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Africa "Southern Africa"), the [Southern Cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Cone "Southern Cone") of South America, and [Alaska](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaska "Alaska") in North America might be considered further examples.[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Baldwin-66)[\[66\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-67)

In many of these cases, the "subcontinents" concerned are on different [tectonic plates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_tectonics "Plate tectonics") from the rest of the continent, providing a geological justification for the terminology.[\[67\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Molnar2015-68) [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"), generally considered the [world's largest island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area "List of islands by area") on the northeastern periphery of the [North American Plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_Plate "North American Plate"), is sometimes referred to as a subcontinent.[\[68\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-69)[\[69\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FarmerCook2013-70) This is a significant departure from the more conventional view of a subcontinent as comprising a very large peninsula on the fringe of a continent.[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Baldwin-66)

Where the Americas are viewed as a single continent (America), it is divided into two subcontinents ( [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America") and [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"))[\[70\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Gallay2015-71)[\[71\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-InnesHaron2000-72)[\[72\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Vivares2014-73) or three ( [Central America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_America "Central America") being the third).[\[73\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Leonard2005-74)[\[74\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-75) When Eurasia is regarded as a single continent, Asia and Europe are treated as subcontinents.[\[65\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Baldwin-66)

### Submerged continents

Main article: [Submerged continent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submerged_continent "Submerged continent")

Further information: [List of lost lands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_lost_lands "List of lost lands")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Zealandia%2C_topographic_map.jpg/250px-Zealandia%2C_topographic_map.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Zealandia,_topographic_map.jpg) [Zealandia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zealandia "Zealandia"), the largest submerged landmass or continent

Some areas of [continental crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_crust "Continental crust") are largely covered by the ocean and may be considered [submerged continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submerged_continent "Submerged continent"). Notable examples are [Zealandia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zealandia "Zealandia"), emerging from the ocean primarily in [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand") and [New Caledonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Caledonia "New Caledonia"),[\[75\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-76)\[ _[non-tertiary source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources "Wikipedia:No original research")_\] and the almost completely submerged [Kerguelen Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerguelen_Plateau "Kerguelen Plateau") in the southern [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean").[\[76\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-77)

### Microcontinents

Further information: [Continental fragment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment")

See also: [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar")

Some islands lie on sections of continental crust that have rifted and drifted apart from a main continental landmass. While not considered continents because of their relatively small size, they may be considered [microcontinents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment"). Madagascar, the largest example, is usually considered an island of Africa, but its divergent evolution has caused it to be referred to as "the eighth continent" from a biological perspective.[\[77\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-78)

### Geological continents

See also: [Zealandia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zealandia "Zealandia")

[Geologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologist "Geologist") use four key attributes to define a continent:[\[78\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Mortimer_et_al_2017-79)

1. Elevation – The [landmass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landmass "Landmass"), whether dry or submerged beneath the ocean, should be elevated above the surrounding [ocean crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_crust "Ocean crust").
2. Geology – The landmass should contain different types of rock: [igneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igneous_rock "Igneous rock"), [metamorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphic_rock "Metamorphic rock"), and [sedimentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary_rock "Sedimentary rock").
3. Crustal structure – The landmass should consist of the [continental crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_crust "Continental crust"), which is thicker and has a lower [seismic velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seismic_wave "Seismic wave") than the oceanic crust.
4. Limits and area – The landmass should have clearly defined boundaries and an area of more than one million square kilometres.[\[a\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-80)

With the addition of [Zealandia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zealandia "Zealandia") in 2017, Earth currently has seven recognized geological continents:

- Africa
- Antarctica
- [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)")
- [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia")
- North America
- South America
- Zealandia[\[79\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-81)

Due to a seeming lack of Precambrian [cratonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craton "Craton") rocks, Zealandia's status as a geological continent has been disputed by some geologists.[\[80\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-82) However, a study conducted in 2021 found that part of the [submerged continent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submerged_continent "Submerged continent") is indeed [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian"), twice as old as geologists had previously thought, which is further evidence that supports the idea of Zealandia being a geological continent.[\[81\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-83)[\[82\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-84)

All seven geological continents are spatially isolated by geologic features.[\[83\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-85)

## History of the concept

### Early concepts of the Old World continents

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Strabo.jpg/250px-Strabo.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Strabo.jpg) The Ancient Greek geographer [Strabo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strabo "Strabo") holding a globe showing _Europa_ and _Asia_

The term "continent" translates the Greek word ἤπειρος, meaning "landmass, terra firma", the proper name of [Epirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epirus "Epirus") and later especially used for Asia (i.e. [Asia Minor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia")).[\[84\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-86)

The first distinction between continents was made by [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") mariners who gave the names Europe and Asia to the lands on either side of the waterways of the [Aegean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea"), the [Dardanelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dardanelles "Dardanelles") strait, the [Sea of Marmara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Marmara "Sea of Marmara"), the [Bosporus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosporus "Bosporus") strait and the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea").[\[85\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Toynbee-87) The names were first applied just to lands near the coast and only later extended to include the [hinterlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinterland "Hinterland").[\[86\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-88)[\[87\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Pocock2002-89) But the division was only carried through to the end of navigable waterways and "... beyond that point the Hellenic geographers never succeeded in laying their finger on any inland feature in the physical landscape that could offer any convincing line for partitioning an indivisible Eurasia ..."[\[85\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Toynbee-87)

Ancient Greek thinkers subsequently debated whether Africa (then called _Libya_) should be considered part of Asia or a third part of the world. Division into three parts eventually came to predominate.[\[88\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-90) From the Greek viewpoint, the Aegean Sea was the center of the world; Asia lay to the east, Europe to the north and west, and Africa to the south.[\[89\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199721%E2%80%9322-91) The boundaries between the continents were not fixed. Early on, the Europe–Asia boundary was taken to run from the Black Sea along the [Rioni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rioni "Rioni") River (known then as the _Phasis_) in [Georgia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgia_(country) "Georgia (country)"). Later it was viewed as running from the Black Sea through [Kerch Strait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerch_Strait "Kerch Strait"), the [Sea of Azov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Azov "Sea of Azov") and along the [Don](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_(river) "Don (river)") River (known then as the _Tanais_) in [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia").[\[90\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-92) The boundary between Asia and Africa was generally taken to be the [Nile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nile "Nile") River. [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus")[\[91\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-93) in the 5th century BCE objected to the whole of [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") being split between Asia and Africa ("Libya") and took the boundary to lie along the western border of Egypt, regarding Egypt as part of Asia.[\[92\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-94)[\[93\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-95)[\[94\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-96)[\[95\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-97) He also questioned the division into three of what is really a single landmass,[\[96\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-98) a debate that continues nearly two and a half millennia later. Herodotus believed Europe to be larger (at least in width) than the other two continents:

> I wonder, then, at those who have mapped out and divided the world into Libya, Asia, and Europe; for the difference between them is great, seeing that in length Europe stretches along both the others together, and it appears to me to be wider beyond all comparison.[\[97\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-99)

[Eratosthenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eratosthenes "Eratosthenes"), in the 3rd century BCE, noted that some geographers divided the continents by rivers (the Nile and the Don), thus considering them "islands". Others divided the continents by [isthmuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isthmus "Isthmus"), calling the continents "peninsulas". These latter geographers set the border between Europe and Asia at the isthmus between the Black Sea and the [Caspian Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea"), and the border between Asia and Africa at the isthmus between the [Red Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") and the mouth of [Lake Bardawil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Bardawil "Lake Bardawil") on the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea").[\[98\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-100)

The Roman author [Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder"), writing in the 1st century CE, stated that "The whole globe is divided into three parts, Europe, Asia, and Africa", adding:

> I shall first then speak of Europe, the foster-mother of that people which has conquered all other nations, and itself by far the most beauteous portion of the earth. Indeed, many persons have, not without reason, considered it, not as a third part only of the earth, but as equal to all the rest, looking upon the whole of our globe as divided into two parts only, by a line drawn from the river Tanais to the Straits of Gades.[\[99\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-101)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/T_and_O_map_Guntherus_Ziner_1472.jpg/250px-T_and_O_map_Guntherus_Ziner_1472.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:T_and_O_map_Guntherus_Ziner_1472.jpg) Medieval [T and O map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_and_O_map "T and O map") showing the three continents as domains of the sons of [Noah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noah "Noah")—Asia to Sem ( [Shem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shem "Shem")), Europe to Iafeth ( [Japheth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japheth "Japheth")), and Africa to Cham ( [Ham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ham,_son_of_Noah "Ham, son of Noah")).

Following the fall of the [Western Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire "Western Roman Empire"), the [culture that developed in its place](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_culture "Western culture"), linked to Latin and the Catholic church, began to associate itself with the concept of Europe.[\[87\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Pocock2002-89) Through the Roman period and the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), a few writers took the [Isthmus of Suez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isthmus_of_Suez "Isthmus of Suez") as the boundary between Asia and Africa, but most writers continued to consider it the Nile or the western border of Egypt (Gibbon).\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\] In the Middle Ages, the world was usually portrayed on [T and O maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_and_O_map "T and O map"), with the T representing the waters dividing the three continents. By the middle of the 18th century, "the fashion of dividing Asia and Africa at the Nile, or at the [Great Catabathmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Catabathmus "Great Catabathmus") \[the boundary between Egypt and [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libya "Libya")\] farther west, had even then scarcely passed away".[\[100\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-102)

### European arrival in the Americas

[Christopher Columbus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Columbus "Christopher Columbus") sailed across the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") to the [Caribbean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caribbean "Caribbean") in 1492, sparking a period of European exploration of the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas"). But despite four voyages to the Americas, Columbus never believed he had reached a new continent—he always thought it was part of Asia.\[ _[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_\]

In 1501, [Amerigo Vespucci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci "Amerigo Vespucci") and [Gonçalo Coelho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gon%C3%A7alo_Coelho "Gonçalo Coelho") attempted to sail around what they considered the southern end of the Asian mainland into the [Indian Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean"), passing through [Fernando de Noronha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha "Fernando de Noronha"). After reaching the coast of [Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil"), they sailed along the coast of [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America") much farther south than Asia was known to extend, confirming that this was a land of continental proportions.[\[101\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-OGorman-103) On return to Europe, an account of the voyage, called _Mundus Novus_ ("New World"), was published under Vespucci's name in 1502 or 1503,[\[102\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-formisano1-104) although it seems that it had additions or alterations by another writer.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Zerubavel1-105) Regardless of who penned the words, _Mundus Novus_ credited Vespucci with saying, "I have discovered a continent in those southern regions that is inhabited by more numerous people and animals than our Europe, or Asia or Africa",[\[104\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-formisano2-106) the first known explicit identification of part of the Americas as a continent like the other three.

Within a few years, the name "New World" began appearing as a name for South America on world maps, such as the Oliveriana (Pesaro) map of around 1504–1505. Maps of this time, though, still showed [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America") connected to Asia and showed South America as a separate land.[\[103\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Zerubavel1-105)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Waldseemuller_map_2.jpg/500px-Waldseemuller_map_2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Waldseemuller_map_2.jpg) _Universalis Cosmographia_, Waldseemüller's 1507 world map—the first to show the Americas separate from Asia

In 1507 [Martin Waldseemüller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Waldseem%C3%BCller "Martin Waldseemüller") published a world map, _[Universalis Cosmographia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waldseem%C3%BCller_map "Waldseemüller map")_, which was the first to show North and South America as separate from Asia and surrounded by water. A small inset map above the main map explicitly showed for the first time the Americas being east of Asia and separated from Asia by an ocean, as opposed to just placing the Americas on the left end of the map and Asia on the right end. In the accompanying book _[Cosmographiae Introductio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmographiae_Introductio "Cosmographiae Introductio")_, Waldseemüller noted that the earth is divided into four parts, Europe, Asia, Africa, and the fourth part, which he named "America" after Amerigo Vespucci's first name.[\[105\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Zerubavel2-107) On the map, the word "America" was placed on part of South America.

### Beyond four continents

The Sanskrit text [Rig Veda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rig_Veda "Rig Veda") often dated 1500 BCE[\[note 1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-dating-114) has the earliest mention of seven continents in the Earth, the text claims that the Earth has seven continents and [Lord Vishnu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Vishnu "Lord Vishnu") Measured the entire universe from his first foot from the land of Earth which has 7 continents.[\[112\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-:9-115)

>
> trīṇi padā vi cakrame viṣṇurghopā **adābhyaḥ** \|
>

In regard to the above-quoted verses, it is commonly accepted that there are Seven Continents or 'regions of the earth'. A. Glucklich adds that 'In the [Matsya Purana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matsya_Purana "Matsya Purana"), for instance, there is a seven-part map of the world ... \[it has\] one centre, where an immense mountain – [Mount Meru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Meru "Mount Meru") (or Maha Meru, Great Meru) – stands ... The continents encircle the mountain in seven concentric circles ... It seems clear that the Himalayas were the approximate location of Mt. Meru and the text is clear that the earth has seven continents.[\[112\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-:9-115)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Thevenot_-_Hollandia_Nova_detecta_1644.png/330px-Thevenot_-_Hollandia_Nova_detecta_1644.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Thevenot_-_Hollandia_Nova_detecta_1644.png) _Hollandia Nova_, 1659 map prepared by [Joan Blaeu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_Blaeu "Joan Blaeu") based on voyages by [Abel Tasman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel_Tasman "Abel Tasman") and [Willem Jansz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willem_Jansz "Willem Jansz"), this image shows a French edition of 1663

From the late 18th century, some geographers started to regard North America and South America as two parts of the world, making five parts in total. Overall though, the fourfold division prevailed well into the 19th century.[\[115\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-lewis30-118)

Europeans discovered [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)") in 1606, but for some time it was taken as part of Asia. By the late 18th century, some geographers considered it a continent in its own right, making it the sixth (or fifth for those still taking America as a single continent).[\[115\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-lewis30-118) In 1813, [Samuel Butler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(schoolmaster) "Samuel Butler (schoolmaster)") wrote of Australia as " [New Holland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Holland_(Australia) "New Holland (Australia)"), an immense island, which some geographers dignify with the appellation of another continent" and the _[Oxford English Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary "Oxford English Dictionary")_ was just as equivocal some decades later.[\[116\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-119) It was in the 1950s that the concept of Oceania as a "great division" of the world was replaced by the concept of Australia as a continent.[\[117\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-lewis32b-120)

[Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica") was sighted in 1820 during the [First Russian Antarctic Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Russian_Antarctic_Expedition "First Russian Antarctic Expedition") and described as a continent by [Charles Wilkes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Wilkes "Charles Wilkes") on the [United States Exploring Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Exploring_Expedition "United States Exploring Expedition") in 1838, the last continent identified, although a great "Antarctic" ( [antipodean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodes "Antipodes")) landmass had been [anticipated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terra_Australis "Terra Australis") for millennia. An 1849 atlas labelled Antarctica as a continent but few atlases did so until after [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II").[\[118\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-121)

Over time, the western concept of dividing the world into continents spread globally, replacing conceptions in other areas of the world. The idea of continents continued to become imbued with cultural and political meaning. In the 19th century during the [Meiji period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiji_period "Meiji period"), Japanese leaders began to self-identify with the concept of being Asian, and renew relations with other "Asian" countries while conceiving of the idea of Asian solidarity against western countries. This conception of an Asian identity, as well as the idea of Asian solidarity, was later taken up by others in the region, such as Republican China and Vietnam.[\[119\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-122)

From the mid-19th century, atlases published in the United States more commonly treated North and South America as separate continents, while atlases published in Europe usually considered them one continent. However, it was still not uncommon for American atlases to treat them as one continent up until World War II.[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-lewis32-123) From the 1950s, most U.S. geographers divided the Americas into two continents.[\[120\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-lewis32-123) With the addition of Antarctica, this made the seven-continent model. However, this division of the Americas did not appeal to [Latin Americans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America"), who saw their region spanning an _América_ as a single landmass, and there the conception of six continents remains popular.

Some geographers regard Europe and Asia together as a single continent, dubbed _[Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia")_.[\[121\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-124) In this model, the world is divided into six continents, with North America and South America considered separate continents.

## Geology

Further information: [Continental crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_crust "Continental crust") and [Plate tectonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_tectonics "Plate tectonics")

Geologists use the term _continent_ in a different manner from geographers. In geology, a continent is defined by continental crust, which is a platform of [metamorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphic_rock "Metamorphic rock") and [igneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Igneous_rock "Igneous rock") rocks, largely of [granitic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granite "Granite") composition. Continental crust is less dense and much thicker than oceanic crust, which causes it to "float" higher than oceanic crust on the dense underlying [mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle "Earth's mantle"). This explains why the continents form high platforms surrounded by deep ocean basins.[\[122\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-125)[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Glossary2005-3)

Some geologists restrict the term _continent_ to portions of the crust built around stable regions called _[cratons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craton "Craton")_. Cratons have largely been unaffected by mountain-building events ( [orogenies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orogeny "Orogeny")) since the [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian"). A craton typically consists of a _[continental shield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shield_(geology) "Shield (geology)")_ surrounded by a _[continental platform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platform_(geology) "Platform (geology)")_. The shield is a region where ancient crystalline [basement rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basement_(geology) "Basement (geology)") (typically 1.5 to 3.8 billion years old) is widely exposed at the surface. The platform surrounding the shield is also composed of ancient basement rock, but with a cover of younger [sedimentary rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary_rock "Sedimentary rock").[\[123\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevin201083-126) The continents are accretionary crustal "rafts" that, unlike the denser basaltic crust of the ocean basins, are not subjected to destruction through the plate tectonic process of subduction. This accounts for the great age of the rocks comprising the continental cratons.[\[124\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevin2010194-127)

The margins of geologic continents are either [active](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergent_boundary "Convergent boundary") or [passive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_margin "Passive margin"). An active margin is characterised by mountain building, either through a continent-on continent collision or a subduction zone. Continents grow by [accreting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(geology) "Accretion (geology)") lighter volcanic island chains and microcontinents along these active margins, forming [orogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orogenic_belt "Orogenic belt"). At a passive margin, the continental crust is stretched thin by extension to form a [continental shelf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"), which tapers off with a gradual slope covered in sediment, connecting it directly to the oceanic crust beyond. Most passive margins eventually transition into active margins: where the oceanic plate becomes too heavy due to cooling, it disconnects from the continental crust, and starts subducting below it, forming a new subduction zone.[\[125\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-128)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Tectonic_plates_%282022%29.svg/500px-Tectonic_plates_%282022%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tectonic_plates_(2022).svg) Sixteen principal tectonic plates of the continents and the floor of the oceans

There are many microcontinents, or [continental fragments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment"), that are built of continental crust but do not contain a craton. Some of these are fragments of [Gondwana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondwana "Gondwana") or other ancient cratonic continents: [Zealandia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zealandia_(continent) "Zealandia (continent)"),[\[78\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Mortimer_et_al_2017-79) which includes [New Zealand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_New_Zealand "Geography of New Zealand") and [New Caledonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_New_Caledonia "Geography of New Caledonia"); [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Madagascar "Geography of Madagascar"); the northern [Mascarene Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau "Mascarene Plateau"), which includes the [Seychelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seychelles "Seychelles"). Other islands, such as several in the [Caribbean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caribbean_Sea "Caribbean Sea"), are composed largely of granitic rock as well, but all continents contain both granitic and basaltic crust, and there is no clear boundary as to which islands would be considered microcontinents under such a definition. The [Kerguelen Plateau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerguelen_Plateau "Kerguelen Plateau"), for example, is largely volcanic, but is associated with the breakup of Gondwanaland and is considered a microcontinent,[\[126\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-129)[\[127\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-130) whereas volcanic [Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Iceland "Geography of Iceland") and [Hawaii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands "Hawaiian Islands") are not. The [British Isles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Isles "British Isles"), [Sri Lanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sri_Lanka "Sri Lanka"), [Borneo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borneo "Borneo"), and [Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newfoundland_(island) "Newfoundland (island)") were on the margins of the [Laurasian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laurasia "Laurasia") continent—only separated from the main continental landmass by inland seas flooding its margins.

The movement of plates has caused the continual formation and breakup of continents, and occasionally supercontinents, in a process called the [Wilson Cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilson_Cycle "Wilson Cycle"). The supercontinent [Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia_(supercontinent) "Columbia (supercontinent)") or Nuna formed during a period of 2.0–1.8 billion years ago and broke up about 1.5–1.3 billion years ago.[\[128\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Zhao1-131)[\[129\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-Zhao2-132) The supercontinent [Rodinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodinia "Rodinia") is thought to have formed about 1 billion years ago and to have embodied most or all of Earth's continents, and broken up into eight continents around 600 million years ago. The eight continents later reassembled into another supercontinent called [Pangaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea"); Pangaea broke up into Laurasia (which became North America and Eurasia) and [Gondwana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gondwana "Gondwana") (which became the remaining continents).[\[130\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-133)

## Criticism

Some academics, such as the historical geographer [Martin W. Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_W._Lewis "Martin W. Lewis"), argue that the systems we understand today are more rooted in social, political, and cultural history than in geological fact, a view particularly outlined in his book _The Myth of Continents: A Critique of Metageography_.[\[131\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent#cite_note-134)

### Which continent does Russia belong to?

Russia is part of both Europe and Asia. In the 7 continent model in fact, it is not always clear where to place Russia. In the map pictured above Russia is divided into two parts (European Russia and the "Asian part" of the Russian Federation) along the Ural Mountains line, from the source of the Ural River down to the Greater Caucasus from the Caspian Sea to the Black Sea (following the modern definition of Europe as provided by the National Geographic Society). However, in the list of continents, we had to place Russia in one continent or the other, so we placed it in Europe, following the United Nations classification.

About 75% of the Russian population lives in the European continent. On the other hand, 75% of Russian territory is located in Asia.

### Which continent does Hawaii belong to?

None. Hawaii is politically part of North America, but geographically it is not part of any continent.
