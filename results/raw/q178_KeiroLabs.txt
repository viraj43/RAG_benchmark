Title: History of Linux - Wikipedia
Linux began in 1991 as a personal project by Finnish student Linus Torvalds to create a new free operating system kernel. The resulting Linux kernel has been marked by constant growth throughout its history. Since the initial release of its source code in 1991, it has grown from a small number of C files under a license prohibiting commercial distribution to the 4.15 version in 2018 with more than 23.3 million lines of source code, not counting comments, under the GNU General Public License v2 with a syscall exception meaning anything that uses the kernel via system calls are not subject to the GNU GPL.

## The Birth of Linux (1991-1994)
The story of Linux began with the concept of Unix, a powerful and versatile operating system. However, Unix was expensive and proprietary, limiting its accessibility. In 1984, a simpler Unix-like system called Minix emerged, sparking the interest of a young Finnish student named Linus Torvalds.
August 25th, 1991: Linus Torvalds made a now-famous post to the MINIX newsgroup announcing his work on a free operating system for 386(486) AT clones. He stated it was “just a hobby, won’t be big and professional like gnu.” Little did he know, this “hobby” would revolutionize the tech world.
September 17th, 1991: Linux version 0.01 was released, containing the initial code and basic functionality. The first usable version of Linux, version 0.02, came out in October 1991, consisting of approximately 10,239 lines of code.


## 1992: The Year Linux Became Open Source
The early development of the Linux kernel saw its distribution restricted by non-commercial licenses. However, a crucial decision was made to the Linux kernel in 1992 with the release of version 0.12. It was relicensed under the GNU General Public License (GPL), marking a significant shift in Linux evolution.
This switch from a non-commercial license to the GPL made Linux open source, allowing anyone to freely use, modify, and distribute the kernel. Linus Torvalds himself considers this decision to be the best he ever made.


## The Rise of Linux
The fledgling Linux kernel found a perfect partner in the GNU Project: a group developing a free and open-source operating system. The GNU Project provided essential components like utilities and Linux tools that, when combined with the Linux kernel, formed the foundation for a complete GNU/Linux operating system.
As Linux grew, the need for complete operating systems built around it also grew. This critical need led to the creation of Linux distributions, or “distros.”
1992 saw the release of MCC Interim Linux, one of the first distributions. Others soon followed:
Slackware (1993)
Debian (1993)
Red Hat Linux (1994)
SUSE Linux (1994)
These distributions packaged the Linux kernel with other free and open-source software, creating complete operating systems and propelling the Linux evolution forward.


## 1994: Linux 1.0.0 Arrived
On March 14th, Linux 1.0.0 was released, consisting of 176,250 lines of code and providing support for single-processor Intel 80386 (i386) systems.


## Tux: Symbol of Linux
In 1996, Linus Torvalds announced that Linux would have a penguin mascot, inspired by an incident where he was bitten by a little penguin during a visit to the National Zoo & Aquarium in Canberra, Australia. Larry Ewing created the original draft based on this idea. The name Tux, suggested by James Hughes, is a play on “Torvalds’ UniX” and “tuxedo,” reflecting the penguin’s appearance. Since its creation, Tux has remained the symbol of Linux for over two decades.

## Linux's Impact on Other Operating Systems
The emergence of Linux has had a huge impact on the development and design of other operating systems. The open-source philosophy and community-driven development opened new paths for software distribution and use. The flexibility and customizability inspired major companies to better adapt to user needs; for example, Windows learned from easier usability and direct feedback mechanisms provided by the developer community.
Additionally, the open-source nature of the Linux kernel enabled faster technological innovation, shaping standards for writing and distributing software. Principles and elements taken from Linux are found in the development of macOS and other UNIX-based systems, shaping modern computing.


## The Birth of Linux
The birth of the Linux operating system dates back to 1991, when Linus Torvalds, a young Finnish programmer, announced his project to create a free, open-source operating system. Inspired by UNIX systems, Torvalds aimed to create an easy-to-use alternative accessible to everyone. The first Linux kernel released by Torvalds had basic functionality, but soon, with the support of the community, programmers and enthusiasts began expanding its capabilities.
Early development made Linux quickly popular, as users could tailor it to their own needs. Its transparency and flexibility offered an attractive alternative to mainstream commercial operating systems. Thanks to Torvalds' vision and community support, Linux became not just an operating system, but a culture.


## The Legacy and Future of Linux
The development of Linux operating systems over the past decades has been impressive, as solutions once used by a small circle have become globally recognized systems. The open-source philosophy enabled the diversity of distributions, so every user can find the version that suits them best. Linux has become an integral part of everyday life, playing a significant role not only in technology but also in community collaboration.


## Why Use Linux
Using Linux offers many advantages worth considering. First, Linux is open-source, so users can freely modify, distribute, and develop the system. This community-based development ensures Linux is always up-to-date and flexible. Furthermore, Linux is generally more secure than many other operating systems, as it is less often targeted by malware.
Its stability and low system requirements make it ideal for older computers. The wide variety of Linux distributions allows everyone to find the version that best fits their needs, so both beginners and experienced developers can use it. Finally, the Linux community is highly supportive, so help and advice are always easy to find.


## Manjaro: The Friendly Face of Arch Linux
Manjaro is an easy-to-use Linux distribution based on Arch Linux, aiming to offer friendlier and more accessible solutions. Arch Linux is traditionally popular among experienced users due to its extreme flexibility and customizability. In contrast, Manjaro's graphical installer and pre-configured software environment allow even beginner Linux users to smoothly enjoy the Arch world.
Manjaro's community support is outstanding, with active forums and documentation helping users solve problems. Thanks to continuous updates, the latest software and security fixes are always easily accessible. Manjaro is not just a distribution, but a friendly community supporting all users.


## Linux Mint: The Expert in Simplification
Linux Mint aims to provide users with a seamless and intuitive experience. Designed especially for new users, Mint's interface is familiar, resembling the popular Windows operating system. This design approach helps reduce frustration when switching.
Linux Mint's simple installation process, well-structured menu, and pre-installed applications like music player, browser, and text editor all contribute to making it easy for users to dive into the world of Linux. The Mint community provides ongoing support, so users can always get help when needed.


## Arch Linux: For Those Seeking Flexibility
Arch Linux is one of the most exciting Linux distributions, emphasizing flexibility and customizability. Its philosophy, the Arch Way, highlights minimal installation and maximizing user choices. Users have full control over which software and settings they choose, building a unique, personalized system.
Arch Linux is ideal for advanced users who want to deeply understand Linux and take advantage of the distribution's possibilities. Through the AUR (Arch User Repository), users access a vast community-maintained software collection, allowing them to install and customize even more programs. The system's continuous updates ensure the latest developments are always available.
