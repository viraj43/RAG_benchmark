# Jobs and Wozniak Found Apple Computer

Apple Computer was founded on April 1, 1976, by Steve Jobs and Stephen Wozniak, who initially aimed to assemble computers for friends but quickly recognized the potential of their innovation to transform the personal computer market. Operating from Jobs's parents' garage, they created the Apple I, a pioneering single-board computer, which gained popularity among hobbyists. This success led to the development of the Apple II, one of the first fully assembled personal computers, featuring user-friendly capabilities that allowed broader access to technology. As demand surged, they secured additional funding and strategic guidance, leading to significant growth and a successful initial public offering in 1980.

Apple's approach set it apart by integrating hardware and software, paving the way for innovations in user interface design, including the iconic Macintosh, known for its graphical user interface. The founding duo's vision focused on making computing accessible and efficient, challenging the notion that computers were overly complex and expensive. Over the years, Apple evolved from its humble beginnings to become a major player in the tech industry, known for its innovative products that reshaped the way people interact with technology. Their legacy continues to influence personal computing and software development today.

## Jobs and Wozniak Found Apple Computer

**Date** April 1, 1976

*With their founding of Apple Computer in 1976, Steven Jobs and Stephen Wozniak established a company that would revolutionize personal computing.*

**Locale** Santa Clara Valley, California

## Key Figures

* Steve Jobs (b. 1955), cofounder of Apple Computer
* Stephen Wozniak (b. 1950), cofounder of Apple Computer
* Mike Markkula (b. 1942), former Intel Corporation marketing manager appointed as Apple’s first chairman in May, 1977
* Michael Scott (b. 1943), first president of Apple Computer
* John Sculley (b. 1939), business executive who joined Apple Computer in 1983 and later became chief executive officer

## Summary of Event

Apple Computer was officially founded on April 1, 1976, by twenty-one-year-old Steven Jobs and twenty-six-year-old Stephen Wozniak. Their initial idea was that they would assemble [computers](/research-starters/computer-science/computers-and-energy-use) for their friends. They did not realize the potential their ideas had to revolutionize the personal computer industry. Ultimately, Jobs and Wozniak’s goal became making computer technology widely accessible to the mass population. These entrepreneurs recognized that most consumers at that time saw computers as too expensive and too complex to use. Jobs envisioned the firm offering products that would contribute to human efficiency as much as had the electric typewriter, the calculator, and the photocopy machine.

Jobs and Wozniak, both graduates of Homestead High School in Santa Clara, California, began collaborating in 1976 at the Homebrew Computer Club, an organization of young computer enthusiasts located in Palo Alto, California. Wozniak was a superior product engineer and designer, and Jobs had a grasp on the demands of the marketplace. They designed their first machine in Jobs’s bedroom and used $1,300 from the sale of Jobs’s Volkswagen and Wozniak’s scientific calculator to assemble their first working model in Jobs’s parents’ garage. They chose Apple as the name for their venture because they thought it conveyed a nonthreatening yet high-technology image. The name also recalled Jobs’s fond memories of time he had spent on an Oregon farm. Jobs and Wozniak’s original plan was to limit production to circuit boards. After Jobs’s first sales call yielded an order for fifty units, they rethought their strategy and decided to offer fully assembled microcomputers.

Their first model, the Apple I, was introduced and sold without a monitor, keyboard, or casing, at a price of $666. It was the first single-board computer with onboard read-only memory (ROM), which told the machine how to load other programs from an external source, and with a built-in video interface. Orders for their “personal computer,” mainly from hobbyists, soon reached six hundred units.

Jobs and Wozniak now faced the problem of improving the original model without sacrificing its key selling features, its simplicity and compactness. Their efforts resulted in the introduction in 1977 of the Apple II, the first fully assembled, programmable microcomputer that did not require users to know how to solder, wire, or program. The Apple II had a keyboard, a color monitor, and expansion capabilities for peripheral devices. These features gave the new model considerable flexibility and inspired numerous independent firms to develop third-party add-on devices and software programs. The resulting software library soon included more than ten thousand programs ranging from games to sophisticated business applications.

Demand soon outstripped the founders’ ability to produce the machine. They turned to [Mike Markkula](/research-starters/biography/mike-markkula), who had been a marketing manager at [Intel Corporation](/research-starters/computer-science/intel), a fast-growing manufacturer of integrated circuits. Markkula contributed at least $91,000 to the company (by some estimates, as much as $250,000), secured a line of credit with Bank of America, and raised more than $500,000 from venture capitalists; he was named chairman of the company in May, 1977. One month later, [Michael Scott](/research-starters/history/michael-scott) was placed in the position of president of the firm.

Markkula wrote Apple’s first business plan. Its objectives included capturing a market share at least twice that of the nearest competitor, realizing at least 20 percent pretax profit, and growing to $500 million in annual sales within ten years by continuing to make significant contributions to the home computer industry. In addition, the plan called for the establishment and maintenance of a corporate culture that would be conducive to personal growth and development for the firm’s employees. The plan also called for an “easy exit” for its founders within five years, should they wish to disassociate themselves from the enterprise.

The firm’s strategy called for continual marketing of peripheral products for the basic computer so as to generate sales equal to or greater than the initial computer purchase, the allocation of funds for research and development to guarantee technological leadership, and the ability to attract and retain outstanding personnel. The plan called for initially targeting the hobbyist market as a stepping-stone to wider distribution. The company also sought to refine [manufacturing processes](/research-starters/business-and-management/manufacturing-processes) to reduce costs. Apple computers were to be designed and marketed as more economical than a dedicated system in specific applications, even though a particular user might not use all the features of the computer.

By 1980, with the help of Regis McKenna, a well-respected [public relations](/research-starters/power-and-energy/public-relations-energy-industry) expert in Silicon Valley, the California center for computer technology, Apple had sold more than 130,000 units. Revenues grew from less than $8 million in 1978 to $117 million. The company went public in 1980 with one of the largest stock offerings in history, underwritten in cooperation with Morgan Stanley, Inc. The first day of trading took Apple stock from the underwriters’ price of $22 per share to $29, bringing the market value of Apple to $1.2 billion.

The Apple II Plus model did not fare as well as its predecessors. The Apple III, aimed at the professional market, was hampered by production problems that resulted in a recall of some units. These problems and the attention required to solve them offered International Business Machines (IBM) the opportunity to introduce its long-awaited entry in the personal computer market.

The problems with the Apple II Plus and Apple III models were at least partially responsible for the firm’s first major managerial shake-up. Apple president Scott fired forty employees and was then himself dismissed by Markkula, who became president. Jobs assumed Markkula’s former position as chairman. Meanwhile, Wozniak was injured in 1981 and took a leave of absence from the firm. After his recovery, he founded an organization dedicated to fostering a spirit of cooperation among people. He expressed an interest in returning to Apple in a troubleshooting capacity, with a mission to restore the spirit that led Apple to its early successes.

In January, 1983, Jobs announced the introduction of the Apple IIe, a successor to the Apple II Plus. He simultaneously announced the introduction of Lisa, the first of a generation of computers aimed at the business market. Lisa incorporated many of the technological advances made up to that time and added several unique features, including the first handheld “mouse” input device. This mechanism allowed the user to execute commands by invoking a series of user-friendly “menus” by moving the mouse and clicking buttons rather than by typing commands. This innovation also allowed the user to produce high-quality graphics that previously would have required a complex series of keystrokes. Computer novices could now master the use of the computer in a matter of minutes rather than weeks, as had been the case in the past.

As Apple Computer evolved, its approach to management changed dramatically. Realizing that selling computers had become an increasingly complex marketing problem, Jobs sought help. Computers no longer would sell themselves on the basis of their technological innovations. In April, 1983, perceiving that marketing expertise was lacking within the firm, Jobs recruited John Sculley from [PepsiCo](/research-starters/business-and-management/pepsico). The move was controversial, given that Sculley had developed his reputation selling soft drinks in a mature market, an environment very unlike the growth industry of personal computers. Some foresaw a conflict of corporate cultures between the freewheeling style of Silicon Valley and the more traditional style that Sculley embodied. Although an outsider, Sculley brought marketing skills to Apple that had been missing.

In 1984, Apple introduced the Macintosh. This model, dubbed the computer “for the rest of us,” incorporated a [graphical user interface](/research-starters/computer-science/graphical-user-interface) inspired by Xerox’s Alto Computer. Macintosh was developed for the business (focusing on productivity and desktop publishing) and education markets. Its compact design and ease of use caught the attention of the market, although the original models were criticized for lacking the computing power required for some business applications. After a series of modifications and upgrades, the Macintosh gained widespread acceptance.

In 1985, after a series of tumultuous conflicts with Sculley and Apple’s board of directors, Jobs resigned his position, closing the chapter on Apple’s origin and founders. Jobs later formed his own firm, Next, Inc., dedicated to providing sophisticated workstations for the education market.

## Significance

Apple’s unique approach to personal computing altered the manner in which computer manufacturers competed. Apple pioneered the concept of integrating hardware and software to offer new possibilities. For example, integration of high-resolution displays with scalable fonts (alphanumeric characters that can be printed in a variety of sizes) and graphics capabilities allowed people to create sophisticated documents on their personal computers. Through the integration of a microphone and a CD-ROM drive with specialized software, users could now work with sound, video, and animation. Most other computer manufacturers could not integrate hardware and software as expediently because they did not manage the software development for their systems. Most, instead, licensed the same system software (MS-DOS) from one company ([Microsoft](/research-starters/computer-science/microsoft-1980s)). As a result, many of their products were indistinguishable, and companies often competed solely on the basis of price. Manufacturers of “clones” of IBM computers set off price wars in the hardware arena. Although Apple lowered prices to remain competitive, much of its sales growth came through [product innovation](/research-starters/business-and-management/product-innovation).

By 1986, with the introduction of the Mac Plus and the LaserWriter printer, Apple began to make significant inroads into the business market. The company also embarked on a program to reduce both costs and prices, allowing it to sell aggressively to large businesses, a historically weak market for Apple. Combining traditional computer applications such as word processing and spreadsheets with pioneering concepts such as desktop publishing, three-dimensional computer-assisted design, and [interactive multimedia](/research-starters/computer-science/interactive-media) tools (with text, animation, and sound) carried the Apple tradition for innovation forward.

In a surprising change in direction, given the maverick style of the firm’s beginnings, strategic partnerships became increasingly important for Apple. The company collaborated with Sony in introducing the Macintosh Powerbook notebook computer, and in 1991, in a move that shocked the computer industry, Apple and rival IBM announced a joint venture to develop new software, operating systems, and hardware that would allow easier integration of the products of the two firms.

Apple Computer rose from its modest origins in a garage to become the second-largest manufacturer of personal computers, behind IBM. The company’s Macintosh line became widely known for its user-friendliness and superior graphics capabilities. As a result of planning and the vision of Jobs and Wozniak in Apple’s early years, personal computing became accessible to the general population. Apple successfully changed how general users view personal computing, and some of the company’s significant product features were soon mimicked by competing firms. Through its continual emphasis on product innovations, Apple redefined how people process and transmit information.

## Bibliography

Levering, Robert, Michael Katz, and Milton Moskowitz. *The Computer Entrepreneurs: Who’s Making It Big and How in America’s Upstart Industry*. New York: New American Library, 1984. Provides a series of brief biographical sketches of the pioneers of the computer industry, including Apple’s founders and their contemporaries.

Linzmayer, Owen W. *Apple Confidential 2.0: The Definitive History of the World’s Most Colorful Company*. 2d ed. San Francisco: No Starch Press, 2004. History of the company by a writer who has covered Apple for computer industry magazines since 1980. Includes bibliography and index.

Moritz, Michael. *The Little Kingdom: The Private Story of Apple Computer*. New York: William Morrow, 1984. Covers the early history of the firm and provides a behind-the-scenes look into the founders and the unique corporate culture they fostered.

Price, Rob, Jill Savini, and Thom Marchionna. *So Far: The First Ten Years of a Vision*. Cupertino, Calif.: Apple Computer, 1987. Although published as a public relations vehicle for the firm, richly illustrated volume provides an interesting and entertaining historical overview of the firm’s early years.

Rose, Frank. *West of Eden: The End of Innocence at Apple Computer*. New York: Viking Press, 1989. A behind-the-scenes account of the managerial upheaval at Apple Computer that led Steve Jobs to leave the company as it sought to penetrate the business market.

Sculley, John, with John A. Byrne. *Odyssey: Pepsi to Apple—A Journey of Adventure, Ideas, and the Future*. New York: Harper & Row, 1987. Readable personal account of the firm from the standpoint of Apple’s chief executive officer and successor to Steve Jobs. Focuses on the struggle between Sculley and Jobs and its implications for the direction of the firm.

Young, Jeffrey S., and William L. Simon. *iCon: Steve Jobs—The Greatest Second Act in the History of Business*. Hoboken, N.J.: John Wiley & Sons, 2005. Presents a detailed portrait of Jobs’s career, including the creation of Apple.

# The Founding of Apple Computer, Inc.

Apple Computer, Inc. was founded on April 1, 1976, by college dropouts Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak, who brought to the new company a vision of changing the way people viewed computers. Jobs and Wozniak wanted to make computers small enough for people to have them in their homes or offices. Simply put, they wanted a computer that was user-friendly.[1](#note1)

Jobs and Wozniak started out building the Apple I in Jobs' garage and sold them without a monitor, keyboard, or casing (which they decided to add on in 1977). The Apple II revolutionized the computer industry with the introduction of the first-ever color graphics. Sales jumped from $7.8 million in 1978 to $117 million in 1980, the year Apple went public.[2](#note2)

Wozniak left Apple in 1983 due to a diminishing interest in the day-to-day running of Apple Computer. Jobs then hired PepsiCo's John Sculley to be president. However, this move backfired and after much controversy with Sculley, Jobs left in 1985 and went on to new and bigger things. He founded his own company NeXT Software and he also bought Pixar from George Lucas, which would later become a huge success in computer animation of such movies as *Toy Story, A Bug's Life, Monsters, Inc.,* and *Finding Nemo.*[3](#note3)

Through the rest of the 1980s, Apple was still doing well and in 1990 it posted its highest profits yet. This was, however, mostly due to the plans that Jobs had already set in motion before he left, most notably his deal with a tiny company by the name of Adobe, creator of the Adobe Portable Document Format (PDF). Together the two companies created the phenomenon known as desktop publishing.[4](#note4)

Back in 1985 Sculley turned down an appeal from Microsoft founder Bill Gates to license its software. This decision would later come back to haunt him because Microsoft, whose Windows operating system (OS) featured a graphical interface similar to Apple's, became their toughest competition in the late 1980s and throughout the 1990s.

Over the course of a few years, Apple's market share suffered slowly after its peak in 1990 and by 1996, experts believed the company to be doomed. It was not until 1997, when Apple was desperately in need of an operating system, that it bought out NeXT Software (Jobs' company) and the board of directors decided to ask for some help from an old friend: Steve Jobs. Jobs became an interim CEO, or iCEO as he called himself (Jobs was not officially the CEO until 2000). Jobs decided to make some changes around Apple. He forged an alliance with Microsoft to create a Mac version of its popular office software. Not long after this decision was the turning point for the company. Jobs revamped the computers and introduced the iBook (a personal laptop) followed by iPod, an mp3 player, which became market leader.

The iPhone, a touch screen cellular phone, introduced in 2007 was one of the world' most successful products and the company has released several new versions since. Not long after the announcement of iPhone and Apple TV, the company dropped "Computer" from its name to become "Apple Inc.," indicating that it is about more than just computers. Other popular products include iPad tablet and Apple Watch. Most recently Apple has expanded its services segments with its credit card (Apple Card), Apple News for news, Apple Arcade for games and the Apple TV+ for streaming original content produced by Apple.[5](#note5) Steve Jobs died October 5, 2011, but Apple continues on with his legacy with Tim Cook at the helm as the CEO. The popularity of iPhones made Apple the first company valued at one trillion dollars in 2018 and two years later it doubled that figure.[6](#note6)

Apple is one of the most popular and powerful companies in the world. Not only that, it revolutionized how we communicate and more. Starting by creating computers, Apple surged to the forefront of the most important companies in history with the creation of the iPod, the iPhone, and the iPad. All of this success stemmed from the mind of its co-founder, Steve Jobs.

On October 5, 2011, Steve Jobs passed away at the age of 56. He had just left the CEO post at Apple, the company he co-founded, and returned to for a second period as its head.12 Jobs was an entrepreneur through and through, and the story of his rise is the story of Apple as a company, along with some very interesting twists.

In this article, we'll look at the career of Steve Jobs and the company he founded, as well as some of the lessons Apple offers for potential [entrepreneurs](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/entrepreneur.asp).

The fact that Apple was the first company to surpass the $1 trillion market capitalization mark, and later the $2 trillion and $3 trillion marks, is in no small part connected to the legacy and lessons learned from [Steve Jobs](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/012815/how-steve-jobs-changed-world.asp).34

## From Blue Boxes to the Birth of Apple

Steve Jobs [got his start in business](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/042815/story-behind-apples-success.asp) with another Steve, Steve Wozniak, building the blue boxes phone phreakers used to make free calls across the nation.

The two were members of the HomeBrew Computer Club, where they quickly became enamored with kit computers and left the blue boxes behind. Founding Apple in 1976, the next product the two sold was the Apple I, which was a kit for building a PC. In order to do anything with it, the customer needed to add their own monitor and keyboard.56

With Wozniak doing most of the building and Jobs handling the sales, the two made enough money off the hobbyist market to invest in the Apple II. It was the Apple II that made the company, which was incorporated in 1977.7 Jobs and Wozniak created enough interest in their new product to attract [venture capital](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/v/venturecapital.asp). This meant they were in the big leagues and the company would continue to grow.

## The Rise and Challenges of Apple

By 1979, Apple was making over $5 million in net income solely on the strength of the Apple II.8 The Apple II wasn't state of the art, but it did allow computer enthusiasts to create and sell their own programs. Among these user-generated programs was VisiCalc, a type of proto-Excel that represented the first software with business applications.9

Although Apple did not profit directly from these programs, they did see more interest as the uses for the Apple II broadened. This model of allowing users to create their own programs and sell them would reappear in the app market of the future but with a much tighter business strategy around it.

By the time Apple [went public](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/goingpublic.asp) in 1980, the dynamic of the company was more or less set.6 Steve Jobs was the fiery visionary, with an intense and often combative management style, and Steve Wozniak was the quiet genius who made the vision work.

Apple's [board of directors](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/boardofdirectors.asp) wasn't too fond of such a power imbalance in the company, however. Jobs and the board agreed to add John Sculley to the executive team in 1983. In 1985, the board ousted Jobs in favor of Sculley.10

Steve Jobs' net worth at the time of his death in 2011.11

## Steve Jobs' Years Away from Apple

Steve Jobs was rich and unemployed. Although he wasn't working at Apple, he was far from idle. During this time, from 1985 to 1996, Jobs was involved in two big deals; the first of which was an investment.

In 1986, Jobs purchased a controlling stake in a company called Pixar from George Lucas. The company was struggling, but its eventual success in digital animation led to an [initial public offering (IPO)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/ipo.asp#axzz1iJWkopEg) that earned Jobs around $1 billion.112

The second was a return to his old obsession with computers, founding NeXT to create high-end computers. These were expensive machines with an operating system representing the best attempt yet at making the power of UNIX fit into a graphical user interface. When Tim Berners-Lee created the World Wide Web, he did so using a NeXT machine.1314

Of these two deals, NeXT proved the most important, as it turned out Apple was looking to replace its operating system. Apple bought NeXT in 1996 for its operating system, bringing Steve Jobs back to the first company he founded.15

## Revitalizing Apple: Steve Jobs' Return

When Jobs returned, the company wasn't in a good place. Apple had begun to flounder as cheap PCs running Windows flooded the market. Jobs found himself in the driver's seat again and took some drastic steps to turn around Apple's decline.

The company asked for and received a $150 million investment from Bill Gates.16 Jobs used the money to ramp up advertising and highlight the products Apple already offered while choking off [research and development](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/randd.asp) (R&D) money in non-producing areas.

The NeXT operating system was used to create the iMac, Apple's first hit PC in a long time.17 Jobs followed this up with a list of successes from the iPod in 2001 to the iPhone in 2007, and the iPad in 2010.181920

The years between saw Apple dominate the smartphone market with the iPhone, open up an e-commerce store with iTunes, and launch [branded retail outlets](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/brand-identity.asp) called, what else, the Apple Store. When Jobs stepped down as CEO, Apple was scrapping with Exxon for the world's largest market cap.21

## How Much of Apple Did Steve Jobs Own?

Surprisingly, at the time of his death, Steve Jobs only owned 0.24% of Apple. The bulk of his wealth came from the shares he owned in Disney, which was approximately 7% of the company.11

## Why Did Steve Jobs Create Apple?

There are a few reasons why Steve Jobs created Apple. One being his entrepreneurial spirit, another being his interest in computers, and also his and Wozniak's vision of making computers accessible to the larger public by making them small enough to fit into homes and offices.

## How Long Did Steve Jobs Leave Apple?

Steve Jobs was fired from Apple in 1985 and was brought back in 1996 when his company, NeXT, was purchased by Apple. This means he was gone from Apple for 11 years.1015

## The Bottom Line

It's impossible to sum up Jobs's career in a single article, but a few lessons stick out. First, innovation counts for a lot, but [innovative products](https://www.investopedia.com/financial-edge/1111/steve-jobs-10-most-innovative-creations.aspx) fail without proper marketing. Second, there are no straight paths to success.

Jobs did get wealthy early on, but he would be a footnote today if he hadn't returned to Apple in the 90s. At one point, Jobs was kicked out of the company he helped create for being hard to work with. Rather than change, he bided his time, then took over again, and this time his attitude was seen as part of his genius.

There is much more to be learned from the life of Steve Jobs, as there is in the life of every [successful entrepreneur](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/personal-finance/101014/10-characteristics-successful-entrepreneurs.asp). The sheer hubris of the entrepreneurial spirit, the idea that you can do something bigger and better than it has ever been done before, always bears watching and studying, whether to imitate or just to marvel at.

# History of Apple Inc.

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Apple_logo_black.svg/250px-Apple_logo_black.svg.png)

[Apple Inc.](/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc."), originally Apple Computer, Inc., is an American [multinational corporation](/wiki/Multinational_corporation "Multinational corporation") that creates and markets [consumer electronics](/wiki/Consumer_electronics "Consumer electronics") and attendant computer software, and is a [digital distributor](/wiki/Digital_distribution "Digital distribution") of media content. Apple's core product lines are the [iPhone](/wiki/IPhone "IPhone") line of [smartphones](/wiki/Smartphone "Smartphone"), the [iPad](/wiki/IPad "IPad") line of [tablet computers](/wiki/Tablet_computer "Tablet computer"), and the [Mac](/wiki/Mac_(computer) "Mac (computer)") line of [personal computers](/wiki/Personal_computer "Personal computer"). The company offers [its products](/wiki/List_of_Apple_products "List of Apple products") online and has a chain of retail stores known as [Apple Stores](/wiki/Apple_Stores "Apple Stores"). Founders [Steve Jobs](/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs"), [Steve Wozniak](/wiki/Steve_Wozniak "Steve Wozniak"), and [Ronald Wayne](/wiki/Ronald_Wayne "Ronald Wayne") created Apple Computer Co. on April 1, 1976, to market Wozniak's [Apple I](/wiki/Apple_I "Apple I") desktop computer,[[2]](#cite_note-partnership-2) and Jobs and Wozniak incorporated the company on January 3, 1977,[[3]](#cite_note-Apple_incorporation-3) in [Cupertino, California](/wiki/Cupertino,_California "Cupertino, California").

For more than three decades, Apple Computer was predominantly a manufacturer of personal computers, including the [Apple II](/wiki/Apple_II "Apple II"), Macintosh, and [Power Mac](/wiki/Power_Macintosh "Power Macintosh") lines, but it faced rocky sales and low market share during the 1990s. Jobs, who had been ousted from the company in 1985, returned to Apple in 1997 after his company [NeXT](/wiki/NeXT "NeXT") was bought by Apple.[[4]](#cite_note-4) The following year he became the company's interim CEO,[[5]](#cite_note-interim_CEO-5) which later became permanent.[[6]](#cite_note-norr-6) Jobs subsequently instilled a new corporate philosophy of recognizable products and simple design, starting with the [original iMac](/wiki/IMac_G3 "IMac G3") in 1998.

With the introduction of the successful [iPod](/wiki/IPod "IPod") music player in 2001 and [iTunes Music Store](/wiki/ITunes_Store "ITunes Store") in 2003, Apple established itself as a leader in the consumer electronics and media sales industries, leading it to drop "Computer" from the company's name in 2007. The company is also known for its [iOS](/wiki/IOS "IOS") range of smartphone, media player, and tablet computer products that began with the iPhone, followed by the [iPod Touch](/wiki/IPod_Touch "IPod Touch") and then iPad. As of June 30, 2015[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Apple_Inc.&action=edit), Apple was the [largest publicly traded corporation in the world by market capitalization](/wiki/List_of_corporations_by_market_capitalization "List of corporations by market capitalization"),[[7]](#cite_note-7) with an estimated value of US$1 trillion as of August 2, 2018[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Apple_Inc.&action=edit).[[8]](#cite_note-8) Apple's worldwide annual revenue in 2010 totaled US$65 billion, growing to US$127.8 billion in 2011[[9]](#cite_note-9) and $156 billion in 2012.[[10]](#cite_note-10-K-2012-10)

## 1971–1985: Jobs and Wozniak

### Pre-foundation

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Blue_Box_in_museum.jpg/250px-Blue_Box_in_museum.jpg)

[Steve Jobs](/wiki/Steve_Jobs "Steve Jobs") and [Steve Wozniak](/wiki/Steve_Wozniak "Steve Wozniak"), referred to collectively as "the two Steves", first met in mid-1971, when their mutual friend [Bill Fernandez](/wiki/Bill_Fernandez "Bill Fernandez") introduced then 21-year-old Wozniak to 16-year-old Jobs.[[11]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20041–2-11)[[12]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Grady20091–2-12) Their first business partnership began in the fall of that year when Wozniak, a self-educated electronics engineer, read an article in *Esquire* magazine that described a device that could place free long-distance phone calls by emitting specific tone chirps. Wozniak started to build his original “[blue boxes](/wiki/Blue_box "Blue box")”, which he tested by calling the [Vatican City](/wiki/Vatican_City "Vatican City") pretending to be [Henry Kissinger](/wiki/Henry_Kissinger "Henry Kissinger") wanting to speak to the pope.[[13]](#cite_note-13) Jobs managed to sell some two hundred blue boxes for $150 each, and split the profit with Wozniak.[[11]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20041–2-11)[[12]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Grady20091–2-12) Jobs later told his biographer that if it hadn't been for Wozniak's blue boxes, "there wouldn't have been an Apple."[[14]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201530-14)

By 1972, Jobs had withdrawn from [Reed College](/wiki/Reed_College "Reed College") and Wozniak from [UC Berkeley](/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley "University of California, Berkeley"). Wozniak designed a video terminal that he could use to log on to the minicomputers at Call Computer. Alex Kamradt commissioned the design and sold a small number of them through his firm. Aside from their interest in up-to-date technology, the impetus for the two Steves seems to have had another source. In his essay *From Satori to Silicon Valley* (published 1986), cultural historian [Theodore Roszak](/wiki/Theodore_Roszak_(scholar) "Theodore Roszak (scholar)") made the point that Apple Computer emerged from within the West Coast [counterculture](/wiki/Counterculture "Counterculture") and the need to produce print-outs, letter labels, and databases. Roszak offers a bit of background on the development of the two Steves' prototype models.

In 1975, the two Steves started attending meetings of the [Homebrew Computer Club](/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club").[[15]](#cite_note-Apple_Confidential-15) New microcomputers such as the [Altair 8800](/wiki/Altair_8800 "Altair 8800") and the [IMSAI 8080](/wiki/IMSAI_8080 "IMSAI 8080") inspired Wozniak to build a microprocessor into his video terminal circuit to make a complete computer. At the time the only [microcomputer](/wiki/Microcomputer "Microcomputer") [CPUs](/wiki/Central_processing_unit "Central processing unit") generally available were the $179 [Intel 8080](/wiki/Intel_8080 "Intel 8080") (equivalent to $1,071 in 2025), and the $170 [Motorola 6800](/wiki/Motorola_6800 "Motorola 6800") (equivalent to $1,017 in 2025). Wozniak preferred the 6800, but both were out of his price range. So he watched, and learned, and designed computers on paper, waiting for the day he could afford a CPU.

When [MOS Technology](/wiki/MOS_Technology "MOS Technology") released its $20 (equivalent to $113 in 2025) [6502](/wiki/MOS_Technology_6502 "MOS Technology 6502") chip in 1976, Wozniak wrote a version of [BASIC](/wiki/BASIC "BASIC") for it, then began to design a computer for it to run on. The 6502 was designed by the same people who designed the 6800, as many in Silicon Valley left employers to form their own companies. Wozniak's earlier 6800 paper-computer needed only minor changes to run on the new chip.

By March 1, 1976, Wozniak completed the machine and took it to a [Homebrew Computer Club](/wiki/Homebrew_Computer_Club "Homebrew Computer Club") meeting to show it off.[[16]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20045-16) When Jobs saw Wozniak's computer, which later became the [Apple I](/wiki/Apple_I "Apple I"), he was immediately interested in its commercial potential.[[17]](#cite_note-17) Initially, Wozniak intended to share schematics of the machine for free, but Jobs insisted that they should instead build and sell bare [printed circuit boards](/wiki/Printed_circuit_board "Printed circuit board") for the computer.[[18]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201562-18) Wozniak originally offered the design to [Hewlett-Packard](/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard") (HP), where he worked at the time, but was denied by the company on five occasions.[[19]](#cite_note-AI-19) Jobs eventually convinced Wozniak to go into business together and start a new company of their own.[[20]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20045–6-20) In order to raise the money they needed to produce the first batch of printed circuit boards, Jobs sold his [Volkswagen Type 2](/wiki/Volkswagen_Type_2 "Volkswagen Type 2") [minibus](/wiki/Minibus "Minibus") for $1,500, and Wozniak his [HP-65](/wiki/HP-65 "HP-65") [programmable calculator](/wiki/Programmable_calculator "Programmable calculator") for $500.[[18]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201562-18)[[16]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20045-16)[[21]](#cite_note-21)[[22]](#cite_note-22)

### Apple I and company formation

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Apple_Garage.jpg/250px-Apple_Garage.jpg)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Apple_1_Woz_1976_at_CHM.agr_cropped.jpg/250px-Apple_1_Woz_1976_at_CHM.agr_cropped.jpg)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Apple_first_logo.png/250px-Apple_first_logo.png)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Apple_Computer_Logo_rainbow.svg/250px-Apple_Computer_Logo_rainbow.svg.png)

On April 1, 1976, Apple Computer Company was founded by Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and [Ronald Wayne](/wiki/Ronald_Wayne "Ronald Wayne").[[25]](#cite_note-25)[[20]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20045–6-20) The company was registered as a California [business partnership](/wiki/Business_partnership "Business partnership").[[26]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201639-26)
Wayne, who worked at [Atari, Inc.](/wiki/Atari,_Inc. "Atari, Inc.") as a chief draftsman, became a co-founder in return for a 10% stake.[[27]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwaine2014336–338-27)[[20]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20045–6-20)[[2]](#cite_note-partnership-2) Wayne was gun-shy due to the failure of his own venture four years earlier. On April 12, less than two weeks after the company's formation, Wayne left Apple, selling his 10% share back to the two Steves for $800.[[28]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201565–66-28)[[29]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20046–8-29)

According to Wozniak, Jobs proposed the name “Apple Computer” when he had just come back from [Robert Friedland](/wiki/Robert_Friedland "Robert Friedland")'s All-One Farm in Oregon.[[29]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20046–8-29) Jobs told [Walter Isaacson](/wiki/Walter_Isaacson "Walter Isaacson") that he was "on one of my fruitarian diets," when he conceived of the name and thought "it sounded fun, spirited and not intimidating ... plus, it would get us ahead of Atari in the phone book."[[30]](#cite_note-30)

The two Steves made a last trip to the Homebrew Computer Club and demonstrated the [Apple I](/wiki/Apple_I "Apple I") (AKA: The Apple Computer).[[31]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201639–40-31) [Paul Terrell](/wiki/Paul_Terrell "Paul Terrell"), who operated the computer store chain [Byte Shop](/wiki/Byte_Shop "Byte Shop"), was impressed,[[27]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwaine2014336–338-27) and gave the two Steves his card, asking them to keep in touch.[[32]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201566-32) The next day, Jobs visited Terrell at the [Mountain View](/wiki/Mountain_View,_California "Mountain View, California") Byte Shop store, and tried to sell him the bare circuit boards for the Apple I.[[29]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20046–8-29) Terrell said he was only interested in purchasing the machine fully assembled, and that he would order 50 assembled computers and pay US$500 each on delivery (equivalent to $2,800 in 2025).[[33]](#cite_note-iCon-p35-33)[[34]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20047-34)[[27]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwaine2014336–338-27) Jobs took the purchase order from the Byte Shop to national electronic parts distributor Cramer Electronics, and ordered the components needed. When asked by the credit manager how he would pay for the parts, Jobs replied, "I have this purchase order from the Byte Shop chain of computer stores for 50 of my computers and the payment terms are [COD](/wiki/Cash_on_delivery "Cash on delivery"). If you give me the parts on net 30-day terms I can build and deliver the computers in that time frame, collect my money from Terrell at the Byte Shop and pay you."[[35]](#cite_note-35)[[36]](#cite_note-wozniak198412-36)

To verify the purchase order, the credit manager called Paul Terrell, who assured him if the computers showed up, Jobs would have more than enough money for the parts order. The two Steves and their small crew spent day and night building and testing the computers, and delivered to Terrell on time. Terrell was surprised to receive a batch of assembled circuit boards, as he had expected complete computers with a case, monitor and keyboard.[[37]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201567–68-37)[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38) Nonetheless, he kept his word and paid the two Steves the money promised.[[39]](#cite_note-VintageNews-39)[[37]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201567–68-37)[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38)[[40]](#cite_note-iCon-p36-40)

The Apple I went on sale in July 1976 as an assembled circuit board with a retail price of $666.66.[[41]](#cite_note-41)[[42]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20047–8-42)[[43]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201569–70-43) Wozniak later said he had had no idea about the relation between the number and the [mark of the beast](/wiki/Mark_of_the_beast "Mark of the beast"), and that he came up with the price because he liked repeating digits.[[39]](#cite_note-VintageNews-39) About 200 units of the Apple I were eventually sold.[[44]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200414-44)

The Apple I computer had some notable features, including the use of a [TV](/wiki/Television "Television") display, whereas many machines had no display at all. This was not like the displays of later machines; the text was displayed at 60 characters per second – still faster than the teleprinters of contemporary machines of that era. The machine had [bootstrap](/wiki/Booting "Booting") code on [ROM](/wiki/Read-only_memory "Read-only memory"), making it easier to start up. At the insistence of Paul Terrell, Wozniak designed a cassette interface for loading and saving programs, at the then-rapid pace of 1200 bit/s. The simple machine was a masterpiece of design using far fewer parts than anything in its class, and earned Wozniak his reputation as a designer.

Jobs looked for investments to expand the business,[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38) but banks were reluctant to lend him money; the idea of a computer for ordinary people seemed absurd at the time. In August 1976, Jobs approached his former boss at Atari, [Nolan Bushnell](/wiki/Nolan_Bushnell "Nolan Bushnell"), who recommended that he meet with [Don Valentine](/wiki/Don_Valentine "Don Valentine"), the founder of [Sequoia Capital](/wiki/Sequoia_Capital "Sequoia Capital").[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38) Valentine was not interested in funding Apple, but in turn introduced Jobs to [Mike Markkula](/wiki/Mike_Markkula "Mike Markkula"), a millionaire who had worked under him at [Fairchild Semiconductor](/wiki/Fairchild_Semiconductor "Fairchild Semiconductor").[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38) Markkula saw great potential in the two Steves, and became an [angel investor](/wiki/Angel_investor "Angel investor") of their company.[[45]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201645–47-45) He invested $92,000 in Apple out of his own property while securing a $250,000 (equivalent to $1,410,000 in 2025) line of credit from Bank of America.[[45]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201645–47-45)[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38) In return, Markkula received a one-third stake in Apple.[[45]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201645–47-45) Apple Computer, Inc. was incorporated on January 3, 1977.[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38) The new corporation bought out the partnership the two Steves had formed nine months earlier.[[46]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201577–78-46)

In February 1977, Markkula recruited [Michael Scott](/wiki/Michael_Scott_(Apple) "Michael Scott (Apple)") from [National Semiconductor](/wiki/National_Semiconductor "National Semiconductor") to serve as the first president and CEO of Apple Computer, as the two Steves were both insufficiently experienced and he was not interested in taking that position himself.[[47]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200411-47)[[48]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201648–50-48)
That same month, Wozniak resigned from his job at [Hewlett-Packard](/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard") to work full-time for Apple.[[46]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201577–78-46)[[49]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200421-49)

### Apple II

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Apple_II_Plus_cropped.jpg/250px-Apple_II_Plus_cropped.jpg)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Disk_II.jpg/250px-Disk_II.jpg)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/20863_Stevens_Creek_Boulevard.jpg/250px-20863_Stevens_Creek_Boulevard.jpg)

Almost as soon as Apple had started selling its first computers, Wozniak moved on from the Apple I and began designing a greatly improved computer: the [Apple II](/wiki/Apple_II_(original) "Apple II (original)").[[45]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201645–47-45) Wozniak completed a working prototype of the new machine by August 1976.[[38]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer20048–10-38)[[50]](#cite_note-50) The two Steves presented the Apple II computer to the public at the first [West Coast Computer Faire](/wiki/West_Coast_Computer_Faire "West Coast Computer Faire") on April 16 and 17, 1977. In the May 1977 issue of [*Byte*](/wiki/Byte_(magazine) "Byte (magazine)"), Wozniak said of the Apple II design, "To me, a personal computer should be small, reliable, convenient to use, and inexpensive."[[51]](#cite_note-wozniak197705-51)

The Apple II went on sale on June 10, 1977, with a retail price of $1,298.[[52]](#cite_note-52) The computer's main internal difference from its predecessor was a completely redesigned TV interface, which held the display in memory. Now not only useful for simple text display, the Apple II included graphics and, eventually, color. During the development of the Apple II, Jobs pressed for a well-designed plastic case and built-in keyboard, with the idea that the machine should be fully packaged and ready to run out of the box.[[53]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201571–74-53) This was almost the case for the Apple I computers, but one still needed to plug various parts together and type in the code to run BASIC. Jobs wanted the Apple II case to be "simple and elegant", and hired an industrial designer named [Jerry Manock](/wiki/Jerry_Manock "Jerry Manock") to produce such a case design.[[53]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201571–74-53) Apple employee #5 [Rod Holt](/wiki/Rod_Holt "Rod Holt") developed the [switching power supply](/wiki/Switched-mode_power_supply "Switched-mode power supply").[[54]](#cite_note-wozorgAppleII-54) While early Apple II models use ordinary cassette tapes as storage devices, they were superseded in 1978 by the introduction of a 5+1⁄4-inch [floppy disk](/wiki/Floppy_disk "Floppy disk") drive and interface called the [Disk II](/wiki/Disk_II "Disk II").[[55]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200413–15-55)[[56]](#cite_note-56) The Disk II system was designed by Wozniak and released with a retail price of $495.[[55]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200413–15-55)

In 1979, the Apple II was chosen to be the desktop platform for what became the first [killer application](/wiki/Killer_application "Killer application") of the business world: [VisiCalc](/wiki/VisiCalc "VisiCalc"), a [spreadsheet](/wiki/Spreadsheet "Spreadsheet").[[55]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200413–15-55) So important that the Apple II became what [John Markoff](/wiki/John_Markoff "John Markoff") described as a "VisiCalc accessory",[[57]](#cite_note-markoff19820705-57) the application unexpectedly created a business market for the computer; Wozniak later said that small businesses bought 90% of Apple IIs, not the hobbyists he and Jobs had expected.[[58]](#cite_note-byte198501-58) It gave home users an additional reason to buy Apples: Compatibility with the office.[[55]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200413–15-55) Before VisiCalc, Apple had been a distant third place competitor to [Commodore](/wiki/Commodore_64 "Commodore 64") and [Tandy](/wiki/Tandy_Computers "Tandy Computers").[[59]](#cite_note-59)[[60]](#cite_note-60)

The Apple II was one of the three "[1977 Trinity](/wiki/History_of_personal_computers#1977_and_the_emergence_of_the_"Trinity" "History of personal computers")" computers generally credited with creating the [home computer](/wiki/Home_computer "Home computer") market (the other two being the [Commodore PET](/wiki/Commodore_PET "Commodore PET") and the Tandy Corporation [TRS-80](/wiki/TRS-80 "TRS-80")).[[61]](#cite_note-61) A number of different models of the Apple II were built thereafter, including the [Apple IIe](/wiki/Apple_IIe "Apple IIe") and [Apple IIGS](/wiki/Apple_IIGS "Apple IIGS"),[[62]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200417–20-62) which continued in public use for nearly two decades. The [Apple II series](/wiki/Apple_II "Apple II") went on to sell about six million units in total before it was discontinued in 1993.[[63]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201584–85-63)[[64]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201665–66-64)

### Apple III

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Apple3.jpg/250px-Apple3.jpg)

While the Apple II was already established as a successful business-ready platform because of VisiCalc, Apple management was not content. The Apple III was designed to take on the business environment in an attempt to compete with [IBM](/wiki/IBM "IBM") in the business and corporate computing market.[[65]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Grady20096-65) The development of the Apple III started in late 1978 under the guidance of Wendell Sander,[[66]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200481–82-66) and was subsequently developed by a committee headed by Jobs.[[67]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200441–42-67) The Apple III was first announced on May 19, 1980, with a retail price ranging from $4,340
to $7,800, and released in November 1980.[[67]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200441–42-67)

The Apple III was a conservative design for the era, however Jobs wanted the heat generated by the electronics to be dissipated through the [chassis](/wiki/Chassis "Chassis") of the machine rather than by the more usual cooling fan. The case was not sufficient to cool the components and the Apple III was prone to overheating, causing the [integrated circuit](/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit") chips to disconnect from the motherboard. Customers who contacted Apple customer service were told to raise their computers into the air, and then let go to cause the integrated circuits to fall back into place. Thousands of Apple III computers were recalled. A new model was introduced in 1983 to try to rectify the problems, but the damage was already done.

### Apple IPO

In the July 1980 issue of *[Kilobaud Microcomputing](/wiki/Kilobaud_Microcomputing "Kilobaud Microcomputing")*, publisher [Wayne Green](/wiki/Wayne_Green "Wayne Green") stated that "the best consumer ads I've seen have been those by Apple. They are attention-getting, and they must be prompting sale."[[68]](#cite_note-green198007-68) In August, the *[Financial Times](/wiki/Financial_Times "Financial Times")* reported that

Apple Computer, the fast growing Californian manufacturer of small computers for the consumer, business and educational markets, is planning to go public later this year. [It] is the largest private manufacturer in the U.S. of small computers. Founded about five years ago as a small workshop business, it has become the second largest manufacturer of small computers, after the [Radio Shack](/wiki/Radio_Shack "Radio Shack") division of the [Tandy](/wiki/Tandy_Corporation "Tandy Corporation") company.[[69]](#cite_note-69)

On December 12, 1980, Apple went public on the [NASDAQ](/wiki/NASDAQ "NASDAQ") stock exchange with the ticker symbol "AAPL", selling 4.6 million shares at $22 per share ($.10 per share when adjusting for [stock splits](/wiki/Stock_split "Stock split") as of November 30, 2020[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Apple_Inc.&action=edit)),[[70]](#cite_note-Apple_FAQ-70) generating over $100 million, which was more capital than any IPO since [Ford Motor Company](/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company "Ford Motor Company") in 1956.[[71]](#cite_note-EDNAAPLSTOCK-71) Several venture capitalists cashed out, reaping billions in long-term capital gains. By the end of the day, the stock rose to $29 per share and 300 millionaires were created, including the two Steves.[[72]](#cite_note-CNBC-72)[[73]](#cite_note-DEDIPO-73) Around this time Wozniak offered $10 million of his own stock to early Apple employees, something Jobs refused to do.[[72]](#cite_note-CNBC-72) Apple's market cap was $1.778 billion at the end of its first day of trading.[[71]](#cite_note-EDNAAPLSTOCK-71)[[73]](#cite_note-DEDIPO-73)

In January 1981, Apple held its first shareholders meeting as a public company in the [Flint Center](/wiki/Flint_Center "Flint Center"), a large auditorium at nearby [De Anza College](/wiki/De_Anza_College "De Anza College") (which is often used for symphony concerts) to handle the larger numbers of shareholders post-IPO. The business of the meeting had been planned so that the voting could be staged in 15 minutes or less. In most cases, voting proxies are collected by mail and counted days or months before a meeting. In this case, after the IPO, many shares were in new hands.

Jobs started his prepared speech, but after being interrupted by voting several times, he dropped his prepared speech and delivered a long, emotionally charged talk about betrayal, lack of respect, and related topics.[[74]](#cite_note-74) The results of the vote were surprising: a young programmer, [Randy Wigginton](/wiki/Randy_Wigginton "Randy Wigginton"), received enough votes to be added to Apple's board of directors, through the use of [cumulative voting](/wiki/Cumulative_voting "Cumulative voting") by a few major shareholders.

### Competition from the IBM PC

By August 1981 Apple was among the three largest microcomputer companies, perhaps having replaced Radio Shack as the leader;[[75]](#cite_note-hogan19810831-75) revenue in the first half of the year had already exceeded 1980's $118 million, and *InfoWorld* reported that lack of production capacity was constraining growth.[[76]](#cite_note-hogan19810914state-76) Because of VisiCalc, businesses purchased 90% of Apple II computers;[[77]](#cite_note-Mace1984-77)[[58]](#cite_note-byte198501-58) large customers especially preferred Apple.[[78]](#cite_note-mcmullen19840221-78)

[IBM](/wiki/IBM "IBM") entered the personal computer market that month with the [IBM PC](/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer "IBM Personal Computer")[[79]](#cite_note-pollack19810813-79)[[80]](#cite_note-isaacson2013-80) in part because it did not want products without IBM logos on customers' desks,[[81]](#cite_note-pollack19830327-81) but Apple had many advantages. While IBM began with one microcomputer, little available hardware or software, and a couple of hundred dealers, Apple had five times as many dealers in the US and an established international distribution network. The Apple II had an [installed base](/wiki/Installed_base "Installed base") of more than 250,000 customers, and hundreds of independent developers offered software and peripherals; at least ten databases and ten word processors were available, while the PC had no databases and one word processor.[[82]](#cite_note-rosen19811130-82)

The company's customers gained a reputation for devotion and loyalty. *[BYTE](/wiki/BYTE "BYTE")* in 1984 stated that[[83]](#cite_note-lemmons198412-83)

There are two kinds of people in the world: people who say Apple isn't just a company, it's a cause; and people who say Apple isn't a cause, it's just a company. Both groups are right. Nature has suspended the principle of noncontradiction where Apple is concerned.
Apple is more than just a company because its founding has some of the qualities of myth ... Apple is two guys in a garage undertaking the mission of bringing computing power, once reserved for big corporations, to ordinary individuals with ordinary budgets. The company's growth from two guys to a billion-dollar corporation exemplifies the [American Dream](/wiki/American_Dream "American Dream"). Even as a large corporation, Apple plays [David to IBM's Goliath](/wiki/David_and_Goliath "David and Goliath"), and thus has the sympathetic role in that myth.

The magazine noted that the loyalty was not entirely positive for Apple; customers were willing to overlook real flaws in its products, even while holding the company to a higher standard than for competitors.[[83]](#cite_note-lemmons198412-83) The Apple III was an example of its autocratic reputation among dealers[[78]](#cite_note-mcmullen19840221-78) that one described as "Apple arrogance".[[84]](#cite_note-dvorak19831128-84) After examining a PC and finding it unimpressive, Apple confidently purchased a full-page advertisement in *The Wall Street Journal* with the headline "Welcome, IBM. Seriously".[[85]](#cite_note-iw19811005-85)[[80]](#cite_note-isaacson2013-80) The company prioritized the III for three years, spending what Wozniak estimated as $100 million on marketing and R&D while not improving the Apple II to compete with the PC, as doing so could hurt III sales.[[58]](#cite_note-byte198501-58)

Microsoft head [Bill Gates](/wiki/Bill_Gates "Bill Gates") was at Apple headquarters the day of IBM's announcement and later said "They didn't seem to care. It took them a full year to realize what had happened".[[80]](#cite_note-isaacson2013-80) The PC almost completely ended sales of the III, the company's most comparable product. The II still sold well,[[81]](#cite_note-pollack19830327-81) with Apple being the leading computer manufacturer in the United States where 7 million units were sold between 1978 and 1982.[[86]](#cite_note-86) But by 1983, the PC surpassed the Apple II as the best-selling personal computer.[[87]](#cite_note-sanger19850805-87) IBM recruited the best Apple dealers while avoiding the discount [grey market](/wiki/Grey_market#Electronics "Grey market") they disliked.[[81]](#cite_note-pollack19830327-81) The head of a retail chain said "It appears that IBM had a better understanding of why the Apple II was successful than had Apple".[[78]](#cite_note-mcmullen19840221-78) [Gene Amdahl](/wiki/Gene_Amdahl "Gene Amdahl") predicted that Apple would be another of the many "brash young companies" that IBM had defeated.[[88]](#cite_note-greenwald19830711-88)

By 1984 the press called the two companies archrivals,[[89]](#cite_note-bulman19841105-89) but IBM had $4 billion in annual PC revenue, more than twice that of Apple and as much as the sales of it and the next three companies combined.[[90]](#cite_note-libes198509-90) A *[Fortune](/wiki/Fortune_(magazine) "Fortune (magazine)")* survey found that 56% of American companies with personal computers used IBM PCs, compared to 16% for Apple.[[91]](#cite_note-Kennedy_1985-91) Small businesses, schools, and some homes became the II's primary market.[[77]](#cite_note-Mace1984-77)

### Xerox PARC and the Lisa

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Apple_Lisa-2-IMG_1730_modified.png/250px-Apple_Lisa-2-IMG_1730_modified.png)

In 1979, in return for the right to purchase $1 million worth of Apple’s pre-IPO stock, [Xerox](/wiki/Xerox "Xerox") granted Apple access to its [Palo Alto Research Center](/wiki/PARC_(company) "PARC (company)") (PARC).[[92]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Grady20097–8-92)[[93]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200473–76-93) Jobs, along with his Apple colleagues, visited PARC twice in November and December of 1979,[[94]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200474–75-94)[[95]](#cite_note-95)[[96]](#cite_note-Wired-96)[[97]](#cite_note-Jobsjourney-97) and he was greatly impressed by the advanced [graphical user interface](/wiki/Graphical_user_interface "Graphical user interface") (GUI) of the [Xerox Alto](/wiki/Xerox_Alto "Xerox Alto") computer.[[92]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Grady20097–8-92)[[98]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson201596–97-98)

After the visits to PARC, Jobs decided to implement a GUI like the one he had seen there in the [Apple Lisa](/wiki/Apple_Lisa "Apple Lisa"), a business-oriented computer that had been under development at Apple since July 1979.[[93]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200473–76-93)[[99]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201681–89-99) Jobs took the lead of the Lisa project from early 1980, but the development of the Lisa was disrupted due to his excessive technical demands.[[99]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201681–89-99) In September 1980, Jobs was removed from the Lisa project.[[100]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015110-100)

The first iteration of Apple's [WIMP](/wiki/WIMP_(computing) "WIMP (computing)") interface was a floppy disk where files could be spatially moved around. After months of usability testing, Apple designed the Lisa interface of windows and icons.

Finally released in January 1983, the Lisa featured many innovative functions such as a GUI and a [mouse](/wiki/Computer_mouse "Computer mouse"), but its extremely high price of US$9,995 (equivalent to $32,300 in 2025) and the lack of software compatibility drove business users away, resulting in sluggish sales.[[101]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200468-101) More than $150 million was reportedly spent on the development of the Lisa, but only about 10,000 units were sold, resulting in significant losses for Apple.[[102]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Grady200972-102)

### Macintosh and the "1984" commercial

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Macintosh%2C_Google_NY_office_computer_museum_cropped.png/250px-Macintosh%2C_Google_NY_office_computer_museum_cropped.png)

The Macintosh project was started in September 1979 by [Jeff Raskin](/wiki/Jeff_Raskin "Jeff Raskin"), an early Apple employee and a computer scientist.[[103]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELInzmayer200487–88-103)[[104]](#cite_note-104) The [Macintosh](/wiki/Mac_(computer) "Mac (computer)"), named after Raskin’s favorite [type of apple](/wiki/McIntosh_(apple) "McIntosh (apple)"), was originally conceived as a low-cost [portable computer](/wiki/Portable_computer "Portable computer").[[105]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwaine2000308-105)[[106]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELInzmayer200487-106) The project entered a new phase when Steve Jobs, having been removed from the Lisa project, joined the team in January 1981.[[107]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELInzmayer200492-107)[[108]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015109–113-108) Jobs soon took over the project from Raskin to redefine the Macintosh as a high-performance desktop machine similar to the Lisa, featuring a [mouse](/wiki/Computer_mouse "Computer mouse")-driven [graphical user interface](/wiki/Graphical_user_interface "Graphical user interface") and powered by the [Motorola 68000](/wiki/Motorola_68000 "Motorola 68000") microprocessor.[[109]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015109–113,_136-109) Wozniak, who led the initial design and development with Raskin was on leave during this time due to an airplane crash, making it easier for Jobs to seize control of the program.[[110]](#cite_note-TheVerge-110)[[111]](#cite_note-wozorg-111)

By 1984 computer dealers saw Apple as the only clear alternative to IBM's influence;[[112]](#cite_note-sanger19841119-112) some even promoted its products to reduce dependence on the PC.[[81]](#cite_note-pollack19830327-81) The company announced the [Macintosh 128K](/wiki/Macintosh_128K "Macintosh 128K") to the press in October 1983, followed by an 18-page brochure included with magazines in December.[[113]](#cite_note-113) Its debut was announced by a single national broadcast of a US$1.5 million television commercial, "[1984](/wiki/1984_(advertisement) "1984 (advertisement)")" (equivalent to $4,600,000 in 2025). Directed by [Ridley Scott](/wiki/Ridley_Scott "Ridley Scott") and aired during the third quarter of [Super Bowl XVIII](/wiki/Super_Bowl_XVIII "Super Bowl XVIII") on January 22, 1984,[[114]](#cite_note-114) it is considered a "watershed event"[[115]](#cite_note-115) and a "masterpiece."[[116]](#cite_note-masterpiece-116) The commercial alludes to [George Orwell](/wiki/George_Orwell "George Orwell")'s novel *[Nineteen Eighty-Four](/wiki/Nineteen_Eighty-Four "Nineteen Eighty-Four")* which describes a [dystopian future](/wiki/Dystopia "Dystopia") of enforced conformity. In the commercial a heroine represents the coming of the Macintosh to save humanity,[[117]](#cite_note-cellini-117) and ends with the words: "On January 24th, Apple Computer will introduce Macintosh. And you'll see why 1984 won't be like 1984.”[[118]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer2004110–113-118)

On January 24, 1984, the Macintosh went on sale with a retail price of $2,495.[[119]](#cite_note-119)[[120]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200491-120) It came bundled with two applications designed to show off its interface: [MacWrite](/wiki/MacWrite "MacWrite") and [MacPaint](/wiki/MacPaint "MacPaint"). On the same day, an emotional Jobs introduced the computer to a wildly enthusiastic audience at Apple's annual shareholders meeting held in the Flint Auditorium;[[121]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015167–170-121)[[122]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201682–83-122) Macintosh engineer [Andy Hertzfeld](/wiki/Andy_Hertzfeld "Andy Hertzfeld") described the scene as "pandemonium".[[123]](#cite_note-The_Times_They_Are_A-Changin-123) The Macintosh was widely acclaimed by the media with strong initial sales supporting it.[[124]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015185–187-124)[[125]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201684–88-125) However, the slow processing speed and limited software led to a rapid sales decline in the second half of 1984.[[124]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015185–187-124)[[125]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201684–88-125)[[126]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer200498-126)

The Macintosh was too radical for some, who labeled it a mere "toy". Because the machine was entirely designed around the GUI, existing text-mode and [command-driven applications](/wiki/Command_line_interface "Command line interface") had to be redesigned and the programming code rewritten; this was a challenging undertaking that many [software developers](/wiki/Software_developer "Software developer") shied away from, and resulted in an initial lack of software for the new system. In April 1984 [Microsoft](/wiki/Microsoft "Microsoft")'s [Multiplan](/wiki/Multiplan "Multiplan") migrated over from [MS-DOS](/wiki/MS-DOS "MS-DOS"), followed by [Microsoft Word](/wiki/Microsoft_Word "Microsoft Word") in January 1985;[[127]](#cite_note-hotwyu-127) in return for Microsoft's commitment to Macintosh, Apple agreed to not provide [software development kits](/wiki/Software_development_kit "Software development kit") and prototype computers to companies with applications competing with Microsoft's.[[128]](#cite_note-farber20140123-128) In 1985, [Lotus Development](/wiki/Lotus_Development "Lotus Development") introduced [Lotus Jazz](/wiki/Lotus_Jazz "Lotus Jazz") after the success of [Lotus 1-2-3](/wiki/Lotus_1-2-3 "Lotus 1-2-3") for the IBM PC, although it was largely a flop.[[129]](#cite_note-129) Apple introduced [Macintosh Office](/wiki/Macintosh_Office "Macintosh Office") the same year with the [lemmings ad](/wiki/Lemmings_(advertisement) "Lemmings (advertisement)"), infamous for insulting potential customers. It was not successful.[[130]](#cite_note-Worst_Business-130)

For a special post-election edition of *[Newsweek](/wiki/Newsweek "Newsweek")* in November 1984, Apple spent more than US$2.5 million to buy all 39 of the advertising pages in the issue.[[131]](#cite_note-131) Apple also ran a "Test Drive a Macintosh" promotion, in which potential buyers with a credit card could take home a Macintosh for 24 hours and return it to a dealer afterwards. While 200,000 people participated, dealers disliked the promotion, the supply of computers was insufficient for demand, and many were returned in such a bad shape that they could no longer be sold. This marketing campaign caused CEO [John Sculley](/wiki/John_Sculley "John Sculley") to raise the price from US$1,995 (equivalent to $6,200 in 2025) to US$2,495 (equivalent to $7,700 in 2025).[[130]](#cite_note-Worst_Business-130)

### Jobs and Wozniak leave Apple

By early 1985, the Macintosh's failure to defeat the IBM PC[[124]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015185–187-124)[[125]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201684–88-125) triggered a power struggle between Jobs and CEO [John Sculley](/wiki/John_Sculley "John Sculley"), who had been hired two years earlier by Jobs[[132]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer2004156-132)[[133]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIsaacson2015153–154-133) using the famous line, "Do you want to sell sugar water for the rest of your life or come with me and change the world?"[[134]](#cite_note-134) Sculley and Jobs' visions for the company greatly differed. The former favored [open architecture](/wiki/Open_architecture "Open architecture") computers like the Apple II, sold to education, small business, and home markets less vulnerable to IBM. Jobs wanted the company to focus on the [closed architecture](/wiki/Closed_architecture "Closed architecture") Macintosh as a business alternative to the IBM PC. President and CEO Sculley had little control over chairman of the Board Jobs' Macintosh division; it and the Apple II division operated like separate companies, duplicating services.[[135]](#cite_note-robbeloth198510_11-135) Although its products provided 85% of Apple's sales in early 1985, the company's January 1985 [annual meeting](/wiki/Annual_meeting "Annual meeting") did not mention the Apple II division or employees. This frustrated Wozniak, who left active employment at Apple in the spring of that year to pursue other ventures, stating that the company had "been going in the wrong direction for the last five years" and sold most of his stock.[[136]](#cite_note-rice19850415-136)[[137]](#cite_note-wozemployee-137)[[138]](#cite_note-138) Despite these grievances, Wozniak left the company amicably and as of January 2018[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Apple_Inc.&action=edit) continued to represent Apple at events or in interviews,[[137]](#cite_note-wozemployee-137) receiving a stipend over the years for this role estimated in 2006 to be $120,000 per year.[[139]](#cite_note-iWoz-139) Wozniak also remained an Apple shareholder following his departure.[[140]](#cite_note-wozstock-140)

Wozniak's first venture after leaving Apple was founding [CL 9](/wiki/CL_9 "CL 9") in 1985 and creating the first programmable [universal remote](/wiki/Universal_remote "Universal remote") control two years later, called the "CORE", stating that "I never felt like I was turning my back on my own company [Apple]." He told Apple's director of engineering [Wayne Rosing](/wiki/Wayne_Rosing "Wayne Rosing") about his decision to step away from the company, but not his longtime business partner and friend Steve Jobs. Wozniak guessed that Jobs first heard the news from an article in *[The Wall Street Journal](/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal "The Wall Street Journal")* where he mentioned that he wasn't leaving because he was disgruntled with Apple, but that he was excited to build a new remote control. The article nevertheless included some of Wozniak's criticisms of Apple, and Wozniak later said "it was an accident, but it's been picked up by every book and every bit of history [since]."[[139]](#cite_note-iWoz-139): 289

In April 1985, Sculley decided to remove Jobs as the general manager of the Macintosh division, and gained unanimous support from the Apple board of directors.[[141]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchlender201687–92-141)[[132]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer2004156-132) Rather than submit to Sculley's direction, Jobs attempted to oust him from his leadership role at Apple.[[142]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer2004156–157-142) Informed by [Jean-Louis Gassée](/wiki/Jean-Louis_Gass%C3%A9e "Jean-Louis Gassée"), Sculley found out that Jobs had been attempting to organize a [coup](/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat#Other "Coup d'état") and called an emergency executive meeting at which Apple's executive staff sided with Sculley and stripped Jobs of all operational duties.[[142]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinzmayer2004156–157-142)

Jobs, while taking the position of chairman of the firm, had no influence over Apple's direction and resigned in September 1985, taking a number of Apple employees with him to found [NeXT Inc.](/wiki/NeXT "NeXT")[[143]](#cite_note-143) In a show of defiance at being set aside by Apple Computer, Jobs sold all but one of his 6.5 million shares in the company for $70 million. Jobs then acquired the [visual effects](/wiki/Visual_effects "Visual effects") house, [Pixar](/wiki/Pixar "Pixar") for $5M (equivalent to $14,700,000 in 2025). NeXT Inc. built computers with futuristic designs and the UNIX-derived [NEXTSTEP](/wiki/NEXTSTEP "NEXTSTEP") operating system. NeXTSTEP eventually developed into Mac OS X. While not a commercial success, due in part to its high price, the NeXT computer introduced important concepts to the history of the personal computer, including serving as the initial platform for [Tim Berners-Lee](/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee "Tim Berners-Lee") as he was developing the [World Wide Web](/wiki/World_Wide_Web "World Wide Web").[[144]](#cite_note-144)

Sculley reorganized the company, unifying sales and marketing in one division and product operations and development in another.[[145]](#cite_note-145)[[135]](#cite_note-robbeloth198510_11-135) Despite initial marketing difficulties, the Macintosh brand was eventually a success for Apple, due to its introduction of [desktop publishing](/wiki/Desktop_publishing "Desktop publishing")[[146]](#cite_note-pournelle198712-146) (and later [computer animation](/wiki/Computer_animation "Computer animation")) through Apple's partnership with [Adobe Systems](/wiki/Adobe_Systems "Adobe Systems"). This partnership bore both the [LaserWriter](/wiki/LaserWriter "LaserWriter") printer, one of the first commercially successful laser printers (containing a built-in interpreter for Adobe's [PostScript](/wiki/PostScript "PostScript") page description language), and [Aldus PageMaker](/wiki/Aldus_PageMaker "Aldus PageMaker"), the first commercially successful desktop publishing software. The Macintosh became the default platform for many arts industries including cinema, music, advertising, and publishing.

## 1985–1997: Sculley, Spindler, Amelio

Apple is a lot like Italy. It's a highly creative company, but with that comes chaos.

— [Regis McKenna](/wiki/Regis_McKenna "Regis McKenna")[[147]](#cite_note-fortune19960219-147)

### Corporate performance

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Macintosh_SE.jpg/250px-Macintosh_SE.jpg)

Under leadership of [John Sculley](/wiki/John_Sculley "John Sculley"), Apple issued its first corporate stock dividend on May 11, 1987. A month later on June 16, Apple stock split for the first time in a 2:1 split. Between March 1988 and January 1989, Apple undertook five acquisitions, including software companies Network Innovations,[[148]](#cite_note-148) Styleware,[[149]](#cite_note-149) Nashoba Systems,[[150]](#cite_note-150) and Coral Software,[[151]](#cite_note-151) as well as satellite communications company Orion Network Systems.[[152]](#cite_note-152)

Apple continued to sell both lines of its computers, the Apple II and the Macintosh. A few months after introducing the Mac, Apple released a compact version of the Apple II called the [Apple IIc](/wiki/Apple_IIc "Apple IIc"). And in 1986 Apple introduced the [Apple IIGS](/wiki/Apple_IIGS "Apple IIGS"), an Apple II positioned as something of a hybrid product with a mouse-driven, Mac-like operating environment. Even with the release of the first Macintosh, Apple II computers remained the main source of income for Apple for years.[[153]](#cite_note-153)

### The Mac family

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Powerbook_100_pose.jpg/250px-Powerbook_100_pose.jpg)

At the same time, the Mac was becoming a product family of its own. The original model evolved into the [Mac Plus](/wiki/Macintosh_Plus "Macintosh Plus") in 1986 and spawned the [Mac SE](/wiki/Macintosh_SE "Macintosh SE") and the [Mac II](/wiki/Macintosh_II "Macintosh II") in 1987 and the [Mac Classic](/wiki/Macintosh_Classic "Macintosh Classic") and [Mac LC](/wiki/Macintosh_LC "Macintosh LC") in 1990. Meanwhile, Apple attempted its first portable Macs: the failed [Macintosh Portable](/wiki/Macintosh_Portable "Macintosh Portable") in 1989 and then the more popular [PowerBook](/wiki/PowerBook "PowerBook") in 1991, a landmark product that established the modern form and [ergonomic](/wiki/Ergonomics "Ergonomics") layout of the laptop. Popular products and increasing revenues made this a good time for Apple. [MacAddict](/wiki/MacAddict "MacAddict") magazine has called 1989 to 1991 the "first golden age" of the Macintosh.

### Early-mid-1990s

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Apple_Newton_MessagePad_100_with_stylus.jpg/250px-Apple_Newton_MessagePad_100_with_stylus.jpg)

In the late 1980s, Apple's fiercest technological rivals were the [Amiga](/wiki/Amiga "Amiga") and [Atari ST](/wiki/Atari_ST "Atari ST") platforms. But computers based on the [IBM PC](/wiki/IBM_PC "IBM PC") were far more popular than all three, and by the 1990s, they finally had a comparable [GUI](/wiki/GUI "GUI") thanks to [Windows 3.0](/wiki/Windows_3.0 "Windows 3.0"), and were out-competing Apple.

Apple's response to the PC threat was a profusion of new Macintosh lines including [Quadra](/wiki/Macintosh_Quadra "Macintosh Quadra"), [Centris](/wiki/Macintosh_Centris "Macintosh Centris"), and [Performa](/wiki/Macintosh_Performa "Macintosh Performa"). These new lines were marketed poorly by what was now "arguably one of the worst-managed companies in the industry". There were too many models, differentiated by very minor graduations in [technical specifications](/wiki/Technical_specifications "Technical specifications"). The profusion of arbitrary model numbers confused consumers and hurt Apple's reputation for simplicity. Resellers like [Sears](/wiki/Sears,_Roebuck_and_Company "Sears, Roebuck and Company") and [CompUSA](/wiki/CompUSA "CompUSA") often failed to sell or even competently display these Macs. Inventory grew as Apple consistently underestimated demand for popular models and overestimated demand for others.[[154]](#cite_note-alsop19960205-154)

In 1991, Apple partnered with long-time competitor [IBM](/wiki/IBM "IBM") and [Motorola](/wiki/Motorola "Motorola") to form the [AIM alliance](/wiki/AIM_alliance "AIM alliance"), with the ultimate goal to create a revolutionary new computing platform, known as [PReP](/wiki/PReP "PReP"), using IBM and Motorola hardware and Apple software. As the first step, Apple started the [Power Macintosh](/wiki/Power_Macintosh "Power Macintosh") line in 1994, using [PowerPC](/wiki/PowerPC "PowerPC") processors from Motorola and IBM. The [RISC](/wiki/RISC "RISC") architecture of these processors differed substantially from the [Motorola 68000 series](/wiki/Motorola_68000_series "Motorola 68000 series") architecture used by previous Macs. Parts of Apple's operating system were rewritten to allow some older Mac software to run in [emulation](/wiki/Emulator "Emulator") on the PowerPC series.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] In 1993, Apple released the [Newton](/wiki/Newton_(platform) "Newton (platform)"), an early [personal digital assistant](/wiki/Personal_digital_assistant "Personal digital assistant") (PDA).

Apple by the mid-1990s was nonetheless in such serious difficulty that CFO Jim Graziano in 1995 asked the board of directors to find a buyer because the company was no longer financially viable, and quit when the board refused.[[155]](#cite_note-seymour19960109-155) *Fortune* in February 1996 wrote "the company that invented the personal computer will likely disappear". Noting that by the time readers received the magazine the company might be acquired by [Sun Microsystems](/wiki/Sun_Microsystems "Sun Microsystems") or [Philips Electronics](/wiki/Philips_Electronics "Philips Electronics"), *Fortune* said that $1,000 invested in Apple stock ten years earlier would be worth $2,900, compared to $22,000 in [Compaq](/wiki/Compaq "Compaq").[[147]](#cite_note-fortune19960219-147)

Contributing to Apple's problems was its inability to manufacture enough PowerPC-based Macintoshes to meet demand.[[155]](#cite_note-seymour19960109-155) Aware of the company's lack of scale, Apple had by then repeatedly sought larger partners for a decade. It almost persuaded [Digital Equipment Corporation](/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation "Digital Equipment Corporation") (DEC) to have the former manage the latter's personal computer business;[[147]](#cite_note-fortune19960219-147) it refused IBM's offer to purchase the company, but later unsuccessfully sought another IBM offer;[[156]](#cite_note-markoff19970901-156) and at one point was "hours away" from an acquisition by Sun,[[154]](#cite_note-alsop19960205-154)[[157]](#cite_note-preimesberger20110225-157) one of several attempts by Sun to buy the company. Markkula said during Sun merger negotiations, "Since 1986, we held serious discussions with DEC, [Kodak](/wiki/Kodak "Kodak"), [Sony](/wiki/Sony "Sony"), Sun, Compaq, IBM, and other companies I'd rather not name now. They were very thoughtful discussions. We considered everything from 'Let's trade technology' to 'Let's put the companies together' with each one of them".[[147]](#cite_note-fortune19960219-147)

### Need for a new OS

In 1994 Apple launched [eWorld](/wiki/EWorld "EWorld"), an online service providing email, news and a bulletin board system to replace [AppleLink](/wiki/AppleLink "AppleLink"). It was shut down in 1996.[[158]](#cite_note-158) In 1995, to achieve deeper market penetration and extra revenue, Apple officially began licensing the Mac OS and Macintosh ROMs to 3rd party manufacturers. The "[Clonintoshes](/wiki/Clonintosh "Clonintosh")" competed with Apple's own Mac's and reduced Apple's sales.[[159]](#cite_note-lemjosh-159) Apple had market share of over 10% until Jobs was re-hired in 1997 as interim CEO to replace Gil Amelio, and found a loophole to terminate the Macintosh OS licensing program. Macintosh's market share fell to around 3%.[[160]](#cite_note-160)

Among Apple's most important needs was a new operating system.[[147]](#cite_note-fortune19960219-147) During the 90's, "project *Pink*" had Apple and IBM collaborating to develop a new operating system, named *[Taligent](/wiki/Taligent "Taligent")* to replace *[System 7](/wiki/System_7 "System 7")*. Infighting resulted in Apple leaving the project and IBM finishing it. Apple started project *[Copland](/wiki/Copland_(operating_system) "Copland (operating system)")*, another effort to replace System 7, but it was affected by [Feature creep](/wiki/Feature_creep "Feature creep") then [Development hell](/wiki/Development_hell "Development hell") due to software planned for Taligent being reworked for Copland. Ultimately Copland was scrapped.[[161]](#cite_note-161) With the Copland project in disarray, Apple decided it needed to acquire another company's operating system. Candidates considered were [Sun](/wiki/Sun_Microsystems "Sun Microsystems")'s [Solaris](/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system) "Solaris (operating system)") and [Windows NT](/wiki/Windows_NT "Windows NT"). Hancock was in favor of Solaris, while Amelio preferred Windows. Amelio called [Bill Gates](/wiki/Bill_Gates "Bill Gates"), and Gates promised Microsoft engineers would port [QuickDraw](/wiki/QuickDraw "QuickDraw") to NT.[[162]](#cite_note-162)

### Acquisition of NeXT

In 1996, the struggling [NeXT](/wiki/NeXT "NeXT") company beat Be Inc.'s [BeOS](/wiki/BeOS "BeOS") bid to sell its operating system to Apple.[[163]](#cite_note-163) On December 20, 1996, Apple announced it would purchase NeXT, and its NeXTstep operating system, for $429 million and 1.5 million shares of Apple stock.[[164]](#cite_note-apple-acquisition-164) This brought Jobs back to Apple's management for the first time since 1985, and NeXT technology became the foundation of the [Mac OS X](/wiki/Mac_OS_X "Mac OS X") operating system.

## 1997–2001: Apple's comeback

If I were running Apple, I would milk the Macintosh for all it's worth--and get busy on the next great thing. The PC wars are over. Done. Microsoft won a long time ago.

— Jobs, February 1996[[147]](#cite_note-fortune19960219-147)

### Return of Steve Jobs

On July 9, 1997, [Gil Amelio](/wiki/Gil_Amelio "Gil Amelio") was ousted as CEO of Apple by the board of directors. [Fred D. Anderson](/wiki/Fred_D._Anderson "Fred D. Anderson") was the head of the directors in short term and obtained short-term working capital from the banks in July 1997.[[165]](#cite_note-165)[[166]](#cite_note-166) In August 1997, Jobs stepped in as the interim CEO to begin a critical restructuring of the company's product line.[[5]](#cite_note-interim_CEO-5) He eventually became CEO and served in that position from January 2000 to August 2011.[[6]](#cite_note-norr-6) On August 24, 2011, Jobs resigned his position as chief executive officer of Apple before his long battle with pancreatic cancer took his life on October 5, 2011.[[167]](#cite_note-167) On November 10, 1997, Apple introduced the [Apple Store](/wiki/Apple_Store_(online) "Apple Store (online)"), an online retail store based upon the [WebObjects](/wiki/WebObjects "WebObjects") application server the company had acquired in its purchase of [NeXT](/wiki/NeXT "NeXT"). The new direct sales outlet was tied to a new build-to-order manufacturing strategy.[[168]](#cite_note-168)[[169]](#cite_note-169)

### Microsoft deal

At the 1997 [Macworld Expo](/wiki/Macworld_Expo "Macworld Expo"), Jobs announced that Apple would begin a partnership with [Microsoft](/wiki/Microsoft "Microsoft"), with terms including a five-year commitment from Microsoft to release [Microsoft Office](/wiki/Microsoft_Office "Microsoft Office") for Macintosh, and a US$150 million investment in Apple. The long-standing dispute over whether Windows infringed Apple patents was settled,[[170]](#cite_note-Microsoft_investment-170) and [Internet Explorer](/wiki/Internet_Explorer_for_Mac "Internet Explorer for Mac") would ship as the Macintosh's default browser, with the user able to have a preference. Microsoft chairman [Bill Gates](/wiki/Bill_Gates "Bill Gates") appeared on-screen explaining plans for developing Mac software, and expressing excitement to be helping Apple return to success. Jobs addressed the audience:

If we want to move forward and see Apple healthy and prospering again, we have to let go of a few things here. We have to let go of this notion that for Apple to win, Microsoft has to lose. We have to embrace a notion that for Apple to win, Apple has to do a really good job. And if others are going to help us that's great, because we need all the help we can get, and if we screw up and we don't do a good job, it's not somebody else's fault, it's our fault. So I think that is a very important perspective. If we want Microsoft Office on the Mac, we better treat the company that puts it out with a little bit of gratitude; we'd like their software.
So, the era of setting this up as a competition between Apple and Microsoft is over as far as I'm concerned. This is about getting Apple healthy, this is about Apple being able to make incredibly great contributions to the industry and to get healthy and prosper again.

The day before the announcement Apple had a market cap of $2.46 billion,[[171]](#cite_note-171) and had ended its previous quarter with quarterly revenues of US$1.7 billion and cash reserves of US$1.2 billion,[[172]](#cite_note-172) making the US$150 million amount of the investment largely symbolic. Apple CFO Fred Anderson stated that Apple would use the additional funds to invest in its core markets of education and creative content.[[170]](#cite_note-Microsoft_investment-170)

### iMac, iBook, and Power Mac G4

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/IMac_Bondi_Blue.jpg/250px-IMac_Bondi_Blue.jpg)

While discontinuing Apple's licensing of its operating system to third-party computer manufacturers, one of Jobs's first moves as new acting CEO was to develop the [iMac](/wiki/IMac "IMac"), which bought Apple time to restructure. The original iMac integrated a CRT display and CPU into a streamlined, translucent plastic body. The line became a sales smash, moving about one million units each year. It helped re-introduce Apple to the media and public and announced the company's new emphasis on the design and aesthetics of its products.

In 1999, Apple introduced the [Power Mac G4](/wiki/Power_Mac_G4 "Power Mac G4"), which utilized the Motorola-made PowerPC 7400 containing a 128-bit instruction unit known as [AltiVec](/wiki/AltiVec "AltiVec"), its flagship processor line. Apple unveiled the [iBook](/wiki/IBook "IBook") that year, its first consumer-oriented laptop, the first Macintosh to support the use of [Wireless LAN](/wiki/Wireless_LAN "Wireless LAN") via the optional AirPort card. Based on the 802.11b standard, it helped popularize Wireless LAN technology to connect computers to networks.

### Mac OS X

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Apple_Headquarters_in_Cupertino.jpg/250px-Apple_Headquarters_in_Cupertino.jpg)

In 2001, Apple introduced [Mac OS X](/wiki/MacOS "MacOS"), an operating system based on [NeXT](/wiki/NeXT "NeXT")'s [NeXTSTEP](/wiki/NeXTSTEP "NeXTSTEP") and incorporating parts of the [FreeBSD](/wiki/FreeBSD "FreeBSD") kernel.[[173]](#cite_note-173) Aimed at consumers and professionals alike, Mac OS X married the stability, reliability and security of [Unix](/wiki/Unix "Unix") with the ease of a completely overhauled user interface. To help users transition, the new operating system allowed the use of Mac OS 9 applications through the [Classic](/wiki/Classic_(Mac_OS_X) "Classic (Mac OS X)") environment. Apple's [Carbon](/wiki/Carbon_(API) "Carbon (API)") API allowed developers to adapt Mac OS 9 software to use Mac OS X's features.

### Retail stores

In May 2001, after much speculation, Apple announced the opening of a line of Apple retail stores, to be located throughout the major U.S. computer buying markets. The stores were designed for two primary purposes: to stem the tide of Apple's declining share of the computer market and to respond to poor marketing of Apple products at third-party retail outlets.

## 2001–2007: iPods, iTunes Store, Intel transition

### iPod

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/IPod_1G.jpg)
![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/IPod_mini.png/250px-IPod_mini.png)

In October 2001, Apple introduced its first [iPod](/wiki/IPod "IPod") portable digital audio player. Then iPod started as a 5 gigabyte player capable of storing around 1000 songs. It then evolved into an array of products including the [Mini](/wiki/IPod_Mini "IPod Mini") (discontinued), the [iPod Touch](/wiki/IPod_Touch "IPod Touch") (discontinued), the [Shuffle](/wiki/IPod_Shuffle "IPod Shuffle") (discontinued), the [iPod Classic](/wiki/IPod_Classic "IPod Classic") (discontinued), the [Nano](/wiki/IPod_Nano "IPod Nano") (discontinued), the [iPhone](/wiki/IPhone "IPhone") and the [iPad](/wiki/IPad "IPad"). Since March 2011, the largest storage capacity for an iPod has been 160 gigabytes.[[174]](#cite_note-174) Speaking to software developers on June 6, 2005, Jobs said the company's share of the entire portable music device market stood at 76%.[[175]](#cite_note-175)

The iPod gave an enormous lift to Apple's financial results.[[176]](#cite_note-176) In the quarter ending March 26, 2005, Apple earned US$290 million, or 34¢ a share, on sales of US$3.24 billion. The year before in the same quarter, Apple earned just US$46 million, or 6¢ a share, on revenue of US$1.91 billion.

### Moving on from colored plastics and the PowerPC G3

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/IMac_G4_sunflower7.png/250px-IMac_G4_sunflower7.png)

In early 2002, Apple unveiled a completely redesigned iMac, using the G4 processor and a [liquid-crystal display](/wiki/Liquid-crystal_display "Liquid-crystal display"). The new [iMac G4](/wiki/IMac_G4 "IMac G4") design had a white hemispherical base and a flat panel all-digital display supported by a swiveling chrome neck. After several iterations increasing the processing speed and screen sizes from 15" to 17" to 20" the iMac G4 was discontinued and replaced by the [iMac G5](/wiki/IMac_G5 "IMac G5") in the summer of 2004.

Later in 2002, Apple released the [Xserve](/wiki/Xserve "Xserve") [1U](/wiki/Rack_unit "Rack unit") [rack mounted](/wiki/Rack_mount "Rack mount") server. Originally featuring two G4 chips, the Xserve was unusual for Apple in two ways. It represented an earnest effort to enter the enterprise computer market, and it was cheaper than competitors' similar machines. This was largely due to Fast ATA drives as opposed to the SCSI hard drives used in traditional rack-mounted servers. Apple later released the [Xserve RAID](/wiki/Xserve_RAID "Xserve RAID"), a 14 drive [RAID](/wiki/RAID "RAID") that was again cheaper than competing systems.

In mid-2003, Jobs launched the [Power Mac G5](/wiki/Power_Mac_G5 "Power Mac G5"), based on [IBM](/wiki/IBM "IBM")'s [G5 processor](/wiki/PowerPC_970 "PowerPC 970"). Its all-metal [anodized aluminum](/wiki/Anodized_aluminum "Anodized aluminum") chassis finished Apple's transition away from colored plastics in their computers. Apple claims this was the first 64-bit computer sold to the general public. The Power Mac G5 was used by Virginia Tech to build its prototype [System X](/wiki/System_X_(supercomputer) "System X (supercomputer)") supercomputing cluster, which at the time was considered the third-fastest supercomputer in the world. It cost only US$5.2 million to build, far less than the previous No. 3 and other ranking supercomputers. Apple's Xserves were updated to use the G5 as well. They replaced the Power Mac G5 machines as the main building block of [Virginia Tech](/wiki/Virginia_Tech "Virginia Tech")'s [System X](/wiki/System_X_(supercomputer) "System X (supercomputer)"), which was ranked in November 2004 as the world's seventh-fastest [supercomputer](/wiki/Supercomputer "Supercomputer").[[177]](#cite_note-177)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/IMac_G5_-_Frontal_view.png/250px-IMac_G5_-_Frontal_view.png)

A new iMac based on the G5 processor was unveiled August 31, 2004, and was made available in mid-September. This model dispensed with the base altogether, placing the CPU and the rest of the computing hardware behind the flat-panel screen, which is suspended from a streamlined aluminum foot. This new iMac, dubbed the [iMac G5](/wiki/IMac_G5 "IMac G5"), was the "world's thinnest desktop computer",[[178]](#cite_note-178) measuring in at around two inches (around 5 centimeters).[[179]](#cite_note-179)

In 2004, after creating a sizable financial base to work with, the company began experimenting with new parts from new suppliers. Apple could produce new designs quickly, and released the iPod Video, then the iPod Classic, and eventually the iPod touch and iPhone. On April 29, 2005, Apple released [Mac OS X v10.4](/wiki/Mac_OS_X_v10.4 "Mac OS X v10.4") "Tiger".

Apple's successful [PowerBook](/wiki/PowerBook "PowerBook") and [iBook](/wiki/IBook "IBook") relied on previous generation G4 architecture produced by [Freescale Semiconductor](/wiki/Freescale_Semiconductor "Freescale Semiconductor"), a spin-off from Motorola. IBM engineers had some success in making their PowerPC G5 processor consume less power and run cooler, but not enough to run in iBook or PowerBook formats. In October 2005, Apple released the Power Mac G5 Dual featuring a Dual-Core processor – two cores in one rather than two separate processors. The Power Mac G5 Quad uses two Dual-Core processors. The Power Mac G5 Dual cores run individually at 2.0 GHz or 2.3 GHz. The Power Mac G5 Quad cores run individually at 2.5 GHz, and all variations have a graphics processor with 256-bit memory bandwidth.[[180]](#cite_note-180)

### Retail store expansion

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Apple_Store_Ginza_%282006.05%29.jpg/250px-Apple_Store_Ginza_%282006.05%29.jpg)

Initially, [Apple Stores](/wiki/Apple_Store "Apple Store") were only in the United States, but in late 2003, Apple opened its first Apple Store abroad, in Tokyo's [Ginza](/wiki/Ginza "Ginza") district. It was followed by a store in [Osaka](/wiki/Osaka,_Osaka "Osaka, Osaka"), Japan in August 2004. In 2005, Apple opened stores in [Nagoya](/wiki/Nagoya,_Aichi "Nagoya, Aichi"), the [Shibuya](/wiki/Shibuya,_Tokyo "Shibuya, Tokyo") district of Tokyo, [Fukuoka](/wiki/Fukuoka,_Fukuoka "Fukuoka, Fukuoka"), and [Sendai](/wiki/Sendai,_Miyagi "Sendai, Miyagi"). A store opened in [Sapporo](/wiki/Sapporo,_Hokkaid%C5%8D "Sapporo, Hokkaidō") in 2006. Apple's first European store opened in London, on [Regent Street](/wiki/Regent_Street "Regent Street"), in November 2004. A store in the [Bullring](/wiki/Bull_Ring,_Birmingham "Bull Ring, Birmingham") shopping centre in [Birmingham](/wiki/Birmingham "Birmingham") opened in April 2005, and the [Bluewater shopping centre](/wiki/Bluewater_Shopping_Centre "Bluewater Shopping Centre") in [Dartford](/wiki/Dartford "Dartford"), Kent opened in July 2005. Apple opened its first store in Canada in the middle of 2005 at the [Yorkdale Shopping Centre](/wiki/Yorkdale_Shopping_Centre "Yorkdale Shopping Centre") in [North York](/wiki/North_York "North York"), [Toronto](/wiki/Toronto "Toronto"). Later in 2005 Apple opened the [Meadowhall](/wiki/Meadowhall_Centre "Meadowhall Centre") Store in [Sheffield](/wiki/Sheffield "Sheffield") and the [Trafford Centre](/wiki/Trafford_Centre "Trafford Centre") Store in [Manchester](/wiki/Manchester "Manchester"), UK. Later additions in the London area include [Brent Cross](/wiki/Brent_Cross "Brent Cross") (January 2006), [Westfield](/wiki/Westfield_London "Westfield London") in Shepherd's Bush (September 2008), and [Covent Garden](/wiki/Covent_Garden "Covent Garden") (August 2010), which at 40,000 square feet (3,700 m2) was, as of 2015[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Apple_Inc.&action=edit), the largest Apple Store in the world.[[181]](#cite_note-181)

Apple opened several "larger" stores in October 2004 to capture markets where demand does not necessarily dictate a full-scale store. The first of these stores was opened at [Stanford Shopping Center](/wiki/Stanford_Shopping_Center "Stanford Shopping Center") in [Palo Alto, California](/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California "Palo Alto, California"). These stores are only one half the square footage of the smallest normal store.

### Apple and "i" Web services

In 2000, Apple introduced iTools, a set of free web-based tools that included an email account, internet greeting cards called iCards, a Web site review service called [iReview](/wiki/IReview "IReview"), and "KidSafe", to prevent children browsing inappropriate websites. The latter two services were canceled because of lack of success. iCards and email were integrated into Apple's [.Mac](/wiki/.Mac ".Mac") subscription-based service introduced in 2002 and discontinued in mid-2008 to make way for MobileMe, coinciding with the iPhone 3G release. MobileMe, at the same US$99.00 annual subscription as its dotMac predecessor, featured "push" services to instantly and automatically send emails, contacts and calendar updates directly to users' iPhones. Controversy around the release of MobileMe resulted in downtime and a significantly longer release window. Apple extended existing MobileMe subscriptions by 30 days free-of-charge.[[182]](#cite_note-182) At the WWDC event in June 2011, Apple announced [iCloud](/wiki/ICloud "ICloud"), keeping most MobileMe services but dropping iDisk, Gallery, and iWeb. It added Find my Mac, iTunes Match, Photo Stream, Documents & Data Backup, and iCloud backup for iOS devices. The service requires iOS 5 and OS X 10.7 Lion.

### iTunes Store

The [iTunes Music Store](/wiki/ITunes_Store "ITunes Store") was launched in April 2003, with 2 million downloads in the first 16 days. Music was purchased through the [iTunes](/wiki/ITunes "ITunes") application, which was initially Macintosh-only; in October 2003, support for [Windows](/wiki/Microsoft_Windows "Microsoft Windows") was added. Initially, the music store was only available in the United States due to licensing restrictions.

In June 2004 Apple opened its iTunes Music Store in the United Kingdom, France, and Germany. A version for the [European Union](/wiki/European_Union "European Union") version opened October 2004, but it was not initially available in the [Republic of Ireland](/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland "Republic of Ireland") due to the intransigence of the [Irish Recorded Music Association](/wiki/Irish_Recorded_Music_Association "Irish Recorded Music Association") (IRMA) but was opened there a few months later on Thursday, January 6, 2005. A version for Canada opened in December 2004. On May 10, 2005, the iTunes Music Store was expanded to [Denmark](/wiki/Denmark "Denmark"), Norway, [Sweden](/wiki/Sweden "Sweden"), and [Switzerland](/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland").

On December 16, 2004, Apple sold its 200 millionth song on the iTunes Music Store to Ryan Alekman from [Belchertown, Massachusetts](/wiki/Belchertown,_Massachusetts "Belchertown, Massachusetts"). The download was *[The Complete U2](/wiki/The_Complete_U2 "The Complete U2")*, by [U2](/wiki/U2 "U2").[[183]](#cite_note-183) Just under three months later Apple sold its 300 millionth song on March 2, 2005.[[184]](#cite_note-184) On July 17, 2005, the iTunes Music Store sold its 500 millionth song.[[185]](#cite_note-185) At that point, songs were selling at an accelerating annualized rate of more than 500 million.

On October 25, 2005, the iTunes Store went live in Australia, with songs selling for [A$](/wiki/Australian_dollar "Australian dollar")1.69 each, albums at (generally) A$16.99 and music videos and Pixar short films at A$3.39. Before the loophole was closed, people in [New Zealand](/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand") were briefly able to buy music from the Australian store On February 23, 2006, the iTunes Music Store sold its 1 billionth song.[[186]](#cite_note-186)

The iTunes Music Store changed its name to iTunes Store on September 12, 2006, when it began offering video content (TV shows and movies) for sale. Since iTunes' inception, it has sold over 2 billion songs, 1.2 billion of which were sold in 2006. Since downloadable TV and movie content was added 50 million TV episodes and 1.3 million movies have been downloaded. In early 2010, Apple celebrated the 10 billionth song downloaded from the iTunes Music Store.[[187]](#cite_note-187)

### Intel transition

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/MacBook_Pro.png/250px-MacBook_Pro.png)

In a keynote address on June 6, 2005, Jobs announced that Apple would produce Intel-based Macintosh computers beginning in 2006.[[188]](#cite_note-188) Jobs confirmed rumors that the company had been secretly producing versions of [Mac OS X](/wiki/MacOS "MacOS") for both PowerPC and Intel processors over the past 5 years, and that the transition to Intel processor systems would last until the end of 2007. Rumors of cross-platform compatibility had been spurred by the fact that Mac OS X is based on [OPENSTEP](/wiki/OPENSTEP "OPENSTEP"), an operating system that was available for many platforms. Apple's own [Darwin](/wiki/Darwin_(operating_system) "Darwin (operating system)"), the [open source](/wiki/Open-source_software "Open-source software") underpinnings of Mac OS X, was also available for Intel's x86 architecture.[[189]](#cite_note-189)[[190]](#cite_note-190)[[191]](#cite_note-191)

On January 10, 2006, the Intel-based [iMac](/wiki/IMac "IMac") and [MacBook Pro](/wiki/MacBook_Pro "MacBook Pro") were introduced,[[192]](#cite_note-192)[[193]](#cite_note-193) based on the [Intel Core](/wiki/Intel_Core "Intel Core") Duo platform. They came alongside news that Apple would complete the transition to Intel processors on all hardware by the end of 2006, a year ahead of the originally quoted schedule.

## 2007–2011: Apple Inc., iPhone, iOS, iPad

On January 9, 2007, Apple Computer, Inc. shortened its name to simply Apple Inc. In his [Macworld Expo](/wiki/Macworld_Expo "Macworld Expo") keynote address, Jobs explained that with their current product mix consisting of the iPod and Apple TV as well as their Macintosh brand, Apple really wasn't just a computer company anymore. At the same address, Jobs revealed a product that would revolutionize an industry in which Apple had never previously competed: the Apple [iPhone](/wiki/IPhone "IPhone"). The iPhone combined Apple's first widescreen iPod with the world's first mobile device boasting visual voicemail, and an internet communicator able to run a fully-functional version of Apple's web browser, [Safari](/wiki/Safari_(web_browser) "Safari (web browser)"), on the then-named [iPhone OS](/wiki/IOS_version_history#Releases "IOS version history") (later renamed [iOS](/wiki/IOS "IOS")).

### iOS evolution: iPhone and iPad

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/IPhone_First_Generation.jpg/250px-IPhone_First_Generation.jpg)

The first version of the iPhone became publicly available on June 29, 2007, in selected countries/markets. It was another 12 months before the [iPhone 3G](/wiki/IPhone_3G "IPhone 3G") became available on July 11, 2008. Apple announced the [iPhone 3GS](/wiki/IPhone_3GS "IPhone 3GS") on June 8, 2009, along with plans to release it later in June, July, and August, starting with the U.S., Canada, and major European countries on June 19. This 12-month iteration cycle has continued with the [iPhone 4](/wiki/IPhone_4 "IPhone 4") model arriving in similar fashion in 2010, a Verizon model was released in February 2011, and a Sprint model in October 2011, shortly after Jobs' death.

On February 10, 2011, the iPhone 4 was made available on both Verizon Wireless and AT&T. Now two iPod types are multi-touch: the iPod nano and the iPod touch, a big advance in technology. Apple TV currently has a 2nd-generation model, which is 4 times smaller than the original Apple TV. Apple has also gone wireless, selling a wireless trackpad, keyboard, mouse, and external hard drive. Wired accessories are still available.

The Apple [iPad](/wiki/IPad "IPad") was announced on January 27, 2010, with retail availability commencing in April and systematically growing in markets throughout 2010. The iPad fits into Apple's iOS product line, being twice the screen size of an iPhone without the phone abilities. While there were initial fears of product cannibalization the FY2010 financial results released in Jan 2011 included commentary of a reverse 'halo' effect, where iPad sales were leading to increased sales of iMacs and MacBooks.[[194]](#cite_note-194)

### Resurgence compared to Microsoft

Since 2005, Apple's revenues, profits, and stock price have grown significantly. On May 26, 2010, Apple's stock market value overtook Microsoft's,[[195]](#cite_note-195)[[196]](#cite_note-196)[[197]](#cite_note-197) and Apple's revenues surpassed those of Microsoft in the third quarter of 2010.[[198]](#cite_note-198)[[199]](#cite_note-199) After giving their results for the first quarter of 2011, Microsoft's net profits of $5.2 billion were lower for the quarter than those of Apple, which earned $6 billion in net profit for the quarter.[[200]](#cite_note-Apple_Microsoft_revenue-200)[[201]](#cite_note-201) The late April announcement of profits by the companies marked the first time in 20 years that Microsoft's profits had been lower than Apple's,[[202]](#cite_note-Apple_Microsoft_20_years-202) a situation described by *[Ars Technica](/wiki/Ars_Technica "Ars Technica")* as "unimaginable a decade ago".[[200]](#cite_note-Apple_Microsoft_revenue-200)

*[The Guardian](/wiki/The_Guardian "The Guardian")* reported that one of the reasons for the change was because PC software, where Microsoft dominates, has become less important compared to the tablet and smartphone markets, where Apple has a strong presence.[[202]](#cite_note-Apple_Microsoft_20_years-202) One reason for this was a surprise drop in PC sales in the quarter.[[202]](#cite_note-Apple_Microsoft_20_years-202) Another issue for Microsoft was that its online search business had lost a lot of money, with a loss of $700 million in the first quarter of 2010.[[202]](#cite_note-Apple_Microsoft_20_years-202)

## 2011–2020: Restructuring and Apple Watch

On March 2, 2011, Apple unveiled the iPad's second-generation model, the [iPad 2](/wiki/IPad_2 "IPad 2"). Like the 4th-generation iPod Touch and iPhone, the iPad 2 comes with a front-facing camera as well as a rear-facing camera, along with three new apps that utilize these new features: Camera, [FaceTime](/wiki/FaceTime "FaceTime"), and [Photo Booth](/wiki/Photo_Booth "Photo Booth"). On August 24, Jobs resigned from his position as CEO,[[203]](#cite_note-203) with Tim Cook taking his place.

On October 29, 2012, Apple announced structural changes to increase collaboration between hardware, software, and services.[[204]](#cite_note-204) This involved the departure of [Scott Forstall](/wiki/Scott_Forstall "Scott Forstall"), responsible for the launch of iOS (iPhone OS at the time of launch), who was replaced with [Craig Federighi](/wiki/Craig_Federighi "Craig Federighi") as head of iOS and OS X teams. [Jony Ive](/wiki/Jony_Ive "Jony Ive") became head of HI (Human Interface), whilst [Eddy Cue](/wiki/Eddy_Cue "Eddy Cue") was announced as head of online services including Siri and Maps. The most notable short term difference of this restructuring was the launch of [iOS 7](/wiki/IOS_7 "IOS 7"), the first version of the operating system to use a drastically different design to its predecessors, headed by Jony Ive,[[205]](#cite_note-205) followed by [OS X Yosemite](/wiki/OS_X_Yosemite "OS X Yosemite") a year later with a similar design.

During this time, Apple released the [iPhone 5](/wiki/IPhone_5 "IPhone 5"), the first iPhone to have a screen larger than 3.5",[[206]](#cite_note-206) the [iPod Touch 5](/wiki/IPod_Touch_5 "IPod Touch 5") with a 4" screen, the [iPhone 5S](/wiki/IPhone_5S "IPhone 5S") with fingerprint scanning technology in the form of [Touch ID](/wiki/Touch_ID "Touch ID"), and [iPhone 6](/wiki/IPhone_6 "IPhone 6") and [iPhone 6 Plus](/wiki/IPhone_6_Plus "IPhone 6 Plus"), with screens at 4.7" and 5.5". They released the [3rd-generation iPad](/wiki/IPad_(3rd_generation) "IPad (3rd generation)") with Retina Display, followed by the [4th-generation iPad](/wiki/IPad_(4th_generation) "IPad (4th generation)") just half a year later. The [iPad Mini](/wiki/IPad_Mini "IPad Mini") was announced alongside the iPad 4th gen, and was the first to feature a smaller screen than 9.7". This was followed by the [iPad Mini 2](/wiki/IPad_Mini_2 "IPad Mini 2") with Retina Display in 2013, alongside the [iPad Air](/wiki/IPad_Air "IPad Air"), a continuation of the original 9.7" range of iPads, which was subsequently followed by the [iPad Air 2](/wiki/IPad_Air_2 "IPad Air 2") with Touch ID in 2014. Apple released various major Mac updates, including the [MacBook Pro with Retina Display](/wiki/MacBook_Pro_with_Retina_Display "MacBook Pro with Retina Display"),[[207]](#cite_note-207) whilst discontinuing the original [MacBook](/wiki/MacBook "MacBook") range for a short period, before reintroducing it in 2015 with various new features, a Retina Display and a new design that implemented [USB-C](/wiki/USB-C "USB-C"), while removing all other ports.[[208]](#cite_note-208) The [Mac Pro](/wiki/Mac_Pro "Mac Pro") and [iMac](/wiki/IMac "IMac") were updated with more power and a drastically smaller and thinner profile. On November 25, 2013, Apple acquired a company called PrimeSense.[[209]](#cite_note-209)

On May 28, 2014, Apple acquired [Beats Electronics](/wiki/Beats_Electronics "Beats Electronics"), producers of the popular [Beats by Dre](/wiki/Beats_by_Dre "Beats by Dre") headphone and speaker range, as well as streaming service [Beats Music](/wiki/Beats_Music "Beats Music"). On September 9, 2014, Apple announced the [Apple Watch](/wiki/Apple_Watch "Apple Watch"), the first new product range since the departure of Jobs.[[210]](#cite_note-210) The product cannot function beyond basic features without being within Bluetooth or WiFi range to an iPhone and contains basic applications (many acting as a remote for other devices, such as a music remote, or a control for an Apple TV) and fitness tracking. The Apple Watch received mixed reviews, with critics suggesting that whilst the device showed promise, it lacked a clear purpose, similar to many of the devices already on the market.[[211]](#cite_note-211) The Apple Watch was released on April 24, 2015.[[212]](#cite_note-212)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Apple_Watch_Demo.jpg/250px-Apple_Watch_Demo.jpg)

On September 9, 2015, Apple announced the [iPhone 6S](/wiki/IPhone_6S "IPhone 6S") and [iPhone 6S Plus](/wiki/IPhone_6S_Plus "IPhone 6S Plus") with 3D Touch, the [iPad Pro](/wiki/IPad_Pro "IPad Pro"), and the fourth-generation [Apple TV](/wiki/Apple_TV_(device) "Apple TV (device)"), along with the fourth-generation [iPad Mini](/wiki/IPad_Mini "IPad Mini").
On March 21, 2016, Apple announced the [first-generation iPhone SE](/wiki/IPhone_SE_(1st_generation) "IPhone SE (1st generation)") and the smaller [iPad Pro](/wiki/IPad_Pro "IPad Pro").

On September 7, 2016, Apple announced the [iPhone 7](/wiki/IPhone_7 "IPhone 7") and [iPhone 7 Plus](/wiki/IPhone_7_Plus "IPhone 7 Plus") with an improved camera and a faster processor than the previous generation. The iPhone 7 and iPhone 7 Plus have high storage options.
On October 27, 2016, Apple announced the new 13 and 15 inch [MacBook Pro](/wiki/MacBook_Pro "MacBook Pro") with a retina Touch Bar.
On March 21, 2017, Apple announced the [iPad (2017)](/wiki/IPad_(2017) "IPad (2017)"). This is the iPad Air 2 successor, equipped with a faster processor, and starts at $329. Apple also announced the (Product)RED iPhone 7 and iPhone 7 Plus.

On June 5, 2017, Apple announced [iOS 11](/wiki/IOS_11 "IOS 11") as well as new versions of [macOS](/wiki/MacOS "MacOS"), [watchOS](/wiki/WatchOS "WatchOS"), and [tvOS](/wiki/TvOS "TvOS"). Updated versions of the iMac, MacBook Pro, and MacBook were released, along with the 10.5 and 12.9 inch [iPad Pro](/wiki/IPad_Pro "IPad Pro"), and "[HomePod](/wiki/HomePod "HomePod")", a Siri speaker similar to the [Amazon Echo](/wiki/Amazon_Echo "Amazon Echo"). On September 12, at the [Steve Jobs Theater](/wiki/Steve_Jobs_Theater "Steve Jobs Theater"), Apple introduced the [iPhone 8](/wiki/IPhone_8 "IPhone 8") and iPhone 8 Plus with better camera features, more improvements in [product design](/wiki/Product_design "Product design"), [user experience](/wiki/User_experience "User experience"), performance and more, and announced the [iPhone X](/wiki/IPhone_X "IPhone X") with facial recognition technology and wireless charging. Apple announced the 4K Apple TV with 4K, HDR and Dolby Vision experience, and the Apple Watch Series 3, supporting a cellular connection, running [watchOS 4](/wiki/WatchOS "WatchOS").

In March 2018, [*Bloomberg*](/wiki/Bloomberg_News "Bloomberg News") reported that Apple was developing [MicroLED](/wiki/MicroLED "MicroLED") screens for the iPhone, iPad, Mac, watch, AR glasses, and [electric car](/wiki/Apple_car_project "Apple car project").[[213]](#cite_note-213)[[214]](#cite_note-214)[[215]](#cite_note-215) It was linked to a Research and Development facility in [Santa Clara, California](/wiki/Santa_Clara,_California "Santa Clara, California") code named Aria[[216]](#cite_note-216) by the *Bay Area News Group*.[[217]](#cite_note-217) On September 12, at the [Steve Jobs Theater](/wiki/Steve_Jobs_Theater "Steve Jobs Theater"), Apple introduced the [iPhone XS](/wiki/IPhone_XS "IPhone XS"), [iPhone XS Max](/wiki/IPhone_XS "IPhone XS"), and [iPhone XR](/wiki/IPhone_XR "IPhone XR"), running [iOS 12](/wiki/IOS_12 "IOS 12"), with improved facial recognition and HDR in the display as well as better cameras for all 3 phones. They also announced the Apple Watch Series 4, running [watchOS 5](/wiki/WatchOS "WatchOS"), with an all-new design and larger display as well as many more health-related features.

In October 2018, *Bloomberg* reported that, as early as 2015, a specialized unit of China's [People's Liberation Army](/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army "People's Liberation Army") began inserting chips into [Supermicro](/wiki/Supermicro "Supermicro") servers that allowed for [backdoor](/wiki/Backdoor_(computing) "Backdoor (computing)") access to them.[[218]](#cite_note-BB-218) Approximately 30 companies reportedly had their servers compromised via the chips, including Apple Inc.[[218]](#cite_note-BB-218) On September 20, 2019, the [iPhone 11](/wiki/IPhone_11 "IPhone 11"), [iPhone 11 Pro](/wiki/IPhone_11_Pro "IPhone 11 Pro"), and iPhone 11 Pro Max were introduced. The iPhone 11 Pro was the first iPhone to feature three cameras.

## 2020–present: 5G and Apple silicon

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Macbook_Air_M1_Silver_PNG.png/250px-Macbook_Air_M1_Silver_PNG.png)

In 2020, Apple was fined £21 million euros, claiming they intentionally slowed down older models of iPhones to [encourage people to buy newer models](/wiki/Planned_obsolescence "Planned obsolescence"). The company somewhat admitted to this practice in 2017, saying the phones were slowed to respond to the [decay](/wiki/Capacity_loss "Capacity loss") of iPhones' [lithium-ion batteries](/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery "Lithium-ion battery"), which made it harder for the batteries to reach the phones' expected power demands.[[219]](#cite_note-219) The [COVID-19 pandemic](/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic") heavily affected China, hurting Apple financially, because they invested into China enough to become increasingly dependent on the country.[[220]](#cite_note-O'Flaherty-220) Chinese factories closed and demand for Apple products went down.[[221]](#cite_note-221) However, they recovered and eventually reached a US$2 trillion market cap later that year.[[222]](#cite_note-222) The [iPhone 12](/wiki/IPhone_12 "IPhone 12"), [12 Pro, and 12 Pro Max](/wiki/IPhone_12_Pro "IPhone 12 Pro") were introduced, being the first iPhones to support [5G](/wiki/5G "5G") connectivity.[[223]](#cite_note-223) The company also started using its own [Apple silicon](/wiki/Apple_silicon "Apple silicon") processors in Macs, instead of chips made by Intel.[[224]](#cite_note-224)[[225]](#cite_note-225)

In April 2021, the M1-powered iPad was launched, along with a new M1-powered iMac offered in 7 colors, recalling the [iMacs offered in 5 colors](/wiki/IMac_G3#Model_line-up "IMac G3") announced in 1999. Apple launched a GPS tracking device called [AirTag](/wiki/AirTag "AirTag") that uses the Apple's [Find My](/wiki/Find_My "Find My") device network. In 2021 and 2022, Apple repeated its pattern of introducing four new iPhones in September, with 2021's [iPhone 13](/wiki/IPhone_13 "IPhone 13") and [iPhone 13 Pro](/wiki/IPhone_13_Pro "IPhone 13 Pro") lines and 2022's [iPhone 14](/wiki/IPhone_14 "IPhone 14") and [iPhone 14 Pro](/wiki/IPhone_14_Pro "IPhone 14 Pro") lines. The iPhone 14 Pro ditched the [notch](/wiki/Notch_(engineering) "Notch (engineering)") containing the sensors for a "dynamic island", which allows for space between the top edge of the screen and the sensors for Face ID. 2022 saw Apple announce the first Macs with Apple's [M2](/wiki/Apple_M2 "Apple M2") chip and a new sub-series of Apple Watch with increased performance for outdoor activities named [Apple Watch Ultra](/wiki/Apple_Watch_Ultra "Apple Watch Ultra").

Apple experienced a period of unprecedented employee unrest in 2021 and 2022, resulting in outspoken employees organizing over labor issues and the company's treatment of women at the company in its corporate offices and retail stores.[[226]](#cite_note-:3-226)[[227]](#cite_note-227) Employees engaged in [hashtag activism](/wiki/Hashtag_activism "Hashtag activism") on social media inspired by the [#MeToo movement](/wiki/MeToo_movement "MeToo movement") called [#AppleToo](/wiki/AppleToo "AppleToo")[[228]](#cite_note-228) and two American stores [unionized](/wiki/Apple_Inc._and_unions "Apple Inc. and unions") for the first time.[[229]](#cite_note-229)

In 2022, Apple paused all product sales in Russia in response to the country's [invasion of Ukraine](/wiki/Russian_invasion_of_Ukraine "Russian invasion of Ukraine").[[230]](#cite_note-230) Tim Cook began focusing on privacy features, which lowered App Store developers' revenue. They also developed App Tracking Transparency, a set of privacy features which cost Facebook US$12 billion.[[220]](#cite_note-O'Flaherty-220) The company also announced they would start including ads in the Books, Maps, and TV iOS apps,[[231]](#cite_note-231) and that it would eventually move production out of China.[[232]](#cite_note-232)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Apple_Vision_Pro_on_display.jpg/250px-Apple_Vision_Pro_on_display.jpg)

Apple launched a [buy now, pay later](/wiki/Buy_now,_pay_later "Buy now, pay later") service called 'Apple Pay Later' for its [Apple Wallet](/wiki/Apple_Wallet "Apple Wallet") users in 2023. The program allows its users to apply for loans between $50 and $1,000 to make online or in-app purchases, and then repaying them through four installments spread over six weeks without any interest or fees.[[233]](#cite_note-233)[[234]](#cite_note-234) In June, Apple released the [Apple Vision Pro](/wiki/Apple_Vision_Pro "Apple Vision Pro"), a computer in the form of an [augmented reality](/wiki/Augmented_reality "Augmented reality") headset which runs the [visionOS](/wiki/VisionOS "VisionOS") operating system. Wearers of the headset use hand gestures to navigate the user interface.[[235]](#cite_note-235) It initially released at $3,500 in the United States, and while sales figures have not been released as of June 2024[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_Apple_Inc.&action=edit), it is considered to be a financial failure in its initial stages.[[236]](#cite_note-236) The company then reached a $25 million settlement in a [U.S. Department of Justice](/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice "United States Department of Justice") case that alleged they were discriminating against U.S. citizens in hiring; they created jobs that were not listed online and required paper submission to apply for, while advertising these jobs to foreign workers as part of recruitment for [PERM](/wiki/Permanent_Labor_Certification#Program_electronic_review_management "Permanent Labor Certification").[[237]](#cite_note-237)

In April 2024, Apple laid off more than 600 employees working in facilities linked to the electric car project and microLED development.[[238]](#cite_note-238) The projects had been reported to have been shut down in the preceding two months.[[239]](#cite_note-239)[[240]](#cite_note-240)

In June 2024, Apple announced [iOS18](/wiki/IOS_18 "IOS 18") and [macOS Sequoia](/wiki/MacOS_Sequoia "MacOS Sequoia") in their [WWDC](/wiki/Worldwide_Developers_Conference "Worldwide Developers Conference") 2024 event.[[241]](#cite_note-241)[[242]](#cite_note-242) After the development of powerful [AI](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence") software like [OpenAI](/wiki/OpenAI "OpenAI")'s [ChatGPT](/wiki/ChatGPT "ChatGPT") in the early 2020s, Apple was accused of being behind their competitors in innovating using AI;[[243]](#cite_note-243) alongside the new operating systems, the company announced they would introduce AI features into their products, such as using ChatGPT to improve Siri. The first product to feature these improvements, named [Apple Intelligence](/wiki/Apple_Intelligence "Apple Intelligence"), is planned to be the [iPhone 15 Pro](/wiki/IPhone_15_Pro "IPhone 15 Pro") running [iOS 18](/wiki/IOS_18 "IOS 18").[[244]](#cite_note-244) Criticism was leveled towards the plan to incorporate recorded user inputs into ChatGPT's [data set](/wiki/Data_set "Data set").[[245]](#cite_note-245) Apple did not pay OpenAI for these features, believing that exposure to OpenAI products will already financially benefit them.[[246]](#cite_note-246) The announcement improved Apple's stock, temporarily making them the world's most valuable company instead of Microsoft.[[247]](#cite_note-247)

In 2024, Apple was sued by two female employees seeking class action who claimed that the company's hiring and performance-review practices are biased against women.[[248]](#cite_note-248) Meanwhile, the [National Labor Relations Board](/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Board "National Labor Relations Board") (NLRB) charged Apple with violations of the [National Labor Relations Act of 1935](/wiki/National_Labor_Relations_Act_of_1935 "National Labor Relations Act of 1935") in spring and fall of 2024. The NLRB accused Apple of maintaining unlawful employee contracts, rules around social media and [Slack](/wiki/Slack_(software) "Slack (software)") usage, for interrogating unionizing employees, and for illegally firing [Janneke Parrish](/wiki/Janneke_Parrish "Janneke Parrish"), a labor activist who had co-lead the #AppleToo movement.[[249]](#cite_note-249)[[250]](#cite_note-250)[[251]](#cite_note-251)

### Stock

'AAPL' is the [stock symbol](/wiki/Stock_symbol "Stock symbol") under which [Apple Inc.](/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.") trades on the [NASDAQ](/wiki/NASDAQ "NASDAQ") [stock market](/wiki/Stock_market "Stock market"). Apple originally went public on December 12, 1980, with an [initial public offering](/wiki/Initial_public_offering "Initial public offering") at US$22.00[[256]](#cite_note-256) per share. The stock has split 2 for 1 three times on June 15, 1987, June 21, 2000, and February 28, 2005. Apple initially paid dividends from June 15, 1987, to December 15, 1995. On March 19, 2012, Apple announced that it would again start paying a dividend of $2.65 per quarter (beginning in the quarter that starts in July 2012) along a $10 billion [share buyback](/wiki/Share_repurchase "Share repurchase") that would commence September 30, 2012, the start of its fiscal 2013 year. Gene Munster and Michael Olson of [Piper Jaffray](/wiki/Piper_Jaffray "Piper Jaffray") are the main analysts who track Apple stock. Piper Jaffray estimates future stock and revenue of Apple annually, and have been doing so for several years.[[257]](#cite_note-257)

**Apple Inc.** is an American multinational technology company that revolutionized the [technology sector](/money/information-technology-stocks) through its innovation of [computer software](/technology/software), [personal computers](/technology/personal-computer), mobile tablets, [smartphones](/technology/smartphone), and computer peripherals.

One of the most recognizable [brands](/money/brand) in the world, Apple created the first commercially successful personal computer and was also the first to bring the [graphical user interface](/technology/graphical-user-interface) (GUI) into mass adoption.

Founded by [Steve Jobs](/money/Steve-Jobs) and [Steve Wozniak](/biography/Stephen-Gary-Wozniak) in 1976, Apple set new benchmarks in product innovation, user-centric functionality, aesthetics and design, and multiproduct integration. Apple redefined and transformed the capabilities of modern computing. Further, Apple innovated the industry by establishing a marketplace ecosystem for third-party application developers, leveraging this new economy to expand its products’ functionalities and strengthen its position. The company is headquartered in Cupertino, [California](/place/California-state).

## Key products and services

* **Mac computers.** Evolving since 1984, the Mac line has set standards in the world of personal computing.
* **iPhone.** Essentially a sophisticated computer packaged in a flat cube, the iPhone was a game changer in mobile technology, altering the way people communicate and consume media.
* **iPad.** Bridging the gap between smartphones and laptops, the iPad opened up a new market for [digital tablets](/technology/tablet-computer) since its debut in 2010.
* **iPod.** A digital music player, the likes of which hadn’t been seen since the Sony Walkman, the iPod revolutionized the music industry in 2001, transforming the way listeners consume music.
* **Apple Watch.** Launched in 2015, the Apple Watch is among the most dominant products in the wearable tech sector.
* **MacBook.** Apple’s brand of laptop computer, the MacBook has become a mainstay in personal and professional environments.
* **Apple TV+.** As Apple’s entry into the streaming entertainment services, Apple TV+ has become a dominant player in the subscription-based entertainment sector.
* **AirPods.** Since the product’s launch in 2016, AirPods have become a leader in the wireless audio market.

## 1976: Garage days and the first Apple computer

The genesis of Apple Inc. sprang, not from a vision of an ambitious venture, but from a more modest aspiration: Stephen G. Wozniak had a lifelong dream of building his own personal computer.

Wozniak was inspired by the Altair 8800, the first commercially successful microcomputer launched in 1975. Further encouraged by his friends at the Homebrew Computer Club, a hobbyist group, he quickly developed a simple gaming motherboard: his first personal computer.

![Steve Wozniak and Steve Jobs holding an Apple I circuit board in 1976](https://cdn.britannica.com/02/24402-050-2C2345B1/Steven-Jobs-Apple-I-Stephen-Wozniak-circuit-1976.jpg?w=800)

Open full sized image

Wozniak, then a 26-year-old engineering intern at [Hewlett-Packard](/money/Hewlett-Packard-Company), presented his design to his employer. When the company expressed no interest, Wozniak teamed up with a former [high school](/topic/high-school) classmate, 21-year-old Steve Jobs. The two decided to set up their operations in the Jobs family garage, founding Apple Inc. on April 1, 1976, along with Ronald Wayne, an experienced tech industry executive who would play a role in helping facilitate and integrate the young founders’ company vision and operational pursuits.

Jobs and Wozniak each held a 45% stake in the company; Wayne had a 10% partnership stake. To fund their venture with working capital, Jobs sold his Volkswagen minibus and Wozniak his programmable calculator.

With the help of its first [angel investor](/money/venture-capitalist-angel-investing) and Intel Corporation veteran Armas Clifford “Mike” Markkula, the company unveiled its inaugural product, the Apple I, in July 1976 at a Homebrew Computer Club meeting. Selling for $666.66, the Apple I was a bare motherboard with a basic [CPU](/technology/central-processing-unit), random access memory (RAM), and video capabilities, but no keyboard or monitor. Nevertheless, the company’s first product marked the garage start-up’s first success—however minor—selling 200 units that year. Later that year, Ronald Wayne decided to leave the fledgling company, selling back his 10% share for $800.

## 1977–1978: Apple II and early commercial success

In January 1977, with Markkula providing his expertise and seed funding of $250,000, Apple Inc. was [incorporated](/money/types-of-business-structures).

[Apple II](/technology/Apple-II), the company’s next product, was released in April 1977 and became the first widely distributed microcomputer. It was a commercial success; some 5 or 6 million units were sold over the next decade.

This new iteration hinted at a slight design breakthrough with its custom-molded plastic casing, a departure from the steel-encased designs of the time. The Apple II also included color graphics, sound, and television plug-in capabilities. Selling for a base price of $1,298, it was perceived as a user-friendly product, contributing to its success among general consumers.

Having achieved its first milestone in the larger consumer market, the company boosted its competitive position when Wozniak introduced a disk controller enabling an affordable [floppy disk](/technology/floppy-disk) drive. This inclusion enhanced Apple II’s data storage and retrieval, making it faster and more reliable.

## 1979: Innovations—GUI, mice, and icons

In 1979, Jobs led a team of engineers to see the innovations created at the [Xerox Corporation](/money/Xerox-Corporation)’s [Palo Alto Research Center](/topic/PARC-company) (PARC) in California.

![Apple's Lisa computer](https://cdn.britannica.com/05/24405-050-217BD4E9/Apple-Lisa-computer.jpg?w=385)

Open full sized image

Apple's Lisa computer, introduced in 1983, featured a graphical user interface.

Courtesy of Apple Computer, Inc.

At PARC, they were shown three features that would serve as the company’s game-changing innovations:

* The functional [graphical user interface](/technology/graphical-user-interface) (GUI), featuring on-screen windows
* A pointing device known as a mouse
* The use of icons (pictographs) in place of awkward command line instructions

These features would be integrated into two key projects in development: the Apple Lisa (released in 1983) and the Apple Macintosh (1984). (Xerox would later sue Apple in 1989, claiming it stole the idea for its graphical user interface. The lawsuit was dismissed in 1990 on technical grounds, with the court saying Xerox chose the wrong venue to argue most of its legal points.)

The idea was to develop low-cost and user-friendly personal computers that would introduce the two innovative features—the GUI and mouse—to mass consumers. Although both computers sported these new features, they were designed to appeal to different audiences. The Lisa was geared toward business professionals, while the Macintosh targeted the broader consumer market.

## 1980–1985: Competition from IBM, Macintosh, and Steve Jobs’ exit

Apple’s profits and size grew at a historic rate. By 1980 the company netted over $100 million and had more than 1,000 employees. In December 1980, Apple went public, offering 4.6 million shares at $22 each.

Apple’s [initial public offering](/money/ipo-initial-public-offering) (IPO) was the biggest since 1956 when [Ford Motor Company](/money/Ford-Motor-Company) went public. Further, by the end of 1980, Apple’s nearly $2 billion valuation was greater than Ford’s.

**IBM enters the PC market.** As a formidable contender in the emerging PC market, Apple would soon face competition from [IBM](/money/International-Business-Machines-Corporation), the computer industry’s leading player.

IBM launched its own version of the personal computer in 1981, breaking with its tradition of using only proprietary hardware components and software. Instead, IBM built its PC using readily available components, including an [Intel](/money/Intel) microprocessor and a [disk operating system](/technology/MS-DOS) (DOS) from [Microsoft Corporation](/money/Microsoft-Corporation).

Because other manufacturers could use the same hardware components that IBM used and license DOS from Microsoft, new software developers could count on a wide [IBM PC](/technology/IBM-PC)–compatible market for their software. The system soon had a new [spreadsheet](/technology/spreadsheet) program, Lotus 1-2-3, that changed computing and created what would later be known as a “killer app”: a software program (application) so useful that it propels hardware sales.

IBM’s PC won instant support and popularity in the business community, and the Apple II was unable to dislodge IBM’s position. Flanked by its existing suite of business computing products, IBM’s new offering struck a fatal blow to the yet-to-be-released Apple Lisa.

Upon its debut in 1983, the Lisa’s $9,995 price tag was more than five times the cost of an IBM PC. If cost alone didn’t guarantee its commercial underperformance compared to IBM, the Lisa also had numerous hardware issues and incompatibilities that made its performance sluggish compared to its competition.

The Lisa was an epic failure for the company’s foray into the business computing market. But Apple fared better in the general consumer market with the release the following year of a product that might even be considered iconic, culturally perhaps more so than functionally.

**Apple launches the Macintosh.** Following its failed Lisa launch, Apple prepared for a second product release, hoping to gain a stronger, if not dominant, position in the market. The new product was the [Macintosh](/technology/Macintosh) computer.

Although Jef Raskin led the Macintosh research project as early as 1979, Steve Jobs took over the project in 1981, having been removed from the Lisa project due to collaborative tensions. Jobs’ intervention and contentious disagreements with Raskin on the project’s vision and direction prompted Raskin to leave the company in 1982.

Introduced to the world via television commercial during [Super Bowl](/sports/Super-Bowl) XVIII on January 22, 1984, Apple immediately became a household name. The commercial, directed by [Ridley Scott](/biography/Ridley-Scott), struck a hard, albeit symbolic, blow against IBM, associating it with the dystopian and villainous authority of [George Orwell’s](/biography/George-Orwell) novel *1984*.

In contrast, the ad portrayed Apple as the countercultural hero of the story—a symbol of freedom, progress, and innovation.

The Macintosh’s integration of a user-friendly GUI, all-in-one design, and affordable price of $2,500 made it accessible and appealing to businesses and technical users but also, and perhaps more importantly, to general consumers. Although the ad profoundly affected viewers, becoming one of the most iconic commercials in Super Bowl history, it generated more cultural buzz than commercial sales.

## 1985–1997: Leadership struggles after Jobs and Wozniak

Despite ecstatic media reaction, the Macintosh initially sold below Apple’s expectations. The Mac, as it came to be known, had insufficient memory and storage capacity. It lacked essential features like cursor keys and a color display, both of which were considered standard amenities for PCs at the time.

Furthermore, skeptics doubted that adults would ever want to use a machine that relied on the GUI, condemning it as “toylike” and wasteful of computational resources.

In the wake of the poor sales performance, Jobs was ousted from the company in September 1985 by [chief executive officer](/money/chief-executive-officer) (CEO) [John Sculley](/biography/John-Sculley). (Wozniak had left Apple in February 1985 to become a teacher.)

Upon exiting Apple, Jobs founded another computer company, NeXT Inc., in 1985. The next year, he purchased Lucasfilm’s Computer Graphics Division to create [Pixar Animation Studios](/money/Pixar-Animation-Studios). The founding of both companies, although outside the scope of Apple’s development at the time, would prove instrumental to the company’s forthcoming era of innovation in technology (NeXT’s technology was the foundation of macOS) as well as design and user experience (what is now called UX).

Under Sculley, Apple made steady improvements to the Mac—most notably, the introduction of an affordable laser printer along with Aldus Corporation’s PageMaker, the Mac’s first killer app (1985). Together, these two innovations launched the [desktop publishing](/topic/desktop-publishing) revolution for consumers and businesses alike. The graphic arts and publishing industries quickly became the Mac’s single most important market.

In 1987, the Mac II and Mac SE were introduced. The Mac II was the first of the Mac line to incorporate color graphics and a modular design, while the Mac SE was the first of its series to offer an expansion slot (later including a hard drive bay as a standard feature).

Although these products boosted Apple’s popularity among consumers, as evidenced by its growing base of one million users, the company would soon be mired in legal challenges that impeded its pace and progress.

**Apple litigates while PCs innovate.** The Mac’s incompatibility with Apple II software, a problem the company initially ignored, slowed educational sales and compelled the company to retain its outmoded Apple II line through 1993, hampering its progress as an industry innovator.

Consumer sales suffered as Apple discouraged game development out of fear that the Mac would not be taken seriously in the business community. Moreover, [Microsoft](/money/Microsoft-Corporation), after an unsuccessful attempt to secure an agreement to market the [Mac OS](/technology/macOS) on the Intel processor, introduced [Windows](/technology/Microsoft-Windows), its own graphical [operating system](/technology/operating-system).

Apple litigated for years, in vain, to stop Microsoft from copying the “look and feel” of its operating system, although the Mac OS itself drew upon Xerox’s PARC GUI. Meanwhile, as successive versions of Windows improved and competition among multiple [PC](/technology/personal-computer) manufacturers led to greater innovation and lower prices, fewer consumers were willing to pay the premium Apple had commanded owing to its reputation for quality.

As the 1980s drew to a close, Apple would find itself facing a few more legal headwinds (including its legal battle with Xerox over the graphical user interface):

* In 1989, Apple Corps Ltd., the Beatles’ record label, sued Apple for trademark issues related to the company’s name and logo. The legal battle spanned several years; it escalated in the 2000s when Apple introduced [iTunes](/technology/iTunes) and the [iPod](/technology/iPod), thereby entering the music industry.
* Also in 1989, Apple disposed of unsold Lisa units in a landfill in Logan, Utah. This event became part of tech lore, a symbol of the company’s biggest innovative flop. Meanwhile, Jobs released the NEXTSTEP operating system—a significant development, as it would later serve as the basis for Apple’s Mac OS X upon Jobs’ return to the company.

**Apple-IBM rapprochement.** In a surprising move, Apple and [IBM](/money/International-Business-Machines-Corporation) announced an alliance in 1991. In addition to signing a technology agreement with [Motorola, Inc.](/money/Motorola-Inc) to develop a next-generation chip, known as the PowerPC, Apple and IBM created two new software companies to develop operating system software: Taligent, Inc., and Kaleida Labs, Inc.

Taligent was expected to enable versions of both the Mac OS and the [IBM OS/2](/technology/IBM-OS-2) to run on a new computer hardware standard, the common hardware reference platform (CHRP). Kaleida Labs was to develop multimedia software.

But as both companies began to quarrel over CHRP engineering specifications and costs mounted to about $400 million for Taligent and $200 million for Kaleida Labs, Apple pulled out with little to show for its investment.

**Apple’s Newton and Claris flops.** Sculley also promised more than Apple could deliver with Newton, a personal digital assistant ([PDA](/technology/PDA)) that suffered from poor handwriting recognition and diverted company engineering and financial resources.

In addition, the company vacillated over Claris Corporation, its software division, first reorganizing it as an independent company and then reabsorbing it when it began shifting more resources to Windows software.

**Apple continues to flounder.** In 1993, Sculley was replaced by Michael Spindler as CEO. Spindler’s most notable achievements were the successful migration of the Mac OS to the PowerPC microprocessor and the initiation of a shift away from Apple’s proprietary standards.

Nevertheless, Apple struggled with marketing projections, accumulating large unsalable inventories of some models while simultaneously unable to meet a billion dollars in orders for other models.

Combined with drastic quality control problems, notably a defective line of monitors and some highly publicized combustible portable computers, these failings brought Spindler’s reign to an end in early 1996 with the appointment of Gilbert F. Amelio.

## 1997–2010: Renaissance and reinvention

In 1996, Apple made a pivotal move that would profoundly affect its strategic direction. After several attempts to develop a replacement for its aging Mac operating system, Apple purchased NeXT Software, Inc., the company that Jobs had founded after being ousted from Apple in 1985.

Building on this move, Apple, under Amelio’s leadership, began cutting operating costs and reestablishing quality controls. Despite these efforts, only a small percentage of new computer buyers were choosing Macs over Windows machines. Apple’s financial situation was dire toward the end of its second decade.

Jobs, now an advisor to Apple’s embattled CEO, quickly became disenchanted with the company’s direction, selling all but one share of the Apple stock he had received in the NeXT sale.

When Apple failed to become profitable under Amelio and its worldwide market share fell to roughly 3%, the board of directors in mid-1997 recruited a surprising temporary replacement: Jobs, who for the first time became the undisputed leader of the company he cofounded.

**The return of Jobs: iMac, iTunes, iPod, iPhone, and iPad.** Upon retaking the helm, Jobs immediately set about revitalizing the company, announcing an alliance with erstwhile foe Microsoft, ending the half-hearted (and profit-draining) program to license the Mac OS, streamlining the company’s multiple (and confusing) product lines, and launching the [iMac](/technology/iMac), the first of many affordable and innovative products to come from the now-revitalized company.

Apple branded its products as part of an upscale lifestyle, or “iLife,” by emphasizing sleek, minimalist, and aesthetically pleasing designs (like the iMac) that looked more like [modern art](/art/modern-art-to-1945-2080464) than machinery.

Designed to revive Apple’s consumer and educational appeal, the iMac quickly became the all-time best-selling Mac, lifting Apple’s U.S. market share to roughly 13.5% in August 1998 from a record low of 2.6% in December 1997. Moreover, Apple had a profitable [fiscal year](/money/fiscal-year) in 1998, its first since 1995.

In 2001 Apple introduced [iTunes](/technology/iTunes), a media player and library software to store, organize, and play digital audio files converted to [MP3](/technology/MP3) format. Later the same year, Apple began selling the [iPod](/technology/iPod), a portable MP3 player, which quickly became the market leader. (The term *[podcast](/art/podcast)*, combining *iPod* and *broadcast*, is used as both a noun and a verb to refer to audio or video material downloaded for portable or delayed playback.) Later models added larger storage capacities or smaller sizes, color screens, and video playback features.

In 2003 Apple began selling downloadable copies of major record company songs in MP3 format over the [Internet](/technology/Internet). The iPod Nano digital music player, one-fifth the size of the original iPod, was introduced by Apple in 2005. By 2006 more than one billion songs and videos had been sold through Apple’s [website](/technology/website).

In 2007 Apple introduced the touch screen [iPhone](/technology/iPhone), a [cellular telephone](/technology/mobile-telephone#ref279849) with capabilities for playing MP3s and videos and accessing the Internet. More than just a mobile computer and media player merged with a phone, it pioneered multi-touch features that shaped the way users interact with technology.

In response, Apple sought to dislodge RIM’s position in 2007–2009 by differentiating the iPhone and innovating mobile functionalities that changed consumers’ needs and expectations, including:

* Multi-touch screen functionalities that allowed users to swipe, pinch (zoom), and type on a digital keyboard. This was a significant departure from RIM’s products, which had physical keys.
* Combining text and email—the functionalities the BlackBerry specialized in—with phone, Internet browsing, and iPod capabilities.
* Compatibility with Mac computers, giving the iPhone integrative capabilities that far exceeded those of the BlackBerry.
* Upgrading the iPhone in 2008 to make it compatible with 3G wireless networks and retrofitting it with [global positioning system (GPS)](/technology/GPS) capabilities.
* Creating an ecosystem of third-party apps (Apple’s App Store) that accelerated the iPhone’s extended capabilities beyond devices, like the BlackBerry, that relied on in-house development.

Apple’s dual focus on consumer needs and business integration, positioning the iPhone as a daily extension of home and work life, eventually gained market share from RIM.

By June 19, 2009, when Apple released the [iPhone 3GS](/topic/iPhone-3GS) (which sold one million units in the first three days), the company’s share of the [smartphone](/technology/smartphone) market had reached about 20% (compared with about 55% for the BlackBerry line of smartphones).

In addition to hardware changes such as a three-megapixel [digital camera](/technology/digital-camera) that could record digital videos and an internal digital compass capable of working with various mapping software, the iPhone 3GS included a new operating system, the iPhone OS 3.0.

The new system included support for voice-activated controls and peer-to-peer ([P2P](/technology/P2P)) play of [electronic games](/topic/electronic-game) with other iPhone users over [Wi-Fi](/technology/Wi-Fi) Internet connections. The latter feature was part of Apple’s strategy to compete in the portable gaming market with the [Nintendo Company](/technology/Nintendo-console)’s DS and the [Sony Corporation](/money/Sony)’s PSP. The iPhone can also be used for reading electronic books, or e-books. E-books in iPhone-compatible formats can be purchased over the Internet from [electronic book](/technology/e-book) dealers such as the iTunes store and [Amazon.com](/money/Amazoncom).

In 2010 Apple unveiled the [iPad](/technology/iPad), a touch screen device whose size and function bridged the gap between a [laptop computer](/technology/laptop-computer) and a smartphone. The iPad was a multimedia tablet that allowed users to operate it with the same set of finger gestures as the iPhone. Designed with a touch screen capable of displaying high-definition video, the iPad also provided access to apps via iTunes and the App Store.

In 2011 Apple introduced [iCloud](/topic/iCloud), a [cloud computing](/technology/cloud-computing) service in which a user’s applications, photographs, documents, calendars, and recently purchased music would be stored on iCloud servers and automatically updated on a user’s devices. Some analysts saw iCloud as Apple’s plan for a future in which users could dispense with the personal computer as the primary place to store data.
