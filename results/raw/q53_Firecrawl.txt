Definition

EBITDA measures earnings before the impact of financing costs, taxes, and certain non-cash accounting charges.

### Key Takeaways

- EBITDA measures operating profitability before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization.
- It is calculated by adding interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization back to net income or operating income.
- Because it excludes capital costs, EBITDA can make companies appear more profitable than they are.
- The SEC requires companies to reconcile EBITDA to net income.

## What Is EBITDA?

EBITDA, short for earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization, measures a company’s operating performance by excluding financing costs, taxes, and certain non-cash expenses. Although it is not recognized under [generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/gaap.asp), EBITDA is widely used to compare financial performance across companies. Critics argue that it can overstate profitability, which is why the [U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sec.asp) requires companies to reconcile EBITDA to [net income](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/netincome.asp).

## EBITDA Formulas and Calculation

If a company doesn't report EBITDA, it can be easily calculated from its [financial statements](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/financial-statements.asp). Software like Excel can [make the calculation process easy](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/031915/how-do-i-calculate-ebitda-margin-using-excel.asp).

The earnings (net income), tax, and interest figures are found on the [income statement](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/incomestatement.asp), while the [depreciation](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/depreciation.asp) and [amortization](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/amortization.asp) figures are normally found in the notes to operating profit or on the [cash flow statement](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/cashflowstatement.asp).

There are two EBITDA formulas, one based on net income and the other on operating income, both of which will arrive at basically the same result. (Net income is operating income minus non-operating expenses, such as taxes and interest.)

The respective EBITDA formulas are:

## What Does EBITDA Actually Tell You?

By adding [interest](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/interest.asp), [taxes](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/taxes.asp), depreciation, and amortization back to net income, EBITDA can be used to [track and compare the underlying profitability](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/050415/how-does-product-pricing-affect-gross-profit-and-ebitda.asp) of companies regardless of their depreciation assumptions or financing choices.

Like earnings, EBITDA is often used in valuation ratios, notably in combination with [enterprise value](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/enterprisevalue.asp) as EV/EBITDA, also known as the [enterprise multiple](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/ev-ebitda.asp).

EBITDA is widely used in the analysis of [asset-intensive industries](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/052015/which-industries-tend-have-greatest-ebitda-margins.asp) with a lot of [property, plant, and equipment](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/ppe.asp) and correspondingly high non-cash depreciation costs. In those sectors, the costs that EBITDA excludes may obscure changes in the underlying profitability—for example, as with energy pipelines.

Meanwhile, amortization is often used to expense the cost of software development or other [intellectual property](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/intellectualproperty.asp). That's one reason early-stage technology and research companies may use EBITDA when discussing their performance.

Annual changes in tax liabilities and assets that must be reflected on the income statement may not relate to operational performance. Interest costs depend on debt levels, interest rates, and management preferences regarding debt vs. equity financing. Excluding all of these items keeps the focus on the cash profits generated by the company's business.

Of course, not everyone agrees. "References to EBITDA make us shudder," Berkshire Hathaway Inc. ( [BRK.A](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=BRK.A)) CEO [Warren Buffett](https://www.investopedia.com/warren-buffett-4689826) has written. According to Buffett, depreciation is a real cost that can't be ignored and EBITDA is not "a meaningful measure of performance."1

## Example of EBITDA

Suppose a company generates $100 million in revenue and incurs $40 million in [cost of goods sold (COGS)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/cogs.asp) and another $20 million in [overhead](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/overhead.asp). Depreciation and amortization expenses total $10 million, yielding an operating profit of $30 million. Interest expense is $5 million, leaving earnings before taxes of $25 million. With a 20% tax rate, net income equals $20 million after $5 million in taxes is subtracted from pretax income. If depreciation, amortization, interest, and taxes are added back to net income, EBITDA equals $40 million.

| Net Income | **$20,000,000** |
| Depreciation & Amortization | +$10,000,000 |
| Interest Expense | +$5,000,000 |
| Taxes | +$5,000,000 |
| EBITDA | **$40,000,000** |

## History of EBITDA

EBITDA is the invention of one of the very few investors with a record rivaling Buffett's: Liberty Media Chairman John Malone. The cable industry pioneer came up with the metric in the 1970s to help sell lenders and investors on his leveraged growth strategy, which deployed debt and reinvested profits to minimize taxes.

During the 1980s, the investors and lenders involved in [leveraged buyouts (LBOs)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/leveragedbuyout.asp) found EBITDA useful in estimating whether the targeted companies had the profitability to service the debt that was expected to be incurred in the acquisition. Since a buyout would likely entail a change in the capital structure and tax liabilities, it made sense to exclude the interest and tax expense from earnings. As non-cash costs, depreciation and amortization expense would not affect the company's ability to service that debt, at least in the near term.2

The LBO buyers tended to target companies with minimal or modest near-term capital spending plans, while their own need to secure financing for the acquisitions led them to focus on the [EBITDA-to-interest coverage ratio](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/ebitdacoverinterestratio.asp), which weighs core operating profitability as represented by EBITDA against debt service costs.2

EBITDA gained notoriety during the [dotcom bubble](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dotcom-bubble.asp), when some companies used it to exaggerate their financial performance.3

The metric received more bad publicity in 2018 after WeWork Companies Inc., a provider of shared office space, filed a prospectus for its [initial public offering (IPO)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/ipo.asp) defining its "Community Adjusted EBITDA" as excluding [general and administrative](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/073115/what-are-some-examples-general-and-administrative-expenses.asp) as well as sales and [marketing](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketing.asp) expenses.4

## Criticisms of EBITDA

Because EBITDA is a [non-GAAP](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/financial-analysis/062716/gaap-vs-nongaap-which-should-you-consider-evaluation.asp) measure, the way it is calculated can vary from one company to the next. It is not uncommon for companies to emphasize EBITDA over net income because the former makes them look better.

An important red flag for investors is when a company that hasn't reported EBITDA in the past starts to feature it prominently in results. This can happen when companies have borrowed heavily or are experiencing rising capital and development costs. In those cases, EBITDA may serve to distract investors from the company's challenges.

These are among the other criticisms of EBITDA:

### EBITDA Ignores Asset Costs

A common misconception is that EBITDA represents cash earnings. However, unlike [free cash flow](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/freecashflow.asp), EBITDA ignores the cost of assets. One of the most common criticisms of EBITDA is that it assumes profitability is a function of sales and operations alone—almost as if the company's assets and debt financing were a gift. To quote Buffett again, "Does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?"1

### Earnings Figures May Be Suspect

While the formulas for calculating EBITDA may seem simple enough, different companies use different earnings figures as the starting point. In other words, EBITDA is susceptible to the earnings accounting games found on the income statement.

### Company Valuation Can Be Obscured

All the cost exclusions in [EBITDA](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/061515/which-metric-should-i-pay-more-attention-evebitda-or-pe.asp) can make a company appear much less expensive than it really is. When analysts look at stock price multiples of EBITDA rather than at bottom-line earnings, they produce lower multiples.

Consider the historical example of wireless telecom operator Sprint Nextel. On April 1, 2006, the stock was trading at 7.3 times its forecast EBITDA. That might sound like a low multiple, but it didn't mean that the company was a bargain. As a multiple of forecast operating profits, Sprint Nextel traded at a much-higher 20 times, and the company traded at 48 times its estimated net income.

"There's been some real sloppiness in accounting, and this move toward using adjusted EBITDA and adjusted earnings has produced some companies that I think are trading on valuations that are not supported by the real numbers," [hedge fund](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/hedgefund.asp) manager Daniel Loeb said in 2015.

Not much has changed on that front since then. Investors using solely EBITDA to assess a company's value or results risk getting the wrong answer.

## EBITDA vs. EBIT vs. EBT

[Earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/ebit.asp) is a company's net income plus income tax and interest expenses. EBIT is used to analyze the profitability of a company's core operations. The following formula is used to calculate EBIT:

> EBIT=Net Income+Interest Expense+Tax Expense\\begin{aligned}\\text{EBIT}=\\text{Net Income}+\\text{Interest Expense}+\\text{Tax Expense}\\end{aligned}EBIT=Net Income+Interest Expense+Tax Expense​

Since net income includes interest and tax expenses, to calculate EBIT, these deductions from net income must be reversed.

[Earnings before tax (EBT)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/ebt.asp) reflects how much of an operating profit has been realized before accounting for taxes, while EBIT excludes both taxes and interest payments. EBT is calculated by adding just tax expense to the company's net income.

By excluding [tax liabilities](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/taxliability.asp), investors can use EBT to evaluate performance after eliminating a variable typically not within the company's control. In the United States, this is most useful for comparing companies that might be subject to different state tax rates or federal tax rules.

Unlike EBITDA, EBT and EBIT do include the non-cash expenses of depreciation and amortization.

## EBITDA vs. Operating Cash Flow

[Operating cash flow](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/operatingcashflow.asp) is a better measure of how much cash a company is generating because it adds non-cash charges (depreciation and amortization) back to net income while also including changes in [working capital](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/w/workingcapital.asp), including receivables, [payables](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/accountspayable.asp), and [inventory](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/inventory.asp), that use or provide cash.

Working capital trends are an important consideration in determining how much cash a company is generating. If investors don't include working capital changes in their analysis and rely solely on EBITDA, [they can miss clues](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/012015/what-difference-between-cash-flow-and-ebidta.asp)—for example, [difficulties with receivables collection](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/accountsreceivable.asp)—that may impair cash flow.

## Explain It Like I'm Five

Think of a company that makes money, but also has to pay interest on loans, pay taxes, and deal with equipment getting old. EBITDA focuses just on the money the business earns from its main activities before these other costs are included.

## How Do You Calculate EBITDA?

The formula for calculating EBITDA is: _EBITDA = Operating Income + Depreciation + Amortization._ You can find this figure on a company’s income statement, cash flow statement, and balance sheet.

## What Is a Good EBITDA?

A strong EBITDA is considered to be at least two times the company's interest expense. For example, if a company's annual interest expense is $1 million, then a strong EBITDA would be at least $2 million.

In some industries, a higher EBITDA margin above 15% may be considered favorable. A good EBITDA varies by industry, company size, industry norms, growth stage, and capital structure.

## What Is the Difference Between EBITDA and EBITA?

Like EBITDA, [EBITA](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/ebita.asp) (earnings before interest, taxes, and amortization) is a measure of a company's profitability that's used by investors and an example of a non-GAAP financial measure. EBITA is not as commonly used as EBITDA, which adds depreciation to the calculation. EBITDA may offer a better view of operating profitability in asset-heavy industries such as utilities, telecom, and manufacturing, but EBITA may be more useful for companies that don't have extensive capital expenditures that might skew the numbers.

## Is EBITDA the Same As Gross Profit?

No, EBITDA is not the same as gross profit. While they are related, EBITDA and gross profit are distinct financial metrics. Gross profit represents revenue minus the cost of goods sold (COGS), indicating the profitability of core business operations before deducting other expenses. EBITDA, however, reflects operating performance by excluding interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization, providing a clearer view of operational profitability by excluding non-operating expenses and non-cash items.

## What Is Amortization in EBITDA?

As it relates to EBITDA, amortization is the gradual discounting of the book value of a company's [intangible assets](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/intangibleasset.asp). Amortization is reported on a company's income statement. Intangible assets include intellectual property, such as patents or trademarks, as well as [goodwill](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/goodwill.asp).

## The Bottom Line

EBITDA can be a useful tool for comparing companies subject to disparate tax treatments and capital costs, or analyzing them in situations where these are likely to change. It also omits non-cash depreciation costs that may not accurately represent future capital spending requirements. At the same time, excluding some costs while including others has opened the door to abuse of EBITDA by unscrupulous corporate managers. The best defense for investors against such practices is to read the fine print, reconciling the reported EBITDA to net income.

# What is EBITDA? A brief guide for small businesses

Banks use the EBITDA method to assess whether your business is able to pay off its debts.

If you approach a bank for a business loan or [another form of finance](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/other-forms-of-finance), it will likely use EBITDA to determine whether your business is able to repay its debts.

This method of measuring a company first became popular in the 1980s, at the height of the leveraged buyout era.

Then, it was common for investors to financially restructure distressed companies (those that were [unable to meet their financial obligations](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/managing-bankruptcy)).

It was used mainly as a yardstick of whether a business [could afford to pay back the interest](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/struggling-with-debt) associated with restructuring.

Read on to learn more about EBITDA in the modern day - what it is, how it's calculated and what it might mean for your business.

## What does EBITDA mean? What does it stand for?

**EBITDA** (pronounced "ee-bit-dah") is a standard of measurement banks use to judge a business' performance.

It stands for earnings before [interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortisation](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/business-finance-glossary).

To understand what each part of this means, see **How to calculate EBITDA** below.

As EBITDA doesn't account for the different ways a company may use [debt](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/good-debt-versus-bad-debt), [equity](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/what-is-equity-finance), [cash](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/establishing-a-cash-culture) or other sources to capital to finance itself, banks often use it to:

- compare two similar businesses
- understand a company's ability to [generate cash flow](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/what-is-cash-flow-how-do-you-manage-it).

## How to calculate EBITDA

You can calculate EBITDA in two ways:

1. By adding depreciation and amortisation expenses to operating profit (EBIT)
2. By adding interest, tax, depreciation and amortisation expenses back on top of net profit

To use EBITDA, you need to understand what each part of this formula means.

### Earnings

Normally, this is your net profit as you report it to HMRC.

Net profit is the total revenue you've generated from sales, minus the total amount you deduct as a legitimate business cost.

### Before

The 'B' stands for 'before'.

The items below, when added to your net profit calculation, should change the amount of your net profit and your assets in your favour.

### Interest

The interest you're charged when [repaying your debt](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/reduce-business-debt) is added back to your earnings.

### Taxes

EBITDA adds back taxes, which can vary from one period to the next and are affected by numerous conditions that may not relate directly to your business' operating results.

### Depreciation

When you use tangible (physical) assets - such as machinery or vehicles - over time, they fall in value.

EBITDA adds back this loss in value.

### Amortisation

This relates to the eventual expiring of intangible (non-physical) assets, such as patents or copyright.

In EBITDA, amortisation is added back.

## What is the debt to EBITDA ratio?

A **debt to EBITDA ratio** measures a company's ability to pay off its debt.

A high ratio may indicate that the company's debt is [too heavy a financial burden](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/business-guidance/guidance-articles/finance/10-steps-to-help-resolve-debt).

If your business were to borrow from a bank, the bank might include a debt to EBITDA ratio in the loan agreement.

This means you'd have to keep to the ratio set out in the agreement, or risk having to pay back the entire loan immediately.

# The Importance of the EBITDA Calculation in Business Valuation

Investors often focus on net income, revenue, and cash flow to measure a company’s financial health and value. However, another measure has gained importance in quarterly reports and accounts over the years: EBITDA. This measure analyzes and compares profitability between different corporations and industries. Read on to learn more about EBITDA calculation, evaluation, and limitations.

## What is EBITDA?

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It measures the company’s overall financial performance and is often used as an alternative to other metrics, such as earnings, revenue, and income. Before discussing what EBITDA signifies and how the numbers are employed, we will examine the key terms: interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization.

- **Interest** includes business expenses caused by interest rates, including interest on loans by banks or third-party lenders.

- **Taxes** comprise any federal income taxes and state or local taxes imposed by the government and regulatory authorities in the region.

- **Depreciation** is an expense that indicates the reduced value of fixed company assets. It is a non-cash expense that signifies asset value reduction.
- **Amortization** is another noncash expense that involves the cost of intangible assets. It can be defined as a process that gradually writes off the initial cost of an asset.

EBITDA focuses on the financial outcome of operating decisions by eliminating the impact of non-operating management decisions, such as tax rates, interest expenses, and significant intangible assets.

Consequently, the measure reflects a business’s operating profitability, which owners, investors, and stakeholders can compare with other companies. For this reason, EBITDA is often preferred over other metrics when deciding which business is more attractive in a [mergers and acquisition (M&A) strategy](https://windes.com/services/advisory/mergers-and-acquisitions-strategy/).

EBITDA may be calculated for a company’s accounting period. LTM (Last Twelve Months) EBITDA is a valuation metric representing all earnings before adjustments associated with interest, tax, depreciation, and amortization in the past 12 months. LTM EBITDA is also known as TTM (Trailing Twelve Months) EBITDA.

EBITDA excludes depreciation and amortization because these expenses are subjective, meaning their calculations can vary significantly between companies. This subjectivity arises from the difficulty of accurately estimating the useful life of tangible and intangible assets. Different depreciation and amortization methods can lead to varying expenses, impacting a company’s net income. By removing these subjective elements, EBITDA provides a more focused view of a company’s operational performance, highlighting its earnings from core activities.

## EBITDA in Business Valuations

EBIT is one of the many variations of EBITDA. The main difference between EBIT and EBITDA is the number of steps taken to reach a relevant and meaningful value that helps owners and stakeholders make decisions based on the company’s financial health.

EBIT, or Earnings Before Interest and Tax, represents the value of earnings without the effect of tax rates and interest. EBITDA takes this further by removing and identifying depreciation and amortization expenses. By breaking down different expenses and their impact on a company’s worth, EBIT and EBITDA help us understand the bigger picture of its value.

In addition to EBIT, there are several other variations of EBITDA, including EBIAT (Earnings Before Interest After Tax), EBIDA (Earnings Before Interest, Depreciation, and Amortization), EBITDAR (Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, Amortization, and Restructuring/Rent Costs), EBITDAC (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization, and Coronavirus) and more. All values help build a picture of the company’s value.

Potential buyers and investors generally use EBITDA and its variations to compare the valuations of different companies. On the other hand, business owners can benefit from EBITDA by enhancing their [exit strategy](https://windes.com/exit-planning-what-is-it-why-do-it/) and making numerous business decisions, particularly operating decisions.

EBITDA is also compared with Seller’s Discretionary Earnings (SDE), which determines the business’s historical cash flow. Think of it as a recasting process that starts with net profit. The owner’s salary and non-cash profits are added to the net profit. SDE is similar to EBITDA. The main difference that settles the [EBITDA vs. SDE](https://windes.com/advisory/value-acceleration-and-exit-planning/ebitda-vs-sde/) debate relates to the size of the business. EBITDA is generally employed for larger corporations and organizations, whereas SDE is more suitable for smaller companies.

In essence, private equity firms prefer EBITDA because it removes financial variables that could skew comparisons, allowing for a more transparent evaluation of a company’s core business performance. This standardization is crucial when making investment decisions or valuing potential acquisitions across an industry. By focusing on EBITDA, investors can more accurately gauge the company’s potential for growth and profitability, regardless of its capital structure or tax environment.

## How is EBITDA Used in Financial Modeling?

EBITDA plays a crucial role in financial modeling. It is often a foundational metric for calculating unleveraged free cash flow.

### Key Uses of EBITDA in Financial Models

**Starting Point for Cash Flow Analysis** EBITDA is typically utilized as a baseline for generating cash flow projections. This is because it strips away the effects of financial decisions, such as debt structure and non-cash accounting factors, providing a clearer picture of operational profitability.

**Benchmark for Valuation**

While discounted cash flow (DCF) models value a business by focusing on free cash flows, EBITDA is a reference point that analysts often use to compare companies within the same industry. Its ability to standardize earnings makes it easier to assess relative value.

**Performance Measurement**

EBITDA offers insight into a company’s operational performance, independent of its capital structure or tax situation. It is a popular metric for investors and analysts to evaluate a company’s underlying performance by excluding interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization.

**Simplified Profit Assessment** For many, EBITDA provides a simplified snapshot of a company’s earnings potential without the noise of financial and accounting decisions. This makes it a go-to metric for quick assessments and comparisons.

## EBITDA Calculation: How to Calculate EBITDA

Accurate EBITDA calculation is a key part of the overall company valuation. Accountants employ two formulas to calculate the EBITDA value:

- **EBITDA = Net Profit + Interest + Taxes + Depreciation + Amortization**
- **EBITDA = Operating Income + Depreciation + Amortization**

Both formulas ultimately provide the same outcome through different methods. The first formula starts from the net profit and adds back interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization, making it comprehensive for understanding all adjustments. The second formula begins with operating income and focuses on adding non-cash charges, which might be more straightforward depending on the available financial statement data.

The information required for EBITDA calculation should be included in the company’s income statement. Here, it is essential to reiterate the importance of accurate results, as inaccuracy can result in overvaluation or undervaluation of the business and the company’s profitability. Therefore, it is advisable to invest in a reliable accounting system or consider working with experienced and trusted accountants when keeping your financials up to date to ensure accurate [EBITDA analysis](https://windes.com/services/advisory/mergers-and-acquisitions-strategy/).

Incorporating these formulas into your financial review process gives a clearer picture of operating performance, stripping away non-operational expenses and non-cash charges. This clarity is crucial for potential investors or stakeholders assessing the company’s core earnings capacity.

## Evaluating EBITDA Calculation

EBITDA calculation is merely the first step. The next step is determining if your calculated EBITDA reflects a good and profitable standing. Companies employ different measures to evaluate their EBITDA calculation and derive meaningful information. These measures include the following:

### EBITDA Margin

EBITDA margin determines the percentage of calculated EBITDA against the overall revenue generated by the company. The goal is to determine the amount of cash profit that a company makes in a year. If the EBITDA margin of a company is greater than the EBITDA margin of other businesses, it indicates that the company has greater growth potential. The formula used to calculate the EBITDA margin is as follows:

**EBITDA Margin = Calculated EBITDA / Total Revenue**

Let’s look at an example to understand how businesses calculate and use EBITDA margin. Suppose that two companies calculate their EBITDA margins based on their respective EBITDA and total revenue as follows:

- Company A’s EBITDA is $650,000, and its total revenue is $7,000,000. The resulting EBITDA margin is 9.3%.
- Company B’s EBITDA is $600,000, and its total revenue is $6,000,000. The resulting EBITDA margin is 10%.

Despite its higher EBITDA, Company A has a lower EBITDA margin than Company B, indicating higher growth potential and a more promising future than Company B. Based on these results, analysts, potential buyers, investors, and business owners weighing different options will find Company B to be a more lucrative option regardless of its lesser total revenue and smaller EBITDA.

### EBITDA Coverage Ratio

The EBITDA coverage ratio is another measure of EBITDA value. It is a solvency ratio that measures a business’s ability to pay off its liabilities associated with leases and debts using EBITDA.

After the EBITDA calculation, you can derive the EBITDA coverage ratio by dividing the sum of lease payments and EBITDA by the sum of principal and interest (debt) payments and lease payments. The formula used to calculate the EBITDA coverage ratio is as follows:

- **EBITDA Coverage Ratio: (EBITDA + Lease Payments)/ (Interest Payments + Principal Payments + Lease Payments)**

When the resulting value is greater than 1, the company is in a strong position to pay off its liabilities, debts, and other obligations. As a result, it may be an attractive option for investors and potential buyers.

Businesses calculate the EBITDA coverage ratio because, unlike the EBITDA margin, this measure is not based on comparing different companies. Instead, it indicates the company’s independent performance.

### Adjusted EBITDA

The distinctions between EBITDA and adjusted EBITDA are minor but important to understand. Adjusted EBITDA normalizes this metric depending on a company’s revenue and expenses. These can differ significantly among businesses, making it difficult for analysts and buyers to assess whether one is more enticing than another.

By standardizing cash flows and income and removing anomalies, such as owner bonuses, redundant assets, rent paid above market value, etc., Adjusted EBITDA enables people to analyze multiple enterprises at once, regardless of variances in industry, geography, or other factors. However, several additional considerations should be made when comparing EBITDA ratios between companies to ensure an accurate evaluation.

#### Industry Comparison

It is crucial to compare companies within the same industry. Different industries have vastly different average EBITDA ratios. For instance, high-growth industries tend to exhibit higher ratios, while low-growth sectors often display lower ones.

Operational Alignment is also important to ensure companies have similar operations and customer bases. This alignment helps provide a consistent benchmark.

#### Financial Metrics

Look at the margins and growth rates of the companies. Identical or similar financial dynamics are essential for a fair comparison. A high Enterprise Value/EBITDA ratio may suggest an overvalued company, while a low ratio might indicate undervaluation.

Considering these factors makes adjusted EBITDA even more powerful for evaluating companies. This comprehensive approach allows investors to make informed decisions by understanding the numbers and their context.

#### Other Irregularities

Accountants use any of the basic EBITDA formulas discussed above to calculate adjusted EBITDA. However, before this step, removing the costs associated with various irregular and one-time expenses that do not impact daily business operations is crucial.

Standardizing EBITDA gives an adjusted or normalized EBITDA value that is easier to compare for different companies than EBITDA. It also allows businesses and individuals to compare the company’s EBITDA with the industry. Here is a list of common EBITDA adjustments:

- Non-operating income
- Noncash expenses
- Unrealized loss or gain
- Single-time loss or gain
- Litigation expense
- Donations
- Goodwill impairments
- Above market compensation for the owner (in private firms)
- Assets write down
- Gain or loss in foreign exchange

Companies typically determine Adjusted EBITDA on an annual basis for valuation analysis. This approach provides a comprehensive view of financial performance over a longer period, which can be crucial for making strategic decisions.

However, businesses can also calculate Adjusted EBITDA every quarter for internal use. This practice helps monitor performance trends more frequently and adjust strategies as needed.

Companies may calculate EBITDA monthly for even more immediate insights. This is particularly useful when anticipating a potential M&A transaction, as it provides a more detailed and up-to-date financial picture.

- **Monthly Calculations**: Offers granularity, which is especially valuable in dynamic situations like M&A.
- **Quarterly Calculations**: Useful for regular performance reviews and strategic adjustments.
- **Annual Calculations**: Provides a broad overview for valuation and long-term planning.

By leveraging these different time frames, companies can better tailor their financial analysis to suit their specific needs and objectives.

## Why Is It Important to Calculate EBITDA?

Understanding EBITDA calculation and evaluation is important for business owners for two main reasons. First, EBITDA provides a clear idea of the company’s value. Second, it demonstrates the company’s worth to potential buyers and investors, painting a picture of its growth opportunities.

It analyzes and compares profitability among companies and industries, eliminating the impacts of financing, government, and other accounting decisions to provide a raw indication of earnings. Therefore, it is readily used by owners, buyers, private equity investors, and analysts worldwide. It is an important standout formula that provides an overview of business value, assisting companies and individuals in making crucial business decisions. It can also be used as an alternative for cash flow.

Moreover, the EBITDA multiple can provide an estimated valuation range for the company.

When using EBITDA for business valuation, several factors should be considered to ensure an accurate assessment:

**Industry Context:** Different industries have distinct average EBITDA ratios influenced by growth dynamics. High-growth industries typically exhibit higher ratios, making comparing companies within the same sector essential.

**Operational Similarity:** Comparisons should be made between companies with similar operations, customers, and margins. This ensures that the valuation reflects the business’s performance rather than external market conditions.

**Growth Rates:** Understanding a company’s growth rate is crucial, as it can affect the perceived value. High-growth companies may justify higher valuations due to future profit potential.

**Overvaluation vs. Undervaluation:** By comparing a company’s EBITDA ratio to industry averages, investors can determine whether it is overvalued or undervalued, guiding informed investment decisions.

Incorporating these considerations allows for a more nuanced and reliable use of the EBITDA metric, ensuring that it is a robust tool for valuing businesses across diverse industries.

### EBITDA Multiple

An EBITDA multiple is a ratio that compares the annual EBITDA with the company’s overall enterprise value (EV). Like several other EBITDA variations, it determines and compares a company’s value with similar businesses. The formula used to calculate the EBITDA multiple is as follows.

- **EBITDA Multiple = Enterprise Value / EBITDA**

### Calculating Enterprise Value

As the formula demonstrates, determining the company’s enterprise value is the first step in calculating the EBITDA multiple. However, enterprise value is only applicable to public companies. Still, private companies can benefit from this calculation by applying it to publicly traded peer companies to infer an approximate EBITDA multiple for their business.

Determining a company’s enterprise value can be calculated by determining the sum of debt, minority interest, market capitalization, and preferred shares. Next, subtract the cash and cash equivalents, including marketable securities, bank accounts, treasury bills, etc.

The formula for calculating Enterprise value is:

- **Enterprise Value = (Market Capitalization + Value of Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred Shares) – (Cash & Cash Equivalents)**

Once you have calculated the Enterprise value, divide it by the company’s EBITDA to find the EV/EBITDA Multiple ratio. It indicates if the company’s value is under or over-stated. A high ratio shows that the company value might be overstated, while a low ratio may indicate that the company is undervalued.

One benefit of using the EBITDA multiple is that it considers the company debt, which other multiples like the Price to Earnings ratio (or P/E ratio) overlook. Business owners must include EBITDA in the financial terms when preparing to market a business as part of the exit strategy.

The goal is to speak the language of the buyer. In other words, use terms and measures that they are familiar with. Since EBITDA is widely accepted worldwide, it can help owners provide potential buyers with valuable insights into the business. Another measure that you should include is income. There are two types of income to consider: Net Income and Operating Income.

### Net Income & EBITDA

EBITDA indicates a company’s ability to consistently profit, while net income indicates a company’s total earnings. Net income is generally used to identify the value of earnings for every share of the business. It can be calculated using the following formula.

- **Net Income = Revenue – Business Expense**

### Operating Income & EBITDA

Operating income can be defined as the profit after removing the company’s operating expenses, including amortization and depreciation. An operating expense is an ongoing cost of running a business. It can be calculated using the following formula.

- **Operating Income = Revenue – Cost of Goods Sold – Operating Expenses**

EBITDA takes it to the next level by removing these expenses and focusing solely on the company’s profitability. Nonetheless, Operating income and EBITDA are important calculations that can be employed to value a business. Operating income is used to analyze the production efficiency of the company’s operations.

## What Can You Do to Increase Company EBITDA?

When presenting their company’s financials to exit the business, company owners strive to present EBITDA and other statistics dating back at least 3 to 5 years. This data shows how the company has grown, telling purchasers that the potential for continuing growth is good.

Higher EBITDA indicates better company performance. Therefore, business owners can improve the company’s EBITDA to make the company more attractive to potential buyers and investors. This can be achieved by recasting company financials.

### Recasting EBITDA Value

Recasting is the process of amending or re-releasing earning statements with specific intent. The financial experts will review company financials to reinsert any one-off expenses or earnings. Reexamining the financials with the required adjustments will lead to a more precise and promising business picture for potential buyers.

Recasting is not the same as hindering the numbers to manipulate buyers. It is not an opportunity to hide facts, as discrepancies will be uncovered during [due diligence](https://windes.com/due-diligence-checklist-for-ma-before-buying-a-business/).

Several aspects of previously published statements can be re-casted to enhance the EBITDA of a company to present a more accurate and relevant picture of its value. Here are some of the aspects you may want to recast.

1. **Expenses or Revenue from Non-Essential Assets:** Buyers may not recognize expenses or revenue associated with unnecessary assets. For example, imagine that you rent a property in the country for a corporate retreat every year. A potential buyer may not recognize this cost.
2. **One-Time Fees:** Buyers are more interested in learning about the ongoing costs they will incur once they take over. Therefore, if you spent money on a one-time marketing campaign or litigation, these costs do not represent the ongoing costs the buyer would have to take on.
3. **Owner’s Salary & Bonuses:** Owner salaries and bonuses are typically higher than employee salaries and benefits. However, the owner’s salary and bonuses may vary depending on the owner. Therefore, this is not a cost that the buyer must follow.
4. **Repair & Maintenance Expense:** Business owners often categorize capital expenses as repair or maintenance costs to bring down taxes. However, it is important to understand that this practice may hurt the company’s valuation as it reduces its historical EBITDA.
5. **Non-Arms-Length Expenses & Revenue:** This category includes all payment transactions in which the company paid an amount smaller or greater than the market rate. For example, imagine a business renting a building owned by one of the partners for a price lower than the market value. Such costs must be adjusted to reflect the right market value, as the buyer is unlikely to maintain the same values.

These are the five key areas that companies can adjust to increase the EBITDA and provide a better view of the business to potential buyers. However, make sure that EBITDA and other financials represent a fair and honest reflection of your business valuation.

## EBITDA in Business Valuation: Benefits and Limitations

EBITDA significantly benefits business owners, stakeholders, investors, analysts, and buyers by indicating the company’s fair value. However, there is room for exploitation, which may lead to undesired consequences in the long run for all parties involved.

### Benefits of EBITDA

Here are some reasons you may want to calculate company EBITDA and include it in the financials.

- The use of EBITDA is widespread. Different groups, including buyers and sellers worldwide, commonly employ it. Therefore, diverse stakeholders can use EBITDA to compare various business valuations and make informed decisions.
- EBITDA removes unique variables that vary from business to business. It includes tax rates, interest rates, depreciation, and amortization. This approach provides an accurate representation of the company’s operating performance.
- EBITDA is easy to calculate and evaluate. The calculations are rather straightforward and provide highly valuable information.
- Finally, EBITDA is a reliable business valuation metric that allows buyers to focus on the baseline profitability of the company.

### Why Do Some Investors Criticize EBITDA?

Many investors express skepticism towards EBITDA for several key reasons:

1. **Lack of Recognition by Accounting Standards:** EBITDA is not a metric recognized by standard accounting practices like GAAP or IFRS. This lack of formal endorsement raises concerns about its reliability and the consistency of its application across different companies.
2. **Overly Optimistic Financial Picture:** By focusing solely on earnings before interest, taxes, and non-cash deductions, EBITDA can paint an overly positive picture of a company’s financial health. It ignores the costs of debt (interest) and taxes, which are crucial elements of real-world business operations.
3. **Omission of Depreciation and Capital Expenditures:** EBITDA does not account for depreciation, which represents the natural wear and tear on assets over time. Similarly, it overlooks capital expenditures, the funds used to sustain or expand a company’s asset base. This omission can inflate the perceived profitability, as it does not reflect necessary spending to maintain or enhance assets.
4. **Potential for Misleading Comparisons:** Companies might have vastly different debt levels or face different tax rates. By omitting these key elements, EBITDA can make disparate companies appear comparable when, in reality, their financial situations are quite different.
5. **Lack of Cash Flow Insight:** Since EBITDA excludes the cash outflows for taxes and interest, it does not provide an accurate picture of a company’s cash generation ability, a crucial aspect of assessing operational health.

In summary, while EBITDA can offer a quick snapshot of operating performance, its exclusions, and optimistic nature lead many investors to approach it cautiously, seeking a fuller picture through more comprehensive financial metrics.

## What is EBITDA?

**EBITDA**—short for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization—measures a company’s normalized operating cash flow generated by its core business activities.

In simple terms, EBITDA is a proxy for the recurring operating profitability of a company since the effects of non-cash items like depreciation and amortization (D&A) are removed.

EBITDA is calculated by adjusting operating income (EBIT) for non-cash items, namely the add-back of depreciation and amortization (D&A). In contrast, the formula to calculate EBITDA can start with net income, from which taxes, interest expense, depreciation, and amortization are added back.

- EBITDA stands for "Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization" and reflects the normalized, pre-tax operating cash flow generated by the core business activities of a company.
- EBITDA measures a company's operational performance since only the pre-tax cash flow generated by its core business activities is factored in.
- The formula to calculate EBITDA adjusts operating income (EBIT) for non-cash items, such as the add-back of depreciation and amortization (D&A), and non-recurring items.
- The depreciation and amortization (D&A) expense is excluded from EBITDA and treated as a non-cash add-back because there was no real outflow of cash incurred.
- EBITDA is a non-GAAP measure, yet the most widely used profit metric in finance because the metric is unaffected by discretionary management decisions, like the capital structure.

## How to Calculate EBITDA

EBITDA reflects the operating performance attributable to a company’s core business activities, expressed on a normalized basis.

The widespread use of EBITDA is attributable to the fact that the profit metric is independent of discretionary capital structure decisions and is unaffected by the tax rate, which is jurisdiction-dependent.

EBITDA measures the normalized operating performance of a particular company, and its capacity to generate consistent, recurring [cash](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cash-equivalents/) flow from its core business activities.

EBITDA is a non-GAAP financial measure that deliberately excludes interest and income taxes, as well as adjusts for non-cash items, such as [depreciation](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/depreciation/) and [amortization](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/intangible-assets-amortization/) (D&A).

Therefore, U.S. GAAP accounting standards prohibit the recognition of EBITDA on the income statement.

The step-by-step process to calculate EBITDA is as follows:

- **Step 1** ➝ Calculate Operating Income (EBIT)
- **Step 2** ➝ Add Back Non-Cash Expenses (D&A)
- **Step 3** ➝ Adjust for Non-Recurring Items (Extraordinary, One-Time Items)

## EBITDA Formula

The top-down EBITDA bridge starts with operating profit (EBIT) from the income statement and adds back D&A from the [cash flow statement (CFS)](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cash-flow-statement/).

The formula for calculating EBITDA starts with operating income (EBIT) and adjusts for non-cash items, such as depreciation and amortization (D&A).

**EBITDA =** EBIT **+** Depreciation **+** Amortization

On the income statement, the non-cash D&A expense is seldom broken out as a separate line item, apart from [COGS](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cogs-cost-of-goods-sold/) and [operating expenses](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/operating-expenses/) (SG&A).

Therefore, one practical tip is to retrieve the D&A expense from the [cash flow statement](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/cash-flow-statement/) (CFS) to ensure accuracy.

On the other hand, the bottom-up EBITDA bridge starts with [net income](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/net-income/) and adds back non-operating items (e.g. income taxes and interest) to arrive at EBIT, from which non-cash items like D&A are added back to arrive at EBITDA.

**EBITDA =** Net Income **+** Taxes **+** Interest Expense **+** Depreciation **+** Amortization

The formula to calculate EBITDA starts with net income—from which taxes, interest expense, depreciation, and amortization are added back.

## EBIT vs. EBITDA: What is the Difference?

EBITDA and EBIT are each pre-tax, capital-structure-neutral profit metrics with more commonalities than differences.

- **EBITDA** ➝ “Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization”
- **EBIT** ➝ “Earnings Before Interest and Taxes”

The operating costs incurred by a company—excluding non-cash items like D&A—are subtracted from revenue to calculate EBITDA.

On the other hand, the operating expenses incurred, including non-cash items (D&A), are subtracted from gross profit to calculate EBIT.

**EBIT =** Gross Profit **–** Operating Expenses

EBIT is an accrual-accounting-based GAAP profit metric, whereas EBITDA is a non-GAAP, hybrid measure of profitability.

The exclusion of depreciation and amortization in the EBITDA formula—and inclusion in EBIT—is the differentiating factor between the two metrics.

The percent differential between EBITDA and EBIT—i.e., the “spread”— is contingent on industry-specific factors, like capital intensity.

For instance, the variance between the two metrics can be substantial for capital-intensive companies with significant [Capex](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/capital-expenditure-capex/) spending requirements.

## What are the Full-Form EBITDA Components?

The full-form components of EBITDA are each described in the following table.

| Full-Form EBITDA | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Earnings** | - The “Earnings” component refers to a company’s operating income (EBIT) in a specified period. EBIT is computed by subtracting the company’s operating costs (e.g., COGS, SG&A, R&D) from its net revenue in the corresponding period. |
| **Interest** | - The “Interest” component comprises two parts, consolidated on a “net” basis.<br>- Interest expense is the periodic payments owed to lenders as part of the financing arrangement over the borrowing term, i.e., the cost of debt (“cash outflow”).<br>- Interest income is the earnings brought in from investing cash in fixed-income securities, government bonds, marketable securities, and more (“cash inflow”). |
| **Taxes** | - The “Taxes” paid are a mandatory obligation attributable to all public and private companies, but the EBITDA metric deliberately ignores the tax expense.<br>- The rationale for neglecting taxes is intuitively the same as the treatment of interest, where non-operating items are excluded.<br>- The tax rate at which the dollar amount of owed taxes is different per jurisdiction.<br>- Company-specific factors can also impact the income tax provision, such as net operating losses (NOLs), deferred taxes, and tax credits. |
| **Depreciation** | - The “Depreciation” component reduces the value of fixed assets (PP&E) across its useful life assumption, which is the estimated number of years in which the fixed [asset](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/asset/) is expected to contribute positive economic benefits.<br>- The annual depreciation is recorded as an expense on the income statement but treated as an add-back because there was no real movement of cash. |
| **Amortization** | - The “Amortization” component is conceptually identical to depreciation, with the only distinction being that amortization incrementally reduces the value of intangible assets, such as patents and copyrights, rather than tangible assets like machinery.<br>- The depreciation and amortization (D&A) expense represents a non-cash item recorded on the income statement to abide by U.S. GAAP accounting standards. |

## Why is EBITDA a Non-GAAP Measure?

EBITDA is a non-GAAP measure, thereby, there is no standardized, consistent set of rules dictating the specific items that belong in the formula.

Non-GAAP measures are not permitted to be reported on a company’s financial statements filed with the [Securities and Exchange Committee (SEC)](https://www.sec.gov/corpfin/non-gaap-financial-measures.htm).

However, [sell-side](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/sell-side-vs-buy-side/) equity research analysts, [buy-side](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/sell-side-vs-buy-side/) analysts, and other participants in the public equities market pay considerable attention to non-GAAP measures.

In fact, certain public companies prioritize their EBITDA in management presentations, press releases, and earnings calls based on the notion that non-GAAP metrics reflect their operating performance more accurately than accrual-based metrics.

## EBITDA vs. Net Income: What is the Difference?

EBITDA and net income are each common measures of profitability, but the former is oriented around a company’s core operating performance, whereas the latter represents the [accounting profitability](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/net-income/) per GAAP reporting standards.

From net income, EBITDA can be calculated by adding back interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization.

**EBITDA =** Net Income **+** Interest **+** Taxes **+** Depreciation **+** Amortization

The adjustments applied to net income—e.g. interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization—are each non-operating items (and EBITDA only measures operating performance).

The net income (or the “bottom line”) is the accounting profitability of a company, inclusive of operating and non-operating expenses and non-operating income.

**Net Income =** Revenue **–** Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) **–** Operating Expenses **–** Interest **–** Income Taxes

Unlike EBITDA, net income is recognized on the income statement since the metric abides by the reporting guidelines established under GAAP.

The drawback to net income, however, is that [accrual accounting](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/accrual-accounting-vs-cash-basis-accounting/) is imperfect, and the metric is impacted by one-time and non-recurring items, distorting the implied profitability. Hence, a company that reports positive net income can still be at [risk](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/ai-in-risk-management/) of becoming distressed and filing for bankruptcy.

EBITDA is, therefore, far better suited for comparison between companies operating in the same (or adjacent) industries with different capital structures, tax rates, and depreciation policies.

## Why is D&A an Add-Back to EBITDA?

The cash flow statement (CFS) reconciles net income—the GAAP-based accounting profit of a company—for non-cash items and the change in net [working capital](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/working-capital/) (NWC) to track the real movement of cash in a given period.

The recognition of the D&A expense on the income statement is to abide by the accrual accounting reporting guidelines (U.S. GAAP) established by the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB).

- **Depreciation** ➝ The [depreciation expense](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/depreciation/) is embedded within the cost of goods sold (COGS) or operating expenses (SG&A) section on the income statement. The recognition of depreciation reduces the carrying value of a company’s fixed assets (PP&E) over its useful life.
- **Amortization** ➝ The amortization expense is virtually identical to the concept of depreciation. The distinction is that amortization is the incremental reduction in the carrying value of intangible assets.

The depreciation expense is the allocation of the initial cash outlay associated with [capital expenditures](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/capital-expenditure-capex/) (Capex)—i.e., the purchase of a fixed asset (PP&E)—over its useful life to “smoothen” the recognition of the expenditure on the income statement and abide by the matching principle in accrual accounting.

The term “normalized operating expenses” refers to a company’s recurring operating costs, like SG&A and R&D, while excluding non-cash expenses.

Hence, the depreciation and amortization expense (D&A) is treated as a non-cash add-back on the cash flow statement (CFS) since no actual outflow of cash occurred. Likewise, the equation to solve for EBITDA adds back the non-cash D&A expense to operating income (EBIT) based on the same logic.

## What is a Good EBITDA?

The EBITDA profit metric, by itself as a standalone metric, does not offer much practical insight into a company’s recent operating performance.

For the sake of comparability, EBITDA must be divided by revenue in the corresponding period to calculate the EBITDA margin, a standardized measure of profitability widely used across a broad range of industries.

The formula to calculate the EBITDA margin is equal to EBITDA divided by revenue, expressed as a percentage.

**EBITDA Margin (%) =** EBITDA **÷** Revenue

The EBITDA margin answers the following question, _“For each dollar of revenue generated, what percentage of it trickles down to EBITDA?”_

The EBITDA margin is expressed as a percentage and measures a company’s operational efficiency in producing sustainable operating profits.

Generally speaking, an EBITDA margin above 10% is viewed as “good,” while an EBITDA margin that exceeds 20% is perceived as “great.”

- **Historical Benchmark** ➝ Once converted into percentage form, a company’s EBITDA margin can be compared to historical periods to analyze the recent trajectory of its margin profile.
- **Comparative Analysis** ➝ The company’s EBITDA margin can also be used to grasp its relative standing with regard to the industry benchmark, which is set by its comparable industry peers.

There is no “good” EBITDA value per se without adequate context, such as the specific industry in which the company operates, the market size of the industry (i.e., potential revenue opportunity), and the company’s current placement in its overall life cycle, at the very least.

_**Learn More** **→** [Profit Margins by Sector](https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/%7Eadamodar/New_Home_Page/datafile/margin.html) (Source: Aswath Damodaran, NYU Stern)_

## How to Forecast EBITDA

EBITDA is seldom forecasted in practice because of the implicit assumption that the underlying components of the metric are tied to revenue at a fixed percentage.

However, one exception is to perform a “back-of-the-envelope” analysis on a late-stage, mature company exhibiting stable operating performance and minimal growth (e.g., 3% to 5% YoY growth).

For instance, a private equity firm might forecast the EBITDA of a company to build a short-form [LBO model](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/lbo-modeling-test-example-solutions/) to vet a potential acquisition.

The formula to forecast EBITDA is equal to the product of the EBITDA margin assumption and the projected revenue in the coinciding period.

**Forecasted EBITDA =** EBITDA Margin **×** Projected Revenue

The EBITDA margin assumption is normally set based on the historical margin profile of the company and recent trajectory in the trailing couple of periods, including the industry benchmark (“comps”).

But to reiterate—as it bears repeating—forecasting EBITDA is merely a “quick-and-dirty” method to determine the economic feasibility of an investment opportunity and precedes a more granular analysis, assuming the acquisition is deemed worthwhile.

## What are the Pros and Cons of EBITDA?

EBITDA is frequently used across all fields of corporate finance because of the ease of computing the metric (i.e.,” back of the envelope”), despite the negative criticism supporting the notion that EBITDA is a flawed measure of profitability.

- **Widespread Use** ➝ The prevalence of EBITDA is particularly common in corporate valuation and mergers and acquisitions (M&A), such as the offer price (or the purchase multiple).
- **Capital Structure Neutral** ➝ Since EBITDA removes the impact of one-time, extraordinary items and is considered a capital-structure neutral metric, comparisons among different companies are easier (i.e., closer to being “apples-to-apples.”).
- **Unaffected by Taxes** ➝ The prevalence of EBITDA in valuation multiples is tied to the unlevered aspect of the metric, wherein the effects of financing and taxes are excluded. The tax rate, which determines the income tax owed, can vary based on jurisdiction.
- **Non-Cash Items** ➝ The EBITDA metric is adjusted to remove the effects of non-cash expenses, such as depreciation and amortization (D&A). In effect, the operating metric is not distorted by non-cash items that can be substantial for certain companies, particularly those operating in capital-intensive sectors (e.g., manufacturing, industrials, and telecom).
- **Non-Recurring Items** ➝ Often, non-recurring items or one-time events, such as inventory impairment and PP&E impairment, lead to write-downs or write-offs for bookkeeping purposes, which affects a company’s GAAP-based financials. Considering such extraordinary events are non-recurring, removing the effects is rational for forecasting and peer comparisons (i.e., “scrubbed”).

However, despite its widespread use, EBITDA receives criticism for portraying an inaccurate and potentially misleading representation of a company’s cash flow profile (and profitability).

The common sources of criticism supporting the notion that EBITDA is a flawed measure of cash flow are as follows:

- **Capital Expenditure (Capex)** ➝ The primary source of criticism surrounding EBITDA is the neglect of Capex. For most companies, Capex is a major recurring cash outflow captured on the cash flow statement, but the full expenditure is not recognized on the income statement. Instead, Capex is allocated across the fixed asset’s useful life assumption via depreciation because the monetary benefits are anticipated to exceed one year (>12 months).
- **Change in Net Working Capital (NWC)** ➝ The [change in NWC](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/change-in-net-working-capital-nwc/) is the increase (or decrease) in a company’s operational current assets and operational current liabilities. The change in NWC can significantly impact free cash flow (FCF), but EBITDA neglects the cash needed to fund working capital needs.
- **Non-GAAP Measure** ➝ Since EBITDA is a non-GAAP metric, the lack of standardization and inconsistency by which specific items are included (or excluded) offers the management team more discretion on the adjustments to apply (“wiggle room” for earnings manipulation).

## How to Calculate FCFF from EBITDA

The [free cash flow to firm (FCFF)](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/free-cash-flow-to-firm-fcff/) metric—or “unlevered free cash flow”—is the residual cash flow attributable to all capital providers, including common equity shareholders, preferred stockholders, and debt lenders.

The formula to calculate the [free cash flow to firm](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/free-cash-flow-to-firm-fcff/) (FCFF) starts with [NOPAT](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/nopat-net-operating-profit-after-tax/) and adjusts for D&A, the change in NWC, and Capex.

**Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)** = NOPAT **+** D&A **–** Change in NWC **–** Capex

Therefore, to calculate free cash flow to firm (FCFF) starting from EBITDA, the first step is to deduct D&A from EBITDA to arrive at operating income (EBIT).

The next step is to tax-affect EBIT to calculate net operating profit after taxes (NOPAT), or “EBIAT”.

**Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT) =** EBIT **×** (1 **–** Tax Rate)

Once NOPAT has been calculated, the subsequent steps are to adjust for non-cash expenses (D&A), subtract the [change in net working capital (NWC)](https://www.wallstreetprep.com/knowledge/change-in-net-working-capital-nwc/), and deduct Capex, resulting in FCFF.

**Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF) =** \[(EBITDA **–** D&A) **×** Tax Rate\] **+** D&A **–** Change in NWC **–** Capex

The “\[(EBITDA – D&A) × Tax Rate\]” component of the equation computes NOPAT. Therefore, the adjustments applied are identical thereafter to arrive at FCFF from EBITDA.

## 1\. Income Statement Assumptions

Suppose we’re tasked with calculating the EBITDA for a company that generated $100 million in revenue as of its latest fiscal year.

The operating costs incurred by the company were $25 million in COGS, $20 million in SG&A, and $10 million in R&D.

- Revenue = $100 million
- Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) = ($25 million)
- Selling, General and Administrative (SG&A) = ($20 million)
- Research and Development (R&D) = ($10 million)

By subtracting COGS from revenue, we can calculate our company’s gross profit, which comes out to $75 million (and gross margin of 75%).

- Gross Profit = $100 million – $25 million = $75 million

The next profit metric to calculate is operating income (EBIT), which equals gross profit minus operating expenses, i.e., the SG&A and R&D expenses.

Upon inserting the provided historical data into the formula, the operating income (EBIT) of our company comes out to $45 million (and operating margin of 45%).

- Operating Income (EBIT) = $75 million – $20 million – $10 million = $45 million

## 2\. GAAP to Non-GAAP Reconciliation

The next section from the operating income line is the non-operating items section, where the only line item recorded is $5 million in interest expense.

If interest expense is deducted from EBIT, we are left with earnings before taxes (EBT), otherwise known as pre-tax income.

- Interest Expense, net = ($5 million)
- Pre-Tax Income (EBT) = $45 million – $5 million = $40 million

Only one step is left before we reach our company’s net income, which is calculated by subtracting taxes from pre-tax income (EBT).

The tax rate is assumed to be 20%, which we’ll multiply by our pre-tax income (EBT), which comes out to $8 million in taxes.

After subtracting the $8 million tax expense from our EBT, we can determine our company’s net income to be $32 million, which reflects the accrual-accounting based profitability inclusive of all operating and non-operating costs.

- Taxes = ($8 million)
- Net Income = $40 million – $8 million = $32 million

However, before calculating EBITDA starting from either revenue (“top-down bridge”) or net income (“bottom-up bridge”), we are still missing one critical assumption: the depreciation and amortization (D&A) expense.

The D&A expense is embedded within COGS and operating expenses on the income statement (and rarely broken out separately).

For illustrative purposes, we’ll assume the company recognized $5 million in depreciation and amortization (D&A) on the cash flow statement (CFS).

- Depreciation and Amortization (D&A) = $5 million

## 3\. EBITDA Calculation Example (Top-Down Bridge)

Our next section comprises two parts, where we’ll calculate the EBITDA of our hypothetical company using the top-down bridge followed by the bottom-up bridge.

Under the top-down method, we’ll start by linking to EBIT from our income statement and adding back the $5 million in D&A, which equals $50 million in EBITDA.

- EBITDA = $45 million + $5 million = $50 million

By dividing our company’s EBITDA by revenue, the EBITDA margin is 50%.

- EBITDA Margin (%) = $50 million ÷ $100 million = 50.0%

## 4\. EBITDA Calculation Example (Bottom-Up Bridge)

In contrast, the bottom-up method to calculate EBITDA starts with net income (or “accounting profits”) from the income statement.

The net income (the “bottom line”) is inclusive of all operating and non-operating costs, such as COGS, SG&A, and R&D.

To calculate EBITDA from net income, we’ll add back taxes, interest expense, and D&A to arrive at an implied EBITDA of $50 million (and an EBITDA margin of 50%), confirming our prior calculation is, in fact, correct.

- EBITDA = $32 million + $8 million + $5 million + $5 million = $50 million
- EBITDA Margin (%) = $50 million ÷ $100 million = 50.0%

# What is EBITDA?

_Please note that this article is intended for educational purposes only and should not be deemed to be or used as legal, employment, or health & safety advice. For guidance or advice specific to your business, consult with a qualified professional._

## What does EBITDA stand for?

EBITDA stands for ‘Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, [Depreciation](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/depreciation) and Amortisation’. It is a measure of profitability. The benefit of EBITDA is that it focuses on a company’s core performance rather than the effects of non-core financial expenses.

The main drawback of EBITDA is that financial expenses can make a great difference to a company’s financial health, thus creating a misleading impression.

## Example of EBITDA

There are two ways to perform the EBITDA calculation. The first EBITDA formula is:

Net Income + Taxes + Interest Expenses + Depreciation + Amortisation = EBITDA.

The second is:

Operating Income + Depreciation & Amortisation = EBITDA.

All of these variables can be found on a company’s [profit and loss statement](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/profit-and-loss-statement) or its [balance sheet](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/balance-sheet). Most are shown as individual line items. Operating income may not be shown but can easily be calculated: simply subtract the [operating expenses](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/operating-expense) from the gross income.

### EBITDA Margin

The EBITDA margin measures a company’s operating income as a percentage of its overall revenue. The formula for calculating it is:

EBITDA/total revenue = EBITDA margin

Overall the EBITDA margin has much the same advantages and disadvantages as EBITDA itself. The key difference is that it is expressed as a percentage. This makes it usable in like-for-like comparisons, for example, between a company and its competitors in the same industry.

## EBITDA vs EBITDAX

EBITDAX stands for ‘Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation (or Depletion), Amortisation, and Exploration Expense’. It is used only in connection with exploration and production companies. These are companies that aim to extract and sell natural resources such as oil and minerals.

Using EBITDAX essentially has the same effect as using EBITDA. It should, however, be noted that the EBITDAX calculation will be influenced by the methods used to calculate the exploration costs.

The successful-efforts method, as its name suggests, only allocates the expenses of successful explorations to EBITDAX. The full-cost method allocates all exploration-related expenses to EBITDAX.

To use EBITDAX to calculate the EBITDAX margin, be absolutely sure that you’re comparing like with like. If one company uses the successful-efforts method and the other company uses the full-costs method, your comparison could be highly misleading.

## Benefits of EBITDA

EBITDA indicates how well the company is managing its day-to-day operations, including its core expenses such as the [cost of goods sold](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/cost-goods-sold). As such, it is a very fair indicator of a business’s current state and potential. In some cases, it is much fairer than either [gross profit](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/gross-profit) or [net income](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/net-income).

Gross profit shows a business’s turnover but not how much of that money is being retained after expenses. Net income does this, but it can be heavily influenced by factors beyond a business’s direct control.

For example, if governments or local authorities are struggling to balance their own books, they may resort to raising [taxes](https://squareup.com/gb/en/glossary/corporation-tax). There is nothing any company can do about this, but their net income is still reduced.

## Downsides of EBITDA

The very nature of EBITDA means that it only looks at part of a business’s financial story. This part is undoubtedly important but so are the parts ignored by EBITDA. In particular, companies which pay high interest on their debts could find themselves dangerously exposed to changing circumstances.

For example, if its debts are at variable interest rates (also known as tracker rates), they will increase if the Bank of England raises the [base rate](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/boeapps/database/Bank-Rate.asp), also known as the inter-bank rate. Any rate increases might hit their customers too, which could result in their own cash-flow issues.

## In short

EBITDA is a perfectly valid measure and is useful to investors. For example, it is the basis for the EBITDA margin used to benchmark a company against its peers. EBITDA should always be assessed together with other key performance indicators such as operating income and net income.
