Title: Pricey Nvidia AI processors weigh on cash-strapped startups | Technology | sfexaminer.com
Key Highlights:
As AI startups in San Francisco and elsewhere are raising more money to pay for pricey processors, there’s less money to go around, experts say."/
[...]
What hasn’t been as obvious is how the Santa Clara company’s success has affected the software and venture ecosystem — particularly here in San Francisco, ground zero for the AI industry.
[...]
Nvidia’s control of the AI chip market has allowed it to charge a premium for its processors. That high price in turn has forced AI startups here and elsewhere to raise more money than they would otherwise and spend more of what they raise on hardware.
[...]
The high stakes to play in the game already appear to be leading to consolidation, even in these early days. Companies that aren’t in AI or aren’t among the leaders in the field are losing out on funding as the venture industry directs more funds to meet the needs of a smaller number of AI startups.
[...]
“Nvidia describes itself as a democratizing force, but really, I don’t think we’ve seen that based on the pricing of its chips and the supply crunch,” said Jacob Bourne, an analyst with industry research firm Emarketer.
[...]
In turn, that AI fervor — along with the fact that OpenAI had used a previous version of Nvidia’s processors to develop ChatGPT, and that Nvidia also offered the most well-developed AI chip technology available — ignited demand for Nvidia’s semiconductors, particularly its newly released H100. Despite the H100’s price tag of around $30,000 a piece, a months-long backlog for it quickly developed.
[...]
Although AMD and other companies also offer processors that can be used for
[...]
training, Nvidia dominates the industry. In terms of revenue, Nvidia’s share of the market for graphics chips used for
[...]
processing in data centers is about 97%, according to
[...]
Research, an industry research firm.
[...]
Last year, the market for all chips in data centers that do some kind of AI processing — including graphics chips, or GPUs; traditional central processing units, or CPUs; and specialty chips — was $37.3 billion, according to IDC, another tech research firm. Nvidia’s share of that entire market was about 73%, according to IDC estimates.
[...]
That premium came at a heavy cost for startups and their investors.
[...]
Those who wanted to develop their own generative AI models often invested heavily in Nvidia chips. Those who couldn’t afford to buy chips of their own typically relied on those in data centers operated by the big cloud providers, including Amazon, Microsoft, and Google.
[...]
that option is pricey.
[...]
And that’s assuming startups can even access the chips or the Nvidia-powered servers. Nvidia’s chips have been in such high demand that some startups have struggled to find any to buy or to get time with them on the cloud giants’ servers even if they could afford them, those in and around the industry say.
[...]
“Access to the newer hardware is few and far between,” said John Adams, the chief information officer of San Francisco-based AI startup Alembic, in an email. “This presents an impediment to newer startups which may not have the political or business relationships to acquire and use such hardware.”
[...]
The need to cover — or attempt to cover — the costs of Nvidia’s hardware can be seen in startups’ fundraising efforts.
[...]
Last year, four of the 10 biggest funding rounds raised by startups went to companies developing generative AI technologies using Nvidia chips, according to data from PitchBook, a research firm focused on the venture industry.
[...]
Those big rounds included the massive $10 billion one raised by OpenAI, the largest by far. They also included a $1.3 billion round to Palo Alto-based Inflection AI, which planned to use the money to build out what it described as the world’s largest cluster of AI chips with 22,000 H100 processors.
[...]
But the need for funds went far beyond the biggest companies. AI startups last year raised far bigger funding rounds at all stages of development than startups as a whole, according to data from PitchBook.
[...]
Not only were the rounds bigger, but the companies that raised them devoted far greater portions of their new funds to hardware or cloud-computing services than startups have in the past, said Brendan Burke, an analyst who focuses on AI startups for PitchBook.
[...]
In the past, the typical startup might have devoted about 30% of its funding to cloud-computing services or to paying for Facebook or Google ads to market its services. By contrast, today’s AI startups are devoting up to 60% or 70% of their funding to buying chips or paying for cloud services to run their models, Burke said.
[...]
The high cost of the hardware has had follow-on effects. Relatively speaking, AI startups have less money to spend on hiring people. Coupled with the fact that AI experts are in high demand right now, the workforces of such companies tend to be smaller than comparable non-AI startups, Burke said.
[...]
In addition to the effect on employment, hardware cost have impacted startup funding more generally. Although the amount invested in AI startups ticked up, overall venture investment fell, as did the number of deals — both in AI and outside of it.
[...]
So, essentially, the venture industry is investing increasing amounts of money in fewer AI startups, and companies that aren’t in the AI industry are losing out.
[...]
“The bulk of software startups can be overlooked for the few AI leaders,” Burke said. Venture funding, he said, “is more concentrated.”
[...]
But even being one of the near-term winners in the funding game is no guarantee of long-term success, especially for companies contending with high hardware costs.
[...]
Last week, Microsoft hired away Inflection CEO and co-founder Mustafa Suleyman and much of the startup’s team. Suleyman decided to jump ship after failing to find a successful business model for Inflection’s consumer chatbot service, Suleyman told Bloomberg. The startup is now seeking to sell some of its pricey hardware and has turned to licensing the models it developed to enterprises, Bloomberg reported.
[...]
Pete Flint, a partner at San Francisco venture firm NFX, said he sees a huge opportunity for startups building AI-powered apps using already existing models, even with current hardware prices. But hardware costs remain a huge barrier for companies such as Inflection that are trying to create large models to rival the OpenAIs of the world.
[...]
“The capital needed to do that would be enormous,” he said.

Title: Bay Area energy constraints are slowing AI data center growth
Key Highlights:
Bay Area energy constraints are slowing AI data center growth
[...]
# The Bay Area is losing the data center race. Economists say that could be a good thing
[...]
Work began six years ago on the four-story data center, called SJC37 and owned by Digital Realty, one of the world’s biggest data center landlords. The tan exterior is finished, but there’s no equipment installed — and it could be years before the project gets the power it needs to start operations.
[...]
Silicon Valley Power, a public utility owned by the city of Santa Clara, is working on a $450 million system upgrade to meet demand from data centers and other users, but isn’t scheduled to finish until 2028. Another nearby project owned by data center company Stack Infrastructure is also sitting empty and isn’t expecting power until 2027.
[...]
The delays, first reported by Bloomberg, are emblematic of the Bay Area’s disparities in the AI industry.
[...]
The region is the runaway leader in artificial intelligence, home to both the world’s deepest engineering talent pool and status as headquarters of industry giants like Nvidia, Google and OpenAI.
[...]
But data centers, the physical architecture needed for AI computing, are a different story — and energy shortfalls are a key reason why the Bay Area is losing ground to other U.S. regions when it comes to data center construction.
[...]
As of June, Silicon Valley’s data center power capacity grew by only 25.2 megawatts for a total of 484.4 megawatts compared to a year earlier, making it the sixth-largest market in the country, according to real estate brokerage CBRE. Larger markets like Atlanta added a whopping 969 megawatts over the year.
[...]
“There’s lots of reasons not to put data centers in California — from a cost perspective and an energy supply perspective,” said Bill Dougherty, CBRE executive vice president of data center solutions, who is based in San Francisco. “From the supply side, there’s lots of physical and political constraints.”
[...]
Though some data centers need to be physically close to the AI companies using them, many aren’t constrained by location and can seek the cheapest power, Dougherty said. “The market is naturally constrained by the amount of power we can get,” he said.
[...]
The biggest data center cluster in the world is in Northern Virginia, home to the first non-governmental Internet Exchange Point, a vital connection point for various network service providers, built in 1992. Historically low power costs and a state sales tax exemption on equipment purchases for major data centers cemented its dominance as “one of the best places in the world” for the industry, said Dougherty. The region has seven times as much power capacity as Silicon Valley, and nearly three times as much as Atlanta, the next largest region, according to CBRE.
[...]
In contrast, California’s energy costs are some of the highest in the country, as well as its taxes — and no tax breaks are offered for data centers. Land near urban areas, particularly in Silicon Valley, is pricey and constrained.
[...]
Some economic experts are fine with the Bay Area missing out on the data center boom.
[...]
Ted Egan, San Francisco’s chief economist, is skeptical that data centers bring much economic benefit beyond their construction activity and some tax revenue.
[...]
Digital Realty, one of the world’s biggest data center landlords, owns the SFO12 Data Center center in San Francisco. Compared with other regions, the Bay Area is relatively inhospitable to data centers.
[...]
“They need some electricity, but they don’t need a lot of people,” he previously said. There’s far more economic upside to having highly paid AI engineers working in offices, he said — a key reason why Mission Bay is seeing more growth in economic activity compared to the rest of San Francisco.
[...]
Egan has also said that if AI turns out to be another tech bubble that ends up bursting, data center projects around the country could be canceled first, rather than offices shuttering. That could mean the Bay Area is less vulnerable to an AI downturn than rural data center boomtowns.
[...]
“We remain optimistic about bringing capacity to market as soon as utility infrastructure allows,” the spokesperson said, adding that “sustainability is part of every decision” and the company is focused on supporting “grid improvements to support the growth of AI without wasting resources.”
[...]
Unlike other regions, the Bay Area has largely avoided big electricity price increases attributed to data centers, while the data center-rich Mid-Atlantic region has seen soaring bills attributed to the industry’s boom. But studies are split on data centers’ impact on energy bills, which have risen in much of the country in the past year.
[...]
In a study published earlier this month, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory researchers found that data centers have likely reduced power bills by spreading out utility upgrade costs among more customers. But the study said it was difficult to forecast whether the benefits would continue as energy demand from data centers continues to grow.
[...]
Meanwhile, a June study at Carnegie Mellon University and North Carolina State University warned that more data centers, along with growth in cryptocurrency, could cause U.S. electricity prices to spike 8% by 2030.
[...]
center owners, on data centers’ role in increasing energy costs.
[...]
At the same time, an Amazon-commissioned study by Energy and Environment Economics found that the company’s data centers across four states including California were a net positive for customers, with a 100 megawatt data center generating $3.4 million more for utility companies beyond the costs to power it.
[...]
That figure is projected to reach $6.1 million by 2030, if utilities continue to adjust their rates based on market demand.
[...]
“I think it showed that we are continuing to grow the business smartly without negatively impacting residential ratepayers,” Brandon Oyer, head of energy and water for the Americas at Amazon Web Services, said in an interview. “There’s no evidence that California residents are paying to power our Amazon data centers … data centers can actually help spread out infrastructure costs over a broader base.”
[...]
The utility said in May that for every 1 gigwatt of new data center energy demand, customers could save between 1-2% on monthly bills. Demand for PG&E electricity had swelled to 10 gigawatts for new projects planned over the next decade as of July, enough to power 7.5 million homes.
