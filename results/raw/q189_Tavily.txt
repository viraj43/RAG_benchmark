# Continent

![Page semi-protected](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1b/Semi-protection-shackle.svg/20px-Semi-protection-shackle.svg.png)![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Continental_models-Australia.gif/500px-Continental_models-Australia.gif)

A **continent** is any of several large terrestrial geographical regions. Continents are generally identified by [convention](/wiki/Convention_(norm) "Convention (norm)") rather than any strict criteria. A continent could be a single large [landmass](/wiki/Landmass "Landmass"), a landmass and nearby islands either on or beyond its [continental shelf](/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"), or a part of a larger landmass, as in the case of Asia and Europe within [Eurasia](/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia"). Due to these varying definitions, the number of continents varies, up to seven or as few as four. Most [English-speaking countries](/wiki/English-speaking_world "English-speaking world") recognize seven continents. In order from largest to smallest in area, these seven are [Asia](/wiki/Asia "Asia"), [Africa](/wiki/Africa "Africa"), [North America](/wiki/North_America "North America"), [South America](/wiki/South_America "South America"), [Antarctica](/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica"), [Europe](/wiki/Europe "Europe"), and [Australia](/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)") (or sometimes [Oceania](/wiki/Oceania "Oceania") or [Australasia](/wiki/Australasia "Australasia")). Different variations with fewer continents merge some of these regions; examples of this are merging Asia and Europe into [Eurasia](/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia"),[[1]](#cite_note-NatlGeo1-1) North America and South America into the [Americas](/wiki/Americas "Americas") (or simply America), and Africa, Asia, and Europe into [Afro-Eurasia](/wiki/Afro-Eurasia "Afro-Eurasia").

[Oceanic islands](/wiki/Island#Oceanic_islands "Island") are occasionally grouped with a nearby continent to divide all the world's land into geographical regions. Under this scheme, most of the island countries and territories in the [Pacific Ocean](/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean") are grouped together with the continent of Australia to form the geographical region of [Oceania](/wiki/Oceania "Oceania").[[2]](#cite_note-2)

In [geology](/wiki/Geology "Geology"), a continent is defined as "one of [Earth](/wiki/Earth "Earth")'s major landmasses, including both dry land and [continental shelves](/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf")".[[3]](#cite_note-Glossary2005-3) The [geological continents](#Geological_continents) correspond to seven large areas of continental crust that are found on the [tectonic plates](/wiki/List_of_tectonic_plates "List of tectonic plates"), but exclude small [continental fragments](/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment") such as [Madagascar](/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar") that are generally referred to as [microcontinents](/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment"). [Continental crust](/wiki/Continental_crust "Continental crust") is only known to exist on Earth.[[4]](#cite_note-4)

The idea of [continental drift](/wiki/Continental_drift "Continental drift") gained recognition in the 20th century. It postulates that the current continents formed from the breaking up of a supercontinent ([Pangaea](/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea")) that formed hundreds of millions of years ago.

## Etymology

From the 16th century the English noun *continent* was derived from the term *continent land*, meaning continuous or connected land[[5]](#cite_note-oed-5) and translated from the Latin *terra continens*.[[6]](#cite_note-6) The noun was used to mean "a connected or continuous tract of land" or [mainland](/wiki/Mainland "Mainland").[[5]](#cite_note-oed-5) It was not applied only to very large areas of land—in the 17th century, references were made to the *continents* (or mainlands) of the [Isle of Man](/wiki/Isle_of_Man "Isle of Man"), [Ireland](/wiki/Ireland "Ireland") and [Wales](/wiki/Wales "Wales") and in 1745 to [Sumatra](/wiki/Sumatra "Sumatra").[[5]](#cite_note-oed-5) The word *continent* was used in translating Greek and Latin writings about the three "parts" of the world, although in the original languages no word of exactly the same meaning as *continent* was used.[[7]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199729-7)

While *continent* was used on the one hand for relatively small areas of continuous land, on the other hand geographers again raised Herodotus's query about why a single large landmass should be divided into separate continents. In the mid-17th century, [Peter Heylin](/wiki/Peter_Heylin "Peter Heylin") wrote in his *Cosmographie* that "A Continent is a great quantity of Land, not separated by any Sea from the rest of the World, as the whole Continent of Europe, Asia, Africa." In 1727, [Ephraim Chambers](/wiki/Ephraim_Chambers "Ephraim Chambers") wrote in his *Cyclopædia,* "The world is ordinarily divided into two grand continents: the [Old](/wiki/Old_World "Old World") and the [New](/wiki/New_World "New World")." And in his 1752 atlas, [Emanuel Bowen](/wiki/Emanuel_Bowen "Emanuel Bowen") defined a continent as "a large space of dry land comprehending many countries all joined together, without any separation by water. Thus Europe, Asia, and Africa is one great continent, as America is another."[[8]](#cite_note-8) However, the old idea of Europe, Asia and Africa as "parts" of the world ultimately persisted with these being regarded as separate continents.

## Definitions and application

By convention, continents "are understood to be large, continuous, discrete masses of land, ideally separated by expanses of water".[[9]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199721-9) By this definition, all continents have to be an island of some metric. In modern schemes with five or more recognized continents, at least one pair of continents is joined by land in some fashion. The criterion "large" leads to arbitrary classification: [Greenland](/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"), with a surface area of 2,166,086 square kilometres (836,330 sq mi), is only considered the world's largest island, while [Australia](/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)"), at 7,617,930 square kilometres (2,941,300 sq mi), is deemed the smallest continent.

Earth's major landmasses all have coasts on a single, continuous [World Ocean](/wiki/Ocean#World_Ocean "Ocean"), which is divided into several principal oceanic components by the continents and various geographic criteria.[[10]](#cite_note-10)[[11]](#cite_note-UNAoO-11)

The geological definition of a continent has four criteria: high elevation relative to the ocean floor; a wide range of igneous, metamorphic and sedimentary rocks rich in silica; a crust thicker than the surrounding oceanic crust; and well-defined limits around a large enough area.[[12]](#cite_note-nytimes.com-12)

### Extent

The most restricted meaning of *continent* is that of a continuous[[13]](#cite_note-13)[*[non-tertiary source needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources "Wikipedia:No original research")*] area of land or mainland, with the coastline and any land boundaries forming the edge of the continent. In this sense, the term *[continental Europe](/wiki/Continental_Europe "Continental Europe")* (sometimes referred to in Britain as "the Continent") is used to refer to mainland Europe, excluding islands such as [Great Britain](/wiki/Great_Britain "Great Britain"), [Iceland](/wiki/Iceland "Iceland"), [Ireland](/wiki/Ireland "Ireland"), and [Malta](/wiki/Malta "Malta"), while the term *continent of Australia* may refer to the [mainland of Australia](/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia"), excluding [New Guinea](/wiki/New_Guinea "New Guinea"), [Tasmania](/wiki/Tasmania "Tasmania"), and other nearby islands. Similarly, the *[continental United States](/wiki/Contiguous_United_States#Continental_and_mainland_United_States "Contiguous United States")* refers to "the 49 States (including [Alaska](/wiki/Alaska "Alaska") but excluding [Hawaii](/wiki/Hawaii "Hawaii")) located on the continent of North America, and the [District of Columbia](/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.")."[[14]](#cite_note-14)

From the perspective of geology or [physical geography](/wiki/Physical_geography "Physical geography"), *continent* may be extended beyond the confines of continuous dry land to include the shallow, submerged adjacent area (the [continental shelf](/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"))[[15]](#cite_note-15) and the islands on the shelf ([continental islands](/wiki/Island#Continental_islands "Island")), as they are structurally part of the continent.[[16]](#cite_note-16)

From this perspective, the edge of the continental shelf is the true edge of the continent, as shorelines vary with changes in sea level.[[17]](#cite_note-Ollier-17) In this sense the islands of Great Britain and Ireland are part of Europe, while Australia and the island of New Guinea together form a continent. Taken to its limit, this view could support the view that there are only three continents: Antarctica, Australia-New Guinea, and a single mega-continent which joins Afro-Eurasia and America via the contiguous continental shelf in and around the Bering Sea. The vast size of the latter compared to the first two might even lead some to say it is the only continent, the others being more comparable to Greenland or New Zealand.[[12]](#cite_note-nytimes.com-12)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Island_nations.svg/500px-Island_nations.svg.png)

As a [cultural construct](/wiki/Social_constructionism "Social constructionism"), the concept of a continent may go beyond the continental shelf to include [oceanic islands](/wiki/Island#Oceanic_islands "Island") and continental fragments. In this way, Iceland is considered a part of Europe, and [Madagascar](/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar") a part of Africa. Extrapolating the concept to its extreme, some geographers group the [Australian continental landmass](/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia") with other islands in the Pacific Ocean into [Oceania](/wiki/Oceania "Oceania"), which is usually considered a region rather than a continent. This divides the entire land surface of Earth into continents, regions, or [quasi-continents](/w/index.php?title=Quasi-continents&action=edit&redlink=1 "Quasi-continents (page does not exist)").[[18]](#cite_note-18)

### Separation

The criterion that each continent is a discrete landmass is commonly relaxed due to historical conventions and practical use. Of the seven most globally recognized continents, only Antarctica and Australia are completely separated from other continents by the ocean. Several continents are defined not as absolutely distinct bodies but as "*more or less* discrete masses of land".[[19]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199735-19) Africa and Asia are joined by the [Isthmus of Suez](/wiki/Isthmus_of_Suez "Isthmus of Suez"), and North America and South America by the [Isthmus of Panama](/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama "Isthmus of Panama"). In both cases, there is no complete separation of these landmasses by water (disregarding the [Suez Canal](/wiki/Suez_Canal "Suez Canal") and the [Panama Canal](/wiki/Panama_Canal "Panama Canal"), which are both narrow and shallow, as well as human-made). Both of these [isthmuses](/wiki/Isthmus "Isthmus") are very narrow compared to the bulk of the landmasses they unite.

North America and South America are treated as separate continents in the seven-continent model. However, they may also be viewed as a single continent known as [America](/wiki/Americas "Americas"). This viewpoint was common in the United States until [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), and remains prevalent in some Asian and most Latin American six-continent models.[[20]](#cite_note-20)

The criterion of a discrete landmass is completely disregarded if the continuous landmass of [Eurasia](/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia") is classified as two separate continents (Asia and Europe). [Physiographically](/wiki/Physical_geography "Physical geography"), Europe and the [Indian subcontinent](/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent") are large peninsulas of the Eurasian landmass. However, Europe is considered a continent with its comparatively large land area of 10,180,000 square kilometres (3,930,000 sq mi), while the Indian subcontinent, with less than half that area, is considered a subcontinent. The alternative view—in geology and geography—that Eurasia is a single continent results in a six-continent view of the world. Some view the separation of Eurasia into Asia and Europe as a residue of [Eurocentrism](/wiki/Eurocentrism "Eurocentrism"): "In physical, cultural and historical diversity, [China](/wiki/China "China") and [India](/wiki/India "India") are comparable to the entire European landmass, not to a single European country. [...]."[[21]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents1997104–123-21) However, for historical and cultural reasons, the view of Europe as a separate continent continues in almost all categorizations.

If continents are defined strictly as discrete landmasses, embracing all the contiguous land of a body, then Africa, Asia, and Europe form a single continent which may be referred to as [Afro-Eurasia](/wiki/Afro-Eurasia "Afro-Eurasia").[[22]](#cite_note-McColl-22) Combined with the consolidation of the Americas, this would produce a four-continent model consisting of Afro-Eurasia, America, Antarctica, and Australia.

When sea levels were lower during the [Pleistocene](/wiki/Pleistocene "Pleistocene") [ice ages](/wiki/Ice_age "Ice age"), greater areas of the continental shelf were exposed as dry land, forming [land bridges](/wiki/Land_bridge "Land bridge") between [Tasmania](/wiki/Tasmania "Tasmania") and the [Australian mainland](/wiki/Mainland_Australia "Mainland Australia").[[23]](#cite_note-23) At those times, Australia and New Guinea were a single, continuous continent known as [Sahul](/wiki/Sahul "Sahul"). Likewise, Afro-Eurasia and the Americas were joined by the [Bering Land Bridge](/wiki/Beringia "Beringia"). Other islands, such as [Great Britain](/wiki/Great_Britain "Great Britain"), were joined to the mainlands of their continents. At that time, there were just three discrete landmasses in the world: [Africa](/wiki/Africa "Africa")-[Eurasia](/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia")-[America](/wiki/Americas "Americas"), [Antarctica](/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica"), and [Australia](/wiki/Australia "Australia")-[New Guinea](/wiki/New_Guinea "New Guinea") ([Sahul](/wiki/Sahul "Sahul")).

## Other divisions

### Supercontinents

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Pangaea_200Ma.jpg/500px-Pangaea_200Ma.jpg)

Apart from the current continents, the scope and meaning of the term *continent* includes past geological ones. [Supercontinents](/wiki/Supercontinent "Supercontinent"), largely in evidence earlier in the geological record, are landmasses that comprise most of the world's [cratons](/wiki/Craton "Craton") or continental cores.[[64]](#cite_note-Evans_2013-65) These have included [Vaalbara](/wiki/Vaalbara "Vaalbara"), [Kenorland](/wiki/Kenorland "Kenorland"), [Columbia](/wiki/Columbia_(supercontinent) "Columbia (supercontinent)"), [Rodinia](/wiki/Rodinia "Rodinia"), [Pannotia](/wiki/Pannotia "Pannotia"), and [Pangaea](/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea"). Over time, these supercontinents broke apart into large landmasses which formed the present continents.

### Subcontinents

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/India_78.40398E_20.74980N.jpg/250px-India_78.40398E_20.74980N.jpg)

Certain parts of continents are recognized as subcontinents, especially the large peninsulas separated from the main continental landmass by geographical features. The most widely recognized example is the [Indian subcontinent](/wiki/Indian_subcontinent "Indian subcontinent").[[65]](#cite_note-Baldwin-66) The [Arabian Peninsula](/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula "Arabian Peninsula"), [Southern Africa](/wiki/Southern_Africa "Southern Africa"), the [Southern Cone](/wiki/Southern_Cone "Southern Cone") of South America, and [Alaska](/wiki/Alaska "Alaska") in North America might be considered further examples.[[65]](#cite_note-Baldwin-66)[[66]](#cite_note-67)

In many of these cases, the "subcontinents" concerned are on different [tectonic plates](/wiki/Plate_tectonics "Plate tectonics") from the rest of the continent, providing a geological justification for the terminology.[[67]](#cite_note-Molnar2015-68) [Greenland](/wiki/Greenland "Greenland"), generally considered the [world's largest island](/wiki/List_of_islands_by_area "List of islands by area") on the northeastern periphery of the [North American Plate](/wiki/North_American_Plate "North American Plate"), is sometimes referred to as a subcontinent.[[68]](#cite_note-69)[[69]](#cite_note-FarmerCook2013-70) This is a significant departure from the more conventional view of a subcontinent as comprising a very large peninsula on the fringe of a continent.[[65]](#cite_note-Baldwin-66)

Where the Americas are viewed as a single continent (America), it is divided into two subcontinents ([North America](/wiki/North_America "North America") and [South America](/wiki/South_America "South America"))[[70]](#cite_note-Gallay2015-71)[[71]](#cite_note-InnesHaron2000-72)[[72]](#cite_note-Vivares2014-73) or three ([Central America](/wiki/Central_America "Central America") being the third).[[73]](#cite_note-Leonard2005-74)[[74]](#cite_note-75) When Eurasia is regarded as a single continent, Asia and Europe are treated as subcontinents.[[65]](#cite_note-Baldwin-66)

### Submerged continents

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Zealandia%2C_topographic_map.jpg/250px-Zealandia%2C_topographic_map.jpg)

Some areas of [continental crust](/wiki/Continental_crust "Continental crust") are largely covered by the ocean and may be considered [submerged continents](/wiki/Submerged_continent "Submerged continent"). Notable examples are [Zealandia](/wiki/Zealandia "Zealandia"), emerging from the ocean primarily in [New Zealand](/wiki/New_Zealand "New Zealand") and [New Caledonia](/wiki/New_Caledonia "New Caledonia"),[[75]](#cite_note-76)[*[non-tertiary source needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources "Wikipedia:No original research")*] and the almost completely submerged [Kerguelen Plateau](/wiki/Kerguelen_Plateau "Kerguelen Plateau") in the southern [Indian Ocean](/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean").[[76]](#cite_note-77)

### Microcontinents

Some islands lie on sections of continental crust that have rifted and drifted apart from a main continental landmass. While not considered continents because of their relatively small size, they may be considered [microcontinents](/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment"). Madagascar, the largest example, is usually considered an island of Africa, but its divergent evolution has caused it to be referred to as "the eighth continent" from a biological perspective.[[77]](#cite_note-78)

## History of the concept

### Early concepts of the Old World continents

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Strabo.jpg/250px-Strabo.jpg)

The term "continent" translates the Greek word ἤπειρος, meaning "landmass, terra firma", the proper name of [Epirus](/wiki/Epirus "Epirus") and later especially used for Asia (i.e. [Asia Minor](/wiki/Anatolia "Anatolia")).[[84]](#cite_note-86)

The first distinction between continents was made by [ancient Greek](/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") mariners who gave the names Europe and Asia to the lands on either side of the waterways of the [Aegean Sea](/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea"), the [Dardanelles](/wiki/Dardanelles "Dardanelles") strait, the [Sea of Marmara](/wiki/Sea_of_Marmara "Sea of Marmara"), the [Bosporus](/wiki/Bosporus "Bosporus") strait and the [Black Sea](/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea").[[85]](#cite_note-Toynbee-87) The names were first applied just to lands near the coast and only later extended to include the [hinterlands](/wiki/Hinterland "Hinterland").[[86]](#cite_note-88)[[87]](#cite_note-Pocock2002-89) But the division was only carried through to the end of navigable waterways and "... beyond that point the Hellenic geographers never succeeded in laying their finger on any inland feature in the physical landscape that could offer any convincing line for partitioning an indivisible Eurasia ..."[[85]](#cite_note-Toynbee-87)

Ancient Greek thinkers subsequently debated whether Africa (then called *Libya*) should be considered part of Asia or a third part of the world. Division into three parts eventually came to predominate.[[88]](#cite_note-90) From the Greek viewpoint, the Aegean Sea was the center of the world; Asia lay to the east, Europe to the north and west, and Africa to the south.[[89]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELewis_&_Wigen,_The_Myth_of_Continents199721–22-91) The boundaries between the continents were not fixed. Early on, the Europe–Asia boundary was taken to run from the Black Sea along the [Rioni](/wiki/Rioni "Rioni") River (known then as the *Phasis*) in [Georgia](/wiki/Georgia_(country) "Georgia (country)"). Later it was viewed as running from the Black Sea through [Kerch Strait](/wiki/Kerch_Strait "Kerch Strait"), the [Sea of Azov](/wiki/Sea_of_Azov "Sea of Azov") and along the [Don](/wiki/Don_(river) "Don (river)") River (known then as the *Tanais*) in [Russia](/wiki/Russia "Russia").[[90]](#cite_note-92) The boundary between Asia and Africa was generally taken to be the [Nile](/wiki/Nile "Nile") River. [Herodotus](/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus")[[91]](#cite_note-93) in the 5th century BCE objected to the whole of [Egypt](/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") being split between Asia and Africa ("Libya") and took the boundary to lie along the western border of Egypt, regarding Egypt as part of Asia.[[92]](#cite_note-94)[[93]](#cite_note-95)[[94]](#cite_note-96)[[95]](#cite_note-97) He also questioned the division into three of what is really a single landmass,[[96]](#cite_note-98) a debate that continues nearly two and a half millennia later. Herodotus believed Europe to be larger (at least in width) than the other two continents:

I wonder, then, at those who have mapped out and divided the world into Libya, Asia, and Europe; for the difference between them is great, seeing that in length Europe stretches along both the others together, and it appears to me to be wider beyond all comparison.[[97]](#cite_note-99)

[Eratosthenes](/wiki/Eratosthenes "Eratosthenes"), in the 3rd century BCE, noted that some geographers divided the continents by rivers (the Nile and the Don), thus considering them "islands". Others divided the continents by [isthmuses](/wiki/Isthmus "Isthmus"), calling the continents "peninsulas". These latter geographers set the border between Europe and Asia at the isthmus between the Black Sea and the [Caspian Sea](/wiki/Caspian_Sea "Caspian Sea"), and the border between Asia and Africa at the isthmus between the [Red Sea](/wiki/Red_Sea "Red Sea") and the mouth of [Lake Bardawil](/wiki/Lake_Bardawil "Lake Bardawil") on the [Mediterranean Sea](/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea").[[98]](#cite_note-100)

The Roman author [Pliny the Elder](/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder"), writing in the 1st century CE, stated that "The whole globe is divided into three parts, Europe, Asia, and Africa", adding:

I shall first then speak of Europe, the foster-mother of that people which has conquered all other nations, and itself by far the most beauteous portion of the earth. Indeed, many persons have, not without reason, considered it, not as a third part only of the earth, but as equal to all the rest, looking upon the whole of our globe as divided into two parts only, by a line drawn from the river Tanais to the Straits of Gades.[[99]](#cite_note-101)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/T_and_O_map_Guntherus_Ziner_1472.jpg/250px-T_and_O_map_Guntherus_Ziner_1472.jpg)

Following the fall of the [Western Roman Empire](/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire "Western Roman Empire"), the [culture that developed in its place](/wiki/Western_culture "Western culture"), linked to Latin and the Catholic church, began to associate itself with the concept of Europe.[[87]](#cite_note-Pocock2002-89) Through the Roman period and the [Middle Ages](/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), a few writers took the [Isthmus of Suez](/wiki/Isthmus_of_Suez "Isthmus of Suez") as the boundary between Asia and Africa, but most writers continued to consider it the Nile or the western border of Egypt (Gibbon).[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*] In the Middle Ages, the world was usually portrayed on [T and O maps](/wiki/T_and_O_map "T and O map"), with the T representing the waters dividing the three continents. By the middle of the 18th century, "the fashion of dividing Asia and Africa at the Nile, or at the [Great Catabathmus](/wiki/Great_Catabathmus "Great Catabathmus") [the boundary between Egypt and [Libya](/wiki/Libya "Libya")] farther west, had even then scarcely passed away".[[100]](#cite_note-102)

### European arrival in the Americas

[Christopher Columbus](/wiki/Christopher_Columbus "Christopher Columbus") sailed across the [Atlantic Ocean](/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") to the [Caribbean](/wiki/Caribbean "Caribbean") in 1492, sparking a period of European exploration of the [Americas](/wiki/Americas "Americas"). But despite four voyages to the Americas, Columbus never believed he had reached a new continent—he always thought it was part of Asia.[*[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")*]

In 1501, [Amerigo Vespucci](/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci "Amerigo Vespucci") and [Gonçalo Coelho](/wiki/Gon%C3%A7alo_Coelho "Gonçalo Coelho") attempted to sail around what they considered the southern end of the Asian mainland into the [Indian Ocean](/wiki/Indian_Ocean "Indian Ocean"), passing through [Fernando de Noronha](/wiki/Fernando_de_Noronha "Fernando de Noronha"). After reaching the coast of [Brazil](/wiki/Brazil "Brazil"), they sailed along the coast of [South America](/wiki/South_America "South America") much farther south than Asia was known to extend, confirming that this was a land of continental proportions.[[101]](#cite_note-OGorman-103) On return to Europe, an account of the voyage, called *Mundus Novus* ("New World"), was published under Vespucci's name in 1502 or 1503,[[102]](#cite_note-formisano1-104) although it seems that it had additions or alterations by another writer.[[103]](#cite_note-Zerubavel1-105) Regardless of who penned the words, *Mundus Novus* credited Vespucci with saying, "I have discovered a continent in those southern regions that is inhabited by more numerous people and animals than our Europe, or Asia or Africa",[[104]](#cite_note-formisano2-106) the first known explicit identification of part of the Americas as a continent like the other three.

Within a few years, the name "New World" began appearing as a name for South America on world maps, such as the Oliveriana (Pesaro) map of around 1504–1505. Maps of this time, though, still showed [North America](/wiki/North_America "North America") connected to Asia and showed South America as a separate land.[[103]](#cite_note-Zerubavel1-105)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Waldseemuller_map_2.jpg/500px-Waldseemuller_map_2.jpg)

In 1507 [Martin Waldseemüller](/wiki/Martin_Waldseem%C3%BCller "Martin Waldseemüller") published a world map, *[Universalis Cosmographia](/wiki/Waldseem%C3%BCller_map "Waldseemüller map")*, which was the first to show North and South America as separate from Asia and surrounded by water. A small inset map above the main map explicitly showed for the first time the Americas being east of Asia and separated from Asia by an ocean, as opposed to just placing the Americas on the left end of the map and Asia on the right end. In the accompanying book *[Cosmographiae Introductio](/wiki/Cosmographiae_Introductio "Cosmographiae Introductio")*, Waldseemüller noted that the earth is divided into four parts, Europe, Asia, Africa, and the fourth part, which he named "America" after Amerigo Vespucci's first name.[[105]](#cite_note-Zerubavel2-107) On the map, the word "America" was placed on part of South America.

### Beyond four continents

The Sanskrit text [Rig Veda](/wiki/Rig_Veda "Rig Veda") often dated 1500 BCE[[note 1]](#cite_note-dating-114) has the earliest mention of seven continents in the Earth, the text claims that the Earth has seven continents and [Lord Vishnu](/wiki/Lord_Vishnu "Lord Vishnu") Measured the entire universe from his first foot from the land of Earth which has 7 continents.[[112]](#cite_note-:9-115)

ato devā avantu no yato viṣṇurvicakrame |  
pṛthivyāḥ saptadhāmabhiḥ ||  
  
idaṃ viṣṇurvi cakrame tredhā ni dadhe padam |  
samūḷhamasya pāṃsure ||  
  
trīṇi padā vi cakrame viṣṇurghopā **adābhyaḥ** |  
ato dharmāṇi dhārayan ||

The Gods be gracious unto us even from the place whence [Vishnu](/wiki/Vishnu "Vishnu") strode  
Through the seven regions of the earth!  
  
Through all this world strode Vishnu; thrice his foot he planted, and the whole  
Was gathered in his footstep's dust.  
  
Vishnu, the Guardian, he whom **none deceiveth**, made three steps; thenceforth  
Establishing his high decrees.

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/1500-1200_BCE_Rigveda%2C_manuscript_page_sample_ii%2C_Sanskrit%2C_Devanagari.jpg/330px-1500-1200_BCE_Rigveda%2C_manuscript_page_sample_ii%2C_Sanskrit%2C_Devanagari.jpg)

In regard to the above-quoted verses, it is commonly accepted that there are Seven Continents or 'regions of the earth'. A. Glucklich adds that 'In the [Matsya Purana](/wiki/Matsya_Purana "Matsya Purana"), for instance, there is a seven-part map of the world ... [it has] one centre, where an immense mountain – [Mount Meru](/wiki/Mount_Meru "Mount Meru") (or Maha Meru, Great Meru) – stands ... The continents encircle the mountain in seven concentric circles ... It seems clear that the Himalayas were the approximate location of Mt. Meru and the text is clear that the earth has seven continents.[[112]](#cite_note-:9-115)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Thevenot_-_Hollandia_Nova_detecta_1644.png/330px-Thevenot_-_Hollandia_Nova_detecta_1644.png)

From the late 18th century, some geographers started to regard North America and South America as two parts of the world, making five parts in total. Overall though, the fourfold division prevailed well into the 19th century.[[115]](#cite_note-lewis30-118)

Europeans discovered [Australia](/wiki/Australia_(continent) "Australia (continent)") in 1606, but for some time it was taken as part of Asia. By the late 18th century, some geographers considered it a continent in its own right, making it the sixth (or fifth for those still taking America as a single continent).[[115]](#cite_note-lewis30-118) In 1813, [Samuel Butler](/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(schoolmaster) "Samuel Butler (schoolmaster)") wrote of Australia as "[New Holland](/wiki/New_Holland_(Australia) "New Holland (Australia)"), an immense island, which some geographers dignify with the appellation of another continent" and the *[Oxford English Dictionary](/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary "Oxford English Dictionary")* was just as equivocal some decades later.[[116]](#cite_note-119) It was in the 1950s that the concept of Oceania as a "great division" of the world was replaced by the concept of Australia as a continent.[[117]](#cite_note-lewis32b-120)

[Antarctica](/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica") was sighted in 1820 during the [First Russian Antarctic Expedition](/wiki/First_Russian_Antarctic_Expedition "First Russian Antarctic Expedition") and described as a continent by [Charles Wilkes](/wiki/Charles_Wilkes "Charles Wilkes") on the [United States Exploring Expedition](/wiki/United_States_Exploring_Expedition "United States Exploring Expedition") in 1838, the last continent identified, although a great "Antarctic" ([antipodean](/wiki/Antipodes "Antipodes")) landmass had been [anticipated](/wiki/Terra_Australis "Terra Australis") for millennia. An 1849 atlas labelled Antarctica as a continent but few atlases did so until after [World War II](/wiki/World_War_II "World War II").[[118]](#cite_note-121)

Over time, the western concept of dividing the world into continents spread globally, replacing conceptions in other areas of the world. The idea of continents continued to become imbued with cultural and political meaning. In the 19th century during the [Meiji period](/wiki/Meiji_period "Meiji period"), Japanese leaders began to self-identify with the concept of being Asian, and renew relations with other "Asian" countries while conceiving of the idea of Asian solidarity against western countries. This conception of an Asian identity, as well as the idea of Asian solidarity, was later taken up by others in the region, such as Republican China and Vietnam.[[119]](#cite_note-122)

From the mid-19th century, atlases published in the United States more commonly treated North and South America as separate continents, while atlases published in Europe usually considered them one continent. However, it was still not uncommon for American atlases to treat them as one continent up until World War II.[[120]](#cite_note-lewis32-123) From the 1950s, most U.S. geographers divided the Americas into two continents.[[120]](#cite_note-lewis32-123) With the addition of Antarctica, this made the seven-continent model. However, this division of the Americas did not appeal to [Latin Americans](/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America"), who saw their region spanning an *América* as a single landmass, and there the conception of six continents remains popular.

Some geographers regard Europe and Asia together as a single continent, dubbed *[Eurasia](/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia")*.[[121]](#cite_note-124) In this model, the world is divided into six continents, with North America and South America considered separate continents.

## Geology

Geologists use the term *continent* in a different manner from geographers. In geology, a continent is defined by continental crust, which is a platform of [metamorphic](/wiki/Metamorphic_rock "Metamorphic rock") and [igneous](/wiki/Igneous_rock "Igneous rock") rocks, largely of [granitic](/wiki/Granite "Granite") composition. Continental crust is less dense and much thicker than oceanic crust, which causes it to "float" higher than oceanic crust on the dense underlying [mantle](/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle "Earth's mantle"). This explains why the continents form high platforms surrounded by deep ocean basins.[[122]](#cite_note-125)[[3]](#cite_note-Glossary2005-3)

Some geologists restrict the term *continent* to portions of the crust built around stable regions called *[cratons](/wiki/Craton "Craton")*. Cratons have largely been unaffected by mountain-building events ([orogenies](/wiki/Orogeny "Orogeny")) since the [Precambrian](/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian"). A craton typically consists of a *[continental shield](/wiki/Shield_(geology) "Shield (geology)")* surrounded by a *[continental platform](/wiki/Platform_(geology) "Platform (geology)")*. The shield is a region where ancient crystalline [basement rock](/wiki/Basement_(geology) "Basement (geology)") (typically 1.5 to 3.8 billion years old) is widely exposed at the surface. The platform surrounding the shield is also composed of ancient basement rock, but with a cover of younger [sedimentary rock](/wiki/Sedimentary_rock "Sedimentary rock").[[123]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevin201083-126) The continents are accretionary crustal "rafts" that, unlike the denser basaltic crust of the ocean basins, are not subjected to destruction through the plate tectonic process of subduction. This accounts for the great age of the rocks comprising the continental cratons.[[124]](#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevin2010194-127)

The margins of geologic continents are either [active](/wiki/Convergent_boundary "Convergent boundary") or [passive](/wiki/Passive_margin "Passive margin"). An active margin is characterised by mountain building, either through a continent-on continent collision or a subduction zone. Continents grow by [accreting](/wiki/Accretion_(geology) "Accretion (geology)") lighter volcanic island chains and microcontinents along these active margins, forming [orogens](/wiki/Orogenic_belt "Orogenic belt"). At a passive margin, the continental crust is stretched thin by extension to form a [continental shelf](/wiki/Continental_shelf "Continental shelf"), which tapers off with a gradual slope covered in sediment, connecting it directly to the oceanic crust beyond. Most passive margins eventually transition into active margins: where the oceanic plate becomes too heavy due to cooling, it disconnects from the continental crust, and starts subducting below it, forming a new subduction zone.[[125]](#cite_note-128)

![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Tectonic_plates_%282022%29.svg/500px-Tectonic_plates_%282022%29.svg.png)

There are many microcontinents, or [continental fragments](/wiki/Continental_fragment "Continental fragment"), that are built of continental crust but do not contain a craton. Some of these are fragments of [Gondwana](/wiki/Gondwana "Gondwana") or other ancient cratonic continents: [Zealandia](/wiki/Zealandia_(continent) "Zealandia (continent)"),[[78]](#cite_note-Mortimer_et_al_2017-79) which includes [New Zealand](/wiki/Geography_of_New_Zealand "Geography of New Zealand") and [New Caledonia](/wiki/Geography_of_New_Caledonia "Geography of New Caledonia"); [Madagascar](/wiki/Geography_of_Madagascar "Geography of Madagascar"); the northern [Mascarene Plateau](/wiki/Mascarene_Plateau "Mascarene Plateau"), which includes the [Seychelles](/wiki/Seychelles "Seychelles"). Other islands, such as several in the [Caribbean Sea](/wiki/Caribbean_Sea "Caribbean Sea"), are composed largely of granitic rock as well, but all continents contain both granitic and basaltic crust, and there is no clear boundary as to which islands would be considered microcontinents under such a definition. The [Kerguelen Plateau](/wiki/Kerguelen_Plateau "Kerguelen Plateau"), for example, is largely volcanic, but is associated with the breakup of Gondwanaland and is considered a microcontinent,[[126]](#cite_note-129)[[127]](#cite_note-130) whereas volcanic [Iceland](/wiki/Geography_of_Iceland "Geography of Iceland") and [Hawaii](/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands "Hawaiian Islands") are not. The [British Isles](/wiki/British_Isles "British Isles"), [Sri Lanka](/wiki/Sri_Lanka "Sri Lanka"), [Borneo](/wiki/Borneo "Borneo"), and [Newfoundland](/wiki/Newfoundland_(island) "Newfoundland (island)") were on the margins of the [Laurasian](/wiki/Laurasia "Laurasia") continent—only separated from the main continental landmass by inland seas flooding its margins.

The movement of plates has caused the continual formation and breakup of continents, and occasionally supercontinents, in a process called the [Wilson Cycle](/wiki/Wilson_Cycle "Wilson Cycle"). The supercontinent [Columbia](/wiki/Columbia_(supercontinent) "Columbia (supercontinent)") or Nuna formed during a period of 2.0–1.8 billion years ago and broke up about 1.5–1.3 billion years ago.[[128]](#cite_note-Zhao1-131)[[129]](#cite_note-Zhao2-132) The supercontinent [Rodinia](/wiki/Rodinia "Rodinia") is thought to have formed about 1 billion years ago and to have embodied most or all of Earth's continents, and broken up into eight continents around 600 million years ago. The eight continents later reassembled into another supercontinent called [Pangaea](/wiki/Pangaea "Pangaea"); Pangaea broke up into Laurasia (which became North America and Eurasia) and [Gondwana](/wiki/Gondwana "Gondwana") (which became the remaining continents).[[130]](#cite_note-133)

## Criticism

Some academics, such as the historical geographer [Martin W. Lewis](/wiki/Martin_W._Lewis "Martin W. Lewis"), argue that the systems we understand today are more rooted in social, political, and cultural history than in geological fact, a view particularly outlined in his book *The Myth of Continents: A Critique of Metageography*.[[131]](#cite_note-134)

## What is a Continent?

There are [tons of definitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent) that people use to identify a continent. The most commonly used and most simple is to identify a large landmass which is separated by oceans from other large landmasses. The 7 continents that we have in the world are always changing shape because the earth’s water defines the landmass of each continent. Don’t forget that water makes up around 2/3 of the earth’s surface, so boundaries and shapes of continents are altered as the waters change. All of the 7 continents used to be connected as one landmass known as Pangea, but we’re talking millennia ago. Now we can easily recognize that there are 7 distinct continents.

## What Are The 7 Continents?

It is widely accepted that the 7 continents are:

1. Africa
2. Antarctica
3. Asia
4. Australia (also known as Australasia)
5. Europe
6. North America
7. South America

Some people can get confused about the name and mistake continents for regions, I’ll take you through each continent and clear up any confusion.

SIDE NOTE: These 7 continents gave birth to the concept of the [7 SUMMITS](https://onestep4ward.com/what-are-the-7-summits/), the highest mountain on each continent too.

## Does everyone agree with 7 Continents in the world??

Nope! Interestingly enough not everyone agrees that there are 7 continents in the world. The reason being that some people combine North and South America because it is one landmass, therefore, should be classed as one continent in their eyes, calling it the ‘American Continent.’

The other contentious issue for a few people is Europe and Asia, some people combine them into one continent calling it ‘Eurasia.’

## Why Do Some Say There are 5 or 6 Continents in the world?

TLDR? So why do some people say there are 5 or 6 continents?

#### North and South America as 1 continent; The Americas

Because they may not separate North and South America as 2 continents, and call it one continent ‘The Americas’.

#### Europe and Asia as 1 continent; Eurasia

And some include Europe and Asia as 1 continent, calling it Eurasia.

Coming from Ireland, I have only ever been taught that there are 7 continents in the world. I’m pretty sure most of you reading this are the same too. That wasn’t always the case because back in the day people used to be taught that there were 5 continents; Africa, America, Asia, Australia and Europe.

Take a look at the Olympic symbol again. There are 5 rings and that’s because it used to be accepted that there were 5 continents (not separating the 2 Americas and also not including Antarctica).  Some countries teach that there are 6 continents in the world; America, Antarctica, Asia, Africa, Australia and Europe (not separating the 2 Americas).

## What is a continent?

[![Second Grade What Is a Continent? PowerPoint & Google Slides](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_345/image_repo/d5/a1/us-g-80-what-is-a-continent-powerpoint_ver_6.avif)

Second Grade What Is a Continent? PowerPoint & Google Slides](/resource/us-g-80-what-is-a-continent-powerpoint)

A **continent** is a large continuous mass of land.

Approximately 71% of the Earth's surface is covered by water. The remaining 29% of the Earth’s surface is land. This land makes up the seven continents we know and study today.

The names of the continents, from smallest to largest in surface area, are as follows: **Australia**, **Europe**, **Antarctica**, **South America**, **North America**, **Africa**, and **Asia**. Amazingly, these continents make up a total of 57 million square miles of land!

## How many continents are there?

[![Continent Maps With Words and Pictures Pack](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_345/image_repo/dc/d4/us-ss-92-continent-maps-with-words-and-pictures-pack_ver_6.avif)

Continent Maps With Words and Pictures Pack](/resource/continent-maps-with-words-and-pictures-pack-us-ss-92)

There are **seven continents** that make up Earth’s land surface area. The next question asked is how do we determine which countries make up which continent?

When looking at placing countries within a particular a continent, there are two points to consider. These two points are the **proximity to the region** and **tectonic plates**.

Geographers usually include all the islands in the same region as part of a continent. Hence, this is why Madagascar is part of Africa and the United Kingdom is part of Europe.

Each continent also has a geological distinction where [tectonic plates](/teaching-wiki/tectonic-plates) play a part. Countries that share a tectonic plate will be in the same continent. This is why Antarctica, an island on its own tectonic plate, is described as a separate continent. While Greenland, also one of the world’s largest islands, is part of the North American continent. This island shares the same tectonic plate as North America!

## Explore Our World with a Map of Continents and Oceans

A **map** is a type of diagram, usually drawn on a flat surface, that represents an area of land or water. Maps are handy tools children and adults can use to discover interesting facts about the world. And learning [how to read maps](/teaching-wiki/map-skills) is an important life skill.

![Ocean and Continent Map](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_630/u/ux/continent-map_ver_1.jpg)

You can learn a lot from a map. The **oceans and continent map** shown above provides the following information:

* The names of the oceans and continents.

* The sizes and shapes of the continents.

* The position of the continents relative to one another.

Other continent maps may have more details or even very different information. You can do the following with a map:

* Examine a continent’s position according to the four [hemispheres.](/teaching-wiki/hemisphere)
* Find island countries.
* Outline country borders.
* See geological information such as rivers and mountain ranges.
* Learn a country’s population.
* Discover habitats, and much more.

The North American continent map below shows the countries that make up this continent, including the United States. The map also shows a few notable points, such as the Golden Gate Bridge in California and the design of the Mexican flag.

![North America Map](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_630/u/ux/north-america-map_ver_1.jpg)

**World maps for kids** are also widely used in schools to help students answer questions in class. For example, a teacher might ask students to find and label the southernmost continent. Or perhaps find the name of the land that connects Asia and Africa?

(The southernmost continent is Antarctica, and the Sinai Peninsula connects Asia and Africa).

So, now that we know how to find continents on a map, let’s look at each continent in a bit more detail.

## Continent Names and Interesting Facts

[![Continent Flash Cards]()

Continent Flash Cards](/resource/us-g-75-continent-flash-cards)

As we observed on the [Continent and Oceans Map](/resource/us-g-71-continents-and-oceans-map), each landmass is unique. In fact, there's lots to learn about the continents of the world. Let’s explore Earth’s seven amazing continents from smallest to largest. They are **Australia**, **Europe**, **Antarctica**, **South America**, **North America**, **Africa**, and **Asia**!

### Australia

[![Continent Fact Sheet Writing Templates]()

Continent Fact Sheet Writing Templates](/resource/continents-fact-sheets-writing-template-us2-ss-157)

The **continent of Australia**, also known as **Oceania**, is the world’s smallest continent. While it’s easy to assume that only [Australia](/teaching-wiki/australia-for-kids) makes up this continent, there are actually 14 other countries and thousands of tiny islands. And even though the continent spreads over a very large area, the total land surface area is only about 3,291,000 square miles.

There’s also plenty to explore when it comes to vegetation and wildlife. This continent has a diverse landscape. There are tropical rainforests, dry deserts, alpine regions, and marine environments.

There are many types of birds in this region, and many notable animals. **Marsupials**, which are animals that carry their young in a pouch (think [koalas](/teaching-wiki/koala) and kangaroos), are found on this continent.

Since a vast amount of water surrounds the continent, many plants, birds, and animals are **endemic** species. These are species found nowhere else on Earth!

| **Fun Fact:**  The oldest art form in the world is [Aboriginal rock art](/teaching-wiki/aboriginal-art). This type of art dates back 60,000 years. It still is an important part of Aboriginal life and customs today. |

### Europe

[![Continent Flash Cards]()

Continent Flash Cards](/resource/us-g-75-continent-flash-cards)

The **continent of Europe** is located entirely within the Northern Hemisphere. It’s also the second-smallest continent. Yet, this continent has the highest population density per square mile.

There are 47 countries that make up the European continent. This includes a part of Russia west of the **Ural Mountains**.

Many of these countries are **landlocked**. This means they do not share their borders with an ocean, only other countries. Examples of landlocked countries include Austria, Switzerland, Moldova, and the smallest country in the world - Vatican City.

The **Caspian Sea**, known as the world's largest lake, forms part of the border between Europe and Asia.

|  |
| --- |
| **Fun Fact:**  Did you know that in some parts of the world Europe and Asia are considered one continent? This continent goes by the name **Eurasia**. |

### Europe Continent Map Antarctica

[![Continents of the World Worksheet]()

Continents of the World Worksheet](/resource/t-he-347-continents-of-the-world-activity-sheet)

[Antarctica](/teaching-wiki/antarctica) is the highest, driest, windiest, and coldest continent in the world. Almost all the landmass is covered by a vast sheet of ice, and there are [very few species](/teaching-wiki/antarctic-habitat) that call this continent home. Antarctica is also an island in the sense that it is completely surrounded by the **Southern Ocean**.

There are no countries on this continent. Instead, it is populated by a rota of scientists representing 18 countries. These individuals study the climate, geology, weather, and more.

And since the signing of the **Antarctic Treaty System** in 1959, visitors must follow strict guidelines to ensure they have minimal impact when exploring this ice desert.

|  |
| --- |
| **Fun Fact:**  If you were to stand at the southernmost point on Antarctica, every direction you look would be due north! |

### South America

[![What a Wonderful World Continents Matching Cards]()

What a Wonderful World Continents Matching Cards](/resource/t-g-137-what-a-wonderful-world-countries-and-continents-matching-card)

The continent of [South America](/teaching-wiki/south-america) is located primarily in the Western and Southern hemispheres. Only a small part of this landmass is above the [Equator](/teaching-wiki/equator).

On the west of the continent lies the **Pacific Ocean**, and the **Atlantic Ocean** lies to the east. There is a small strip of land called the **Isthmus of Panama** that connects South America to North America.

South America has a diverse landscape. You can explore the Andes Mountains, the longest mountain range in the world. Or visit Angel Falls, the world’s highest waterfall at almost 1,000 meters high!

It’s also where you find the Amazon rainforest. There’s plenty of species, including several endangered ones, that call this continent home.

|  |
| --- |
| **Fun Fact:**  The southernmost city in the world, Ushuaia, is in Argentina, in South America. |

![South American Continent Map](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_630/u/ux/south-america-map_ver_1.jpg)

### North America

[![Continents Of The World Word Cards]()

Continents Of The World Word Cards](/resource/t-t-12534-continents-of-the-world)

The continent of [North America](/teaching-wiki/north-america) is located in the **Western** and **Northern hemispheres**. The United States is one of 23 countries that make up this continent.

Other countries include Canada, the second-largest country after Russia in size. And Mexico, where Mexico City is the most populous city on this continent.

Three oceans, the **Pacific**, **Atlantic**, and the **Arctic**, border this continent. The main habitats, or biomes, found here include deserts, grasslands, forests, and tundra.

Some of the oldest rocks on Earth are found in North America and form part of the **Canadian (Laurentian) Shield**. You can also explore several natural wonders in North America. This includes the Grand Canyon, Niagara Falls, and Yellowstone National Park.

|  |
| --- |
| **Fun Fact:**  North and South America were named after Italian explorer **Amerigo Vespucci**. He introduced a new idea that these lands were part of a separate continent. A map drawn in 1507 by Martin Waldseemüller depicts the continents with the Latinized version of Amerigo’s name - America. |

![North American Continent Map](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_630/u/ux/north-america-map_ver_1.jpg)

### Africa

[![Oceans and Continents Flash Cards]()

Oceans and Continents Flash Cards](/resource/oceans-and-continents-flash-cards-us-ss-1702223309)

[Africa](/teaching-wiki/africa) is the second-largest continent, in both population and size. Several bodies of water, including the **Atlantic** and **Indian** oceans, border this landmass.

Only the **Sinai Peninsula** and the **Suez Canal** connect this continent to Asia. Africa is the only continent that is crossed by both the **Prime Meridian** and the **Equator**.

There's a rich diversity of natural and biological resources found in Africa. You’ll discover some of the largest mammals on the planet, such as the African elephant, and several endangered species here.

And the **Cape Floral Region** in South Africa has the most variety of plants in the world. The longest river in the world, the Nile, is found in Africa.

|  |
| --- |
| **Fun Fact:**  Africa is often considered the *Cradle of Humankind*. In the early 20th century it was thought that humans evolved in either Europe or Asia. Yet, in 1924 a fossil was discovered in South Africa which eventually led to the conclusion that Africa was the birthplace of humans. |

![Africa Continent Map](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_630/u/ux/africa-map_ver_1.jpg)

### Asia

[![World Physical Map Color the Continents by Number Worksheet for 3rd-5th Grade]()

World Physical Map Color the Continents by Number Worksheet for 3rd-5th Grade](/resource/world-map-continents-color-by-number-activity-us-ss-1700156015)

**Asia** is the **largest continent**, both in size and population. As of 2018, there are 4.561 billion people that live here. Russia is the largest country in surface area, but China has the largest population, followed by India.

There are more languages spoken on this continent than anywhere else in the world.

A diverse climate is also found on this continent. You can find arctic, arid, highland, tropical, and more. The extensive variety of climates means there are plenty of different animal and plant species found in this region.

Asia is also home to the **Himalayas**. The Himalayas are a collection of mountain belts that separates the Indian subcontinent from the rest of Asia. The tallest mountain in the world, **Mount Everest**, is part of the Himalayas and is 29,035 feet high at the summit.

|  |
| --- |
| **Fun Fact:**  Asia is the only continent that shares its border with more than one other continent. It shares a border with Europe, Africa, and sometimes North America, when ice forms along the **Bering Strait** in winter. Technically, this means you can walk from the USA to Russia! |

## Our Continents are Constantly Moving

[![Me on the Map Flipbook for K-2nd Grade]()

Me on the Map Flipbook for K-2nd Grade](/resource/me-on-the-map-flipbook-us-ss-1690421346)

The Earth is constantly changing. If you were to look at a map of the continents millions of years ago, it would look very different from the maps today.

About 240 million years ago, much of the land on Earth formed a single, huge continent. This supercontinent is called **Pangaea** (also spelled Pangea), which means “all the Earth” in Greek.

![Pangaea Continent Map](https://images.twinkl.co.uk/tw1n/image/private/t_630/u/ux/pangaea_ver_1.jpg)

Forces, known as **plate tectonics**, began to break the continent apart into separate smaller continents. The continents then moved in different directions. This movement is called **Continental Drift**.

The idea of plate tectonics is that the Earth’s upper crust is made up of different sections floating on a sea of melted rock. Like cookies floating on a gelatin dessert. These sections or plates making up the continental crust move very slowly, and as they move they can separate or collide.

Scientists believe in another 250 million years the continents will join together again. We'll then have to draw a new continent map.

## What are the Seven Continents?

There are seven continents in the world: Africa, Antarctica, Asia, Australia/Oceania, Europe, North America, and South America. However, depending on where you live, you may have learned that there are five, six, or even four continents. This is because there is no official criteria for determining continents. While the position of landmasses on continental crust may be used to determine continents, geopolitical factors also affect their delineation. Below is an overview of the world's continent using the most popular classification system, the seven continent method.

## Other Continental Divisions

### Four Continents

      ![Colored map of the world with the four continents model. Each color represents a continent: Green for Afro-Eurasia, Yellow for America, Blue for Australia / Oceania, and Black for Antarctica.](/img/ncore/continent_model_4.jpg)  

| Continents |
| --- |
| Afro-Eurasia / Old World |
| America / New World |
| Australia / Oceania |
| Antarctica |
| More Information |
| The four continent model promotes the idea that all continents are landmasses divided by ocean. As Europe and Asia inhabit the landmass, and Africa and Asia are connected by the Isthmus of Suez, they are considered to compose one continent called “Afro-Eurasia.” In this model, North America and South America are thought of as one continent connected by the Isthmus of Panama. |

### Five Continents

      ![Colored map of the world with the five continents model. Each color represents a continent: Purple for Africa, Green for Europe, Orange of Asia, Yellow for America, Blue for Australia / Oceania.](/img/ncore/continent_model_5_un.jpg)  

| Continents |
| --- |
| Africa |
| Europe |
| Asia |
| America |
| Pacific |
| More Information |
| In the five continent model, North and South America are considered to be one continent called America. Antarctica is omitted due to its lack of permanent habitation. This model is used by the United Nations and found in the Olympic Charter. |

### Six Continents (Version 1)

      ![Colored map of the world with a version of the six continents model. Each color represents a continent: Purple for Africa, Green for Europe, Orange for Asia, Yellow for America, Blue for Australia / Oceania, and Black for Antarctica.](/img/ncore/continent_model_6_v1.jpg)  

| Continents |
| --- |
| Africa |
| Europe |
| Asia |
| America |
| Australia / Oceania |
| Antarctica |
| More Information |
| This model is most commonly taught in western Europe, including France and Spain. Here, North and South America are considered to be one single continent of America. |

### Six Continents (Version 2)

      ![Colored map of the world with a version of the six continents model. Each color represents a continent: Purple for Africa, Green for Eurasia, Yellow for North America, Red for South America, Blue for Australia / Oceania, and Black for Antarctica.](/img/ncore/continent_model_6_v2.jpg)  

| Continents |
| --- |
| Africa |
| Eurasia |
| North America |
| South America |
| Australia / Oceania |
| Antarctica |
| More Information |
| This model is mostly taught in Eastern Europe and Japan. In this model, North and South America are differentiated by Europe and Asia are combined in one continent of Eurasia. |

# Continent Facts

## 20 Facts about the World's Continents you should know

Our Continent Facts for Kids provide lots of interesting and fun facts on the continents of the world.

![Continent Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xcontinentfacts.png.pagespeed.ic.IzfUCmmdx1.webp "Continent Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")

There are seven continents and five oceans on our planet. The continents are the huge landmasses that separated by the waters of the oceans. On this page we will tell you more about the continents, about their sizes and special features.

The seven continents on our planet are: [Africa](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/africa-facts.html), [Asia](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html), [Antarctica](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/antarctica-facts.html), [Europa](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/europe-facts.html), [North America](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/north-america-facts.html) and [South America](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/south-america-facts.html) and [Australia (in Oceania](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/oceania-facts.html)). These 7 continents vary in size and population numbers.

## 20 Top Continent Facts

1. [**Asia**](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html) is the largest continent in size. It has the biggest land area and the world's biggest population. This means most people on our Earth live on the Asian continent. 

[![Asia Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xasia_300.png.pagespeed.ic.Z9DaZkItxT.webp "Asia Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html "Go to Asia Facts for Kids | Asia For Kids | Geography | Travel | Attractions")

2. About 4.6 billion people live in [Asia](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html). The Asian continent has the most populous cities, which means there are the biggest cities in the world. Many million people live in the urban centres of the Asian continent, in cities such as in Delhi, Tokyo, Shanghai or Beijing.

3. [Asia](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html) houses some of the richest nations in the world. The tiny country [Qatar](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/qatar-facts.html) on the Arabian peninsula is on of the richest nations in the world due to its income from oil exploration and the petroleum industry. Saudi-Arabia, the [United Arab Emirates](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/uae-facts.html) and Bahrain are also among richest countries in the world due to their oil reserves. 

4. The most languages are spoken in [Asia](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html) - over 2,300 languages! However, most of these languages are spoken only by small groups of people. 2,000 languages of the world's 7,000 languages are spoken by less than 1,000 people! 

Chinese is the language with the most native speakers - with almost 1.4 billion people speaking it.  

[Read more about the Asian continent here.](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/asia-facts.html)

﻿5. **[Australia](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/oceania-facts.html)** (which is part of Oceania) is the smallest continent on our planet. Strictly speaking, Oceania not a continent but is a geographic region that includes Australasia (where Australia and New Zealand are located), Polynesia, Micronesia and Melanesia.

[![Oceania Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xoceania_300.png.pagespeed.ic.v2vamG9_Ht.webp "Oceania Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/oceania-facts.html "Go to Oceania Facts for Kids | Geography | Continents | Facts for Kids")

This means [Australia](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/australia-facts.html), [New Zealand](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/new-zealand-facts.html), [Vanuatu](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/facts-about-vanuatu.html) and the many islands north and east of Australia in the Pacific Ocean all belong to this region. Australia is the largest landmass in the region and thus is often also referred to as a continent, even though Oceania itself is a region.

6. About 43 million people live in Oceania. The most populous cities of the region are Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane and Perth - all these cities are in Australia.

[Read more about Oceania here](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/oceania-facts.html) or the [separate page of Australia country facts here](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/australia-facts.html).

﻿7. [**Africa**](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/africa-facts.html) is the continent with the most countries. There are 54 countries on the African continent.

About 1.3 billion people live in Africa. The largest cities of the African continent are Lagos/ Nigeria, Kinshasa/ DR Congo and Cairo/ Egypt.

[![Africa Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xafrica_300.png.pagespeed.ic.hAcHF52llz.webp "Africa Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/africa-facts.html "Go to Africa Facts for Kids | Africa for Kids | Geography | Travel | People")

8. The oldest human fossils such as skeletons and skulls have been discovered in Africa, therefore the African continent is also referred to as the 'cradle of humankind'. 

9. From the 15th - 19th century Africa was colonised by several countries including Portugal, the Netherlands, Spain, France, Italy, Belgium, the United Kingdom and Germany. The only countries that escaped colonialisation in Africa are Ethiopia and Liberia.

10. Africa also houses the longest river in the world, the Nile and the second highest waterfalls in the world, the Tugela Falls in South Africa.

[Read more about the African continent here.](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/africa-facts.html)

## More Continent Facts for Kids

11. **[North America](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/north-america-facts.html)** is a continent which is located entirely on the northern and western hemisphere. There are 23 countries in total on the North American continent. 

North American countries are:

[![North America Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xnorthamerica_300.png.pagespeed.ic.kBf3q7Jhg0.webp "North America Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/north-america-facts.html "Go to North America Facts for Kids | North America | Geography | Superlative")

* [the United States of America (USA)](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/usa-facts.html)
* [Canada](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/canada-facts-for-kids.html)
* [Mexico](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/mexico-facts.html)
* the Caribbean islands
* and various other countries.

12. The the world's largest island Greenland is located on the North American continent. Greenland, however, belongs to Denmark, a country in Europe. Greenland is also known for the northernmost place in the world!

13. About 580 million people live on the North American continent. North America is home to the largest Christian population in the world. Almost 80% of the people in Canada, the USA and Mexico consider themselves as Christians. 

14. The Maya civilisation of Central America and Mexico is considered one of the oldest civilisations of this planet.

15. North America has the largest number of people who speak English either as their first language (231 million people) or as their second language fluently. English, however, is spoken by people in more than 100 countries worldwide.

[Read more about North America here.](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/north-america-facts.html)

16. [**Europe**](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/europe-facts.html) is considered to be the wealthiest and richest continent, however, there are poor regions especially in the eastern parts of the continent too! 

17. [Europe](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/europe-facts.html) houses the two smallest countries in the world:

![Europe Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xeurope_300.png.pagespeed.ic.y3ObUgG46V.webp "Europe Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")

* Vatican City which is located inside Rome/Italy
* Monaco which is bordered on three sides by France.

18. According to the United Nations, there are 44 countries in Europe. Five European countries are among the ten smallest countries in the world: San Marino, Liechtenstein, Vatican City, Monaco and the island country of Malta. The other five tiny countries of our planet are located on islands outside Europe. 

[Read more about the European continent here.](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/europe-facts.html)

1﻿9. South America is a continent of many natural superlatives:

[![South America Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xsouthamerica_300.png.pagespeed.ic.M5a5VtfsoR.webp "South America Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/south-america-facts.html "Go to South America Facts for Kids | Geography | Attractions | People | Food")

* the longest mountain range - the Andes
* the highest waterfalls - the Angel Falls
* and the driest place on earth - the Atacama Desert in Chile.

About 430 million people live in South America.

[Read more amazing facts about the South American continent on our page here.](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/south-america-facts.html)

20. Antarctica is the smallest continent by population numbers. This means the huge continent is only sparsely populated.

[![Antarctica Facts by Kids World Travel Guide](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/images/xantarctica_300.png.pagespeed.ic.j40Vwqj69h.webp "Antarctica Facts by Kids World Travel Guide")](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/antarctica-facts.html "Go to Antarctica Facts for Kids | Geography | Continents | Facts for Kids")

On Antarctica there are only research stations for scientists and no permanent settlements. Antarctica is covered almost completely by ice. 90% of the planet's ice is located on this continent, which also makes up 60% - 70% of the world's freshwater supply. 

[Read more about Antarctica here.](https://www.kids-world-travel-guide.com/antarctica-facts.html)
