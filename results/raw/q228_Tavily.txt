# White House’s Kratsios urges allies to align on trusted AI stack

28 April 2026

[Decode39](https://decode39.com/author/redazione/)

5 Min Read

![Image 4](https://decode39.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/IMG_8770-990x556.jpeg)

At a Washington dialogue, the White House tech chief outlines a Western “trusted AI stack”, warns of an adoption gap, and frames competition with China as a defining axis for allied coordination

**Technology is no longer a neutral domain** of economic exchange but a central arena of geopolitical alignment. That was the underlying message of a fireside conversation between **Michael Kratsios**, director of the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy, and Italy’s ambassador to the United States, **Marco Peronaci**, held at the Italian Embassy as part of the U.S.–Italy Trusted Tech Dialogue.

**Framed around “allied leadership in the age of strategic technology”**, the discussion reflected a growing convergence between Washington and its European partners: the future of technological development — particularly in artificial intelligence — will hinge on the ability of like-minded countries to build interoperable, secure and commercially viable systems.

**From telecom to AI: the evolution of trust.**At the centre of the exchange was a concept that has come to define Western policy thinking: trust.

*   Kratsios traced its origins to earlier debates over telecom infrastructure, when governments were first forced to confront the strategic implications of embedding foreign technologies into critical systems. “You were installing infrastructure… that was carrying data that was very important and personal to your citizens and to your national security,” he noted.
*   That logic has since expanded. Artificial intelligence now represents the next frontier, requiring what Kratsios described as a “trusted AI stack” — spanning semiconductors, models and applications. Trust, in this context, must be embedded across the full system, particularly where technologies intersect with sensitive data and national security.
*   For Washington, the answer lies in coordination among allies. “We as the West should come together and be able to have a trusted stack that we can deploy,” Kratsios said.

**Adoption and the widening gap.**Peronaci pointed to the societal dimension of trust, stressing the role of public perception in shaping AI adoption.

*   Kratsios linked that directly to economic outcomes. “The more trust that you can make into the system, the broader it helps adoption,” he said.
*   Emerging data from US businesses suggest a widening divide: companies investing in AI are seeing “tremendous, almost double-digit growth”, while others risk stagnation. The result is an increasingly “K-shaped” trajectory, with early adopters pulling ahead.
*   “The ones that aren’t adopting AI now are already falling behind,” Kratsios warned.

**Trust, cost and the limits of principle.**The conversation also underscored a more practical constraint: trust is insufficient without competitiveness.

*   Reflecting on the global expansion of Chinese telecom providers, Kratsios noted that “the commercial realities of a highly subsidised stack… still trumped the more expensive Western alternative.”
*   This has informed a shift in US strategy. Through initiatives such as the American AI Export Program, Washington is seeking to deploy a full-stack technological offering — from chips to applications — supported by public financing tools.
*   At the same time, the US has moved to ease access among partners. Kratsios pointed to the decision to roll back earlier restrictions on advanced chip diffusion, with the aim of enabling allies to “build data centres with American chips and run American models.”

**AI sovereignty: integration over autonomy.**Debates over cooperation increasingly turn on the meaning of sovereignty.

*   While some European approaches emphasise full technological self-sufficiency, Washington is advancing a more networked model. “Our fundamental belief is that a Western stack that includes American components and those of our allies is how you can achieve your sovereignty,” Kratsios said.
*   In this view, sovereignty is secured through participation in a trusted ecosystem rather than independence from it.
*   Delaying adoption in pursuit of autonomy, he suggested, risks leaving countries behind. “The opportunities of AI are starting today… we must move as expeditiously as possible.”

**China and the structure of competition.**The geopolitical dimension surfaced most clearly in the discussion of China.

*   Kratsios characterised the global AI landscape as effectively a “two-man game”, dominated by the United States and China. He warned that Chinese entities are working to replicate US advances, including through large-scale model distillation techniques.
*   While expressing confidence in American innovation, the implication was clear: competition will intensify, and alignment among allies will matter more.

**Regulation and divergence.**Differences between the US and Europe remain most visible in the regulatory domain.

*   Kratsios referred to what he described as an ongoing “A-B test” between competing approaches to AI governance, suggesting that regulatory design directly shapes innovation outcomes.
*   Peronaci, for his part, pointed to the need for continued dialogue on principles, governance and control, indicating that transatlantic and broader allied frameworks could serve as venues for alignment.

**AI and the next frontier of power.**Beyond economics and security, Kratsios framed AI as a driver of scientific transformation. He described it as “one of the most powerful unlocks for scientific discovery in the history of humanity”, with implications ranging from material science to space exploration.

*   Referencing the US Artemis programme, he pointed to an agenda that ties technological leadership to long-term scientific ambition.

**A choice of systems.**Taken together, the exchange points to an emerging architecture in which alliances are increasingly defined by shared technological systems.

*   In this context, “allied leadership” is less about hierarchy than about coordination — and about choices. As technological ecosystems consolidate, the question is no longer whether to adopt advanced technologies, but within which system to do so.
*   Between a fragmented landscape and competing models, the contours of alignment are becoming harder to avoid.

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence has transformed industries, redefined productivity, and accelerated the pace of digital innovation. Yet despite its remarkable capabilities, AI continues to face a fundamental limitation: the need for high-quality human input. Without reliable, diverse, and verified data provided by real people, even the most advanced AI systems struggle to achieve accuracy, fairness, and contextual understanding.

This bottleneck has become one of the most pressing challenges in the development of next-generation AI. As companies race to build more sophisticated models, the demand for human-generated data—whether in the form of annotations, feedback, or real-world insights—has grown exponentially. The question now facing the tech world is not just how to improve AI, but how to scale human collaboration in a meaningful and trustworthy way.

A concept gaining traction within the crypto and web3 space suggests that the solution may lie in decentralization. Specifically, the convergence of AI infrastructure and blockchain-powered communities could unlock a new paradigm: a decentralized AI workforce composed of millions of verified individuals contributing to the training and refinement of intelligent systems.

At the center of this discussion is Pi Network, a digital ecosystem that has attracted a global user base through its mobile-first approach to cryptocurrency mining and community building. Unlike many blockchain projects that prioritize technological complexity or speculative trading, Pi Network has focused heavily on user accessibility and identity verification, creating a network of participants who are both active and authenticated.

This human-centric approach positions Pi Network as a potential bridge between blockchain technology and AI development. While AI systems require large volumes of high-quality input, Pi Network offers something increasingly valuable in the digital age: a vast pool of real, verified users who can contribute data, insights, and validation at scale.

The idea of integrating such a network with AI systems is not merely theoretical. It reflects a broader shift toward combining decentralized technologies with machine learning to create more transparent, inclusive, and efficient systems. If successful, this integration could give rise to what some analysts describe as the first truly decentralized AI workforce.

In this model, individuals from around the world would participate in AI training processes through tasks such as data labeling, content moderation, language translation, and contextual analysis. Unlike traditional crowdsourcing platforms, which often operate through centralized intermediaries, a blockchain-based system could enable direct compensation, transparent governance, and verifiable contributions.

The implications of such a system are far-reaching. For one, it could democratize access to the AI economy, allowing people in underserved regions to participate in and benefit from technological advancement. By leveraging blockchain, participants could be rewarded in digital assets such as Picoin, creating new income streams and fostering economic inclusion.

Moreover, the use of verified identities within Pi Network addresses a critical concern in AI development: data integrity. One of the challenges facing current AI models is the prevalence of low-quality or manipulated data. By ensuring that contributions come from real, authenticated users, a decentralized workforce could significantly improve the reliability and trustworthiness of AI training datasets.

The concept also aligns closely with the principles of web3, which emphasize decentralization, user ownership, and the redistribution of value. In a traditional AI ecosystem, a small number of corporations control both the data and the models, capturing the majority of the economic benefits. A decentralized approach, by contrast, would distribute value more equitably among contributors.

The role of emerging AI platforms, such as those being developed by entities like Pi Core Team, is crucial in this context. By building infrastructure that connects human input with machine learning systems, these organizations could enable a new form of collaboration between humans and AI.

Leading the world from analog to digital and beyond.

In 1985, seven former Linkabit colleagues met in Irwin Jacobs’s home to create a company that would build “QUALity COMMunications.” These visionaries departed from the accepted telecommunications standards at the time to develop Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA), forever changing wireless communications. CDMA became the foundation for all 3G networks worldwide, helping to define the latest 4G and 5G technologies for an intelligently connected future.

 • 1985: Qualcomm founded

 • 1995: CDMA rolled out commercially

[Read about CDMA](https://www.qualcomm.com/)[More about Irwin Jacobs](https://www.qualcomm.com/)[Read about Smithsonian’s “Cellphone: Unseen Connections” exhibition](https://www.qualcomm.com/research/cellphone-unseen-connections)

# Data goes mobile.

While mobile voice was an incredible leap, mobile data was a revolution. Thanks to 3G, people could not only use their mobile devices to speak to one another, but they could also watch and share videos, music, and other media. And it was all made possible by our inventions which solved the complex challenges that enabled CDMA, the backbone of 2G, to become the basis for all 3G networks.

*   Our EV-DO technologies optimized mobile broadband
*   Our 3G advancements led the way to 4G LTE

[More about 3G](https://www.qualcomm.com/research/5g/3g)

## Better. Faster. Smarter.

Qualcomm’s 4G LTE technology inventions laid the foundation for the first global standard for mobile broadband. By licensing our technology and increasing our patent portfolio, we enabled the LTE ecosystem to grow to 600+ commercial networks worldwide.

*   We led the evolution of LTE, LTE Advanced and LTE Advanced Pro
*   LTE addressed the massive growth in mobile data traffic

[More about 4G](https://www.qualcomm.com/research/5g/4g)

## Qualcomm 5G is transforming everything.

Qualcomm introduced the world to 5G, making the world more connected than it’s ever been. We’ve been developing 5G for years, investing in R&D to invent the foundational technologies that made 5G a reality. We maximized our 3G and 4G LTE expertise to develop 5G NR and mmWave, and then we licensed our 5G technologies—providing a pathway to making 5G a commercial reality.

*   5G was commercialized in 2019
*   Our breakthrough inventions make 5G possible
