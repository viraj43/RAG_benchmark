### Key takeaways

- The Standard & Poor's 500 index, or S&P 500®,1 is a stock market index that tracks the stock performance of 500 of the largest publicly traded companies in the US.
- Because the stocks it tracks represent companies from a broad range of industries, the S&P 500 is considered a barometer of overall stock market performance.
- Since its launch, the S&P 500 has posted an average return of about 10% a year.
- Although you can’t invest directly in the S&P 500, you can buy shares of an index mutual fund or exchange-traded fund (ETF) that aims to track the index and match its returns.

Curious about the S&P 500? Here’s an overview of this major market index, along with some options for investing in funds that track it.

### What is the S&P 500?

The S&P 500 is an index, or a group of stocks that share one or more common traits. In this case, those shared characteristics include a high number of readily tradeable shares and size—the index is made up of stocks from 500 of the US’s largest companies. (As of February 2026, the index contains 503 stocks because a few of these companies issue more than one class of stock.) Because these stocks come from sectors across the market, changes to the index’s value could tell you a lot about the performance of the stock market as a whole. The S&P 500’s value is determined by the combined average performance of the stocks within the index, with more weight given to larger companies. When the S&P 500’s value rises, that means enough stocks increased in value enough to bring up the total average value.

The S&P 500 started as an index of 233 companies in 1923, chosen by the Standard Statistics Company (now known as Standard & Poor’s). In 1957, S&P expanded it to 500 companies. Today, the S&P 500 index provides a big-picture view of what’s happening in the [stock market](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/smart-money/what-is-the-stock-market "stock market") each trading day—without investors having to check the performance of hundreds of different stocks.

### How does the S&P 500 work?

The S&P 500’s value depends on the performance of the stocks within it. The stocks in the index are chosen partly for those companies’ market capitalization, or total company value. Typically, that value is determined by multiplying the total number of a company’s outstanding shares by the price per share. The S&P 500, however, instead uses something called “free float” or “public float” market capitalization. That’s when certain shares, namely non-trading shares owned by institutional investors, government agencies, and company executives and founders, are subtracted from the total outstanding shares.

The unadjusted total market caps of companies tracked by the S&P 500 can vary widely. Some companies in the S&P 500 have a total market cap of a few trillion dollars, while others have market caps in the tens of billions. The S&P 500 weighs its value so that changes to the stock prices of companies with a larger float-adjusted market cap have a bigger impact on the index’s value than stock price changes for companies with a smaller float-adjusted market cap. Said another way: A trillion-dollar company’s stock price change would have a greater effect on the S&P 500’s value than a stock price change for a company in the index worth, say, a quarter of that.

### What companies are in the S&P 500?

The companies in the S&P 500 include major corporations such as:

- Nvidia
- Apple
- Alphabet
- Microsoft
- Amazon
- Meta Platforms
- Broadcom
- Tesla
- Berkshire Hathaway
- Walmart

Companies are added or removed by a committee according to specific criteria. As of July 2025, a company must be US-based, trade stock on a major US market exchange like the NYSE or Nasdaq, and have an unadjusted market capitalization of at least $22.7 billion to be eligible to join the S&P 500.2 This threshold is reviewed at the start of every calendar quarter to ensure it reflects the market. Additionally, companies must demonstrate strong liquidity (a high volume of trading activity), substantial public float (a large portion of its shares must be available for public trading), and consistent profitability (positive earnings across 4 quarters combined including the most recent one). The committee also considers broader market representation and qualitative factors in its decisions. The list of companies in the S&P 500 can change at any time, like in response to market and corporate news. If a company goes private or bankrupt, it’s removed from the S&P 500 and replaced with a different eligible company.

### The S&P 500 vs. other major indexes

Here’s how the S&P 500 compares to other major market indexes.

**S&P 500 vs. DJIA**

The Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA), aka the Dow, is another closely followed stock market index. Like the S&P 500, it tracks the stock performance of large, well-established companies. One major difference is that the DJIA is composed of just 30 companies and excludes firms in the utilities and transportation sectors; those are represented in other Dow Jones indexes. The S&P 500, meanwhile, tracks many more companies across all major US sectors. Still, there’s a lot of overlap between the 2 indexes because all the companies in the DJIA are typically in the S&P 500 too.

Another difference is how each index’s value is calculated. The DJIA gives more weight to the highest-priced stocks, whereas the S&P 500 gives more weight to the largest market cap companies, using the free float formula.

Both indexes are published by the joint venture, S&P Dow Jones Indices.

**S&P 500 vs. Nasdaq**

The Nasdaq Composite Index,3 often referred to as the Nasdaq, tracks the value of more than 3,000 companies, almost all the ones listed on the Nasdaq stock exchange. The index includes both US and international companies, whereas the S&P 500 tracks only domestic stocks. The Nasdaq Composite index also tracks stocks predominantly of technology companies of varying sizes, whereas the S&P 500 tracks only large-cap companies but across all sectors.

**S&P 500 vs. Russell 2000**

The Russell 20004 tracks the performance of about 2,000 small-cap publicly traded companies in the US. The average market cap for a Russell 2000 company is about $5 billion, and the largest companies on the index hit a market cap of under $40 billion, so these companies are valued a lot less than companies in the S&P 500. Each year, the index gets evaluated and altered. Stocks for companies that have become too large get taken out of the index to ensure it remains a true measure of small-cap domestic companies’ performance.

### Why is the S&P 500 important?

The S&P 500 is important because the stocks in this index make up roughly 80% of the total US equity market. By checking this index, investors can get a quick gauge of what’s happening in US markets. Since the S&P 500 includes hundreds of companies from many different sectors, it also offers a clue about the US economy’s health.

Plus, many investors buy shares in funds that try to mimic the performance of the S&P 500. That’s because it’s a simple option offering broad exposure to the market without having to buy and sell a lot of stocks.

### Historical performance of the S&P 500

Since its launch in 1957, the S&P 500 has posted an average return of about 10.6% a year.5 This doesn’t mean that the S&P 500 reliably provides returns of 10.6% every year. Stock market performance is unpredictable, and some years are much better than others.

In fact, the S&P 500 has experienced significant losses, like during the financial crisis in the late 2000s. It dropped 48% from August 2008 to March 2009. Still, these downturns are often balanced out by years with strong performance like the 23% increase the index notched in 2024.6 Remember that past performance is no guarantee of future results.

### How to invest in the S&P 500

You can’t buy shares of the S&P 500 directly: It’s not a security itself. However, you can invest in [mutual funds](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/investment-products/mutual-funds/what-are-mutual-funds "mutual funds") or [exchange traded funds (ETFs)](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/smart-money/what-are-etfs "exchange traded funds (ETFs)") that aim to track the performance of the S&P 500. These are called [index funds](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/smart-money/what-is-an-index-fund "index funds") because they mimic the index.

The index fund’s manager includes shares of all S&P 500 companies in the fund and weigh it to match the actual S&P 500. While you could try to do the same thing yourself by buying shares of all the companies in the S&P 500, it would mean be a lot more upfront work and maintenance to keep your portfolio’s balance in sync with the S&P 500. If you’d like to invest in a S&P 500 index mutual fund or an ETF, you need an investment account like a 401(k), 403(b), IRA, or [brokerage account](https://www.fidelity.com/learning-center/smart-money/what-is-a-brokerage-account "brokerage account"). You could open an IRA or brokerage account through a brokerage firm, like Fidelity.

Definition

The S&P 500 is a stock market index weighted by market capitalization that is made up of 500 of the largest public companies in the United States.

### Key Takeaways

- The S&P 500 is a float-adjusted market-capitalization-weighted index tracking the [stocks](https://www.investopedia.com/best-stock-screeners-5120586) of 500 leading U.S. public companies.
- The index captures around 80% of the total U.S. equity [market](https://www.investopedia.com/best-online-brokers-4587872) value, serving as a bellwether of economic performance.

## What Is the S&P 500 Index? A Market Cap Benchmark

The S&P 500 Index is a list of 500 of the largest publicly traded U.S. [stocks](https://www.investopedia.com/best-stock-screeners-5120586). They represent the largest and most important companies in the country, and span a broad cross-section of the economy. S&P Dow Jones Indices, a subsidiary of S&P Global, manages the index. Weighted by market capitalization, the index is considered to be one of the best gauges of U.S. equities, the stock [market](https://www.investopedia.com/best-online-brokers-4587872), and the American economy. The index actually includes 503 constituents because three companies have two share classes listed.1

## Weighting Formula and Calculation of the S&P 500

The S&P 500 uses a [market-cap weighting method](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/capitalizationweightedindex.asp) that gives a higher percentage allocation to companies with the largest market capitalizations.2

> Company Weighting in S & P=Company market capTotal of all market caps\\text{Company Weighting in S \\& P}= \\frac{\\text{Company market cap}}{\\text{Total of all market caps}}Company Weighting in S & P=Total of all market capsCompany market cap​

To determine the weighting of each component of the S&P 500, you need to calculate the index's total market cap. This is done by adding together every company's market cap.

A company's market cap is calculated by multiplying its current stock price by its [outstanding shares](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/outstandingshares.asp). The total market cap for the S&P 500, as well as the market caps of individual companies, are published frequently on financial websites, saving [investors](https://www.investopedia.com/best-online-brokers-4587872) the need to calculate them.

The [weighting of each company](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/040215/what-does-sp-500-index-measure-and-how-it-calculated.asp) in the index is calculated by taking the company's market cap and dividing it by the total market cap of the index.3

### Other S&P Indices

The S&P 500 is a part of the S&P Global 1200 family of indices.4 Other indices include the [S&P MidCap 400](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sp-midcap-400-index.asp), which represents mid-cap companies, and the [S&P SmallCap 600](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sp600.asp), which represents small-caps. Together, the S&P 500, S&P MidCap 400, and S&P SmallCap 600 cover 90% of all U.S. capitalization in an index known as the S&P Composite 1500.5

## S&P 500 Index Construction

The S&P uses only [free-floating](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/freefloatmethodology.asp) shares. These are the shares that the public can trade when calculating market cap. The S&P adjusts each company's market cap to compensate for new share issues or company mergers.

## S&P 500 Competitors

### S&P 500 vs. Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)

Another common U.S. stock market benchmark is the [Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/djia.asp). The S&P 500 is often the institutional investor's preferred index given its depth and breadth. The DJIA has historically been associated with significant equities from the retail investor's point of view. Institutional investors perceive the S&P 500 as being more representative of U.S. equity markets because it includes more stocks across all sectors: 500 versus the Dow's 30.

The S&P 500 uses a market-cap weighting method that gives a higher percentage allocation to companies with the largest market caps. The DJIA is a price-weighted index that gives companies with higher stock prices a higher index weighting.9 The market-cap-weighted structure tends to be more common than the price-weighted index across U.S. indexes.

### S&P 500 vs. Nasdaq

[Nasdaq](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/nasdaq.asp) is a global electronic marketplace for trading securities. Several equity market indexes include stocks traded on Nasdaq. A given stock included in the S&P 500 Index may also be in one or more of the various Nasdaq indexes.

Some of the most-watched Nasdaq stock indices include:

- **[Nasdaq 100 Index](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/nasdaq100.asp):** Includes 100 of the largest, most actively traded common equities listed on the Nasdaq10
- **[Nasdaq Composite Index](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/nasdaqcompositeindex.asp):** Often simply referred to as the Nasdaq by the media, it includes more than 2,500 common stocks that trade on the Nasdaq11
- **Nasdaq Global Equity Index (NQGI):** Includes international stocks12
- **[PHLX Semiconductor Sector Index (SOX)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/soxx.asp):** The leading barometer of stocks related to the semiconductor industry13
- **OMX Stockholm 30 Index (OMXS30):** Includes the 30 largest and most actively traded stocks on the Stockholm Stock Exchange14


### S&P 500 vs. Russell Indexes

The S&P 500 is a member of a set of indexes created by Standard & Poor's. This set of indexes is like the Russell index family in that both are market-cap-weighted unless stated otherwise as in the case of [equal-weighted](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/equalweight.asp) indexes.

There are [two significant differences](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/070715/sp-500-vs-russell-2000-etf-which-should-you-get.asp) between the construction of the S&P and the Russell families of indexes.1516 Standard & Poor's chooses constituent companies via a committee. [Russell indexes](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/022416/investment-fundamentals-sp-500-index-vs-russell-1000-index.asp) use a formula to select which stocks to include. There's no name overlap within S&P style indices such as growth versus value. Russell indexes will include the same company in both the value and growth style indexes.

### S&P 500 vs. Vanguard 500 Fund

The [Vanguard 500 Index Fund](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/111215/vanguard-total-stock-index-vs-vanguard-500-index-fund.asp) tracks the S&P 500 Index by investing in the same stocks with similar weights as the index.17 The fund barely deviates from the S&P, which it tracks.

### Important

The S&P 500 is an index so it can't be traded directly. Anyone who wants to invest in the companies that are included in the S&P must invest in a mutual fund or [exchange-traded fund (ETF)](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/etf.asp) that tracks the index, such as the [Vanguard 500 ETF](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/121814/look-vanguards-sp-500-etf.asp) ( [VOO](https://www.investopedia.com/markets/quote?tvwidgetsymbol=voo)).

## Limitations of the S&P 500 Index

One of the limitations of the S&P and other market-cap-weighted indexes occurs when [stocks in the index](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/08/find-stocks-in-sp500.asp) become overvalued. They rise higher than their fundamentals warrant. The stock typically inflates the overall value or price of the index if it has a heavy weighting in the index while being overvalued.

A company's rising market cap isn't necessarily indicative of its fundamentals. It simply reflects the stock's increase in value relative to the shares outstanding. [Equal-weighted indexes](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/equalweight.asp) have become increasingly popular as a result. Each company's stock price movements have an equal impact on these indexes.

## Example of the S&P 500 Market Cap Weighting

The individual market weights must be calculated by dividing the market cap of each company by the total market cap of the index to understand how the underlying stocks affect the S&P index. Let's look at this using Apple as an example.

As noted above, the company's market cap is calculated by multiplying its share price by the total number of outstanding shares. Divide the result by the total market cap of the S&P 500 to determine its weighting in the index.

Apple is one of the largest companies in the index, so it generally has a significant weighting. As a result, changes in its stock price can impact the S&P 500's overall performance. When a company like Apple has a larger weight, the more impact each 1% change in a stock's price will have on the index.

## Why Is It Called Standard and Poor's?

In 1923 the Standard Statistics Company developed its first stock index.9 The original index covered 233 companies and is considered a precursor to the S&P 500. The company merged with Poor's Publishing in 1941 to become [Standard and Poor's](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sp.asp).18

## What Companies Qualify for the S&P 500?

A company must be publicly traded and based in the U.S. to be included in the S&P 500 Index. It must also meet certain requirements for liquidity and market capitalization, have a [public float](https://www.investopedia.com/ask/answers/what-is-companys-float/) of at least 10% of its shares, and have positive earnings over the trailing four quarters.19

## How Do You Invest in the S&P 500?

The simplest way to invest in the S&P 500 Index or any other stock market index is to buy shares of an index fund that targets it. These funds invest in a cross-section of the companies represented on the index so the fund's performance should mirror the performance of the index itself.

## The Bottom Line

The S&P 500 Index is one of the most [widely used indexes](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/market-proxy.asp) for the U.S. stock market. These 500 companies represent the largest and most liquid companies in the U.S. from technology and software companies to banks and manufacturers. The index has [historically been used](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/markets/071616/history-sp-500-dividend-yield.asp) to provide insight into the direction of the stock market. It was created by a private company but the S&P 500 is a popular yardstick for the performance of the market economy at large.

The index is one of the factors in the computation of the [Conference Board Leading Economic Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conference_Board_Leading_Economic_Index "Conference Board Leading Economic Index"), which is used to forecast the direction of the economy.[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-14) The index is associated with many [ticker symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ticker_symbol "Ticker symbol"), including ^GSPC,.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-15) INX,[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-16) and SPX, depending on market or website.[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-17) The S&P 500 is maintained by [S&P Dow Jones Indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_Dow_Jones_Indices "S&P Dow Jones Indices"), a [joint venture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joint_venture "Joint venture") majority-owned by [S&P Global](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_Global "S&P Global"), and its components are selected by a committee.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-S&P500Methodology-3)

The most liquid based on average daily volume is ( [NYSE Arca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NYSE_Arca "NYSE Arca"): [SPY](https://www.nyse.com/quote/ARCX:SPY)), although SPY has a higher annual expense ratio of 0.09% compared to 0.03% for VOO and IVV, and 0.02% for SPYM. ### Derivatives

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=S%26P_500&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Derivatives")\]

In the [derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative_(finance) "Derivative (finance)") market, the [Chicago Mercantile Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange "Chicago Mercantile Exchange") (CME) offers [futures contracts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futures_contract "Futures contract") that track the index and trade on the exchange floor in an [open outcry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_outcry "Open outcry") auction, or on CME's Globex platform, and are the exchange's most popular product. The [Chicago Board Options Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Board_Options_Exchange "Chicago Board Options Exchange") (CBOE) offers [options](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Option_(finance) "Option (finance)") on the S&P 500 as well as on S&P 500 ETFs, inverse ETFs, and leveraged ETFs.

## History

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=S%26P_500&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: History")\]

In 1860, [Henry Varnum Poor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Varnum_Poor "Henry Varnum Poor") formed Poor's Publishing, which published an investor's guide to the railroad industry.[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-history-21) In 1923, Standard Statistics Company (founded in 1906 as the [Standard Statistics Bureau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_Global_Ratings#History "S&P Global Ratings")) began rating mortgage bonds[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-history-21) and developed its first stock market index consisting of the stocks of 233 U.S. companies, computed weekly.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) Three years later, it developed a 90-stock index, computed daily.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) In 1941, Poor's Publishing merged with Standard Statistics Company to form [Standard & Poor's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s "Standard & Poor's").[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-history-21)[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-22)

On Monday, March 4, 1957, the index was expanded to its current extent of 500 companies and was renamed the S&P 500 Stock Composite Index.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) In 1962, Ultronic Systems became the compiler of the S&P indices including the S&P 500 Stock Composite Index, the 425 Stock Industrial Index, the 50 Stock Utility Index, and the 25 Stock Rail Index.[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-23) On August 31, 1976, [The Vanguard Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Vanguard_Group "The Vanguard Group") offered the first [mutual fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutual_fund "Mutual fund") to [retail investors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retail_investor "Retail investor") that tracked the index.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) On April 21, 1982, the [Chicago Mercantile Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Mercantile_Exchange "Chicago Mercantile Exchange") began trading futures based on the index.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) On July 1, 1983, [Chicago Board Options Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Board_Options_Exchange "Chicago Board Options Exchange") began trading [options](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Option_(finance) "Option (finance)") based on the index.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) Beginning in 1986, the index value was updated every 15 seconds, or 1,559 times per trading day, with price updates disseminated by [Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters"). Prior to this, it had been updated once every minute.[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-24)

On January 22, 1993, the Standard & Poor's Depositary Receipts [exchange-traded fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund "Exchange-traded fund") issued by [State Street Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_Street_Corporation "State Street Corporation") began trading.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) On September 9, 1997, [CME Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CME_Group "CME Group") introduced the S&P E-mini futures contract.[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-keydates-1) In 2005, the index transitioned to a public float-adjusted capitalization-weighting.[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-25) Friday, September 17, 2021, was the final trading date for the original SP big contract which began trading in 1982.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-26)

## Selection criteria

\[ [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=S%26P_500&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Selection criteria")\]

Like other indices managed by [S&P Dow Jones Indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_Dow_Jones_Indices "S&P Dow Jones Indices"), but unlike indices such as the [Russell 1000 Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell_1000_Index "Russell 1000 Index") which are strictly rule-based, the components of the S&P 500 are selected by a committee. When considering the eligibility of a new addition, the committee assesses the company's merit using the following primary criteria:[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-S&P500Methodology-3)

1. [Market capitalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_capitalization "Market capitalization") \- Market capitalization must be greater than or equal to US$22.7 billion (effective on July 1, 2025).[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-Guidelines-27) These market cap eligibility criteria are for addition to an index, not for continued membership. As a result, an index constituent that appears to violate criteria for addition to that index is not removed unless ongoing conditions warrant an index change.[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-Guidelines-27)
2. [Market liquidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Market_liquidity "Market liquidity") and [public float](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_float "Public float") – Annual dollar value traded to float-adjusted market capitalization is greater than 0.75.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-sp20230104-28)
3. [Volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_(finance) "Volume (finance)") – Minimum monthly trading volume of 250,000 shares in each of the six months leading up to the evaluation date
4. [Stock exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stock_exchange "Stock exchange") – Must be publicly listed on the [New York Stock Exchange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange "New York Stock Exchange") (including [NYSE Arca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NYSE_Arca "NYSE Arca") or [NYSE American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NYSE_American "NYSE American")), [Nasdaq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasdaq "Nasdaq") (Nasdaq Global Select Market, Nasdaq Select Market or the Nasdaq Capital Market) or [Cboe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cboe_Global_Markets "Cboe Global Markets") (Cboe BZX, Cboe BYX, Cboe EDGA or Cboe EDGX).
5. [Domicile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domicile_(law) "Domicile (law)") – The company must have its primary listing on a U.S. exchange.[\[29\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-29)
6. Securities that are ineligible for inclusion in the index are [limited partnerships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_partnership "Limited partnership"), [master limited partnerships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_limited_partnership "Master limited partnership") and their [investment trust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investment_trust "Investment trust") units, [OTC Bulletin Board](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OTC_Bulletin_Board "OTC Bulletin Board") issues, [closed-end funds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-end_fund "Closed-end fund"), [exchange-traded funds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange-traded_fund "Exchange-traded fund"), [Exchange-traded notes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exchange-traded_note "Exchange-traded note"), [royalty trusts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royalty_trust "Royalty trust"), [tracking stocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracking_stock "Tracking stock"), [preferred stock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferred_stock "Preferred stock"), [unit trusts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_trust "Unit trust"), [equity warrants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Warrant_(finance) "Warrant (finance)"), [convertible bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convertible_bond "Convertible bond"), [investment trusts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Investment_trust "Investment trust"), [American depositary receipts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_depositary_receipt "American depositary receipt"), and [American depositary shares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_depositary_share "American depositary share").[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-S&P500Methodology-3)

Net income from continuing operations must also be positive for the most recent quarter, and the sum of the most recent four consecutive quarters.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-S&P500Methodology-3)

A stock may rise in value when it is added to the index since [index funds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_fund "Index fund") must purchase that stock to continue tracking the index.[\[30\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-30)[\[31\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-31)

A study published by the [National Bureau of Economic Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Economic_Research "National Bureau of Economic Research") in October 2021 alleged that companies' purchases of ratings services from [S&P Global](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_Global "S&P Global") appear to improve their chance of entering the S&P 500, even if they are not the best fit per the rules.[\[32\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-32)[\[33\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-33)

While the index has declined in several years by over 30%,[\[34\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-34) it has posted annual increases 70% of the time,[\[35\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-love-35) with 5% of all trading days resulting in record highs.[\[36\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-36)

Returns are generally quoted as [price returns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_return "Price return") (excluding returns from dividends). However, they can also be quoted as [total return](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_return "Total return"), which includes returns from [dividends](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dividend "Dividend") and the reinvestment thereof, and "net total return", which reflects the effects of dividend reinvestment after the deduction of [withholding tax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Withholding_tax "Withholding tax").[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%26P_500#cite_note-sp500r-2)

## 8 Important Things to Know About the S&P 500

09/23/2025

By: Fidelity Bank

![8 Important Things to Know About the S&P 500 ](https://cdn.firstbranchcms.com/kcms-structure/7b79f90f-415d-46d5-b518-90fe22cadf01/postImage.png)

The S&P 500 is one of the most scrutinized indicators of U.S. stock market health. Representing 500 leading companies, this index offers a snapshot of market performance and investor sentiment. Whether you’re new to investing or looking to deepen your understanding, here are eight key facts about this influential financial benchmark.

### It Tracks Large-Cap U.S. Companies

The S&P 500 includes 500 of the most prominent publicly traded companies in the U.S., large-cap businesses with substantial market value and established track records. For investors, it acts as a baseline to compare individual stock or portfolio performance against a broad slice of the market on major exchanges like the NYSE and Nasdaq.

### It’s Market Cap-Weighted

Not all S&P 500 companies carry equal weight. The index uses market capitalization weighting, which means larger companies have more influence on the index’s movement. Market cap is calculated by multiplying a company’s stock price by its total number of shares. A big swing in the price of a major tech stock can move the entire index more than a smaller company’s gains or losses.

### It Spans a Range of Industries

The S&P 500 is designed to reflect the U.S. economy, which means it includes companies from many sectors like technology, healthcare, finance, consumer goods, and energy. This built-in diversification means the index doesn’t rely too heavily on any single industry, giving investors balanced exposure to different parts of the economy through one investment vehicle.

### It’s Always Evolving

The companies within the S&P 500 are not set in stone. A committee reviews and updates the list based on criteria like market cap, liquidity, and industry classification. Companies can be added when they meet these standards or removed when they no longer qualify or undergo major corporate changes like mergers. This dynamic approach helps the index stay current and relevant as the economy evolves.

### You Can’t Buy It Directly

Since the S&P 500 is a statistical measure rather than an actual stock, you cannot directly purchase shares of the index itself. However, there are ways to invest in its performance. Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and mutual funds are the most common options, holding the same stocks as the index in similar proportions. These products offer convenient and typically cost-effective ways to achieve diversified exposure to America’s largest companies.

### Past Performance Isn’t a Promise

The S&P 500 has historically delivered competitive long-term returns, making it a cornerstone of many investment strategies. However, this success does not guarantee future results. Market conditions change based on economic, political, and global events. Historical trends can offer valuable insights when shaping your investment strategy, if you stay mindful of these risks.

### It Mirrors Economic Momentum

The S&P 500’s performance closely tracks the overall health of the U.S. economy. Multiple factors influence its direction, including economic growth rates, inflation levels, interest rates set by the Federal Reserve, corporate earnings, and geopolitical events. Positive economic indicators generally help boost the index’s valuations, while negative economic news can create market uncertainty and potential declines.

### Dividends Matter Too

In addition to stock price movement, the S&P 500’s total return also includes dividends paid by its companies to shareholders. Over time, these dividend payments can be a substantial portion of overall returns, and reinvesting them can significantly boost an investor’s long-term portfolio.

Understanding the S&P 500 helps investors gain insight into broader market movements and shape smarter financial strategies. Whether you’re directly investing or simply observing trends, knowing how this index works empowers better decision-making.
