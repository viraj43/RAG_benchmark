It is a unique legal framework designed to provide exclusive rights to inventors for their novel, new and useful process, or non-obvious inventions. This exclusivity period, usually 20 years from the filing date of a patent application, ensures that the patent owner has the legal right to prevent others from making, using, selling, or importing the patented invention without permission. Central to the administration of patent law in the United States is the patent office, the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO). This federal agency examines patent applications, issues patents, and maintains a database of issued patents. The role of the USPTO extends to enforcing federal law as it relates to patents, and it plays a critical part in determining the validity of patent claims. While the USPTO reviews all applications, not all result in issued patents. Should an application face final rejection, the applicant can appeal to the Patent Trial and Appeal Board (PTAB), thus ensuring the patent system offers opportunities for reconsideration and fair judgment in patent appeals. Patent law is a multifaceted legal field that revolves around the granting, enforcement, and protection of patents. Importantly, patentable subject matter must fall into at least one of the four statutory categories defined in the Patent Act: process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter. However, not all subject matter is eligible for patent protection. Federal law excludes abstract ideas, laws of nature, and physical phenomena from patentability.
