Title: consumer protection laws | Wex | US Law | LII / Legal Information ...
Historically, under the common law doctrine of caveat emptor (“let the buyer beware”), consumers had little recourse against misleading or one-sided sales. Modern consumer protection regimes developed to address these limits, particularly in adhesion contracts where common law fraud doctrines offered limited remedies. In the United States, consumer protection is governed by both federal and state law. At the federal level, the Federal Trade Commission Act prohibits “unfair or deceptive acts or practices.” The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) regulates lending, credit, and related financial services under the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act. Specialized statutes provide additional protections, including the Truth in Lending Act, the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, and the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act for product safety. States supplement federal law through their own consumer protection statutes and enforcement by state attorneys general. Although these laws provide remedies, enforcement often depends on administrative agencies because individual consumers face cost and time barriers in bringing claims. Since the 1970s, however, federal and state agencies have significantly expanded enforcement efforts, making consumer protection a central feature of modern regulatory law. Title: What is the California Consumer Protection Act?
California's consumer protection laws, notably the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and the Consumers' Legal Remedies Act (CLRA), empower consumers by giving them control over their personal data and banning deceptive practices. Additional laws cover areas such as auto fraud (lemon laws), automatic subscription renewals, and the dissemination of private information (do-not-track). California has historically led in consumer protection by establishing strict laws to defend residents against unfair, deceptive, and fraudulent business practices. The CCPA provides protections against false advertising, fraud, and other dishonest business conduct. It enables consumers to file individual or class action lawsuits to seek damages and halt illegal activities. In an era often referred to as the era of data as the new oil, California has defended consumer privacy with the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and its subsequent amendments and expansions, such as the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA). These laws establish a clear framework to protect consumer data and promote transparency in its management and use.
